*Zuhn Intelligence Brief · Issue 1*

Your company's knowledge base is growing faster than anyone is reading it.

That isn't the problem. The problem is that more growth will make it worse.

Every knowledge tool bought in the last five years — Notion, Confluence, Guru, Glean, Perplexity Enterprise, the RAG layer bolted onto your AI copilot — is a retrieval system. They index what you wrote and help you find it again. "10,000 docs, searchable in under a second." That is impressive, and it is also the wrong problem.

The problem is compression.

## Retrieval vs. compression

Retrieval returns information: *"Here's what marketing said about pricing in Q2 2024."*

Compression returns judgment: *"When margins tightened, pricing decisions stopped being made in marketing and moved to finance — which is why the Q3 repricing stalled."*

The first makes you informed. The second makes you decide. These are not the same output, and no amount of better retrieval produces the second one.

Every major institutional-memory product is optimizing retrieval quality. Most enterprise buyers never realized they wanted compression.

## The pattern shows up everywhere

Richard Feynman kept a notebook. It wasn't a transcript of every physics lecture he attended. It was a small, compressed catalog of unsolved problems and the principles he was using to attack them.

When he met a new problem, he ran it through the notebook. The notebook wasn't searchable by keyword — it was *usable as a judgment tool*. That is a compressed knowledge base. It is why Feynman was Feynman.

Military after-action reviews. The transcript isn't the artifact. The *lesson* is. Every well-run AAR ends with three to five sentences that become doctrine, and that doctrine gets taught to every new unit.

The two-hour debrief that produced them is archived and rarely re-read. The distilled doctrine is what compounds.

Boards of directors. The meeting minutes are retrieval; the board memo is compression. Read any well-run company's board memos and you'll find 80% of that year's decisions folded into five to ten durable principles the board references at every subsequent meeting.

Read the minutes of the same year and you'll find two hundred pages of motions and discussions nobody opens again.

Naval Ravikant's essays versus his tweets. The essays are retrieval-optimized — long, searchable, complete. The tweets are compression-optimized — short, durable, citable. Ask a hundred people what Naval taught them and they'll cite a tweet, not an essay.

The pattern isn't about the amount of content. It's about what happens to content after it's captured.

## Why every AI tool is making this worse

Enterprise AI adoption carries an unspoken assumption: if we can retrieve everything faster with semantic search, we'll make better decisions.

But faster retrieval over a *compressed* corpus beats faster retrieval over an uncompressed one by a lot. And most companies' corpora are uncompressed. The AI tool speeds you to the ten thousand docs. It doesn't reduce the ten thousand docs into fifty usable principles.

The tell: Microsoft has 440 million Office users and roughly 8 million Copilot payers. The technology is real. The retrieval is fine. The compression layer is missing — which is why most Copilot pilots don't convert.

Users don't want another interface to their ten thousand docs. They want a company brain that has already decided what was worth keeping.

The same pattern ran through ad-tech fifteen years ago. Everyone had impression data. Nobody could measure attribution. The market couldn't mature until DoubleClick and Comscore built the attribution layer.

The "we have all the data" era ended when the "we understand what the data means" era began.

We're in the retrieval era of AI. The compression layer isn't built yet.

## What a compression-first knowledge system actually does

Four commitments, all of which most retrieval tools skip.

**1. Every captured item is a directional claim, not a location.**

"The Q2 pricing deck" is a pointer. "Raising prices 15% in Q2 lost us top-of-funnel but converted more mid-market" is a claim that can be cited, contested, or falsified. One is a filing cabinet entry. The other is a decision input.

**2. Items reduce upward.**

Fifty meeting notes become one principle. The principle becomes testable. Tested principles become doctrine. Doctrine is what you hand a new hire in week 2 — not a wiki URL.

**3. Old items retire.**

A principle that was load-bearing in 2023 can be drag in 2026. Compression-first systems actively retire decisions that stop being useful. Retrieval-first systems compound them forever, quietly pulling every future decision toward reasoning that no longer applies.

**4. Predictions carry track records.**

You wrote down what you believed last quarter. The system tells you whether it came true. This is the piece every current knowledge tool skips, and it is the piece that turns memory into *judgment* rather than archive.

Run your current knowledge tool through those four. If the answer is "none," you are paying for a library that grows faster than anyone can read.

## Where the next budget line is

The first AI tool to do compression at the team level — not retrieval-plus-sparkle but real distillation, with retirement and track records — will be the first durable winner in this category.

It will demo less impressively than the retrieval tools, because nothing shines on stage like semantic search over ten thousand documents.

But in year two, the compression tool's users will remember last year's decisions. The retrieval tool's users will still be searching for them.

Your knowledge base is not a library. It is either a judgment engine or a trap.

Most of them, right now, are traps.
