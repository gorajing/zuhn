Aaron Levie argues that enterprise startups should intentionally start small — finding a discrete, painful problem that slips between the gaps of incumbent products. The key is making the user experience on that narrow slice incredible, then expanding either up-market to larger customers or outward to adjacent use cases over time. Incumbents will initially dismiss the wedge as too small or limited, which is exactly the advantage.

This is the pattern Zen Payroll (now Gusto) followed: they took the most painful slice of HR — payroll for small businesses — made it dead simple, then expanded into benefits, onboarding, and larger company sizes. The wedge works because enterprises adopt discrete solutions that solve immediate pain faster than they replace entire technology stacks. The startup's job is to pick a wedge that naturally expands into a large platform opportunity.
