By stocking only 4,000 items compared to a supermarket's 30,000 or Walmart's 140,000, Costco creates artificial scarcity of shelf space. Suppliers compete fiercely for those limited slots, willingly lowering prices and even re-engineering packaging (like the cashew container redesign saving 24,000 pallets annually).

This is a compounding advantage: fewer SKUs means higher volume per item, which means more leverage per supplier relationship, which means lower costs, which means higher renewal rates, which means more members, which means even higher volume. The constraint that appears to limit the business actually powers a flywheel. When the store brand Kirkland Signature captures 25% of sales, it serves as a credible threat that further disciplines supplier pricing.
