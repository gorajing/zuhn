By limiting selection to one or two choices per category, Costco concentrates its entire purchasing volume into a tiny number of suppliers. Each supplier slot becomes enormously valuable—suppliers will lower prices and even reengineer products (like redesigning cashew containers to save 24,000 pallets/year) just to win or keep that position. This is the opposite of the marketplace model where infinite selection dilutes leverage.

The compounding effect is key: fewer SKUs also simplify store operations (easier stocking, no back rooms), reduce decision fatigue for shoppers, and enable the warehouse format that further cuts costs. What looks like a limitation is actually a strategic choice that creates advantages across multiple dimensions simultaneously. If Costco is unsatisfied with suppliers, it creates Kirkland Signature products—the credible threat of vertical integration further strengthens its negotiating position.
