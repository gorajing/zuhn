Paul Graham recounts a VC who invested in founders whose business model he expected to change three times — and these were experienced repeat founders backed by a top firm. The business model at early stage is a collection of guesses about domains founders typically lack expertise in, while the problem and product represent areas where founders have genuine insight and credibility.

The damage is asymmetric: a spectacularly wrong business model will become the memorable thing about your pitch, overwriting the substantive content investors should retain. Meanwhile, even a plausible model adds little signal because investors already discount it. This creates a clear information-theoretic argument for minimizing business model discussion and maximizing time on what you actually know.
