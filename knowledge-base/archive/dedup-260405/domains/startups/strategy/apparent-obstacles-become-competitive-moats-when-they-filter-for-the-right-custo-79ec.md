Costco's membership fee, warehouse layout, and bulk-only format look like obstacles to mainstream retail success. But each constraint serves a dual purpose: the $60 fee selects for households earning ~$100K average income; the confusing layout forces wandering through aisles (increasing discovery and spend); bulk quantities attract business owners who account for two-thirds of sales despite being one-third of members.

This is a general pattern in strategy: friction that filters out low-value customers while deepening engagement with high-value ones creates a self-reinforcing moat. The fee creates sunk-cost loyalty, the layout increases basket size, and the warehouse aesthetic signals authenticity rather than marketing overhead. Each 'obstacle' would be fatal for a mass-market retailer but becomes a competitive advantage when the business model is designed around a specific customer segment.
