RuBisCo, the enzyme responsible for carbon fixation in photosynthesis, is remarkably bad at its job — it confuses oxygen for CO2 roughly half the time, producing toxic byproducts. Yet plants never evolved a replacement. Instead, they produce ~40 billion tons of it globally and evolved workaround pathways for the toxic waste.

This maps directly to startup strategy: an early, imperfect solution that achieves market dominance creates path dependence so strong that switching to a better alternative becomes prohibitively expensive. The lesson isn't to avoid technical debt — it's that speed-to-market with a functional-but-flawed approach can create lock-in that outlasts any theoretical superior competitor. The key is that RuBisCo was 'a heck of a lot better than just hoping to run into some chemically formed organic carbon' — it didn't need to be good, just better than the alternative at the time.
