RuBisCo evolved when atmospheric CO2 was abundant and oxygen scarce, making its sloppiness irrelevant. Plants went 'all-in' on this enzyme and took over the planet. When oxygen levels rose and RuBisCo started confusing O2 for CO2 half the time, plants couldn't swap it out — they were too deeply committed. Instead, they evolved elaborate workarounds (photorespiration pathways, C4 and CAM photosynthesis) to compensate.

This mirrors how companies and platforms accumulate technical debt around a core architecture that worked in early conditions but becomes a liability at scale. The deeper the integration and the more success built on top, the harder it is to replace the foundation — even when it's demonstrably inefficient. The rational response becomes producing more of the bad thing (40 billion tons of RuBisCo) and building compensating systems, rather than redesigning from scratch.
