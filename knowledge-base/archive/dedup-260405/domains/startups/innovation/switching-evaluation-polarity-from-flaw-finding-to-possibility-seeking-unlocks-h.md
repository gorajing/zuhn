Graham distinguishes between merely lowering standards and genuinely switching polarity on how you evaluate new ideas. Most people default to listing reasons an idea won't work, which is statistically safe — most new ideas do fail. But in a power-law distribution of outcomes, the cost of missing the rare massive success dwarfs the cost of entertaining many failures.

This is why Y Combinator's approach works: they train themselves to ask 'how could this succeed?' rather than 'why will this fail?' It's not naivety — it's a calibrated response to the actual payoff structure. The skeptics are right more often in raw frequency, but catastrophically wrong when weighted by outcome. This polarity switch is a learnable skill, not a personality trait, which means organizations can deliberately cultivate it.
