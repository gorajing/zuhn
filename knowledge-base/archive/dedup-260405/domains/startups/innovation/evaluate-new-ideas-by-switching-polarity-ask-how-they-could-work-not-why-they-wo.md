Graham argues the right mental model for evaluating new ideas is polarity reversal: instead of the default posture of listing reasons something won't work, actively try to imagine ways it could succeed. This isn't naive optimism — it's calibrated to the statistical reality that in power-law domains like startups, the cost of missing the outlier vastly exceeds the cost of backing failures.

Silicon Valley has institutionalized this as a cultural norm through decades of practice, and the results validate the approach: despite accusations of being too credulous, the track record measured by outcomes (not prediction accuracy) shows this optimistic evaluation outperforms skeptical default postures. The key insight is that seeming smart by dismissing ideas is rewarded socially but punished economically in power-law environments.
