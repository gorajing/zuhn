Most people's default when encountering a new idea is to assess it against the standard of finished work and list reasons it will fail. Paul Graham argues this isn't just too harsh — it's the wrong polarity entirely. The right approach is to actively try to imagine how the idea could succeed, treating it as a challenge to your imagination.

This is not naive optimism or lowered standards. In a world with power-law distributions of outcomes, the cost of dismissing a future giant far exceeds the cost of spending time on ideas that fail. Silicon Valley learned this through the economic pressure of venture investing — missing the needle in the haystack is painfully obvious. The practical lesson extends beyond startups: in any domain where new ideas have high variance in outcomes, cultivating active imaginative engagement beats cautious skepticism.
