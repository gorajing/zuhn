Walker Williams quantifies an asymmetry most founders ignore: a single person saying 'don't use those guys' can undo the work of ten happy advocates. He describes Teespring reprinting entire orders — sometimes half their monthly GMV — when colors were slightly wrong or sizes were off. The instinct is to rationalize ('it's only a little off'), but the correct move is to absorb the cost and make it right every time. The counterintuitive payoff is that the most frustrated customers, once their problem is genuinely resolved, tend to become the strongest champions and longest-term users. This creates a growth flywheel: problems are inevitable in startups, but the response to problems — not the absence of them — determines whether your user base compounds or churns. This applies beyond startups to any product where word-of-mouth matters.
