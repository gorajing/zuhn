Graham describes a specific mental discipline practiced at Y Combinator: when confronted with a strange-sounding idea from an unknown person, deliberately invert the default critical stance. Instead of listing reasons it won't work, actively try to think of ways it could. This is not naive optimism but a rational response to power-law distributions where missing one outlier is catastrophically expensive.

This polarity switch is a learnable skill, not a personality trait. Graham notes that Silicon Valley's culture of encouraging new ideas started as self-interested behavior (investors want founders to succeed) but has evolved into genuine custom. The broader implication is that any community or individual can train themselves into this evaluation mode, and the economic returns from doing so are large enough that the practice tends to spread wherever it takes root.
