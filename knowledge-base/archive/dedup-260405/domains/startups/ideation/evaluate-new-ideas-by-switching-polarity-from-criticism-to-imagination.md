Most people evaluate new ideas by scanning for flaws—a habit inherited from judging finished work. Paul Graham argues the correct approach is a complete polarity reversal: actively searching for ways the idea could succeed. This isn't naive optimism or lowered standards; it's a different cognitive operation entirely.

This skill becomes urgent in environments with power-law outcomes (like venture investing) where the cost of dismissing one great idea dwarfs the cost of entertaining many bad ones. Silicon Valley has institutionalized this polarity switch, and its outsized returns suggest this evaluation mode is better calibrated to reality than default skepticism.
