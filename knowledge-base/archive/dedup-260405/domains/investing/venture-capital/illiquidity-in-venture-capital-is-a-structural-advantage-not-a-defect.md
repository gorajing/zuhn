Illiquidity in venture capital is commonly perceived as a drawback — investors lock up capital for 7-10+ years with limited ability to exit. However, this illiquidity functions as a structural advantage. It filters the investor base to those with genuine long-term orientation, reducing pressure for premature exits or short-term optimization.

The locked-up structure also enables founders to pursue ambitious, long-horizon strategies that would be impossible under quarterly earnings pressure. Companies can invest in R&D, build network effects, and tolerate early losses knowing their capital base won't flee. For LPs, the illiquidity premium — the excess return earned for accepting illiquidity — has historically been substantial in top-tier venture funds. The inability to panic-sell during downturns paradoxically protects investors from their own behavioral biases.
