The conventional view treats illiquidity as a cost that investors must be compensated for via an illiquidity premium. But there is a deeper mechanism at work: illiquidity functions as a commitment device. Because LPs cannot sell their positions during drawdowns or periods of uncertainty, they are structurally prevented from the panic-selling and performance-chasing behaviors that destroy returns in public markets.

This reframes illiquidity from a bug to a feature — it is the structural equivalent of locking yourself out of your brokerage account during a crash. The implication is that part of the 'illiquidity premium' in VC is actually a 'behavioral discipline premium' — returns that were always available but that liquid-market participants forfeit through their own trading behavior.
