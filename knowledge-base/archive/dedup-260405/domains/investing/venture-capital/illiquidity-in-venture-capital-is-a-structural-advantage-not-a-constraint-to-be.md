Liquidity is typically framed as a benefit—the ability to exit, rebalance, or cut losses. But in venture capital, illiquidity enforces behaviors that systematically improve outcomes. Investors can't panic-sell during downturns, can't be distracted by short-term price signals, and are forced to hold through the J-curve that precedes most outsized returns.

This reframing has broader applicability: constraints that appear limiting often encode discipline that unconstrained actors fail to maintain voluntarily. The locked-up capital in venture forces a time horizon alignment between investor and company that liquid markets structurally cannot achieve. It also creates a selection effect—investors who are comfortable with illiquidity tend to be more sophisticated and longer-term oriented, which further compounds returns.
