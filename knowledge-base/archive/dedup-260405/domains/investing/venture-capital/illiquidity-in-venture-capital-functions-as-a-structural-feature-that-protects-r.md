Public market investors routinely sell their best-performing positions too early due to behavioral biases like loss aversion and profit-taking impulses. Venture capital's forced illiquidity removes this option — LPs cannot exit their position in a fund mid-cycle even if they wanted to. This structural constraint acts as a behavioral guardrail.

The compounding effect is significant: if a fund's best company returns 100x over 10 years, an investor who could have sold at 5x in year 4 might have done so. Illiquidity ensures they hold through the full growth arc. The 'bug' framing misses that the illiquidity premium isn't just compensation for inconvenience — it's partly the mechanism that generates the outsized returns in the first place.
