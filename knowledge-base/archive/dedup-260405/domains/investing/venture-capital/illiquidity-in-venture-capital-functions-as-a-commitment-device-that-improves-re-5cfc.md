Illiquidity in venture capital is typically framed as a cost investors must tolerate, but it can be reframed as a structural advantage. By making it difficult or impossible to sell positions during downturns or uncertainty, illiquidity prevents the behavioral mistakes that destroy returns in liquid markets — panic selling, overreacting to short-term signals, and anchoring to temporary valuations.

This mirrors a broader principle in behavioral finance: removing optionality at the right moments can improve outcomes by eliminating the temptation to act on noise. For founders, this means VC investors are structurally locked into long time horizons, which aligns incentives around building durable value rather than optimizing for short-term metrics.
