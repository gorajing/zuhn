One leading theory for the Indus Valley Civilization's decline is that a massive earthquake altered river courses, drying up tributaries that sustained irrigation. Despite having advanced urban planning, trade networks, and sanitation superior to 18th-century Europe, the civilization couldn't survive the loss of its water infrastructure. The most calories-per-acre region on the planet became uninhabitable.

This pattern maps directly to modern economic concentration risk. Cities built around single industries (Detroit/auto, Aberdeen/oil), countries dependent on one export commodity, or tech companies reliant on a single platform (iOS App Store, Google Search rankings) all face the same non-linear fragility. The sophistication of your other systems is irrelevant if your foundational resource disappears. Diversification of critical dependencies isn't just prudent — it's existential.
