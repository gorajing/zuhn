Conventional wisdom treats overconfidence as a pure cognitive bias to be corrected. Graham observes that across many fields, the most successful people are slightly overconfident — and argues this isn't despite the miscalibration but because of it. The error compensates for two opposing forces: other people's skepticism and your own tendency to judge early work too harshly.

This reframes overconfidence not as a flaw but as a counterweight in a system with multiple biases. Similarly, Hardy's advice to 'exaggerate a little both the importance of your subject and your importance in it' works because the overestimate of the goal's value offsets the underestimate of partial progress. The takeaway isn't to be delusional, but that perfect calibration in an environment full of discouragement is actually suboptimal — you need a slight positive offset to stay in motion.
