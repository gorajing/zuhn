Most people assume optimal performance comes from perfectly calibrated self-assessment. Graham observes that across many fields, the most successful people are slightly overconfident — not delusionally so, but enough to offset two distinct headwinds: other people's skepticism and their own harsh self-judgment.

This works because the errors compound in different directions. If you're slightly overconfident, that single bias counterbalances two separate forces pulling you toward quitting. The expected value of your actions remains roughly correct even though your self-assessment is technically wrong. This reframes overconfidence not as a bug but as a compensating mechanism — a psychological shock absorber for the inevitable friction of doing anything new.
