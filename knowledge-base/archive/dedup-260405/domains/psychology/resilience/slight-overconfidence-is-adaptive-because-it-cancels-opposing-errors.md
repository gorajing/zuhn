Most people assume optimal performance comes from perfectly calibrated self-assessment. Graham argues the opposite: slight overconfidence is adaptive because creators face two systematic downward biases—other people's skepticism and their own harsh judgment of early work. Overconfidence acts as a counterweight that nets out to roughly accurate expected-value calculations.

This reframes overconfidence not as a character flaw but as a functional error-correction mechanism. The implication is that founders and creators should not try to eliminate overconfidence entirely; they should recognize it as load-bearing scaffolding during the fragile early phase of ambitious projects. The danger only emerges when overconfidence persists past the point where real feedback should recalibrate expectations.
