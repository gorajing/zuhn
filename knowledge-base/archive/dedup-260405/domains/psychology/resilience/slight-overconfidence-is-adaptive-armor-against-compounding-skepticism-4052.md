Graham observes that across many fields, the most successful people are slightly overconfident — not delusionally so, but enough to create a buffer. This seems paradoxical since perfectly calibrated self-assessment should be optimal. The resolution is that overconfidence compensates for two error sources pulling in the opposite direction: other people's tendency to dismiss new ideas (especially from unknown people) and your own tendency to judge early work too harshly. The errors roughly cancel out. This isn't about ignoring feedback — it's about having enough psychological armor to survive the period where the work genuinely looks bad to everyone, including yourself.
