Every ambitious project has a stage where it looks unimpressive even to its creator. This is not a sign of failure — it's a structural feature of doing anything genuinely new. The danger is that most people judge early work by the standards of finished work, causing them to abandon projects that would have become great if continued.

The practical implication is that tolerating this discomfort is a core skill, not a personality trait. People who produce great work aren't immune to the cringe of early efforts — they've simply developed mechanisms to push through it. Recognizing the ugly duckling phase as universal and temporary reframes early-stage embarrassment from a stop signal into a progress indicator.
