The most successful people in many fields are slightly overconfident — not delusionally so, but enough to persist through the ugly early stages of ambitious work. This seems paradoxical since accurate self-assessment should theoretically be optimal, but it makes sense as error correction: early work gets judged too harshly by both the creator and observers, so a small positive bias in self-belief compensates for these systematic negative biases. The key word is 'slightly' — enough to keep going, not enough to ignore real feedback.

This reframes overconfidence not as a character flaw but as a calibration tool. The optimal level of self-belief isn't accurate — it's slightly above accurate, because the environment systematically pushes the other way. This is especially relevant for founders, artists, and researchers in the earliest phases of ambitious work where external validation is minimal.
