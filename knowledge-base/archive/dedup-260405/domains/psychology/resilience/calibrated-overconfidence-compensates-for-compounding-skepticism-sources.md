Most people assume optimal performance comes from perfectly accurate self-assessment, but Paul Graham observes that the most successful people across many fields are slightly overconfident. This isn't irrational — it's compensatory. Creators face skepticism from two independent sources: other people's dismissiveness and their own harsh self-judgment. These compound, creating a net-negative bias that kills projects prematurely. Slight overconfidence doesn't make you delusional; it simply offsets the systematic downward pressure that would otherwise cause you to quit too early.

The practical implication is that if you find yourself accurately assessing your early work as mediocre, you're probably already too pessimistic — because that accurate assessment, combined with others' skepticism, produces a net evaluation that's below the true expected value of continuing.
