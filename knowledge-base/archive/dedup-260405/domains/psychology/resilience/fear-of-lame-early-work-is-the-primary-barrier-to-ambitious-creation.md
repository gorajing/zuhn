Great projects almost universally pass through a stage where they look unimpressive even to their creators. The natural human response is to judge early work by the standards of finished work, which makes it look terrible by comparison. This misjudgment kills projects before they have a chance to develop. The key insight is that this fear is not deeply rooted — it's a relatively recent cultural habit born from inexperience with the creation process. Recognizing that lame-looking early versions of ambitious projects are genuinely more valuable than they appear is the first step to overcoming the block.

The practical implication is that creators need deliberate strategies to suspend their inner critic during the formative phase — not permanently, but temporarily, like a painkiller turns off pain without eliminating the pain system entirely.
