Naively, perfect calibration of one's abilities should be optimal. But Graham observes that across many fields, the most successful people are slightly overconfident. The resolution is that overconfidence compensates for multiple independent sources of downward bias: your own harsh judgment of early results, other people's skepticism, and the structural difficulty of evaluating incomplete ambitious work.

This creates a practical heuristic: if you overestimate the importance of your project (treating a goal worth 100 as worth 200) and your early results look like 10% instead of the actual 20%, these two errors cancel out and your expected-value estimate ends up correct. The implication is that deliberate slight overconfidence — especially about the importance of what you're working on — is a rational strategy, not self-delusion.
