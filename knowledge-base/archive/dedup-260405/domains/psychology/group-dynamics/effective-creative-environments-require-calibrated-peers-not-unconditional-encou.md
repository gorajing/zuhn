Graham makes a counterintuitive point about social support for creative work: simply collecting encouraging people backfires because you learn to discount universal positivity. What you actually need are peers with enough domain expertise and ambition of their own to tell the difference between genuinely promising early work and work that's actually bad.

This is why university departments and research labs work — not because they're supportive environments per se, but because they concentrate people working on similar problems who can provide calibrated rather than unconditional feedback. The implication for building creative environments is that the quality of judgment matters more than the warmth of encouragement. A peer who can see genuine promise in rough work and articulate why is far more valuable than ten cheerleaders.
