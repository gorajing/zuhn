Graham makes a subtle but important distinction: you don't want people who are always encouraging — you'd learn to discount that. You need people who can tell the difference between work that looks bad because it's early and work that looks bad because it's bad. The people best equipped for this judgment are those working on similarly ambitious projects, because they've developed calibration through their own experience with the lame phase. This is why university departments and research labs work — not because of the institution, but because they naturally concentrate people with the right judgment. The practical implication is to actively seek out peers who are themselves building ambitious things, rather than just assembling a support group. Their feedback has signal precisely because they're selective about it.
