The social environment around creative work matters enormously, but simply collecting cheerleaders backfires because you quickly learn to discount unconditional praise. What you actually need are colleagues who can evaluate early work on its potential trajectory rather than its current state — people who can distinguish an ugly duckling from a baby swan. This is a rare and specific skill.

The people best equipped for this judgment are those working on similar ambitious projects themselves, which explains why university departments and research labs create disproportionate creative output. Shared context gives peers the vocabulary and calibration to offer meaningful feedback on nascent work. This also explains why Silicon Valley's culture of optimism actually works despite seeming naive — the optimism is informed by deep pattern-matching from seeing thousands of early-stage projects, not by indiscriminate positivity.
