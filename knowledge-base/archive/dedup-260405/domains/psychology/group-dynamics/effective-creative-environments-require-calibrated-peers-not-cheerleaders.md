Graham makes a crucial distinction in the kind of social support that actually helps ambitious projects survive their early phase. Simply collecting encouraging people doesn't work — you'll learn to discount indiscriminate praise, and it won't help you distinguish your genuinely promising early work from your genuinely bad ideas.

What you need are colleagues working on similarly ambitious projects of their own, because they have the pattern-matching ability to recognize early promise. This is why university departments and research labs are effective — not because of institutional structure, but because they concentrate people with calibrated judgment about early-stage work in a specific domain. The implication is that seeking out peers who are themselves attempting ambitious things is more valuable than seeking out supporters.
