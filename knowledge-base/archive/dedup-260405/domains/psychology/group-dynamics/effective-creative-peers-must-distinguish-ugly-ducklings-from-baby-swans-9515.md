Graham makes a crucial distinction that most advice about 'supportive environments' misses: you don't want people who are always encouraging, because you'll learn to discount that. You need colleagues who can actually distinguish an ugly duckling from a baby swan — people with enough domain context to recognize genuine early-stage promise. The best source of such colleagues is people working on similar ambitious projects of their own.

This explains why university departments and research labs are so effective: they naturally concentrate people with the right combination of ambition, domain knowledge, and stake in honest evaluation. The insight also explains why generic 'supportive' communities often fail to produce great work — they optimize for encouragement rather than accurate early-stage judgment, which paradoxically makes their feedback less useful.
