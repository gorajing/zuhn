Graham makes a crucial distinction about the social environment needed for ambitious work. Simply collecting cheerleaders doesn't work — you learn to discount unconditional encouragement. What you need are colleagues who can actually evaluate early-stage work on its merits, distinguishing genuine potential from genuine mediocrity. The people best equipped for this are those working on similar ambitious projects of their own, because they've calibrated their judgment through direct experience with the ugly-duckling phase. This is why university departments and research labs produce disproportionate output — not because of institutional resources, but because they naturally concentrate people with the specific skill of evaluating early work. The implication is that when building your creative environment, selectivity matters more than positivity.
