Graham observes that across many fields, the most successful people are slightly overconfident — which seems paradoxically suboptimal. The resolution is that perfect calibration isn't actually optimal in environments with multiple error sources. External skepticism and internal self-doubt both push toward undervaluing early work, so a slight bias toward overconfidence corrects the net estimate back toward accuracy.

This is structurally similar to Hardy's advice that good work requires exaggerating the importance of your subject: if you overvalue a goal worth 100 as worth 200, but underestimate 20% progress as 10%, the expected value estimate lands correctly. The errors cancel. This suggests overconfidence isn't a character flaw to correct but a rational compensation strategy in creative work.
