Graham observes that across many fields, the most successful people are slightly overconfident—a finding that seems paradoxical since perfectly calibrated self-assessment should be optimal. The resolution is that overconfidence compensates for two systematic downward biases: other people's skepticism and your own harsh judgment of early work. The net error of slight overconfidence is closer to zero than perfect calibration would be. This reframes overconfidence not as a bug to be corrected but as a useful counterweight in environments where the base rate of discouragement is already too high. Hardy's advice to 'exaggerate a little' the importance of your subject serves the same compensating function.
