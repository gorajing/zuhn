Perfect calibration of one's abilities sounds optimal in theory but ignores the asymmetric error landscape. Early work is systematically undervalued by both the creator and observers, and ambitious projects face social headwinds from people who consciously or unconsciously hope you'll fail. Slight overconfidence acts as a correction factor against these biases.

Hardy's observation that good work is not done by humble men supports this — overestimating both the importance of your work and your own role in it compensates for the mistaken harsh judgment of initial results. If you see 20% progress toward a goal worth 100 but believe it's 10% toward a goal worth 200, your expected value estimate is accidentally correct despite both components being wrong. The error is functional.
