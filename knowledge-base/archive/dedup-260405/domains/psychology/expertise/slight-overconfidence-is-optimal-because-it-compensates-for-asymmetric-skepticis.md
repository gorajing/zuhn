It seems paradoxical that miscalibrated self-assessment could be advantageous—surely accurate self-knowledge is optimal. But Graham argues this ignores that overconfidence corrects for multiple opposing errors: other people's skepticism, your own harsh self-judgment of early work, and the general cultural bias toward dismissing ambitious new ideas.

The mechanism is error compensation, similar to how overestimating a project's importance corrects for undervaluing partial progress. If you're 20% overconfident but face 30% skepticism headwind from yourself and others, you end up closer to rational behavior than someone with perfectly calibrated self-assessment who gets crushed by the same headwinds. This explains the empirical observation that the most successful people across many fields tend to be slightly overconfident.
