Graham identifies a powerful cognitive reframe: if you think of a risky project as a way to learn rather than a way to make something, then even complete failure produces value. When the problem is sharply defined, failure itself becomes knowledge — a disproven theorem or a structural test that fails under stress both tell you something real about the world.

This reframe works because it changes the payoff matrix. Under the 'performance' frame, outcomes are binary (success or humiliating failure). Under the 'experiment' frame, every outcome falls on a spectrum of useful information. The practical technique is to explicitly declare the learning goal before starting: 'I'm trying to find out whether X works' rather than 'I'm building X.' This shifts your relationship to early results from judgmental to curious, which is exactly the psychological state that sustains ambitious work through its ugly duckling phase.
