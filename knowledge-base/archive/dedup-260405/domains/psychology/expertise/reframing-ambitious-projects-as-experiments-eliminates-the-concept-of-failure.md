Graham argues that one of the most effective ways to push through the lame early phase of ambitious work is to reframe the project as learning rather than production. If the goal is to learn, then a failed project still delivers value — you've ruled something out or discovered a constraint. Lisa Randall's formulation is precise: treat it as an experiment where ruling something out is a valid result, and if there's a way to vary the approach, do that instead of quitting. This reframe is powerful because it attacks the root fear (wasted effort on something embarrassing) by redefining what counts as waste. Combined with curiosity as a motivator, it makes starting risky projects feel low-cost even when the probability of the original vision succeeding is low.
