The International Prototype Kilogram (Big K) and its 40 replicas were made of the same alloy and stored under virtually identical conditions, yet their masses diverged by up to 50 micrograms over decades — with no way to determine which one was 'right' since Big K was the definition by fiat. This is a universal pattern: any standard anchored to a physical thing inherits that thing's instability.

The silicon sphere project solves this by counting atoms to fix Avogadro's constant, making the kilogram a concept rather than an object. The principle generalizes far beyond metrology: in software, hardcoded magic numbers drift as contexts change while derived-from-specification values stay correct. In organizations, standards documented as principles survive better than standards embodied in a single person's behavior. Whenever you can define something by what it IS rather than by pointing at an example of it, you gain robustness against silent drift.
