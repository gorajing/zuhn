When you frame a project as 'making something,' failure means the thing you made is bad. When you frame it as 'learning something,' failure only occurs if you learned nothing — which almost never happens if the problem is sharply defined. Even disproving a hypothesis (the theorem is false, the structural member fails under stress) constitutes genuine knowledge. This reframe isn't just motivational spin; it changes how you actually process outcomes and what you pay attention to during the work.

This connects to the broader pattern that the biggest barrier to ambitious work is psychological, not technical. The experiment frame doesn't change what you do — it changes how you experience doing it, which determines whether you persist through the ugly early phase or quit. Lisa Randall's version adds nuance: treat it as an experiment in that you genuinely abandon directions that are ruled out, but freely vary and iterate on anything that shows promise.
