The experiment frame transforms the risk calculus of ambitious projects. If a project is a bet, failure means wasted time and embarrassment. If it's an experiment, failure means data — you've learned what doesn't work, which is itself valuable knowledge. This is especially powerful when the problem is sharply defined: a disproven theorem or a failed structural test teaches you something concrete.

Curiosity is the purest version of this motivation. Paul Graham describes starting Y Combinator partly out of curiosity about what would happen, and working on Bel (a Lisp dialect) driven by genuine curiosity about the language's inherent shape. Curiosity converts the painful early phase into an interesting one, because you're focused on discovery rather than quality.
