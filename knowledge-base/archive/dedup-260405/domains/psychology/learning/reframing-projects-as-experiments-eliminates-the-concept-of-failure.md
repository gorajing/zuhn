Lisa Randall's insight, amplified by Graham, identifies a powerful reframe: treating new projects as experiments rather than attempts at success. Under this frame, there is no such thing as failure—only results. If you prove a theorem false, or a structural approach inadequate, you've learned something real even though it wasn't what you hoped to learn. The sharper the problem definition, the more valuable even negative results become.

Graham pairs this with curiosity as a motivation engine: 'I like to try new things just to see how they'll turn out.' This isn't a lesser motivation than ambition—it's a more sustainable one, because it's satisfied by any outcome. Y Combinator itself was started in this spirit. The experiment frame works because it's not a trick—it's actually true. Early ambitious work genuinely is more valuable than it appears, and learning from attempts (successful or not) compounds over time. The mind game is getting yourself to believe what is actually the case.
