Graham identifies a sharp asymmetry in how confidence signals are processed. Explicit claims ('we're passionate,' 'our product is great') are filtered out or actively penalized — audiences treat them as empty rhetoric or, worse, as bullshitter markers. But the same underlying message conveyed through body language, demo quality, and the way you talk about the problem lands with full force.

This maps to a broader persuasion principle: declarative claims about yourself activate the listener's skepticism filters, while demonstrated behavior bypasses them entirely. The practical implication extends beyond pitching — in any high-stakes persuasion context (sales, interviews, negotiations), the channel through which confidence arrives determines whether it builds or destroys credibility. The meta-lesson is that if you genuinely believe in what you've built, that belief leaks through naturally; if you don't, explicit claims won't compensate.
