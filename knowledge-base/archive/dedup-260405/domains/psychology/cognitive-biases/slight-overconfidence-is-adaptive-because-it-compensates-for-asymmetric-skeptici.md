Conventional wisdom says optimal performance requires perfectly calibrated self-assessment. Graham argues this ignores the asymmetric error landscape: the world systematically undervalues early-stage ambitious work, and your own internal critic does the same. A small positive bias in self-confidence compensates for these two negative biases, producing a net estimate closer to reality.

This is not an argument for delusion—it's about error correction. The most successful people in many fields tend to be slightly overconfident, not because overconfidence causes success directly, but because it prevents the premature quitting that accurate confidence (combined with a hostile environment) would produce. The key word is 'slightly'—enough to keep going through the ugly phase, not so much that you ignore genuine failure signals.
