Conventional wisdom says accurate self-assessment is ideal, but Graham argues slight overconfidence is actually optimal because it offsets two systematic biases pulling in the opposite direction: external skepticism and internal harsh judgment of early work. If you overestimate the importance of your project by 2x and underestimate your progress by 0.5x, the errors cancel out and your expected-value calculation lands roughly correct.

This reframes overconfidence not as a character flaw but as error-correction firmware. The people who succeed aren't deluded — they're running a compensating bias that happens to produce more accurate net assessments than strict realism would. This also explains why slightly overconfident people dominate across fields: they're not succeeding despite the bias, they're succeeding because of it.
