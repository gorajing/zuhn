Graham observes that the most successful people across many fields are slightly overconfident, which seems paradoxical since perfect calibration should be optimal. The resolution is that overconfidence compensates for multiple independent sources of error that all push in the negative direction: your own harsh judgment of early work, others' skepticism, and the cultural default of dismissing new ideas. If you overestimate the importance of your work by 2x and underestimate its current quality by 2x, these errors cancel out and you arrive at roughly the correct expected value. This means the 'bias' of overconfidence is actually a correction factor in environments where ambient skepticism is systematically too high.
