It seems paradoxical that miscalibrated self-assessment could be advantageous — surely accurate self-knowledge would be optimal. But this ignores that overconfidence doesn't operate in isolation. It counterbalances two other systematic errors: other people's tendency to dismiss unfamiliar ideas, and your own tendency to judge early work by finished-work standards. When these three biases interact, slight overconfidence produces more accurate net behavior than perfect calibration would.

This is a rare case where a cognitive bias is functionally rational. The implication is that if you find yourself feeling slightly too confident about an ambitious project, that's probably the correct operating point — not a flaw to correct. Conversely, if you feel appropriately uncertain, you may actually be too pessimistic given the systematic headwinds.
