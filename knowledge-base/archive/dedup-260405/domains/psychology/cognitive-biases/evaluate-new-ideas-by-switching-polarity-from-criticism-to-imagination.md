Graham distinguishes between merely having lower standards for early work and a fundamentally different evaluation mode: switching polarity. Instead of the default critical stance (listing why an idea won't work), you actively try to imagine how it could work. This isn't naive optimism — it's a learned skill that Y Combinator partners develop through practice with thousands of pitches under power-law outcome distributions.

This polarity switch is particularly important because the default critical mode has an asymmetric error profile. Dismissing ideas is statistically more likely to be correct (most ideas do fail), but the cost of missing the rare winner is enormous. In domains with power-law outcomes, the expected value of optimistic evaluation dramatically exceeds the expected value of skeptical evaluation.
