It seems paradoxical that miscalibrated self-assessment would be advantageous—surely optimal performance requires accurate self-knowledge. But Graham points out that overconfidence compensates for multiple error sources pulling in the opposite direction: other people's skepticism, your own harsh judgment of early work, and the inherent difficulty of evaluating incomplete projects.

This reframes overconfidence not as a cognitive bias to eliminate but as a strategic counterweight. The most successful people in many fields share this trait. The implication is that if you find yourself with perfectly calibrated confidence, you're actually under-armored for the journey—the net effect of all the negative forces you'll encounter means you need a buffer of irrational optimism just to break even.
