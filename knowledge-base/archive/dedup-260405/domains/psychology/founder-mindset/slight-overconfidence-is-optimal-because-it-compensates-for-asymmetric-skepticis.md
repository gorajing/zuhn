Conventional wisdom says optimal performance comes from perfectly accurate self-assessment, but Graham observes that the most successful people across many fields are slightly overconfident. This makes sense when you account for the multiple sources of downward bias: other people's skepticism, your own harsh judgment of early work, and the cultural default of dismissing unproven ideas.

The overconfidence doesn't need to be large — just enough to compensate for these opposing forces. Similarly, overestimating the importance of your project can correct for undervaluing early-stage results. If something is 20% toward a goal worth 100, but you believe it's 10% toward a goal worth 200, your expected-value estimate is accidentally correct despite both components being wrong.
