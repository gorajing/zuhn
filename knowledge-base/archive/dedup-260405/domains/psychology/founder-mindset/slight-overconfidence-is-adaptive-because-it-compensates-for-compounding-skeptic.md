It seems paradoxical that the most successful people across many fields are slightly overconfident rather than perfectly calibrated. The resolution is that overconfidence serves as error correction: early work is systematically undervalued by external skeptics, cultural norms, and your own internal critic. A small positive bias in self-assessment counteracts these multiple negative biases, producing a more accurate net estimate of expected value. Graham formalizes this with a numerical example: if you see 20% progress toward a goal worth 100 but overestimate the goal's value at 200 and underestimate progress at 10%, your expected value calculation accidentally lands on the correct answer. The implication is that perfect calibration is suboptimal when operating in environments with systematic downward bias.
