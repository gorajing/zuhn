It seems paradoxical that miscalibrated self-assessment could be advantageous — surely the optimal strategy is accurate self-knowledge. But Graham points out that overconfidence compensates for multiple error sources in the opposite direction: other people's dismissiveness, your own harsh internal critic, and the systematic tendency to judge early work by finished-work standards.

This reframes overconfidence from a cognitive bias to correct into a useful counterweight to deploy strategically. The key word is 'slightly' — massive overconfidence leads to delusion, but a modest tilt toward believing in yourself more than the evidence strictly warrants creates the persistence needed to survive the early phase of ambitious projects. Hardy made the same observation about mathematicians: the best ones slightly exaggerate both the importance of their subject and their own importance in it.
