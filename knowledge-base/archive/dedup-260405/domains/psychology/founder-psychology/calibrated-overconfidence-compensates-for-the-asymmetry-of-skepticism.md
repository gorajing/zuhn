It seems paradoxical that the most successful people across many fields are slightly overconfident—shouldn't perfect calibration be optimal? Graham argues this is error compensation: the world systematically undervalues early ambitious work through both external skepticism and internal self-doubt. Slight overconfidence doesn't make you delusional; it zeroes out the net bias.

Hardy's related insight is that overestimating the importance of your subject has the same corrective effect. If you see 20% progress toward a goal you believe is worth 2x its actual value, your expected-value calculation accidentally lands on the correct answer. Both biases are wrong individually, but they cancel each other out. The implication for founders is that feeling slightly more confident than the evidence warrants isn't a cognitive flaw to fix—it's adaptive armor.
