Most cultures default to listing reasons why a new idea won't work, and skeptics are statistically more often right about any individual idea. But in a world with power-law distributions of outcomes, being right about the average case while missing the outlier is catastrophically expensive. Silicon Valley learned this through repeated exposure: YC evaluates thousands of strange-sounding ideas every six months, and the asymmetric payoff makes optimism urgent rather than naive.

The deeper insight is that this optimism, originally rooted in self-interest (investors hoping to profit, colleagues hoping to rise with you), has evolved over time into genuine cultural benevolence. The practice of encouraging new ideas became a custom simply because it was practiced so long. This suggests that other regions and fields could deliberately cultivate the same cultural adaptation—and because the trick is so lucrative, Graham predicts it will inevitably spread.
