The fear of making something lame creates a paralyzing catch-22: you can't produce great work without going through a lame phase, but your standards prevent you from tolerating that phase. The hack of calling serious work 'just a sketch' or 'a quick hack' resolves this by temporarily lowering the standard you judge yourself against. Once the project gains momentum and starts improving visibly, you can 'sneakily convert' it into the ambitious thing it always was.

This technique is amplified by choosing media that allow fast iteration with low upfront commitment—digital over physical, notebooks over carved stone. The smaller the gap between starting and seeing initial results, the easier it is to maintain the fiction that you're just experimenting. Patrick Collison extends this further: making the tenuousness itself a feature ('yes, it's ridiculous, I'm just seeing how far this naive approach goes') turns the vulnerability into a strength.
