Early photosynthetic organisms succeeded so thoroughly that their byproduct — oxygen — accumulated and fundamentally changed the atmosphere. But RuBisCo, the enzyme that powered that success, had evolved in a low-oxygen world and couldn't distinguish O2 from CO2. The very output of photosynthesis became the input that degraded photosynthesis.

This pattern recurs in business: a platform's growth attracts the regulators that constrain it, a marketplace's success invites the competitors it trained, a company's hiring spree dilutes the culture that made it great. The mechanism is always the same — success reshapes the environment, and the reshaped environment punishes the strategy that created it. Founders should ask: what second-order environmental change will my success produce, and is my core capability robust to that change?
