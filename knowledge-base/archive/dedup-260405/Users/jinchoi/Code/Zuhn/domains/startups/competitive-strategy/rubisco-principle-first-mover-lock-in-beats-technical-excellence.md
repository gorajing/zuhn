RuBisCo, the most abundant protein on Earth (~40 billion tons), is objectively bad at its core job — it confuses oxygen for carbon dioxide roughly half the time, producing a toxic byproduct plants must waste energy to neutralize. Yet no plant has evolved a replacement in 450 million years because the entire metabolic architecture depends on it.

This is a powerful model for competitive strategy: the first adequate solution that achieves ecosystem-wide adoption becomes nearly impossible to displace, regardless of quality. The cost of switching increases with every dependent system built on top. Startups should recognize that winning the first wave of adoption with a 'good enough' product often matters more than perfecting the product before launch. Conversely, incumbents with deeply flawed but entrenched systems (like legacy codebases or protocols) persist far longer than their technical merits suggest.
