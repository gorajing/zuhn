Graham describes a deliberate polarity inversion in how he evaluates new ideas at Y Combinator: instead of the default mode of finding flaws, he actively tries to imagine how a strange-sounding idea could succeed. This isn't naive optimism — it's a rational response to power-law distributions where one missed winner costs more than thousands of false positives.

This technique applies beyond investing. In any domain with convex payoffs (research, creative work, career bets), the default human tendency toward skepticism is miscalibrated. Skeptics are more often right on any individual prediction but catastrophically wrong when predictions are weighted by outcome. The practical habit is to catch yourself in dismissal mode and consciously switch to 'how could this work?' mode, especially for ideas that sound strange — strangeness correlates with both failure and breakthrough.
