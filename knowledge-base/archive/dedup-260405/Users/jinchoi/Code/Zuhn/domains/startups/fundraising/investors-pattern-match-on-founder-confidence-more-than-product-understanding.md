Most investors at a Demo Day can't deeply evaluate the technical merit of what you've built — they lack both the background and the time. So they rely heavily on proxy signals, and founder confidence is the strongest one. But this must be shown, not told: saying 'we're passionate' or 'our product is great' gets ignored or marks you as a bullshitter.

The right frame is that if you've built something genuinely good, you're doing investors a favor by telling them about it. This isn't arrogance — it's the logical consequence of believing in what you're building. If you can't muster that belief, it may signal something real about your company's direction. The asymmetry matters: nervousness and apology destroy credibility far more than quiet confidence builds it.
