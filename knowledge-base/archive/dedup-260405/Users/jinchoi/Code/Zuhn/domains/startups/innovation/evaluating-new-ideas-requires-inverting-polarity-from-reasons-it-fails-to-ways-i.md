Most people evaluate new ideas by listing reasons they won't work — a framework suited to incremental improvements but disastrous in domains with power-law distributions. Graham argues the correct approach is to completely invert this polarity: actively try to imagine ways the idea could succeed. This isn't lowering standards but changing the direction of intellectual effort.

The economic logic is asymmetric: in a power-law world, missing one transformative winner costs far more than backing several losers. Silicon Valley learned this through Y Combinator's selection process, where thousands of strange-sounding ideas arrive every six months and the cost of missing the needle in the haystack is painfully obvious. This evaluation inversion has become so practiced that it evolved from a deliberate trick into an unconscious cultural norm — demonstrating that skepticism toward new ideas is a learned habit that can be unlearned.
