Paul Graham observes that great projects routinely go through a stage where they don't seem impressive even to their creators. The tragedy is not that people produce bad early work — it's that most people never start at all because they fear producing bad early work. This means the binding constraint on great work isn't ability but emotional tolerance for looking foolish.

The implication is that building comfort with early-stage ugliness is more valuable than perfecting skills. Studying the early versions of great work (first commits, first demos, first drafts) can recalibrate your sense of what 'good enough to continue' looks like. The internet now makes this easier than ever — you can see first commits, first videos, and first attempts of people who later did remarkable work.
