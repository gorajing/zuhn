The inverse of the malicious streetlight effect is 'directional correctness' — making a claim stronger than the evidence supports, then accusing anyone who corrects you of missing the point or defending the indefensible. If someone committed assault, call it murder; if they committed harassment, call it rape. When corrected, frame the correction as defending an assaulter or harasser.

This creates a powerful social dynamic where accuracy becomes morally suspect. The overclaimer occupies the moral high ground by association with the victim, making factual correction feel like it requires defending the perpetrator. Both failure modes — streetlighting and directional overclaiming — exploit the same asymmetry: it's socially costly to insist on precision when emotions and identities are at stake.
