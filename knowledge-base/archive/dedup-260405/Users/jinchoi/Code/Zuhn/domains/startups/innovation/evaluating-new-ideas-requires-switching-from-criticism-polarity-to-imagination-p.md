Most people evaluate new ideas by defaulting to criticism — listing reasons the idea won't work. This is the wrong polarity entirely. The right approach is to treat a new idea as a challenge to your imagination: given this seed, what could it become? This isn't about being uncritically positive; it's about recognizing that in a world with power-law distribution of outcomes, the cost of dismissing one genuine breakthrough far outweighs the cost of being temporarily wrong about many failures.

Silicon Valley has evolved this as a cultural practice through sheer economic pressure — Y Combinator processes thousands of strange-sounding ideas every six months, and missing the needle in the haystack produces painfully obvious consequences. This forced optimism has grown from self-interested calculation into genuine cultural custom, demonstrating that better evaluation habits can be learned and spread.
