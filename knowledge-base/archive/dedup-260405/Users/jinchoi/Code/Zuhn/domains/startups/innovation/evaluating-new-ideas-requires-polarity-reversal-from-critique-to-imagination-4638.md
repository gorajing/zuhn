Most people evaluate new ideas by default in critic mode—scanning for flaws, risks, and reasons it won't work. Paul Graham argues this isn't just suboptimal but backwards. The correct approach is a complete polarity reversal: instead of looking for reasons an idea will fail, actively try to imagine ways it could succeed.

This is especially critical in domains with power-law distributions of outcomes, like startups. When one outlier success can be worth more than all failures combined, the cost of dismissing a winner far exceeds the cost of entertaining losers. Silicon Valley's culture has evolved this orientation out of necessity—YC partners process thousands of strange-sounding ideas and know that missing the needle in the haystack will be painfully obvious. This makes optimism not naive but urgent, and the skill of imaginative evaluation becomes a competitive advantage that spreads because it's extremely lucrative.
