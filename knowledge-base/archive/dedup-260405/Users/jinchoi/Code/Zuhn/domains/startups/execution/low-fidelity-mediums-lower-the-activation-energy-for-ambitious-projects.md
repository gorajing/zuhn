Graham and John Carmack both observe that the gap between early work and finished work in a given medium determines how many people will attempt ambitious projects in it. Sketching in a notebook feels less committal than carving stone; a quick software hack feels less serious than a formal architecture. This psychological framing trick — telling yourself it's 'just a sketch' or 'just a hack' — lowers the emotional barrier to starting.

Carmack's example of game mods is particularly instructive: Quake's crude graphics meant quick hacks weren't far from the official game, spawning many careers. As commercial quality rose, the mod barrier became prohibitive — until Minecraft and Roblox reset expectations with explicitly crude aesthetics, making gameplay innovation the dominant value again. The lesson generalizes: choose mediums and framings that minimize the distance between early and finished work to maximize the number of ambitious attempts.
