Conventional wisdom treats overconfidence as a pure cognitive bias to eliminate. But Paul Graham observes that the most successful people across many fields are slightly overconfident — not delusionally so, but enough to counterbalance two distinct downward forces: other people's skepticism and their own harsh self-judgment of early work.

This reframes overconfidence not as a flaw but as a calibration offset. If your environment systematically undervalues early-stage work (which most environments do), then perfectly calibrated confidence actually produces under-investment in ambitious projects. The slight upward bias corrects for the environment's downward bias, resulting in behavior closer to what a rational agent with perfect information would choose. The key word is 'slightly' — excessive overconfidence leads to ignoring real signals, but a modest surplus keeps you in the game long enough for the work to mature.
