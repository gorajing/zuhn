Graham identifies a powerful trick for getting through the lame phase: deliberately frame ambitious projects as lower-stakes activities—a painting is 'just a sketch,' software is 'just a quick hack.' This isn't self-deception; it's strategic commitment management. By reducing the perceived stakes, you lower the quality threshold for continuing, which keeps you in the game long enough for the project to prove itself.

The choice of medium amplifies this effect. Digital tools beat physical ones because they require less upfront commitment—you can iterate faster and pivot cheaper. John Carmack's example of game mods illustrates the principle: when the gap between amateur and professional output is small (early Quake, Minecraft), more people experiment and more breakout work emerges. The meta-lesson is that anything reducing the cost of a first attempt—faster tools, lower-fidelity formats, hack framing—increases the supply of ambitious projects that survive their ugly phase.
