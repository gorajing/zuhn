Graham distinguishes between merely having lower standards for new ideas versus completely switching the direction of evaluation. Most people default to listing reasons an idea will fail — this feels intellectually rigorous but produces systematically wrong judgments in domains with power-law outcomes. In a power-law world, the cost of missing a winner vastly exceeds the cost of spending time on losers.

This polarity switch is a learnable skill. Graham developed it through years at Y Combinator, where every six months thousands of strange-sounding ideas arrive and the penalty for missing the needle in the haystack is painfully visible. The key insight is that optimism in this context is not naivety — it's an urgent analytical tool. Silicon Valley's track record, measured by revenue, suggests this switched-polarity evaluation is better calibrated than the rest of the world's default skepticism.
