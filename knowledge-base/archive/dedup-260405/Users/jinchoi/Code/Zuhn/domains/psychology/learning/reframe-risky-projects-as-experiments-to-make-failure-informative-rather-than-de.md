Graham and Lisa Randall both advocate treating new projects as experiments rather than performances. Under the experiment frame, there's no such thing as pure failure — you either get the result you wanted or you learn something that rules out a path. This reframe is powerful because it attacks the root cause of creative paralysis: the fear that failure reflects on your identity rather than on a hypothesis.

Curiosity is the emotional engine that makes this frame feel authentic rather than forced. Graham notes that curiosity — wanting to see how something turns out — was a primary motivator for both Y Combinator and his work on Bel. The curiosity frame naturally implies experimentation: you're not trying to prove yourself, you're trying to see what happens. This subtle shift in self-narrative can be the difference between abandoning a project at the first sign of ugliness and pushing through to where it gets interesting.
