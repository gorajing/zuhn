Graham argues that Silicon Valley's core advantage isn't technical talent but a cultural norm of treating early-stage ideas with optimism rather than dismissal. This isn't naive cheerleading — it's a learned response to the power-law distribution of outcomes, where the cost of dismissing one future giant outweighs being fooled by many impostors.

The key distinction is between generic encouragement (which you learn to discount) and informed judgment from people working on similar problems. University departments and research labs work because colleagues can actually evaluate early work in their domain. The implication for founders is that choosing your peer environment is a higher-leverage decision than developing personal grit — you're fighting social headwinds either way, so create an eddy that flows in your direction.
