Dr. Severance's Python course deliberately front-loads abstract concepts (variables, conditionals, loops, functions) for 6+ chapters before chapter 7's file I/O reveals why they matter. This suspend-disbelief approach mirrors how university math works — you grind through proofs before the application course makes everything click.

The key mechanism is retroactive reframing: once students see file processing, web scraping, and APIs, every earlier concept suddenly has a concrete anchor. This creates stronger retention than the alternative (showing cool demos first, then backfilling theory) because the learner builds their own mental model of 'why this matters' rather than being told. The tradeoff is higher dropout risk in early chapters — you need enough trust-building to keep learners engaged through the abstract phase.
