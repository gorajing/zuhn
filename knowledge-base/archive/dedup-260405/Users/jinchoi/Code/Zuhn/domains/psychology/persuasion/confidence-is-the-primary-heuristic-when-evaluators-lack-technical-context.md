Most investors at Demo Day lack the technical background to independently evaluate what a startup has built, and the time constraint (ten minutes) makes deep evaluation impossible anyway. In this information-poor environment, investors fall back on heuristic signals, and the strongest one is the founder's own conviction.

Critically, this must be shown rather than stated. Saying 'we're passionate' or 'our product is great' triggers skepticism — investors discount explicit self-promotion as cheap talk. But genuine confidence expressed through how you present (not what you claim) reads as credible signal. The framing shift Graham suggests is powerful: if you've truly built something good, you're doing investors a favor by telling them about it. This reframes the pitch from asking for something to offering something, which naturally produces the right kind of confidence.
