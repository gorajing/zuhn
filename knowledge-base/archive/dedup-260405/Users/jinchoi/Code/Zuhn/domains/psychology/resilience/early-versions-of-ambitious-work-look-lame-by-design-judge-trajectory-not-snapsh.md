Paul Graham argues that early versions of ambitious projects are a special case that we lack cultural customs for evaluating. We mistakenly apply finished-work standards to embryonic efforts, causing most people to quit before reaching the impressive stage. The solution isn't lower standards but a polarity switch: instead of listing reasons something won't work, actively imagine how it could. Silicon Valley has already developed this custom out of economic necessity — investors who dismiss strange ideas from unknown people get punished by power-law outcomes. Graham suggests this optimistic evaluation framework can be taught and will eventually spread as a social norm because it's extremely lucrative.
