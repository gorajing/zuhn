Perfect calibration of one's abilities sounds optimal in theory, but Paul Graham observes that the most successful people across many fields are slightly overconfident. This isn't a bug — it's a compensating error. Early work is systematically undervalued: you judge it too harshly, and others are incentivized to dismiss it (to seem clever or to avoid feeling threatened). Slight overconfidence acts as armor against both forces.

The mechanism is mathematical: if you overestimate the importance of what you're working on, that compensates for your mistakenly harsh judgment of initial results. A 20% complete project toward a goal worth 100 that you perceive as 10% toward a goal worth 200 still has the correct expected value — the errors cancel. This suggests the optimal psychological stance for ambitious work is not accuracy but strategic miscalibration.
