In photosynthesis, the thylakoid membrane acts as a dam. Energy from the electron transport chain pumps protons (H+) into the thylakoid interior, creating a steep concentration gradient. When protons flow back out through ATP synthase — the only available exit — that enzyme harnesses the flow to phosphorylate ADP into ATP. The membrane's phospholipid bilayer is crucial: it maintains the gradient by being selectively impermeable.

This chemiosmotic mechanism (discovered by Peter Mitchell, initially controversial) is universal across life — it powers both photosynthesis and cellular respiration. Understanding it reframes how we think about biological energy: cells don't just 'burn fuel,' they create controlled imbalances and harvest the equalization. This same principle — storing potential in gradients rather than in single containers — appears in nerve impulse transmission, kidney function, and muscle contraction.
