Most people evaluate new ideas by defaulting to criticism — listing reasons it won't work. Graham argues the right approach isn't simply being less critical (lowering standards), but completely reversing the direction of evaluation: actively trying to imagine ways the idea could work. This is what experienced startup investors learn to do.

This polarity switch is particularly important in power-law domains like startups, where dismissing ideas makes you statistically right most of the time but catastrophically wrong when weighted by outcome. The skill is not about being indiscriminate — it's about redirecting cognitive effort from easy destruction to harder but more valuable construction. Silicon Valley's cultural adoption of this habit demonstrates it's learnable, not innate, and its economic success suggests it's well-calibrated.
