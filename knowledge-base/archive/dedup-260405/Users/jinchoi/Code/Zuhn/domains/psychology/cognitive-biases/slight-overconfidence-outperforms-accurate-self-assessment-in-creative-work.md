Intuitively, perfectly accurate self-assessment should be optimal. But this ignores that the environment is not neutral — early-stage creators face systematic downward pressure from their own harsh self-judgment and from external skepticism. In this environment, a slight positive bias in self-assessment doesn't produce miscalibration; it corrects for existing miscalibration.

Hardy observed this in mathematics: good work is not done by humble men. The most successful people across many fields are empirically slightly overconfident. This isn't a flaw that succeeds despite itself — it's an error that compensates for other errors in the opposite direction, like noise-canceling headphones.

The implication is that the advice 'just be honest about your abilities' is subtly wrong for creators doing ambitious work. The honest assessment of early work, applied by an honest but unskilled judge of early work, will systematically undervalue it. A corrective bias is not self-deception — it's better epistemics for this specific domain.
