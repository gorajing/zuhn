The sneaker boom of the 2010s was driven by low interest rates, social media hype, and unprecedented resale appreciation. Nike and Adidas hit record earnings. Shoes sold out in minutes. Anyone with time and cash could flip pairs. Sneaker shops opened everywhere. But revenue is a lagging indicator, not a leading one -- when the money was easy, everyone was a player.

When the market crashed, most stores went bankrupt and resellers moved on to flip the next shiny thing. The survivors were those who had built genuine expertise: understanding supply-demand dynamics per model, building face-to-face relationships with sellers, and maintaining reputations for fair dealing in a market now known for bait-and-switch tactics. The bubble exposed that most participants had no durable competitive advantage -- they were simply riding a demand wave. This pattern repeats across markets: crypto, NFTs, and any boom where easy revenue substitutes for genuine skill.
