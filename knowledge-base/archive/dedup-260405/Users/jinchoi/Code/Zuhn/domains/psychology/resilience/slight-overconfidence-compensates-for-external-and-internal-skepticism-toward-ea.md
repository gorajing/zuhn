People systematically undervalue early-stage work because they judge incomplete prototypes by the standards of finished products. This creates a compounding drag: external skepticism from others plus internal skepticism from yourself. A slight calibration error toward overconfidence exactly offsets these two downward biases, producing a more accurate net assessment of expected value.

This reframes overconfidence not as a character flaw but as an adaptive correction. Hardy's observation that good work is not done by humble men reflects the same insight — overestimating both the importance of your subject and your role in it compensates for the harsh early-stage discount everyone applies. The key word is 'slightly' — massive overconfidence leads to delusion, but a small bias toward optimism is strictly better than accurate self-assessment in environments where early work is systematically punished.
