A striking pattern across the 16 unicorn decks: at the time of pitching, each company's vision sounded borderline absurd. Coinbase was pitching cryptocurrency when Bitcoin was $6.25. Airbnb was suggesting strangers would pay to sleep in each other's homes, listing Couchsurfing as a serious competitor. LinkedIn had zero revenue.

This aligns with Peter Thiel's framework that the best investments are contrarian and correct. If a startup's pitch sounds reasonable and incremental, it likely means the opportunity is already well-understood and the upside is limited. The implication for founders is uncomfortable: if your pitch doesn't make smart people skeptical, you may not be thinking big enough. And for investors, the most uncomfortable pitches — the ones that trigger 'that's crazy' — may be exactly where the asymmetric returns hide.
