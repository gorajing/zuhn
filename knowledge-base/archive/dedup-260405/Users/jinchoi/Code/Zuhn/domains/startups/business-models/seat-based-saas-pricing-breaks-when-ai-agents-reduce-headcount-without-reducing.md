Traditional SaaS economics tied revenue directly to headcount: more employees meant more seats meant more revenue. AI agents disrupt this by automating high-volume tasks (email drafting, invoice reconciliation, support summarization) within existing systems rather than replacing them. A firm needing 50 licenses might realize 35 suffice for the same output.

The damage is insidious — it shows up as slowing expansion and rising downgrades long before actual churn appears. Annual contracts mask the erosion for up to a year, but internal metrics reveal the decay. Companies that shift to usage-based or outcome-based pricing can find a floor; those clinging to per-seat models face persistent, structural revenue erosion that no amount of feature bundling can fix.
