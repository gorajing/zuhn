RuBisCo was designed in an atmosphere with abundant CO2 and almost no oxygen. It worked well enough in that environment. But as photosynthesis itself increased atmospheric oxygen, RuBisCo began grabbing oxygen molecules instead of CO2 roughly half the time, producing phosphoglycolate — a toxic compound that interferes with the Calvin Cycle. Plants evolved elaborate photorespiration pathways just to clean up this mess.

The deeper pattern: RuBisCo's own success changed the environment in a way that broke its core assumption. This is a precise biological analog to Goodhart's Law and market regime changes. Hedge fund strategies that work in low-volatility environments blow up when volatility spikes. Companies optimized for growth-stage economics fail when markets mature. The critical question for any optimized system is: what environmental assumption does this design take for granted, and what happens when the system's own success invalidates that assumption?
