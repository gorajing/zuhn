Even after Destin could ride the backwards bicycle, any small distraction — such as a cellphone ringing in his pocket — would instantly throw his brain back to the old steering algorithm, causing him to crash. This reveals a critical asymmetry between new and established neural pathways: old, well-practiced skills run on autopilot (System 1), while newly formed pathways require active conscious attention (System 2) to maintain. The moment attention is diverted, the stronger old pathway recaptures control. This has direct practical applications for anyone learning a new skill that conflicts with an existing habit. During the fragile early period, you must protect your practice environment from distractions. It also explains why behavior change is so hard in real-world environments full of triggers — the new behavior pattern hasn't yet achieved the automaticity needed to compete with the old one without conscious effort.
