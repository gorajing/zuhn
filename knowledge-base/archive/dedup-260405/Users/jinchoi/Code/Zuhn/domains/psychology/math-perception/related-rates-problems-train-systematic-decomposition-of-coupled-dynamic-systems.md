In a related rates problem, you have multiple quantities changing over time (e.g., the radius and volume of an expanding balloon, or the distance and angle of a moving ladder) connected by a constraint equation. The technique is: identify all changing quantities, find the equation relating them, implicitly differentiate with respect to time, then solve for the unknown rate. This seemingly narrow mathematical technique actually encodes a general problem-solving framework.

The key insight is that complex systems with many moving parts can be analyzed by identifying the constraint equations linking variables, then using calculus to determine how changes propagate through those constraints. This is exactly how engineers analyze circuits, economists model market equilibria, and biologists model population dynamics. The 'related rates' framing makes the implicit claim that understanding how change propagates through coupled systems is a fundamental cognitive skill, not just a calculus topic.
