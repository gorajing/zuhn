Dr. Johnson highlights an underappreciated aspect of psychedelic therapy's risk profile: while a heroic dose session involves intense acute risk (which is managed through clinical protocol), it potentially replaces years or decades of daily psychiatric medication that carries its own substantial cumulative risks — weight gain, sexual dysfunction, emotional blunting, dependency, withdrawal syndromes.

This inversion of the treatment model — from chronic low-grade intervention to acute high-intensity intervention — has profound implications beyond psychedelics. It suggests that for conditions rooted in behavioral rigidity rather than ongoing neurochemical deficiency, a single powerful perturbation may be more effective and safer than continuous low-level modulation. The key insight is that risk assessment must account for time horizon: a seemingly 'safer' daily pill accumulates risk over years in ways that a single supervised session does not.
