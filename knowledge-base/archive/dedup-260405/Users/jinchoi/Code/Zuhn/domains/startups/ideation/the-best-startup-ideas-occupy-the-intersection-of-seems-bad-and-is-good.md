Peter Thiel's Venn diagram framework — the intersection of 'seems like a bad idea' and 'is a good idea' — captures why the best opportunities are systematically undervalued. If an idea were obviously good, someone else would already have done it, so the most successful founders work on ideas that few others recognize as valuable.

This creates a double bind for investors: the startups most likely to become massive winners will look the least fundable at the evaluation stage. Facebook seemed like a trivial time-waster for college students. Airbnb seemed absurd. Microsoft and Apple could have been dismissed the same way. Recognizing this pattern is necessary but insufficient — even knowing the pattern, it remains psychologically difficult to act on it.
