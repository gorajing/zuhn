The conventional wisdom that being first to market creates a durable advantage is frequently wrong. First movers bear the full cost of customer education, infrastructure building, and regulatory navigation — all of which fast followers can observe, learn from, and avoid. Historical examples abound: Google was not the first search engine, Facebook was not the first social network, and the iPhone was not the first smartphone. The real competitive moats come from network effects, switching costs, and execution speed — none of which require being first. Founders should focus on being the best rather than the first, and investors should be skeptical when 'first-mover advantage' appears as a primary moat in a pitch deck.
