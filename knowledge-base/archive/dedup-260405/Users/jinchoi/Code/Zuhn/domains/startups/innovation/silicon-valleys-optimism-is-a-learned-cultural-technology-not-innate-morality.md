Graham argues that Silicon Valley's encouraging culture toward startups isn't rooted in moral superiority but in self-interest — investors want founders to succeed because it makes them rich, and others hope to benefit from proximity to success. Over time, this self-interested encouragement calcified into genuine cultural custom. The implication is powerful: because the attitude is rooted in incentives rather than character, it can be transplanted to other environments by aligning incentives similarly.

This also explains why dismissiveness toward new ideas is more common elsewhere — not because people are worse, but because they lack the feedback loop that rewards early support of weird ideas. The existence of Silicon Valley as proof-of-concept shows that cultural norms around innovation can evolve quickly, undermining the fatalist view that skepticism toward new ideas is an immutable part of human nature.
