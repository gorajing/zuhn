Perfectly calibrated self-assessment seems theoretically optimal, but in practice you face systematic downward pressure from two sources: other people's skepticism (especially outside supportive environments) and your own harsh judgment of early results. A slight positive bias in self-confidence compensates for these compounding negative biases, producing a net assessment closer to truth than pure accuracy would.

Graham observes this pattern empirically across many fields—the most successful people tend to be slightly overconfident, not perfectly calibrated. This isn't delusion; it's error-correction. Similarly, overestimating the importance of your work compensates for undervaluing early results: if something is 20% done toward a goal worth 100, but you think it's 10% done toward a goal worth 200, your expected-value estimate is accidentally correct. The key word is 'slightly'—massive overconfidence produces different failure modes.
