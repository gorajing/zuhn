Linear funnels (acquire → activate → retain → monetize) hit a ceiling when channels saturate or CAC spikes. Growth loops restructure this by making one user's action — a referral, shared template, or created content — the input that acquires the next user. Dropbox's referral loop (3,900% growth in 15 months), TikTok's content loop, and Notion's template galleries all follow this pattern.

The key architectural difference is that funnels require continuous top-of-funnel spend to maintain volume, while loops generate their own fuel. Each cycle increases both product value and acquisition velocity. Teams designing growth systems should identify which user action naturally produces an artifact (invite, content, shared workspace) that attracts new users, then instrument and optimize that specific loop rather than pouring budget into paid channels.
