RuBisCo was engineered by evolution in an atmosphere with abundant CO2 and negligible oxygen. It worked well enough under those conditions. But the very success of photosynthetic organisms gradually flooded the atmosphere with oxygen — a byproduct of their own process — and RuBisCo began grabbing O2 instead of CO2 roughly half the time, creating toxic phosphoglycolate.

This is a precise analogy for startups that succeed so thoroughly they destroy the conditions that made them viable. A company that dominates a niche may commoditize it. A platform that attracts developers may become so crowded that early advantages vanish. The critical insight is that the threat doesn't come from competitors — it comes from your own success altering the environment. Engineering teams should periodically audit which assumptions about the operating environment are baked into core architecture, because those assumptions are the first things your own growth will invalidate.
