RuBisCo was the enzyme that made life on land possible: it fixed atmospheric CO2 into organic carbon at a time when oxygen was scarce. As photosynthesis itself flooded the atmosphere with oxygen, RuBisCo started confusing O2 for CO2 roughly half the time, producing a toxic byproduct that requires additional enzymatic machinery to neutralize. Yet plants can't abandon it—they just produce 40 billion tons of the enzyme globally to brute-force around its inefficiency.

This is a near-perfect biological analog to strategic lock-in. A solution that was clearly 'good enough to win' in one competitive landscape can become a structural liability once conditions shift—but the switching cost (losing all accumulated mass, infrastructure, and co-evolutionary adaptations) is higher than tolerating ongoing inefficiency. The lesson for startups and established companies alike: the window to change core architecture is narrow, and once it closes, you are optimizing around the flaw rather than eliminating it.
