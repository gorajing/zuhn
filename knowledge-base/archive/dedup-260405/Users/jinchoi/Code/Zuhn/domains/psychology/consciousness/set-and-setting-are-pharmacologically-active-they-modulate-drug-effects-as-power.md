Johnson makes the case that set (the person's mindset, expectations, preparation) and setting (physical environment, social support, music) are not merely contextual factors but functionally pharmacological variables. The same 30mg psilocybin dose produces radically different outcomes — therapeutic breakthrough versus psychological crisis — depending on these factors. This challenges the reductionist pharmacological model where a drug has fixed effects at a given dose. It suggests that context is a genuine independent variable in drug action, not just noise. The implication extends beyond psychedelics: it raises the question of how much of all pharmacotherapy is context-dependent in ways we systematically ignore. The clinical protocol's emphasis on preparation, rapport, and environment is not hand-waving — it is the active ingredient alongside the molecule.
