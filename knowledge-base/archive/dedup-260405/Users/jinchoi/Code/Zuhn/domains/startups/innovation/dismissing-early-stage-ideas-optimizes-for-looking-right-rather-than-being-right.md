In domains with power-law distributions like startups, most ambitious projects do fail, so dismissing them is statistically 'correct' most of the time. This creates a perverse incentive structure where being a skeptic lets you appear smart — you're right more often than the optimist.

But when outcomes are weighted by magnitude, the optimist's portfolio crushes the skeptic's. Missing one Google or Airbnb in its ugly-duckling phase costs more than all the failed bets combined. Graham argues this is why Silicon Valley's seemingly naive optimism is better calibrated than the rest of the world's — it optimizes for expected value rather than prediction accuracy. This has direct implications for how you evaluate opportunities: the question isn't 'will this probably work?' but 'what's the expected value given both probability and magnitude of success?'
