The Chinese concept of the Mandate of Heaven held that rulers governed by divine permission, but that mandate could be withdrawn when a dynasty became corrupt or incompetent. Natural disasters, famines, and social unrest were interpreted as signs that Heaven had revoked its approval, legitimizing revolution.

This maps precisely to modern organizational leadership: a founder or CEO holds authority not through their title but through continuous delivery of results. When a leader stops performing — missing targets, losing market position, alienating talent — the implicit mandate erodes. Boards, investors, and teams begin seeking replacement, just as Chinese subjects sought a new dynasty. The lesson is that authority maintenance requires ongoing competence, not just initial appointment.
