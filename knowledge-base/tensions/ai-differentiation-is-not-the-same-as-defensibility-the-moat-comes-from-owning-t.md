**Side A:** "AI differentiation is not the same as defensibility — the moat comes from owning the end-to-end workflow and becoming the system of record"
**Side B:** "The new competitive moat is not better models but better judgment on which tasks go to which AI and which stay with humans"
**Reason:** Two competing theories of moat in the AI era. One says own the entire workflow end-to-end and become the system of record (breadth + data lock-in). The other says the moat is in orchestration judgment — knowing which tasks to give AI and which to keep human (depth + wisdom). One strategy invests in coverage and integration; the other invests in decision quality. A company following the workflow-ownership thesis builds a Salesforce-style platform; one following the judgment thesis builds a smaller but more intelligent product.
**Resolution:** These are not competing moat theories but complementary layers that operate at different scales. System-of-record ownership (Side A) is the moat for platform companies that serve entire organizations -- you become the data gravity center that makes switching costly. Intelligence allocation (Side B) is the moat for operators and smaller companies that cannot own entire workflows but can orchestrate AI + human judgment better than competitors. The determining variable is company stage and ambition: if you are building a platform (Salesforce, Cursor, Eve), pursue system-of-record lock-in; if you are building a lean operation or competing within an industry, your edge is better intelligence allocation across the tools available to everyone.

**Prediction:** Companies that combine both -- owning the workflow AND allocating intelligence well within it (e.g., Cursor using 13+ models within an owned developer workflow) -- will outperform companies that pursue only one strategy.
