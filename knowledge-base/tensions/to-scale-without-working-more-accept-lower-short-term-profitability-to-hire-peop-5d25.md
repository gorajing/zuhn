**Side A:** "To scale without working more, accept lower short-term profitability to hire people whose capabilities pull the business up beyond what you could do alone"
**Side B:** "Raising $20M and selling for $30M nets the founder less money over 10 years than a regular salary — bootstrap instead"
**Reason:** Winner-take-all markets reward scaling investment, fragmented markets reward capital efficiency. Both strategies have produced billion-dollar outcomes and both have destroyed companies. The resolution depends on market structure, not universal principle.
**Resolution:** Conditional on TAM and market structure. Hormozi's advice (hire world-class talent, sacrifice short-term profit) is correct for businesses in large markets where human talent is the scaling bottleneck — service businesses, agencies, sales-driven organizations. Barman's counterweight (bootstrap, keep equity) is correct when your realistic exit is sub-$100M and VC dilution makes the math punitive. The key diagnostic: can your business realistically reach a 10x return on invested capital? If yes, hiring talent at the cost of short-term profit is the highest-ROI investment. If your ceiling is a comfortable lifestyle business or modest exit, bootstrapping preserves the economic upside that makes the whole endeavor worthwhile.
