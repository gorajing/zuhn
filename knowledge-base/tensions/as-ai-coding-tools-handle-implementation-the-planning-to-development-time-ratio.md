**Side A:** "As AI coding tools handle implementation, the planning-to-development time ratio has inverted to as high as 9:1 — spend 90% of time planning"
**Side B:** "AI programming tools have a unique competitive dynamic where every model improvement creates new product possibilities, making iteration speed the dominant competitive advantage"
**Reason:** Opposing time allocation strategies for AI-era development. One says the implementation bottleneck is gone so invest heavily in planning (think more, code less). The other says the landscape changes so fast that iteration speed is the only competitive advantage (ship fast, learn fast). Planning-heavy assumes a stable enough landscape to plan against; iteration-heavy assumes the landscape shifts under your feet. Both can't be maximally true — you can't spend 90% planning AND iterate at maximum speed.
**Resolution:** The key variable is what you are building: known product with clear requirements versus novel product in uncertain market. Side A (9:1 planning ratio) applies when requirements can be precisely defined -- agency work, internal tools, rebuilding known systems. When the spec is clear, planning dominates because AI executes faithfully including your mistakes, so specification quality is the ceiling. Side B (iteration speed is the moat) applies when the landscape is shifting and requirements cannot be fully specified in advance -- new AI-native products where each model capability jump creates new possibilities. You cannot spend 90% planning when you do not yet know what is possible. The reconciliation: plan deeply within each iteration, but iterate rapidly across the broader product direction. The 9:1 ratio applies at the task level; iteration speed applies at the product strategy level. These are nested loops, not competing strategies.

**Prediction:** AI development agencies (known specs) will adopt planning-heavy workflows and report higher first-pass quality. AI product startups (unknown specs) will adopt rapid iteration and report higher product-market fit discovery rates. Attempting the wrong strategy for your context will produce measurably worse outcomes.
