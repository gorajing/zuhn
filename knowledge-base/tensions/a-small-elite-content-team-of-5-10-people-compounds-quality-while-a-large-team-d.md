**Side A:** "A small elite content team of 5-10 people compounds quality while a large team dilutes it"
**Side B:** "At scale, your recruiting pipeline needs the same metrics-driven rigor as your customer pipeline: lead gen, nurture, conversion, onboarding, and retention"
**Reason:** Tension between quality-through-smallness and growth-through-systems. One says keep the team elite and tiny because quality compounds with tight collaboration and dilutes with headcount. The other says build rigorous systems for recruiting at scale because growth demands it. The resolution may be domain-dependent: creative/craft work favors small teams, while operational/scaling work demands systematic growth. But many companies face the choice of staying excellent-and-small vs growing-and-systematizing.
**Resolution:** Conditional on function type. Both insights are correct but address different organizational functions. Coconote's Navy SEALs model (small, elite, 1:1 feedback) is optimal for craft-dependent work — content creation, product design, brand voice — where quality compounds with tight collaboration and individual taste matters. Hormozi's systematic talent funnel is optimal for process-dependent work — sales, customer success, operations — where each hire has calculable ROI ($200K cost generating $1M gross profit) and quality is maintained through systems rather than individual brilliance. The synthesis: segment your organization. Keep the creative/product nucleus small and elite. Build systematic, metrics-driven pipelines for roles where throughput scales linearly with headcount.
