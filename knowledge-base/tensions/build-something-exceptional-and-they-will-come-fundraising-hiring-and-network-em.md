**Side A:** "Build something exceptional and they will come — fundraising, hiring, and network emerge from the product itself"
**Side B:** "Having a great product is necessary but nowhere near sufficient — visibility, authority, credibility, marketing strategy, and customer experience are all required"
**Reason:** One claims product quality collapses all other problems into a single variable (citing YC experience where investors emailed the founder). The other dismantles 'build it and they will come' with evidence that great products fail from invisibility (Geico winning through brand personality in a commodity market). Both have real evidence.
**Resolution:** Synthesis — the key variable is market structure and product differentiation magnitude. In winner-take-all tech markets where the product is 10x better (Gumloop's YC experience), the product IS the distribution. Investors email you, customers become employees, word-of-mouth replaces marketing. But this only works when the product improvement is unmistakable and the market has viral mechanics. In commodity or crowded markets (VanDusen's Geico example), even great products are invisible without brand, visibility, and credibility infrastructure. The reconciliation: "build it and they will come" applies when your product creates a new category or offers a step-function improvement. "Build it and then market it" applies when you are competing within an established category where differentiation is incremental.
