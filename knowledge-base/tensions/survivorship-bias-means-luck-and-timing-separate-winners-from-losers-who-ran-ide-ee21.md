**Side A:** "Survivorship bias means luck and timing separate winners from losers who ran identical playbooks"
**Side B:** "Investors who back founders with 3-4 failures are buying battle-tested pattern recognition that lucky first-timers never develop"
**Reason:** Identical strategies produced opposite outcomes (Stasa vs OYO) due to timing, supporting A. But VCs deliberately prefer serial failures for compressed learning curves, supporting B. Is startup success mostly skill (learnable) or mostly luck (making experience an illusion)?
**Resolution:** Synthesis — luck/timing and pattern recognition operate at different layers. Side A is correct that identical playbooks produce divergent outcomes (Stasa vs OYO, Byju's pandemic rise and fall), proving environmental factors dominate any single venture's outcome. Side B is correct that serial founders develop judgment that improves their odds across multiple attempts — VCs backing them are making a portfolio bet on slightly better odds, not a certainty bet. The reconciliation: for any SINGLE startup, luck is the dominant variable. Across a CAREER of startups, pattern recognition compounds and shifts the probability distribution. Smart founders therefore optimize for multiple shots (lean experiments, fast failure) rather than betting everything on one timing window.
