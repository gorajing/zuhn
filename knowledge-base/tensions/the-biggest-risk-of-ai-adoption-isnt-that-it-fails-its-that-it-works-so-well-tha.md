**Side A:** "The biggest risk of AI adoption isn't that it fails — it's that it works so well that humans stop learning"
**Side B:** "95% of AI projects fail — twice the rate of any other emerging technology — driven equally by skills gaps and employee resistance to adoption"
**Reason:** Paradoxical tension: one says AI's danger is succeeding too well (creating dependency and deskilling), the other says AI overwhelmingly fails in practice. Both are empirically supported. The strategic implication flips: the first says slow down and preserve human learning; the second says most organizations can't even get AI to work. Resolution may be temporal — AI fails at the project level (short-term) but deskills at the civilizational level (long-term).
**Resolution:** AI fails at the project level (short-term organizational implementation) but deskills at the civilizational level (long-term cognitive impact). These operate on completely different scales. The 95% project failure rate reflects enterprises fumbling integration, change management, and skills gaps — organizational incompetence, not AI limitation. Meanwhile, the minority of successful deployments are precisely where deskilling danger concentrates, because success means heavy usage. The key variable is adoption depth: shallow adoption (most companies) produces failure; deep adoption (power users, successful projects) produces dependency risk. Both stances are simultaneously true because they describe different populations.

**Prediction:** Companies in the top 10% of AI adoption maturity will report measurable skill gaps in employees who joined post-AI-deployment versus pre-AI-deployment within 3 years of successful rollout.
