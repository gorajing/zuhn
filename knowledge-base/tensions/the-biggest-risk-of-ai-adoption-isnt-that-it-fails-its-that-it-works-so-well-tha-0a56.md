**Side A:** "The biggest risk of AI adoption isn't that it fails — it's that it works so well that humans stop learning"
**Side B:** "AI systems launch knowing only half of what they need — the rest must be learned through real-world usage and continuous human feedback"
**Reason:** If AI needs human feedback perpetually (B), does that prevent the deskilling A warns about? Or does providing feedback require less skill than the original task, enabling deskilling anyway? Both are evidenced and point to different equilibria.
**Resolution:** Providing feedback to AI systems requires less skill than performing the original task, so B's "perpetual human feedback" does not prevent A's deskilling. The key variable is depth of engagement: grading AI responses is shallow review that does not build the deep understanding A warns about losing. The equilibrium is a downward spiral — AI needs human feedback to improve, but feedback quality degrades as humans lose domain expertise from disuse. Organizations must structure AI-human workflows to require genuine reasoning from the human, not just thumbs-up/thumbs-down feedback.

**Prediction:** By 2028, organizations that rely on passive human feedback (approval/rejection) for AI improvement will see measurably lower feedback quality over time compared to organizations that require humans to explain their corrections in writing.
