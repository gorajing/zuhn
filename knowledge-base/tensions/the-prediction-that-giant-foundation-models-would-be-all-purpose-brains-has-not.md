**Side A:** "The prediction that giant foundation models would be all-purpose brains has not materialized — they remain narrow pattern matchers despite massive scale"
**Side B:** "The LLM paradigm applied to biological data — 'large molecule models' — could unlock nature's design intelligence across all five kingdoms of life"
**Reason:** Direct tension on foundation model generality: one says the all-purpose model dream failed, the other says the same paradigm applied to biology could be transformative. The resolution may be that foundation models don't generalize out-of-the-box to arbitrary tasks, but the training PARADIGM (next-token prediction on massive domain data) transfers powerfully to new domains with domain-specific corpora.
**Resolution:** Synthesis. The tension dissolves once you distinguish "one model for everything" from "the training paradigm applied to everything." Horowitz is right that single foundation models are not all-purpose brains -- Cursor uses 13 models, OpenAI abandoned the one-model thesis, Karpathy predicts speciation. Oxman is right that the self-supervised training paradigm applied to domain-specific corpora is transformative. These are compatible claims. The paradigm (next-token prediction on massive sequential data) is domain-general; the resulting models are domain-specific. Large molecule models would not be GPT applied to biology but new models trained on biological sequences using the same architectural recipe. The all-purpose brain failed; the all-purpose training recipe succeeded.

**Prediction:** By 2029, the most impactful AI applications in biology, materials science, and drug discovery will use domain-specific foundation models (trained on molecular/protein data) rather than general-purpose LLMs, confirming that paradigm transfer beats model transfer.
