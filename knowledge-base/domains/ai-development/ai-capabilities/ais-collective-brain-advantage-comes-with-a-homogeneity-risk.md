Henrich acknowledges the intuition pumps for AI as cultural accelerator: population size can grow arbitrarily large, 'brain state' can be copied rather than transmitted via words, one AI can 'meet with everybody at once' by holding the whole internet in context. A fine-tuned SpaceX could be replicated 1,000 times, each copy targeting a different hardware problem. The collective brain scales in ways humans cannot.

But Henrich pushes back on high-fidelity replication as unambiguous benefit. A huge number of historical innovations came from (1) serendipitous meetings — he cites research showing companies cross-reference each other's patents more when their employees frequent the same coffee shops — and (2) 'improper copying' where mistakes produced better results than the original. Evolution may even tune transcription error rates up on purpose to search adjacent possibility spaces. Humans are also constantly hit with external shocks (economic, weather, pandemic, conflict) that inject new information and force adaptation.

The warning: a perfectly-synchronized AI swarm could suffer the Tasmania problem at higher speed — a monoculture running out of variation. If you're designing multi-agent AI systems, you should deliberately introduce stochasticity, cultural divergence across agent populations, and external perturbations. 'Too homogeneous and doesn't have enough noise' is a failure mode Henrich expects to matter.
