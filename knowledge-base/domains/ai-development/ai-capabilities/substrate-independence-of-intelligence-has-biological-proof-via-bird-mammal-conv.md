The 2025 finding that avian DVR and mammalian neocortex evolved independently from different cell types to support comparable higher cognition provides a natural experiment in architectural diversity for intelligence. Evolution, given 320 million years and selection pressure, produced two solutions, not one.

This matters for the recurring debate about whether artificial neural networks can be 'really' intelligent if they don't match biological brains. The existence of multiple biological implementations of intelligence is a strong prior against architectural fundamentalism. If corvids can reach seven-year-old human cognition with a non-layered, non-neocortex brain, the requirement for 'neocortex-like' structures in artificial systems is likely overspecified.

Operationally: when evaluating whether some AI approach is fundamentally limited by its architecture, remember that evolution found at least two radically different paths to intelligence. The space of working substrates is empirically larger than one.
