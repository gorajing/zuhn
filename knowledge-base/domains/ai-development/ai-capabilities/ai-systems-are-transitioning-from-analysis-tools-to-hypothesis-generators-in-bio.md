Google DeepMind's C2S-Scale 27B model, built with Yale researchers, generated an unexpected insight into how tumors evade immune detection — specifically suggesting a mechanism to convert 'cold' tumors (invisible to the immune system) into 'hot' ones responsive to immunotherapy. When tested in living cells, the approach produced roughly a 50% increase in antigen presentation.

This represents a qualitative leap in AI's role in science. Previous AI applications in biology (AlphaFold, drug screening) primarily accelerated existing workflows — finding patterns in known data faster. Here, the AI produced a hypothesis that surprised domain experts and was then validated experimentally. The distinction matters enormously: analysis tools make existing researchers faster, but hypothesis generators expand the frontier of what researchers can even think to test. If this pattern holds, the bottleneck in biological research shifts from 'generating good ideas' to 'running enough wet-lab experiments to validate AI-generated hypotheses.'
