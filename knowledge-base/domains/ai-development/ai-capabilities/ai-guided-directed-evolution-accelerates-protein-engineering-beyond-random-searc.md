Traditional directed evolution of proteins uses massive random mutation libraries — billions of variants — hoping to find a needle in a haystack. Kelsic's approach at Dyno Therapeutics replaces randomness with AI-guided design. They build programmed (not random) capsid libraries, test hundreds of thousands of variants per primate experiment via DNA multiplexing, then train machine learning models on the resulting petabytes of sequencing data.

The models learn which amino acids work at each position, capturing patterns far too complex for human analysis across 735 amino acid positions. These models can be queried billions of times computationally, predicting which sequences are most promising for the next experimental round. The iterative cycle — design, synthesize, test in vivo, train models, query models, design again — keeps humans at the highest judgment points while delegating routine analysis to AI. This is a concrete example of AI augmenting rather than replacing scientific expertise.
