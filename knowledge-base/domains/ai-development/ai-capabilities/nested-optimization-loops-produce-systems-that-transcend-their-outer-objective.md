Alexander identifies three nested optimization levels in both humans and AI. For humans: evolution (outer, optimizes reproduction) → predictive coding (middle, predicts next sense-datum) → world models and thought processes (inner, does 'normal thinking'). For AI: company design choices (outer, optimizes profit/capability) → next-token prediction (middle, updates weights) → internal representations and algorithms (inner, does actual computation).

The key insight is that each inner level creates structures not predictable from the outer level's objective. Evolution didn't 'intend' for monks or mathematicians. Next-token prediction didn't 'intend' for helical manifolds in 6D space. This nested structure means that dismissing a system based on its outermost optimization criterion is always a category error. Understanding AI requires engaging with what the inner levels actually produce, not just what the outer level optimized for.
