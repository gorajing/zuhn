There's a common assumption that fundamental scientific breakthroughs require a qualitatively different kind of cognition — rebuilding conceptual foundations rather than searching within existing frameworks. Grothendieck rebuilt algebraic geometry's foundations; Einstein replaced Newtonian ontology.

But examining history more carefully, most breakthroughs look like sophisticated recombination: Darwin combined Malthus with biogeography with pigeon breeding; Einstein combined Mach's epistemology with Maxwell's equations with thought experiments. If invention is primarily 'high-dimensional recombination with taste,' then LLMs — trained on the entire corpus of human knowledge and increasingly capable of drawing non-obvious connections — may be approaching the right architecture for it. The 'AI can't do X' track record is consistently wrong, with each supposed limitation falling on timescales that surprised experts.
