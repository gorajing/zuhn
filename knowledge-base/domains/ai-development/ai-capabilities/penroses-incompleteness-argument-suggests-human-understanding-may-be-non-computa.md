Goedel showed that any sufficiently powerful logical system contains true statements that cannot be proven within the system. Penrose noticed that human mathematicians can see the truth of certain statements that no algorithm can formally prove — not by brute force calculation, but by insight and understanding. If human understanding were fully equivalent to an algorithm, humans would be bound by the same limitations as formal systems. Yet in practice, they are not.

Penrose's conclusion: human understanding is not computable. It cannot be reduced to a classical algorithm running on biological hardware. This remains controversial but has implications for AI development: if there is a category of understanding that is genuinely non-algorithmic, then current AI architectures (which are fundamentally computational) may have hard limits that scaling alone cannot overcome. The question isn't whether AI can be intelligent, but whether intelligence and understanding are the same thing.
