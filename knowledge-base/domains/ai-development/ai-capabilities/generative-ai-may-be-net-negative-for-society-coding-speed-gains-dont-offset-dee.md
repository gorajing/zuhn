Marcus makes a provocative cost-benefit argument: generative AI certainly helps coders write faster, but weighed against the full ledger of consequences — non-consensual deepfake porn, schools unable to educate students who outsource thinking to ChatGPT, misinformation from deepfakes, unreliable military applications potentially killing innocents, and mounting employment disruption — it's not obvious the technology is net positive for society.

He draws explicit parallels to social media: fun to get likes, but substantial evidence it's had net-negative effects on children and possibly all of society. The distinction matters: this critique isn't about AI broadly, but specifically about generative AI / large language models trained on massive data without deep comprehension. Future AI using different techniques should be evaluated on its own merits. Marcus suggests people shouldn't just boycott ChatGPT — they should question whether they want to give this much power to companies that 'all seem to be fairly irresponsible.'
