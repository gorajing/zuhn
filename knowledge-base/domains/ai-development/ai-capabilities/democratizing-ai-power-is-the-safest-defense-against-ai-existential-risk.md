Howard frames the AI safety debate around two threat paths: (1) a human using superintelligence to destroy humanity, and (2) an agentic AI acting autonomously against human interests. He argues the human misuse scenario comes first chronologically, and centralizing AI power makes this worse, not better — the people who end up controlling centralized resources are precisely those most motivated by power accumulation.

The defense argument is asymmetric: with distributed AI, most people invest in defense (which only needs to recognize and block attack paths), while attackers must find paths, acquire resources, deploy without detection, and overcome defenses. Unlike nuclear weapons which can only destroy, AI can be used defensively — creating vaccines against engineered pandemics, for instance. Howard draws the analogy to virology education: training more virologists has overwhelmingly produced vaccines and treatments rather than bioweapons, suggesting broad access to powerful technology tends toward net positive outcomes.
