Rodriguez offers himself as the proof point: he personally does customer support for Krea in Japanese to users despite not speaking it, providing what he calls excellent 'founder-level' support to a market he otherwise couldn't serve. The framing connects to the Tower of Babel — translation models effectively undo the curse, so everyone can keep speaking their own language yet still coordinate and communicate. This is the 'traffic,' the second-order effect, of solved translation: not just 'text gets translated' but 'the language barrier to operating in a market collapses.'

The immediate, actionable consequence is that language is no longer a gating constraint on market reach for small teams — Krea served global customers with 8-12 people. Founders and operators should treat any market as addressable for support, sales, and relationship-building regardless of the languages their team speaks, and design workflows that lean on real-time translation as core infrastructure rather than a novelty. The leverage compounds with team size: the smaller the team, the more outsized the reach this unlocks.
