One of Smith's four updated skepticism factors is that the rapid emergence of capable AI agents makes China's technological position less defensible than it appeared even 12 months ago. China's edge in technology competition has partly rested on a massive pool of technically-trained workers who can execute at scale — an advantage that is expensive and slow to replicate in smaller economies.

If AI agents can perform substantial portions of software development, data analysis, and engineering iteration, the raw labor-force multiplier diminishes. Countries with fewer engineers but better AI infrastructure access could close capability gaps much faster than traditional workforce-development timelines allow. This effect doesn't eliminate China's advantages (infrastructure, domestic market, state coordination), but it changes the competitive calculus in a domain where China was confident in long-run superiority.

This has implications beyond geopolitics: any competitive strategy that relies primarily on scale of human technical labor — whether a company, institution, or nation — should be stress-tested against an assumption that AI agents will absorb a significant fraction of that work within a decade.
