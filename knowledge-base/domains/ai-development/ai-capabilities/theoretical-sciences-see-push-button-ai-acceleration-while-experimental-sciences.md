There's a fundamental asymmetry in how AI accelerates different types of science. In theoretical fields — mathematics, theoretical physics, theoretical economics — the work consists of deriving logical implications from a set of assumptions. This is something machines excel at, and AI can often produce results in a near push-button manner (though checking the output remains a human task).

Experimental sciences face a categorically different constraint. No matter how powerful the AI, predictions about new material systems, drug interactions, or biological processes must ultimately be verified through physical experiments. This creates a natural speed limit that no amount of compute can overcome. The practical implication is that AI's transformative impact on science will be highly uneven: near-term revolutions in theoretical fields, but a slower, tool-augmented acceleration in experimental ones.
