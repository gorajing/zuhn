Gibson's research reveals that the Piraha people have no words for exact counting — not even for 'one.' You literally cannot ask 'I want two of those' in their language. This isn't a limitation they haven't gotten around to fixing; it's a fundamentally different cognitive toolkit. The finding proves that exact numerical reasoning is a cultural technology (like writing) rather than an innate human capability.

For AI development this has deep implications: if exact counting is learned rather than innate for humans, then LLMs' notorious struggles with arithmetic and counting are less surprising. It may not be a bug in transformer architecture but a reflection of the fact that numerical precision requires specific cognitive scaffolding that emerges from particular cultural practices, not from language exposure alone. This suggests that numerical capabilities in AI may need to be built through specialized architectural features (like tool use for calculation) rather than expecting them to emerge from scale.
