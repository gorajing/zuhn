Zuckerberg pushes back on the software-only singularity view with a concrete list of bottlenecks: building a gigawatt cluster takes years, NVIDIA needs time to stabilize each new system generation, networking has to be designed, buildings permitted, energy procured (gas turbines or green), and the whole supply chain of transformers, cooling, and interconnect coordinated. Even inside Meta, he gives a lived example: their ads-ranking experiment automation was already bottlenecked by compute and by the number of user cohorts available for statistically-significant tests — AI can generate more hypotheses, but Meta can't run them faster.

He adds a second, underrated bottleneck: humans co-evolving with the tools. 'These systems don't just show up fully formed with people magically knowing how to use them.' The feedback loop between users learning to use AI and developers making AI better is itself a gating factor — the personalization data accumulated over two years of use cannot be front-loaded into day one.

This reframes the AGI timeline debate. The question isn't whether recursive self-improvement of the model is possible; it's whether the non-model parts of the stack — energy, silicon, buildings, human workflows — can keep up. Engineering wisdom applies: solve one bottleneck, surface the next. Treat fast-takeoff forecasts that assume uniform scaling across the stack with skepticism.
