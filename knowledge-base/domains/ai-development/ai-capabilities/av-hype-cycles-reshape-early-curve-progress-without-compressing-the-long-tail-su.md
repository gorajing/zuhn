Dolgov's structural diagnosis: AV hype cycles are inevitable because the problem has a particular shape — easy to start, very hard to take all the way to fully autonomous superhuman safety. Whenever a new AI breakthrough lands (convnets, transformers, LLMs), it accelerates the early curve, attracts massive investment, but doesn't compress the long tail. The long tail is what kills companies that bet on silver bullets.

Waymo's survival strategy: combine a deeply important mission (1.19M road deaths/year, one every 26 seconds) with a clear-eyed assessment that no quick solution exists. This combination gives the team stamina to go the distance. The lesson generalizes — any technology with the same shape (easy first 80%, hard final 20%) follows the same hype-bust pattern, and survivors are those who internalized the long-tail reality early.

The 2026 corollary for AI generally: the same shape may apply to agents, AGI, autonomous coding. Each capability inflection (Karpathy's December 2025) compresses early-curve, doesn't necessarily compress the long tail to fully autonomous reliability. Companies betting on 'one more model release will close the gap' may be the next AV-style casualties when the long-tail reality reasserts. For Zuhn editorially: this is a useful frame for evaluating AI startup strategy — does the team treat it as a long-tail problem, or as a silver-bullet bet?
