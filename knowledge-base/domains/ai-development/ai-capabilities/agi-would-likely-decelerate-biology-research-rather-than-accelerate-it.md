When asked whether a million copies of himself running in a data center would 10x biology progress, Church argued it would likely eliminate it. His first concern is safety: we lack assurance that ASI would remain aligned. But setting safety aside, his substantive argument is twofold. First, a silicon AGI would conclude biology is not relevant to its own existence — why would it allocate cycles to wet lab design when it is not made of cells? Second, biology progress is fundamentally gated by experimental throughput: 'if you have nine women, can you do pregnancy in one month? No.' More thinking does not collapse the time it takes to grow an organoid, run a clinical trial, or iterate through safety testing.

He also pushed back on the framing that AGI is inevitable or urgent. Calling it 'a completely artificial emergency' — unlike COVID, there is no crisis that delaying AGI creates, whereas there is a substantial crisis that rushing it creates. His preferred path is doubling down on scientific narrow AI that integrates with wet lab pipelines (AI-designed libraries, protein design, barcoded screening) rather than chasing general intelligence that has no intrinsic reason to care about the biology problems humans care about.

The broader principle: for research fields gated by physical experimentation, adding compute-thinking does not compress the critical path. This applies equally to clinical trials, materials science, and aerospace, where the rate-limit is the physical world's feedback loop rather than human cognition. AGI optimists who assume cognition is the universal bottleneck are making a category error about what actually limits scientific progress.
