Hassabis's AGI architecture audit: pretraining at scale, RLHF, and chain-of-thought reasoning are 'pretty sure' to be part of the final architecture — they've come too far and proven too much to be dead ends. But continual learning, long-term reasoning, and cross-task consistency remain unsolved. The open question is whether these gaps close via incremental innovation on existing techniques, or whether 1-2 fundamentally new ideas are still needed. Hassabis explicitly puts the odds at 50/50.

The practical implication for builders: do not assume linear capability extrapolation from current models to AGI. There is a coin-flip's chance that a non-incremental breakthrough is still required, which means timeline projections that assume 'just scale current methods' are systematically biased toward optimism. Conversely, anyone betting on AGI requiring fundamentally new architecture should know that the world's leading AGI lab thinks the existing stack is at least 50% of the answer. For Zuhn editorially: this is a key data point against both the 'AGI in 18 months' camp and the 'we're decades away' camp.
