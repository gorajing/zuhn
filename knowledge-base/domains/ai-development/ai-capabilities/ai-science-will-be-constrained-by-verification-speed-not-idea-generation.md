In software, AI predictions can be verified near-instantly through compilers and unit tests, which is why AI has been so transformative there. But in experimental sciences like materials science, verifying a single AI-generated hypothesis about a new material system requires physical synthesis, characterization, and testing — processes that take days to months.

This means AI's impact on experimental science will be gated not by how clever the AI is, but by how fast verification infrastructure can operate. Autonomous labs and digital twins can help, but the fundamental asymmetry between hypothesis generation speed and experimental verification speed will persist. This has practical implications: the most impactful AI science investments may be in verification infrastructure (faster experiments, better simulations) rather than in more powerful AI models.
