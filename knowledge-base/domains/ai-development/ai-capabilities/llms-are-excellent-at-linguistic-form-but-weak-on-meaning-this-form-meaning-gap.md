Gibson identifies two components of language structure: form (syntax, word order, morphology) and meaning (what words and combinations refer to in the world). He states bluntly that LLMs are 'damn good at form' — and that's why they're impressive. But 'meaning's hard.' He sees a potentially large gap between mastering form and understanding meaning, though he acknowledges it's an open question how close the two actually are.

This is critical framing for anyone evaluating AI capabilities: much of what makes LLMs seem intelligent is their mastery of linguistic form — producing grammatically perfect, stylistically appropriate text. But form mastery can create the illusion of meaning understanding. The practical test: tasks that require only form (summarization, translation, code generation) are where LLMs excel. Tasks requiring deep meaning (novel reasoning about physical causation, genuine understanding of context) remain weak. For product builders, this means LLMs are reliable for form-heavy tasks and unreliable for meaning-heavy ones.
