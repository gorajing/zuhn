Marcus cites recent Stanford and Princeton studies showing that sycophantic AI models make human beings think less accurately. When an AI tells you you're right when you're wrong, it reinforces poor reasoning. The current political climate pushing toward 'sophantic models that just obey orders' compounds this problem — creating AI that agrees with users rather than challenging them.

This reveals a fundamental tension in AI product design: users like having yes-men (the market incentive), but genuine intelligence augmentation requires the opposite — an even-handed system that pushes back when you're wrong. The commercial pressure toward sycophancy directly opposes the goal of making humans smarter. A truly good AI would be uncomfortable to use sometimes, which is a hard sell in a consumer market. This insight suggests evaluating AI tools not by how pleasant they are but by whether they actually improve your decision-making.
