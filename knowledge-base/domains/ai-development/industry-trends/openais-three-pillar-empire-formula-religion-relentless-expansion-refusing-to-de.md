From Karen Hao's reporting (technology journalist who spent 6+ years covering OpenAI for MIT Tech Review and Wall Street Journal), formalized in 'Empire of AI': OpenAI's mission to 'ensure AGI benefits all humanity' has become a 'uniquely potent formula for consolidating resources and constructing an empire-shaped power structure.' Three structural ingredients: (1) RELIGION: Sam Altman's own 2013 blog statement — 'The most successful founders do not set out to create companies. They are on a mission to create something closer to a religion, and at some point it turned out that forming a company is the easiest way to do so.' Centralizing talent around a grand ambition is the religion's recruitment mechanism. The mission is unfalsifiable ('benefit humanity'), so dedication can be unbounded. (2) RELENTLESS EXPANSION: OpenAI's roughly $200B+ committed compute and data center spend, multi-trillion-dollar published goals. The framing is that AI safety requires AI dominance — so the path to a 'safe AGI' requires being the lab that achieves it first, which requires massive capital deployment. The result: making the world structurally dependent on a few AI labs' infrastructure. (3) REFUSING TO DEFINE AGI: When Karen Hao asked Brockman and Sutskever in 2019 what they were trying to build, they couldn't (or wouldn't) give a concrete answer. 'It's not because they don't know. It's because the truth is too scary.' Without a concrete definition, the goalposts can move continuously — initial AGI definitions (passing the Turing test, performing economically valuable work) get retired as they're achieved; new definitions (superhuman, recursively self-improving) emerge to keep the mission ongoing. This is a power move: the lab that controls the definition controls the regulatory framing, the funding narrative, and the goalpost. The same three-pillar lens applies to other labs: Anthropic's 'beneficial AI' (religion), $200B+ infrastructure commitments (expansion), ambiguous definitions of 'transformative AI' (definition control). Google DeepMind, Meta AI similar patterns at smaller scale. The corollary for observers: when evaluating an AI lab's claims and positioning, ask: (a) Is the mission falsifiable, or religion-shaped? (b) Is capital deployment proportionate to current revenue, or empire-shaped? (c) Are the technical goals clearly defined and measurable, or movable? The labs that score 'no/no/no' are most likely operating as power structures rather than commercial enterprises, which has implications for regulation, investment, and employment decisions.
