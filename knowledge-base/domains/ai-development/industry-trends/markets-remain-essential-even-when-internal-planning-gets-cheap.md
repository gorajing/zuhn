A natural worry with perfectly replicable AI firms is that the first company to automate everything just forms a massive conglomerate and swallows the economy — why would competing firms even exist? Dwarkesh addresses this with a classic Coasean argument: internal planning can be more efficient than markets in the short term, but any sufficiently large organization risks having its internal goals drift from market reality. Without an unbiased external signal, planners optimize for their own measures rather than real success.

He concedes the balance may shift. AI corporations will be more software-like, with perfect replication of successful subdivisions and faster internal feedback loops, raising the scale at which internal planning beats markets. But the fundamental need for an external reality check doesn't disappear — it just changes in degree.

For builders and policymakers, this predicts a world with larger, more internally efficient firms but still multiple competing entities — not a single AI monopolist. It also argues against assuming AI automation automatically produces winner-take-all outcomes across the whole economy.
