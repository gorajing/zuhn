Some goalpost-shifting on AGI is actually justified. Every previous bottleneck — common sense, reasoning, few-shot learning — has been crossed and yet we still don't have AGI in the economically-meaningful sense. The rational update is not that the bears keep moving the goalposts dishonestly, but that the original definition of AGI was too narrow because it under-weighted continual learning and on-the-job generalization.

This predicts the next several years: by 2030 labs will likely have made real progress on continual learning and earn hundreds of billions in revenue, but knowledge work will not be automated and the goalposts will move again — legitimately, because each new milestone reveals more of what intelligence actually requires.

Use this asymmetry when reading capability announcements: separate 'impressive on a benchmark' from 'translates into firm-level productivity' and weight them on different timelines.
