Gwern's observation that exceptional corporations don't clone themselves and take over all market segments reveals a deep structural limit: corporations are made of people, not interchangeable widgets or DNA strands. They can't replicate culture, leadership, or tacit knowledge fast enough to outrun aging and scleroticism. This is why market leadership turns over across generations even for companies with overwhelming advantages.

Dwarkesh argues AI firms break this constraint entirely. Successful subdivisions can be perfectly replicated, cultural drift can be measured and corrected at the weight level, and feedback loops run at compute speed rather than quarterly earnings speed. The analogy he reaches for is biological: the gulf between human firms and AI firms will look like the gulf between prokaryotes (structurally static for 3 billion years) and eukaryotes (which enabled trillion-cell organisms).

The strategic implication is that competitive moats built on human-organizational advantages — culture, institutional knowledge, talent density — become fragile once a competitor can operate on copyable code. Evolvability itself becomes the moat.
