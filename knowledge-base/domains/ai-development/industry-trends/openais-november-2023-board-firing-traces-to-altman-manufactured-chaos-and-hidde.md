Hao's sourcing: ~6-7 people directly involved or with first-hand access to the decision-making. Sequence: (1) Sutskever approaches independent board member Helen Toner with concerns about Altman's behavior leading to bad research outcomes and poor decision-making; (2) Sutskever plus CTO Mira Murati document via email, Slack messages, and other communications that Altman is pitting teams against each other, creating an environment where teams compete rather than collaborate on what both executives considered potentially civilization-altering technology; (3) independent board member Adam D'Angelo, at a San Francisco party, hears rumors that the OpenAI Startup Fund structure is strange; when he finally sees the documents, the OpenAI Startup Fund turns out to be Altman's personal fund, not OpenAI's — a fact never disclosed to the board; (4) multiple similar inconsistencies between Altman's representations and actual state lead the independent directors to the Instacart question: 'if this were Instacart, would behavior warrant firing?' — conclusion 'maybe not, but this is not Instacart.'

The firing executed without stakeholder communication because the board feared Altman's 'persuasive abilities would make it impossible to do' if he found out first. Microsoft got a call minutes before execution. The resulting outrage from uninformed stakeholders caused the 5-day reversal. Sutskever and Murati both later left.

Implication for AI governance: the nonprofit board-independence structure that was supposed to provide public-interest oversight failed because the actual governance problem was not a values disagreement — it was information asymmetry caused by one person manipulating flows of information to all counterparties simultaneously.
