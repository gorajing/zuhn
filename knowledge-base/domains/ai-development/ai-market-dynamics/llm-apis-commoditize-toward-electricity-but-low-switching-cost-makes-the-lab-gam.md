The keynote makes a sharp distinction that cuts against the default 'AI labs are the new enterprise-software incumbents' reasoning. In long run (the speaker says ~15 years), LLM APIs become like electricity — a commodity enabling technology underneath everything. The price may even be high (electricity isn't free), but the role is commodity infrastructure.

The critical difference from traditional enterprise SaaS is switching cost. Salesforce and Workday have horrible UX by many accounts, yet zero Fortune 500 companies switch off them — the switching cost (data, integrations, retraining, process entanglement, fear) is so high that even AI-era alternatives can't dislodge them. LLM API providers do NOT have this moat: the switching cost between Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google is low (not zero — outputs differ, prompts need retuning — but low). Evidence: when OpenAI's Codex pushed hard on coding (after Anthropic surpassed it in revenue), many companies switched 'in a split second,' especially when the cost delta was 5-10x; it was a top-down decision, sometimes sweetened by startup credits.

The consequences: (1) AI-lab revenue, even at spectacular ARR growth (the speaker cites a jump from ~$9B to ~$44B ARR), is structurally less defensible than SaaS revenue because customers can leave cheaply; (2) competition stays brutal and price/performance-driven rather than lock-in-driven; (3) Anthropic's enterprise relationships help but don't create Salesforce-grade stickiness. For investors and founders: do NOT model AI-lab durability on SaaS lock-in analogies. The defensibility, if any, must come from somewhere other than switching cost — distribution, brand/trust, proprietary data, or workflow embedding above the API. This is why 'what sits above the commodity API' (brand, taste, the application/workflow layer) is where the speaker says durable value accrues.
