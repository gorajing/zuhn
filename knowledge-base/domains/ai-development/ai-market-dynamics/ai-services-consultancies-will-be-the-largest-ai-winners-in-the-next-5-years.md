The dominant narrative in AI venture capital has been that pure-play AI products (Claude, ChatGPT, Copilot, vertical AI tools) capture most of the economic value from AI adoption, with services firms capturing leftover implementation dollars. Aaron Levie inverts this: because enterprise AI adoption requires substantial change-management, data-state remediation, workflow redesign, regulatory compliance review, and ongoing agent configuration maintenance — none of which the pure-play products can supply — the services layer will capture a disproportionate share of enterprise AI spend. Accenture's AI practice, Deloitte's AI transformation teams, Capgemini's specialized units, plus the fast-growing AI-native consultancies are positioned to capture this. The economic math: a $1M agent deployment at a bank might generate $50K in AI software spend and $950K in implementation services. Multiply across thousands of enterprise deployments. The structural reason this does not auto-commoditize: services work requires senior people with both AI technical knowledge and industry-specific workflow knowledge, and the supply of such people is severely constrained. The investment implication: allocators looking for AI exposure should weight services firms higher than most current AI-themed portfolios do, and consider boutique AI services firms as potentially higher-margin opportunities.
