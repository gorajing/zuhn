The common tech-industry framing of regulation as inherently anti-innovation ignores historical evidence. Industries like pharmaceuticals, finance, and aviation all scaled dramatically under regulatory frameworks that provided consumer trust and market stability. The FDA model demonstrates that subject-matter experts from industry and academia can evaluate risks while offering fast-track pathways for lower-risk innovations.

For AI, this means the question isn't whether to regulate but how to design regulation that acts as a trellis for growth rather than a cage. Chamath argues that self-regulation fails because profit motives are too strong — the tobacco industry's decades of deception being the canonical example. The implication for AI companies and investors is that proactive engagement with regulation is strategically superior to resistance, because poorly designed late regulation (like Section 230) creates worse outcomes than carefully designed early frameworks.
