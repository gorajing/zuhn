Tracking the Forbes AI 50 year over year, a16z observed that about 40% of last year's companies fell off the list this year — an unusually short half-life for category leaders. This sits on top of the historical pattern that first movers rarely capture the economic value of a market (Google wasn't the first search engine; Facebook wasn't the first social network), but compressed: the underlying technology is shifting so fast that the 'shifting sands underneath' founders move faster than in prior cycles.

The strategic consequence is a divergence between the size and the predictability of outcomes. The prize is getting much larger, but the ability to forecast which specific company captures it is getting much weaker. This is precisely the dynamic that pushes a serious investor toward early-stage breadth (back many of the spaces that could work) rather than concentrated late-stage conviction in a current leader whose position may evaporate within a year.

The data point is noisy — list membership is a crude proxy and a single year-over-year comparison — hence medium confidence. But the directional claim that AI defensibility is decaying faster than in prior platform shifts is corroborated by the speakers' own repeated prior reversals on who captures value (models-are-everything → apps-are-everything → models-leg-up-into-apps).
