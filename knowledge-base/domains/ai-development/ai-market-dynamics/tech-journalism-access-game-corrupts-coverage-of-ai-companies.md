Hao's own history illustrates the mechanism: after her 2020 MIT Tech Review profile of OpenAI, Sam Altman emailed the company that the profile was 'not great,' and OpenAI shut her out for 3 years. When a colleague at MIT Tech Review tried to pass a story to her, OpenAI intervened: 'Oh no, we have a history.' She only reopened communication when she joined the Wall Street Journal — the weight of that brand compelled engagement. When she then wrote Empire of AI, OpenAI initially cooperated, then iced her out after the board fired Altman, refusing to answer any of 40 pages of requests for comment.

Bartlett corroborates from the other side: a 'particular AI person' has dangled the carrot of appearing on his show for 18 months, precisely to train him into not being critical. The strategy is to never say no, just keep delaying — optimal outcome is keeping the journalist compliant forever without ever having to sit for an interview. Hao also documents OpenAI subpoenaing critics like the MIDAS watchdog during the for-profit conversion fight, demanding all their communications involving Musk in what appears to be both intimidation and network mapping.

Practical implication: when reading tech journalism about frontier AI labs, weight the coverage by whether the outlet has access. Outlets with access are systematically softer. The counterintuitive implication: getting the door shut is strategically valuable for a journalist who wants to do real reporting, even though it feels career-ending in the moment.
