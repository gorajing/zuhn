The Rust Belt and China Shock demonstrate that humans and institutions are far less adaptable than economic models assume. If comparative advantage shifts rapidly — making 'doctor' a human job one decade and an AI job the next, then human again when opportunity costs rise — the institutions supporting that work (medical schools, premed programs, licensing bodies) would collapse during the interregnum and couldn't reconstitute quickly.

This is the most practically dangerous scenario because it can occur even under the optimistic comparative advantage framework. The formal model by Korinek and Suh shows human labor remains complementary to machines right up until an abrupt discontinuity when AI masters the last exclusively-human task. This sudden phase transition, not gradual decline, is what makes the adjustment problem so severe.

The policy implication is that even if comparative advantage protects long-run wages, society needs mechanisms to handle rapid occupational churn — more robust than anything current retraining programs offer.
