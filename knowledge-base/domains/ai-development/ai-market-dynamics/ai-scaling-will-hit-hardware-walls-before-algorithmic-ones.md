Musk's argument for orbital data centers stems from a broader claim: AI is transitioning from a software-constrained to a hardware-constrained field. Terrestrial grids face physical limits on the electricity and cooling infrastructure needed for continued model scaling. His prediction that space-based compute (leveraging constant solar power and vacuum cooling) will be the lowest-cost compute within 3 years is aggressive but directionally important.

The insight extends beyond SpaceX's specific bet. As AI models grow, every major lab faces the same infrastructure problem — the 'software people learning a hardware lesson.' This suggests that the next wave of AI competitive advantage may accrue to companies with energy and infrastructure assets, not just those with the best researchers or algorithms. The 100 GW annual compute target requiring near-hourly Starship launches illustrates the sheer physical scale of the challenge.
