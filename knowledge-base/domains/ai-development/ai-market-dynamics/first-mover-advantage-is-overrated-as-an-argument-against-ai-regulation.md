The fear that regulation will cause fatal competitive delays rests on an assumption that first-mover advantage is decisive. Historical evidence contradicts this: Google wasn't the first search engine, Facebook wasn't the first social network, and both dominated by building better products on established foundations.

Applied to AI, this means a regulatory pause measured in months or even years is unlikely to determine who wins the AI race. What matters more is the quality of the framework within which innovation occurs. A country or company that builds on a trusted, well-governed AI infrastructure may ultimately outperform one that moves faster but accumulates regulatory debt and public distrust. This reframes the US-China AI competition: setting global standards proactively is a stronger strategic position than racing unregulated.
