Karpathy argues that recursive self-improvement and 'intelligence explosion' have been happening continuously for centuries — compilers, search engines, IDEs, and now Claude Code are all part of the same automation continuum. He tried to find computers or mobile phones in GDP data and couldn't: they're smoothed into the same 2% exponential because diffusion is always slower than frontier capability.

He expects AI to follow the same pattern. There's no moment where we 'have God in a box' — instead we get an autonomy slider where humans supervise teams of mostly-autonomous agents, gradually raising the abstraction layer. Call center workers get 80% automated with humans handling the 20% long-tail, and the economy refactors around task boundaries rather than job boundaries.

The counter-argument Dwarkesh raises: historical regime changes like the Industrial Revolution did move growth from 0.2% to 2%, and true AGI is labor itself — qualitatively different from productivity-improving technologies. Karpathy concedes he's willing to be convinced but his prior remains that the same smooth exponential absorbs AI too. This timelines question is load-bearing for AI policy, investment, and career planning.
