Developed economies currently grow at roughly 2% nominal and ~0% inflation-adjusted. If AI is genuinely comparable to the Industrial Revolution, that growth rate should visibly change. Anything short of that is supply-side hype disconnected from whether the intelligence is actually being absorbed by the broader economy. The biggest winners of a real AI revolution are not AI labs but the industries consuming the commodity.

This reframes capex debates: Microsoft's $80B build looks small if you believe 10% global growth is imminent (an extra $10T/year in value), and looks reckless if you believe the supply-demand map is broken. Nadella tracks inference revenue explicitly as the governor — if you cannot convert yesterday's capital into today's demand, exponential supply investment will go off the rails. This is why he emphasizes 'complete thoughts' over pure model bets.

For anyone evaluating AI industry claims, Nadella's frame is a useful shield against vibes-based AGI discourse: ignore benchmark announcements and watch GDP, productivity statistics, and whether large enterprise users (Fortune 500 change management cycles) actually absorb the technology. The lag from capability to economic impact is where most forecasts fail.
