OpenAI's 2019 transition is often framed as mission drift — a safety-focused lab gradually seduced by commercial success. But the timeline suggests the opposite: the restructuring was a deliberate capital access decision. Training frontier AI models requires massive compute investment, and the nonprofit structure couldn't attract sufficient capital. The capped-profit model was designed specifically to unlock Microsoft's $1B investment.

Once that structure was in place, commercialization wasn't optional — it was a structural obligation. Investors with 100x upside caps need the company to generate returns. The rapid progression from GPT-3 to ChatGPT to GPT-4 to developer tools wasn't Altman going rogue; it was the inevitable consequence of a capital structure that demanded commercial velocity. This reframes the Altman-Sutskever conflict: it wasn't safety vs. greed, but two co-founders trapped in a structure that made their goals mutually exclusive.
