Eval and ML-monitoring tooling existed and matured for over a decade (H2O, Algorithmia, Seldon from ~2012 onward), yet every year's claim of 'this is the year of evals' fell flat. Dickerson's thesis is that the bottleneck was never technical — it was that AI/ML lived inside an opaque box that never bubbled above the CIO, so the people controlling real budget (CEO, CFO, CISO) had no reason to care.

The inflection came from three non-technical forces converging: (1) ChatGPT gave non-technical executives a single UI to be 'wowed' by AI, making it legible; (2) a 2022 recession-fear budget freeze meant the only unfrozen discretionary money flowed to GenAI 'pet projects' the CEO now personally understood; (3) agentic systems began acting autonomously, forcing a quantitative estimate of risk. Evals became fundable when they became a first-class C-suite discussion point.

The practical lesson for anyone building or selling AI infrastructure: track executive legibility and budget-cycle timing as leading indicators of demand, not benchmark scores or feature parity. A technically superior tool sells nothing until a budget-holder can narrate why it matters in terms they already care about.
