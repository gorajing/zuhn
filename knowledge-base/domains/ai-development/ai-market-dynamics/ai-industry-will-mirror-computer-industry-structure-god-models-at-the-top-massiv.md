Andreessen draws a structural analogy between AI and the entire history of computing. Just as mainframes gave way to minicomputers, PCs, and then embedded chips in everything, AI will have a pyramid structure: a small number of god models running in giant data centers at the top, followed by progressively smaller models cascading down to embedded systems running on individual chips inside every physical object.

The evidence is already visible: capabilities that required the biggest models 6-12 months ago are being replicated in small models (e.g., Kimmy replicating GPT-5 reasoning on one or two MacBooks). The smartest models will always be at the top, but the volume — and likely the total market value — will be in the smaller models that proliferate outward. This mirrors how CPUs evolved: Intel had the lead on high-end processors, but the real volume was ARM chips in every phone and embedded device. For startups, this means building for smaller, more efficient models may capture more total market than targeting only frontier capabilities.
