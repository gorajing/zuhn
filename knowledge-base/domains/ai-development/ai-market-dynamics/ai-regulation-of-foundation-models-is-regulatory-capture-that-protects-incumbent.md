Howard distinguishes between regulating high-risk AI applications (health insurance denials, criminal justice) — which he supports — and regulating the creation of foundation models themselves. The latter was added to the EU AI Act through lobbying from 'AI safety nonprofits' funded by tech billionaire money, and would require government permission to build models exceeding current frontier capabilities.

This is textbook regulatory capture: incumbents lobby for regulations that specifically prevent new entrants from competing. The practical effect would be European AI founders seeking visas to UAE or Singapore, while providing zero safety benefit since the models would still be built elsewhere. Howard draws a parallel to WIPO's global copyright regime — another case where powerful incumbents used international regulation to lock in their advantages under the guise of public benefit. France, Germany, and other member states pushed back on behalf of their own AI startups.
