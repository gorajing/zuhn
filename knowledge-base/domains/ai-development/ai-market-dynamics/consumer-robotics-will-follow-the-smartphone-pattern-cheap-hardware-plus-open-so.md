Thomas Wolf argues that previous consumer robot attempts (like Jibo) failed because they were too expensive and had fixed behaviors, maybe 5-10 tricks before the novelty wore off. The Hugging Face approach inverts this: ship a $100-300 robot as an open platform where the software is endlessly extensible through community-built behaviors, VLM integrations, speech models, and vibe-coded routines.

The parallel to smartphones is deliberate: iPhones shipped with a few default apps but the real value came from the app store ecosystem. Similarly, Reachy Mini ships with basic behaviors but users can plug in any new AI model, code new behaviors, and share them. The fact that startups are already building businesses on top of the $100 robot arm suggests early ecosystem traction. The bet is on entertainment, education, and fun as the entry wedge rather than reliability-critical industrial applications.
