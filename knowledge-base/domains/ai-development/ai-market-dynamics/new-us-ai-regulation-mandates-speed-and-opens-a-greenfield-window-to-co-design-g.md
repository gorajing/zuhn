Myshatyn pushes back on the assumption that government regulation only slows AI down. The April OMB memoranda — replacing the prior administration's executive orders — explicitly tell agencies to 'move faster into bringing this into everything we do,' and to go deeper than buying an office add-in to 'type PowerPoints faster.' The mandate is to bring AI into core mission work, which raises the stakes (these are workloads where 'people can die if we do this wrong') but also the urgency.

Crucially, the memos kick off a roughly 180-day rulemaking period in which agencies must develop their own AI strategies, define what counts as high-risk vs. low-risk in their context, and decide how to govern pilots. As Myshatyn puts it, AI governance is 'still on the drawing board' — 'a clean sheet of paper from a technology perspective that we largely haven't had to tackle,' offering vendors a chance to 'develop the future with your customers right now.'

This is a timing-sensitive strategic signal: regulatory ambiguity in a fast-moving domain is not only a risk but an opening. Vendors and labs who engage during the rulemaking window can shape what the controls actually look like — making 'less obnoxious, less obstructive decisions' — rather than inheriting rules written without them. The window is finite, which is what makes early engagement valuable. Existing scaffolding like the NIST AI Risk Management Framework (2023) provides the prescriptive starting points.
