Evans lays out a chain of argument for why frontier models commoditize: there is no apparent way to build a sustainably differentiated model, no network effect, and no equivalent of YouTube's or Google's structural lock-in — the only durable difference between labs is willingness to spend money. With three-to-six players all selling near-identical capability on near-identical chips, no obvious source of price discipline exists, especially when some (Google with ads) have entirely different business models and pricing incentives. He notes that an enterprise buying legal or manufacturing software no longer asks 'does this run on Claude or OpenAI' any more than they ask which cloud a SaaS app sits on — the model is abstracted away, which is precisely the signature of infrastructure, not platform.

The historical pattern he leans on: chip companies, ISPs, and mobile network operators all built expensive, transformative, high-growth infrastructure and captured little of the value, because the value moved up the stack to those with OS-level levers (Windows, iOS) or to application companies. Mobile data traffic rose ~1,500-2,000x while carrier stocks stayed flat for 20 years. Evans is careful to hold this as a falsifiable hypothesis rather than a certainty — his stated position is 'here is a deterministic-looking argument that these become commodities; explain to me why they won't' — and he flags the alternative worlds (only two viable labs with pricing power, or the model subsuming most software) as live possibilities. The actionable read for builders and investors: do not assume foundation-model providers will be the value-capturing layer; bet on where the leverage actually accrues.
