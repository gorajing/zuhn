The geopolitical-competitive framing the panel offered separates robotics into two layers and argues the battleground has moved:
1. Mechanical layer — building the physical robot. China is excellent at this (deep manufacturing ecosystems, supply-chain density).
2. Intelligence layer — the world models and policies that let machines do useful work in real industrial settings. This is the next wave and where the decisive value sits.

The panelist's optimism for the US rests on its strength in the intelligence layer via hyperscalers (Amazon, Google) and the training infrastructure they provide. The sharp distinction: the impactful application is NOT 'stupid humanoids dancing on stage' (the demos that go viral) but the unglamorous intelligence to do real industrial work — and that requires enormous targeted training for specific use cases.

The sober timeline anchor: Waymo said it would have cars everywhere (including Bangalore) in three years; 12 years later it works in a few cities. The lesson — the training required for real-world reliability in a specific domain is vastly larger and slower than demos suggest. The intelligence layer is hard precisely because real industrial impact needs use-case-specific world models trained on enormous data, not general capability.

The investment/strategy read: in robotics, weight the intelligence/world-model layer over mechanical novelty when assessing where durable value and competitive advantage accrue. Mechanical capability is increasingly commodity; the use-case-specific intelligence layer is the moat. This connects to the world-model and physical-AI-data insights — the intelligence layer is exactly the hard, data-starved part.
