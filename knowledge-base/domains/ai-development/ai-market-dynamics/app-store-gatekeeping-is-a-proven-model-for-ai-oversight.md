Rather than inventing AI regulation from scratch, Chamath points to existing private-sector gatekeeping as a template. Apple's App Store and Google Play already evaluate apps on novelty, risk, and potential for exploitation before allowing distribution. Facebook similarly sandboxed apps to observe behavior before public launch.

Translating this to AI means a federal oversight body could investigate new AI models, stress-test them for worst-case scenarios, and determine safety before deployment — similar to how the FDA offers multiple approval pathways calibrated to risk level. The key insight is that the infrastructure pattern already exists and works at scale; the gap is that it's controlled by private companies rather than accountable public institutions. For AI founders and investors, this suggests that regulatory compliance infrastructure — testing frameworks, safety documentation, sandboxing tools — will become a significant market category.
