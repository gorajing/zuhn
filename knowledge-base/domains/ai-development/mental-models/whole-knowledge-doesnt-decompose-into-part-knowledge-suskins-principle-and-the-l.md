From the quantum entanglement essay, citing Leonard Susskind: 'You can know everything about an entangled system and still know nothing about its individual components.' This is a structural feature of quantum mechanics — the wave function of an entangled pair contains all the information about the system as a whole, but no information about each particle individually. The whole has properties (e.g., total spin = 0) that the parts don't independently possess (each particle has no definite spin alone). The general lesson: the reductionist heuristic 'understand the parts, then understand the whole' works when parts are independent. It fails when parts are deeply relational. This extends far beyond quantum: (1) Codebases with heavy coupling — understanding each module in isolation tells you almost nothing about how the system behaves. (2) Organizations with strong cultural cohesion — knowing each individual's KPIs tells you nothing about how the team collaborates. (3) LLMs with emergent capabilities — knowing each parameter tells you nothing about model behavior at scale. (4) Networked markets — knowing each firm's strategy tells you nothing about market dynamics. The practical implication: when a system shows strong inter-part dependencies, drop the 'analyze parts independently' heuristic. Instead, study the joint behavior — perturb the whole, observe how it responds, model the relationships rather than the entities. The trap: smart analysts default to reductionism because it gives a sense of progress (you can document each part). The output looks rigorous and is fundamentally misleading.
