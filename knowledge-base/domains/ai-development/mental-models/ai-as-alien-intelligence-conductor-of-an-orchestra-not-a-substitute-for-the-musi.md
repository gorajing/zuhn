    to anthropomorphize AI ('it thinks just like me but faster'). This default
    is wrong in ways that lead to systematic errors: (1) Humans correctly intuit
    that other humans have rich context, lived experience, shared cultural
    assumptions — and incorrectly assume AI does too. So we phrase prompts as if
    AI shares our background, and we're surprised when it doesn't. (2) Humans

    AI's confident answers when we should treat them as hypotheses. (3) Humans
    correctly intuit that other humans will catch their own obvious mistakes —
    and incorrectly assume AI does. So we don't verify outputs that a competent
    human would have flagged. The 'alien intelligence' mental model corrects all
    three errors: (a) Assume AI doesn't share your context; specify everything
    explicitly. (b) Assume AI confidence is uncorrelated with correctness;
    verify regardless. (c) Assume AI doesn't self-catch mistakes; you're the
    verifier. The conductor metaphor adds direction: a conductor doesn't play

    judgment about when and how to deploy the orchestra. Same with AI: your
    value as a human collaborator is judgment about when and how to deploy the
    AI's powerful-but-alien capabilities. The corollary: trying to 'compete with
    AI' on its own terms (writing faster than ChatGPT, coding faster than
    Copilot, summarizing faster than Claude) is the wrong frame. You're the
    conductor, not the trombone player. Compete on conducting quality.

Manuel Corpas's mental model: 'You have to think about artificial intelligence as alien intelligence, which is powerful, but it's not going to think exactly the same. And it's not going to have the nuance that human experience can give. Rather than substituting it, what you do is you drive it like a conductor in an orchestra — you tell the musicians when they need to play, at what intensity. You become a coordinator rather than a player.' Why the 'alien intelligence' framing matters: humans have a strong default to anthropomorphize AI ('it thinks just like me but faster'). This default is wrong in ways that lead to systematic errors: (1) Humans correctly intuit that other humans have rich context, lived experience, shared cultural assumptions — and incorrectly assume AI does too. So we phrase prompts as if AI shares our background, and we're surprised when it doesn't. (2) Humans correctly intuit that another human's strong opinion is grounded in evidence and reasoning — and incorrectly assume AI confidence is too. So we trust AI's confident answers when we should treat them as hypotheses. (3) Humans correctly intuit that other humans will catch their own obvious mistakes — and incorrectly assume AI does. So we don't verify outputs that a competent human would have flagged. The 'alien intelligence' mental model corrects all three errors: (a) Assume AI doesn't share your context; specify everything explicitly. (b) Assume AI confidence is uncorrelated with correctness; verify regardless. (c) Assume AI doesn't self-catch mistakes; you're the verifier. The conductor metaphor adds direction: a conductor doesn't play any instrument better than the orchestra members — but coordinates them to produce something none of them could alone. The conductor's value is judgment about when and how to deploy the orchestra. Same with AI: your value as a human collaborator is judgment about when and how to deploy the AI's powerful-but-alien capabilities. The corollary: trying to 'compete with AI' on its own terms (writing faster than ChatGPT, coding faster than Copilot, summarizing faster than Claude) is the wrong frame. You're the conductor, not the trombone player. Compete on conducting quality.
