The Rayleigh-Plesset equation describes how a bubble's radius changes over time in a fluid, accounting for surface tension, viscosity, and pressure differentials. A key feature is that expansion and collapse are not mirror images—bubbles grow gradually but collapse with extreme violence because the nonlinear inertial terms accelerate during contraction.

This asymmetry pattern—where a system's growth phase looks nothing like its collapse phase—is a powerful mental model. Market bubbles inflate slowly through gradual sentiment shifts but crash rapidly when feedback loops reverse. Organizational growth is incremental but organizational failure cascades. Understanding that the same governing equation can produce gentle expansion and violent collapse helps calibrate expectations: never assume a system's unwinding will mirror its winding.
