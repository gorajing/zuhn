From the quantum entanglement essay: 'Decoherence describes what happens when a delicate quantum system interacts with its environment. Instead of remaining isolated, the system becomes entangled with countless surrounding particles, air molecules, thermal radiation, and electromagnetic noise. Each interaction spreads the quantum information outward, distributing it across a vast number of degrees of freedom. What once was a clean isolated superposition becomes entangled with the environment in such a complex way that it can no longer be observed as a coherent whole. The system doesn't lose its quantum nature — it just becomes too entangled to track.' The analogy: trying to hear a whisper in a crowded room. The original signal is still there but is overwhelmed by background. Entanglement is the rule, not the exception — but at large scales it averages out into stable classical behavior. The lesson generalizes to any layered system: 'emergent stability' often hides underlying chaos. Markets look efficient at quarterly time scales but are intensely chaotic at millisecond scales. Organizations look hierarchical on the org chart but are constituted by millions of micro-interactions. LLMs look like coherent reasoners at the conversational level but are stochastic at the token-by-token level. The practical implication for builders: don't be misled by the emergent stability at the scale you observe — the underlying dynamics may be radically different, and they matter when conditions change. The corollary for AI: when your AI behaves consistently at one scale (single prompt), don't assume it will behave consistently at adjacent scales (multi-step agent loops). Decoherence-class effects (state spreading across hidden interactions) may be hiding inconsistencies you'd see if you could observe more granularly.
