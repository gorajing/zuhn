swyx pushes back on the Oxford English Dictionary's framing that ties 'slop' to being 'generated using artificial intelligence.' He accepts 'low-quality, inauthentic, or inaccurate' but rejects the AI clause: any human or AI can be an agent of slop. The same studio (Netflix), the same startup idea, the same exponential chart can land as either 'kino' (the internet-slang opposite of slop — fresh, high-taste) or slop depending entirely on the care behind it.

The practical consequence is that 'don't use AI' is the wrong defense and 'apply taste' is the right one. Treating slop as an AI artifact lets human-generated slop pass unexamined and frames AI as the enemy rather than a tool that can be pointed either way. Once you decouple slop from its means of production, the question for any output — code, content, a release, a tweet — becomes 'is this high-taste and accountable?' rather than 'was a model involved?'
