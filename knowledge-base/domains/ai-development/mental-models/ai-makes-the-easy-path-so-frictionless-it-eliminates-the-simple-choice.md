Rich Hickey's distinction is the load-bearing idea: 'simple' means one fold, no entanglement — each piece does one thing; 'easy' means adjacent, within reach, accessible without effort. You cannot wish something simple (it requires thought, design, untangling), but you can always make something easier by putting it closer — a package, a Stack Overflow snippet, a generated block. Every time you choose easy you choose speed now, complexity later.

That trade historically worked because complexity accumulated slowly enough to refactor before it compounded. The argument here is that AI broke the balance: it is the ultimate 'easy' button, so frictionless that the simple path is never even evaluated — 'why think about architecture when code appears instantly?' The practical takeaway is to treat the absence of friction as a hazard, not a feature, and to deliberately re-insert the design step that the tool removed.
