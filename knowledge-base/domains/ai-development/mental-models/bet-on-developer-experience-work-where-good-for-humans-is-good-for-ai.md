The future of AI tooling is too unpredictable to bet on any single technology, so the highest-value framing is to ask which investments will be valuable 'no matter what happens.' Kanat-Alexander's answer is a 'no-regrets' filter: invest in the things around the agent — the development environment, validation, codebase structure, written context, code-review velocity — that make both humans and agents more effective. Because these are inputs to the agent rather than the agent itself, they survive any churn in the underlying models or coding tools.

The unifying principle is that 'what's good for humans is good for AI.' Clear error messages, testable code, standard tooling, and documented intent were always good engineering practice; agents simply raise the stakes by being unable to compensate with tribal knowledge or mind-reading. The practical payoff of the framing is asymmetric: even when an investment fails to help the agent, it still helps the human team, so the downside is capped while the upside compounds. Treat agent-readiness not as a separate initiative but as an intensified version of developer experience you should have been funding anyway.
