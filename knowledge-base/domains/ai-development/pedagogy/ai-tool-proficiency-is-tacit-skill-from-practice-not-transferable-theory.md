Orosz relays Simon Willison's observation that after years of daily use he is still figuring out what works and constantly changing his workflows — 'there's no manual.' Two properties make AI tools hard for engineers specifically: it takes a long time to get good and you must keep practicing, and understanding the underlying theory does not transfer to skill. This breaks the engineer's usual intuition that mechanistic understanding (how the compiler or CPU works) yields efficiency gains.

The consequence is that AI proficiency is tacit and experiential, not propositional — it behaves more like a craft or a sport than like a body of theory. The teams extracting the most value are characterized by disposition rather than knowledge: low ego, openness to learning, and willingness to 'leave your priors behind' (while keeping your experience). Practically, this means training programs built around explaining model internals will underperform programs built around sustained guided practice, and any individual's proficiency decays/resets when the tools or team context shift, demanding continuous re-learning rather than a one-time skill acquisition.
