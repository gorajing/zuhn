Soria Parra frames a phase shift: 2024 was demos, 2025 was coding agents, and 2026 is about putting general agents into production. Coding agents succeeded because they are the ideal case — local, verifiable, you can call a compiler, a developer sits in front of the screen to fix failures, and you can render a UI. None of those favorable conditions are about the model alone; they're about a tightly controlled, verifiable local environment.

General agents doing real knowledge work — what a financial analyst or a marketer does — don't get that environment. They don't need a local agent calling a compiler; they need to connect to roughly five SaaS applications and a shared drive. For them the single most important capability is connectivity. That reframes where engineering effort matters: the gating factor moves from local execution and verification toward integration, authorization, and data access across heterogeneous systems.

This is why MCP is positioned as central to 2026 — it's the standardized 'connective tissue' for reaching those external systems with proper semantics and enterprise concerns (auth, governance). It's a dated, directional prediction: if it holds, the wave of value shifts from coding copilots to connected general agents, and the infrastructure bottleneck is integration breadth rather than raw model capability.
