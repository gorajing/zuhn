Asked whether the labs simply train for whatever has near-term economic value, Staniszewski says they frequently train models and assemble data that provide no short-term value. The clearest example: a team of over a thousand former voice coaches, musicians, and artists who annotate the 'how' of audio behind the scenes — what emotion was used, how a voice is described, how a piece of music is described — not just the literal content. They are explicit that this annotation 'will not provide value in the next 6 to 12 months, but we think it will in the next 12 to 24,' and that the underlying data is often not even accessible without this effort.

The directional claim challenges the cynical read that labs only chase economic value. The defensible asset is a proprietary, expensively-annotated dataset capturing the qualitative dimensions competitors skip — and it compounds precisely because most teams optimize for the next two quarters. For product strategists building AI products, the lesson is that the moat may live in the annotation layer rather than the model architecture, and capturing it requires tolerating a year-plus of no visible return. The risk is obvious (a long bet that may not pay off, or that synthetic data leapfrogs hand-annotation), which is why it's medium confidence — but it is a deliberate, fundable strategy, not an accident.
