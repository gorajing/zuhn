Asked where the defensible moats in audio models actually are, Staniszewski gives a two-layer answer. On the model layer, real quality still requires sustained focus: getting in front of users, collecting preferences and data, fine-tuning, and handling domain specificity (healthcare differs from financial services differs from education). If you genuinely care about quality, that ongoing model work is a continuous advantage. But he immediately reframes: most use cases treat the model as just a small part of their stack. So ElevenLabs invests heavily beyond the research — understanding the user's workflow, combining audio models with knowledge, integrating telephony so agents work across channels, and providing evaluation, testing, and monitoring. On top of that sits an ecosystem: distribution and a trusted brand, pre-built templates for agents and creative workflows, and a library of over 20,000 user-contributed voices spanning languages and styles.

The directional claim is that in applied AI, model quality is necessary but not sufficient — durable defensibility accrues in the product surface and ecosystem that make the model useful. The framing echoes Jensen Huang's remark to the founder that speech-to-text is 'technology' while text-to-speech is 'artistry': the artistry (emotionality, preference-tuning) is one moat, but the ecosystem of trust, templates, integrations, and contributed assets is the layer that's hardest for a pure model to displace. For founders building on or competing with foundation models, the strategic implication is to compete on the workflow and ecosystem, not on the model in isolation.
