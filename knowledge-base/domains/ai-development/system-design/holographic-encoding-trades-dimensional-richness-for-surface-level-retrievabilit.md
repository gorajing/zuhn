The holographic principle shows that all information about a 3D volume can be fully encoded on its 2D boundary surface. This is not lossy compression — the full information is preserved, just represented differently. A person inside a black hole experiences normal 3D life, while an outside observer sees them as flattened information on the event horizon. Both perspectives are complete and valid.

This maps directly to how modern AI systems handle information. Embedding models compress rich, high-dimensional semantic content (documents, images, conversations) into fixed-dimension vectors. The original richness isn't lost — it's encoded in a form that enables operations (similarity search, clustering) impossible in the original representation. The key insight is that dimensional reduction isn't information destruction; it's information reformatting that enables different operations. The tradeoff is always the same: you lose intuitive readability but gain computational tractability and guaranteed preservation.
