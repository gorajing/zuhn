The debate about China vs US AI dominance typically focuses on chips, talent, and model quality. Kroeber reframes it around power generation. China has more than 2x US electricity generating capacity; renewable capacity alone equals total US generation; they add a UK-sized amount every year. This isn't an accident — it's the result of a strategic decision that cheap abundant electricity is central to all 21st century growth industries.

The argument: when AI becomes economically productive at scale, the question stops being 'whose model is best' and becomes 'how many instances can you deploy?' That's a function of power. China's leadership in ultra-high-voltage distribution, their willingness to build whatever fuel source works, and their renewable self-sufficiency (you don't have to import sun or wind) creates a compounding moat for anything electricity-intensive: AI, manufacturing, crypto, data centers.

The counter-case Kroeber raises: Chinese compute is fragmented across Huawei, ByteDance, Alibaba, Xiaomi — the government cannot force consolidation of scarce chips into one training run. If chip constraints bind harder than power, fragmentation becomes the bottleneck. But if SMIC closes the chip gap in 5-10 years (likely), electricity becomes decisive. Application: when forecasting AI infrastructure competition, price in power-generation trajectories — they're harder to move than chip fabs and harder for trade controls to block.
