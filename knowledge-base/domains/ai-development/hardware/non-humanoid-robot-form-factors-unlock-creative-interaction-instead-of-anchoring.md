Marafioti argues the industry's fixation on humanoid form is a mistake: a spider-like body could move faster and more stably, yet builders force humanoid shapes so people instantly assume 'robot = human, I know what it does.' That assumption is exactly the trap. Reachy Mini is deliberately cute and non-human because the moment a robot stops looking like a person, users start inventing genuinely new interaction patterns instead of expecting a human stand-in.

The practical payoff showed up in the field: owners 3D-printed Halloween pumpkin shells, and someone discovered they could pet the robot and make it purr — a behavior the team never designed. Form factor is not cosmetic; it sets the prior for what users believe is possible. When you want emergent, communal exploration rather than a fixed product use case, deliberately break the human-mimicry frame.
