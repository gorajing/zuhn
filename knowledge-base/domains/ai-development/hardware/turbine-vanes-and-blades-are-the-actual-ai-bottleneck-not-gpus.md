Most AI commentary treats GPUs as the scarce resource. Musk argues the actual binding constraint is several layers deeper. You need power plants to run data centers, and power plants need gas turbines, and gas turbines need cast vanes and blades — and only three companies worldwide make them, with backlogs extending to 2030. Every other component can be sourced 12-18 months ahead of the vanes and blades.

This is a useful lens for bottleneck analysis more broadly: the rate-limiting step is often 3-4 abstraction layers below where everyone is looking. Chip production is currently outpacing the ability to turn chips on, which means the marginal dollar on AI should go to specialized metallurgy, not more fabs. SpaceX and Tesla are being forced to vertically integrate into turbine blade casting because the existing suppliers physically cannot expand fast enough.

For anyone allocating capital or attention in AI infrastructure, the lesson is to trace the supply chain all the way down until you find the component that is truly inelastic. It is usually something unglamorous made by a handful of specialized manufacturers.
