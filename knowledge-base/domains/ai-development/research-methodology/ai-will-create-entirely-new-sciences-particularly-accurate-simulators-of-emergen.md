Hassabis's prediction: AI will unlock new sciences by enabling accurate simulators of emergent systems. Today, economics and social sciences struggle with rigor because you can't run controlled experiments — raise interest rates by 0.5% and there's no way to do it 1,000 times to find the average effect. Biology has the same problem (massive emergent dynamical systems). But machine-learned simulators trained on real-world data could enable thousands of synthetic runs, sampled rigorously.

DeepMind's WeatherNext is already the most accurate weather simulator in the world, faster than what meteorologists use. The same methodology applied to a virtual cell, a biology simulator, an economic simulator could transform those fields. Hassabis goes further: once you have the simulator, you might be able to extract explicit equations from it — making 'machine learning is the perfect description language for biology in the same way maths is for physics.'

The corollary: AI doesn't just speed up existing science, it creates a new layer of scientific tooling that is its own science. There will be a science of AI-system analysis (DeepMind already has this group). For Zuhn editorially this is a useful expansionary frame for AI-for-science coverage — the bigger story isn't 'AI helps biology' but 'AI creates a new method for studying emergent systems generally,' which has implications across economics, climate, social science, and any field where you can't run controlled experiments.
