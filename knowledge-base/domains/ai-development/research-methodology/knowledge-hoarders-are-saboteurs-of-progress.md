Palmer provocatively argues that Leonardo da Vinci was not a scientist. Her definition: a scientist publishes results so a community of other scientists can test and build on them. Leonardo wrote everything in coded mirror writing precisely so no one, not even his own students, could reproduce his work — he wanted future generations to gaze at his paintings and ask 'how did he do that?' the way he gazed at the Colosseum. Brunelleschi, who built Florence's famous dome, deliberately burned all his notes and schematics so no one could replicate the method. In a community-of-science frame, these people are not heroic inventors but active saboteurs who made progress and then cut it off so that no one else could be their peer.

The contrast is Francis Bacon's three-insect model: the ant (encyclopedist who just piles up knowledge), the spider (system-weaver who spins theories from their own body), and the honeybee (who gathers from nature and processes it into something sweet and useful for humankind). Bacon said being a scientist is the ultimate act of charity because you give a gift to every human who will ever live after you. This frames the open-source vs. closed-model debate in AI sharply: labs that refuse to publish capabilities research are making a Leonardo move, optimizing for 'how did they do it?' mystique at the cost of civilizational progress. The Leonardo mode is not morally neutral — it is actively anti-progress compared to the honeybee mode. A useful gut check for any researcher: am I being an ant, a spider, a honeybee, or a saboteur?
