Nadella makes a passing but striking observation: the first utility-scale quantum computer's highest-value workload may be designing its successor. The argument is that atom-by-atom simulation of new quantum gate designs is precisely the kind of data-light, state-space-heavy problem that classical HPC struggles with, and that quantum is theoretically well-suited to.

This is a recursive self-improvement loop structurally similar to the one discussed in AI — each generation of the tool accelerates the design of the next. Unlike AI's version of this loop, the quantum version is gated by physical fabrication cycles, so it will be slower, but the compounding dynamic is real.

For forecasting timelines, this suggests that once the first fault-tolerant quantum computer exists, progress on subsequent generations may accelerate nonlinearly — not because of Moore's Law style scaling, but because the tool itself becomes usable to design its successors.
