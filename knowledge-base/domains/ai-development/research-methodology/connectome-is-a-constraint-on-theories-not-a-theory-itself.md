A common critique of connectomics is that even with every synapse mapped, you still won't 'see' intelligence — the weights of a trained neural net are already interpretable in principle and we still can't extract clean algorithms from them. Marblestone's response reframes the goal: we shouldn't expect the connectome to directly decode the brain's algorithm. Instead it functions as a massive set of constraints that narrows which learning-rule / architecture / cost-function combinations are consistent with what we see.

This parallels how the Human Genome Project's value wasn't one genome — it was the technology curve that came from structuring a competitive program around cost reduction (from $3B per genome to hundreds of dollars). E11 Bio's shift from electron to optical microscopy aims to do the same for connectomics: drive a mouse brain from $3B to tens of millions, making many brains comparable and turning neuroscience into a ground-truth science.

The strategic lesson for any moonshot infrastructure play: structure funding to drive the cost curve of the measurement itself before attempting the grand experiment — the payoff comes from comparability across many samples, not from the first heroic datapoint.
