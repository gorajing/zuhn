The methodological lesson from 50 years of Bell-inequality testing: (1) Clauser 1972 (Lawrence Berkeley) ran the first real test, found Bell's inequality violated. Loophole: maybe the two detectors were physically influencing each other through unmeasured channels. (2) Aspect 1980s closed this loophole by switching measurement settings AFTER the entangled photons had already left the source, in billionths of a second — too fast for any pre-arranged signal to travel between detectors. Bell's inequality violated again. New loophole: maybe a deep cosmic conspiracy somehow correlated the initial preparation with the later 'random' settings. (3) Zeilinger 2017 closed this loophole by using light emitted by distant stars (hundreds of light-years away) to set measurement parameters randomly — meaning any conspiracy would need to have been set in motion centuries before anyone designed the experiment, before Einstein was born. Bell's inequality violated yet again. Each experimental refinement closed a specific alternative explanation. After 50 years, no plausible alternative remains. This is the gold-standard methodology for ruling out alternatives: identify each possible loophole, design an experiment specifically to close it, repeat until none remain. The same pattern applies broadly: economic studies (initial correlation → quasi-experimental → natural experiment → randomized field trial); medical research (observational → controlled → blinded → meta-analysis); AI safety (single capability test → adversarial robustness → diverse benchmark suite → real-world deployment monitoring). When a claim is contested, weaker evidence is dismissable. The discipline of iteratively closing loopholes is what produces conclusions that survive 'but couldn't it be X?' challenges. For AI builders evaluating model claims, the diagnostic question is 'what loopholes would you systematically close?' — if the team can't answer, the claim is fragile.
