The grokking discovery is emblematic of a broader pattern in AI research: major breakthroughs come from running experiments longer, bigger, or differently than theory would suggest, and observing unexpected results. Penicillin-style serendipity—where a mistake or an overlooked anomaly becomes the finding—recurs throughout deep learning history (dropout, batch normalization, attention mechanisms all emerged from empirical surprise).

This has practical implications for how AI labs should allocate research resources. A purely theory-driven program would have stopped training at the overfitting point. The field advances disproportionately from groups willing to run experiments that seem theoretically wasteful or wrong.

Having a mental model still matters—it helps researchers recognize when an anomalous result is signal rather than noise—but the mental model should be held loosely enough to allow updating when observations conflict with it. This is a methodological stance about how to do AI research, not just a historical observation.
