Sutskever's account of his own research taste is unusually concrete. He looks for beauty, simplicity, elegance, and 'correct inspiration from the brain' — but notes that thinking about how the brain works is 'very easy to do incorrectly.' The correct way involves picking out features that feel fundamental (neurons matter because there are many of them; folds probably don't) and features that ought to be true (local learning rules, distributed representation, learning from experience).

The key insight is functional, not aesthetic. Data is noisy — sometimes you're doing the right thing and there's a bug, and you can't tell from the data whether to keep debugging or pivot. What sustains you is the top-down belief: 'something like this has to work, therefore we've got to keep going.' Without that, every failed experiment looks like evidence you're on the wrong path. With it, you can push through weeks or months of apparent failure to find the real issue.

This is practical advice for anyone doing ML research, but it generalizes. Any domain where the feedback signal is noisy (startups, investing, scientific research) rewards practitioners who hold a top-down model strong enough to discount short-term contradictory evidence — but only if that top-down model is grounded in multiple independent lines of beauty/correctness, not in stubbornness. The multifaceted requirement is what separates taste from dogma.
