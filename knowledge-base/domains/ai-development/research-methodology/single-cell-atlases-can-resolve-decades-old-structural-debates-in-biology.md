The Karten vs Puelles debate about whether avian DVR and mammalian neocortex share common ancestry ran from the 1960s until 2025. Anatomy alone couldn't resolve it because similar structures can arise from different developmental origins. The decisive move was building a molecular cell-type atlas of the avian pallium and comparing it to a matched mammalian atlas, which revealed that the cell types arose from different developmental zones despite functional convergence.

The methodological lesson: when a biological debate has been stuck for decades, the path forward is almost always a single-cell atlas of the relevant tissue across the species in question. Cell-type identity — defined by transcriptomic profiles — is the molecular 'ground truth' that anatomy and behavior can only suggest indirectly. Stuck debates about homology, evolution, and developmental origins are increasingly resolvable by applying single-cell methods systematically.

For researchers: identify the oldest unresolved debates in your field and ask whether they're limited by the absence of a cell atlas. That's often where the next big paper is hiding.
