Nielsen pushes back on the 'theory of everything' picture where physics gets 'finished' and then you just do engineering. Computer science has been exploring Turing's 1936 theory for 90 years and keeps uncovering fundamentally new ideas (public-key cryptography, blockchain consensus). Phases of matter went from 'three' to dozens (superconductors, Bose-Einstein condensates, fractional quantum Hall systems) and we're still discovering and designing more. These aren't refinements; they're new primitives.

The dessert-buffet intuition fails because the table gets restocked: each new field opens, twenty-one-year-olds flood in and make major breakthroughs, low-hanging fruit reappears. Combine this with contingency — humans are visual, so our math uses spatial geometry; an aurally-dominated species might develop different primitives. Biology is an 'alien civilization' we've been gifted: hundreds of millions of proteins, each encoding ideas we don't understand, four billion years of accumulated design.

The most interesting implication is strategic: if civilizations end up in genuinely different parts of the tech tree, there will be massive permanent gains from trade between any two, making friendliness far more rewarding than domination. Applied to more mundane settings — teams, companies, research programs — it suggests that diversity of exploration paths isn't inefficiency; it's the main source of long-run compounding value.
