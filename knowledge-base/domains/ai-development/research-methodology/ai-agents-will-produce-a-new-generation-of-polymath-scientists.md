Harrer's speculative claim: with AI agents as helpers, scientists can apply scientific methods and engineering techniques across multiple domains. The traditional barrier — that mastering each field takes 5-10 years of specialized training — collapses when AI handles the methodological depth. Three speculative new categories he names: astrobio chemist (astrobiology + biochemistry), paleoecological geneticist (paleontology + ecology + genetics), quantum bioinformatician (quantum computing + biology + informatics). Whether these specific categories emerge or not, the meta-claim is that AI lets individual scientists span more domains than they previously could.

Application: when planning scientific careers in 2026 and beyond, the optimal strategy may be 'breadth in 2-3 domains + AI fluency' rather than 'depth in 1 domain.' For talent acquisition: look for cross-domain candidates whose AI fluency means they can credibly contribute across more verticals than their PhD title would suggest. For Memric specifically: same logic for the team — a generalist with AI fluency may serve audit-delivery work better than a specialist who can only operate in one domain.
