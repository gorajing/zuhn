Krause describes a chart overlaying where human scientists have explored the high-entropy alloy space (from published literature) against where Radical's AI scientist has gone, and the AI has moved into elemental families nobody had ever published on. When they ask their PhD metallurgists why they never tried those combinations, the answers are all priors: 'I didn't think it would cast,' 'I thought it would evaporate,' 'I didn't think it would give the microstructure I wanted.' These are intuitions that were never tested, an 'unknowing bias' that quietly pruned the search space. The AI, lacking those priors and unafraid to 'take a shot on goal,' tests them and sometimes they work.

The second mechanism is parallelism over the cognitive process itself, not just experiment count. A human scientist works serially: read papers, form a hypothesis, synthesize, study characterization for two weeks, extract one or two learnings from 100 images, repeat. The AI reads 100,000 publications and directly compares them against 100,000 microscopy images simultaneously, drawing conclusions back to hypotheses and property data in real time. The interviewer probes whether this is 'just turning up the sampler temperature' (more random exploration), and the answer is partly yes but also structurally different: the value is the combination of removed priors plus parallel cross-referencing that no human can hold in working memory. The actionable lesson for AI-for-science design is to treat removing human priors and enabling parallel cross-modal reasoning as first-class sources of novelty, distinct from simply scaling experiment count.
