Doug Finkbeiner at the Universe Panel: students working with Claude/Cursor on dust-mapping and ML-architecture-search problems aren't producing more papers, they're producing better ones. One student paper has interactive figures of unusual richness because the student could explore visualization options at scale. Another student found a working ML architecture by 'just trying all the ideas Claude suggested' — six months ago that breadth of search wasn't accessible. The AI tools give breadth back to research where compute or time previously forced choosing one path. The output goes into quality, not volume.

Application: the predictive narrative that 'AI will flood the literature with low-quality papers' may be wrong, at least in disciplines with strong existing quality norms. The actual benefit accrues to depth and exploration. Hiring/funding metrics that focus on paper count may miss this — better metrics would assess paper quality, novelty, breadth of exploration.
