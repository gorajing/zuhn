Hassabis's speculative-but-grounded claim: physicists in the 1920s treated energy and matter as primary (E=mc²); but information may be even more fundamental. The organization of matter, structure, and especially biological systems resisting entropy can be understood as information processing systems at their heart. Energy, matter, and information may be three quantities convertible into each other, with information being the most fundamental.

If true, the implication is profound: AI is not just another technology revolution like electricity or steam. It's a technology that operates on the substrate of reality itself. That reframes how to think about AI's long-term importance — not 'how big is the market for AI applications' but 'how does engaging with the fundamental substrate change civilization.' This is speculative philosophy, not testable claim, but it's the kind of frame that influences capital allocation, research priorities, and political response over decades.

For Zuhn editorially: include this as a speculative framing in long-form pieces, but don't lean on it for empirical claims. It's a useful explanation for why people building AGI feel they're doing something different than building software — but it's not yet load-bearing for any specific decision.
