In the mid-2010s, Google had an almost unbelievable concentration of AI talent: the authors of AlexNet, the future founders of OpenAI and Anthropic, the DeepMind team, Noam Shazeer (transformer co-author), Andrew Ng, Sebastian Thrun, and Jeff Hinton himself. An engineer joked the analogy was IBM hiring every single person who knew how to code in the early computer era. Yet the paper that catalyzed modern AI (Attention Is All You Need) was published by Google in 2017 and the company that commercialized it into the fastest-growing consumer product in history was OpenAI.

The explanation is not that Google lost its talent first — most of the diffusion happened after OpenAI's existence proved there was a commercialization path Google itself wouldn't take. Talented researchers leave when they see the work they want to ship being blocked, not when they're merely recruited. The lesson for AI labs and incumbents: hoarding talent works only if you also create product velocity for their work. When publication is permitted but productization is not, the researchers who produced the breakthrough will leave to ship it themselves, and the talent pool becomes a training ground for future competitors. Research capacity without product willingness is a slow-motion staff-leasing operation for your disruptors.
