James Evans's design principle: same transformer-based models that compress knowledge into deductive predictions can be inverted into surprise-generators. Conditional on this arbitrary function or unexpected observation, what other improbable claims would also be probable? Take these off-manifold predictions as research priorities. Empirical finding: papers that immediately follow surprising-conditional generations are more likely to be award-winning. The mechanism: the conditional generation surfaces non-obvious connections that researchers would have missed, but that are objectively likely to be important once examined. This inverts AI's homogenizing tendency by using its compression as a tool for finding gaps rather than for predicting the center.

Application: any AI-driven research prioritization should include surprise-conditional generation as a routine step. The pattern: train compression model, use it to identify off-manifold claims, prioritize those for research investment. Memric application: the same logic applies to firm-level decision audits. The compression model that captures the firm's principles can be inverted to surface 'conditional on this current proposal, what historical retired principle from your archive should make you skeptical?' — that's surprise-conditional research prioritization at the firm level.
