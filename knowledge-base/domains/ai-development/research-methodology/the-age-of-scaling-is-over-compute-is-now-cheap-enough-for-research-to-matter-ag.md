Sutskever's framing: pre-training was a recipe that let companies invest compute with low risk ('go forth and pre-train'), but data is finite and the belief that 100x more scale would transform anything is no longer credible. Compute is now large enough that proving a new idea doesn't require the largest cluster — AlexNet ran on 2 GPUs, the original transformer on at most 64 GPUs of 2017 (roughly 2 modern GPUs), and o1 reasoning was not the most compute-heavy thing in the world.

The practical implication is that the bottleneck has shifted from compute to ideas. Sutskever notes there are now 'more companies than ideas by quite a bit' and that the scaling mindset 'sucked out all the air in the room' causing everyone to converge on the same approach. A well-funded research lab with $3B can compete with labs spending $5-6B/year on experiments because most of that spend goes to inference, product engineering, and fragmented workstreams rather than to research that moves the frontier.

For investors, operators, and researchers this reframes the landscape: the next breakthrough will look less like 'the company with the biggest cluster won' and more like the 2012-2020 era of small teams finding conceptual advances. Bet on research taste, not on raw capital access.
