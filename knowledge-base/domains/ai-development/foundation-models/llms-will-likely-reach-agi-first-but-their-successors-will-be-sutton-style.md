Patel's synthesis: the current LLM paradigm, despite its sample inefficiency and dependence on finite human data, will cross the AGI line first because it has the accumulated compute, tooling, and incremental improvements. But Sutton's first-principles critique is pointing at real gaps — abysmal sample efficiency, no continual learning, dependence on exhaustible human data — that practitioners underweight because those gaps are pervasive in the current paradigm.

The prediction: once AGIs exist, the successor systems they design will almost certainly look more like Sutton's OaK architecture than like a scaled-up transformer. The first generation gets there through compute and human data; the second generation is designed by the first to transcend both.

This is a useful framing for how to read bitter-lesson critiques today: they may be wrong about which paradigm gets to AGI first, while being right about which paradigm will dominate after AGI.
