Dwarkesh's bearishness on near-term transformation flips into strong bullishness on the decade scale. The key asymmetry: humans each learn in isolation and can't merge experience. AI copies can. Once continual learning is real, a single model deployed across millions of jobs pools the tacit knowledge of every deployment — one AI learning every job simultaneously.

This creates a discontinuity: the value of models jumps sharply the moment the learning loop closes, not gradually. And it doesn't require a software-only recursive self-improvement singularity. The deployment-driven version — models learning while doing different jobs and sharing the results — can produce something that looks functionally like an intelligence explosion from the outside. Dwarkesh forecasts this arriving around 2032.

Practical implication: expect a broken early version (test-time training, partial online learning) to show up before the full thing. Labs release incremental innovations quickly, so there will be warning shots — he doesn't expect the solution to appear in a single OpenAI livestream.
