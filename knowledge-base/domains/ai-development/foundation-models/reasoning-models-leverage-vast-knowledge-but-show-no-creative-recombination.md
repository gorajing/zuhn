Ege Erdil observes that reasoning models look impressive to humans because they can retrieve an obscure 1850 theorem on demand—humans solving the same problem must work creatively from less knowledge. But when you invert the lens: given how much these models know across every field, you'd expect constant 'oh, this structure in one domain matches that structure in another' moments. There aren't any. Not even small, Sunday-magazine-tier novel recombinations.

This suggests the way models approach problems is fundamentally different from human creativity: they effectively leverage knowledge they already have rather than generating new conceptual schemes. A human mathematician producing orders of magnitude fewer tokens than a reasoning model over six months would still be expected to surface a novel idea or two.

Why it matters: any forecasting story that assumes reasoning models are 'almost creative researchers' has weak empirical backing. Real scientific R&D requires generating novel conceptual schemes in messy, long-horizon, fuzzy-objective contexts—precisely what these models haven't demonstrated. This should make you discount claims that automated AI R&D is just around the corner.
