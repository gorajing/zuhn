Bailey frames Gemini's distinctiveness not as accepting video/audio/image/PDF inputs — which she notes most models now do — but as being multimodal on outputs: it can emit text, code, audio, and images, where 'most of the other models on the market are only capable of handling text and code as outputs.' Input multimodality has become table stakes; output multimodality remains scarce and architecturally hard.

The practical consequence is that when you evaluate models for a product, the discriminating question is increasingly 'what can it natively produce?' rather than 'what can it ingest?' A native real-time voice or image-out model collapses pipelines that input-only models force you to assemble downstream. This reframes model selection and competitive analysis: parity on inputs is assumed, and the moat lives on the output side until that too commoditizes.
