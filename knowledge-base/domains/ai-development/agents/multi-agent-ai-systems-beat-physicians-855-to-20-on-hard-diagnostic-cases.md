The AI Index 2026 Chapter 6 reports a dramatic result: Microsoft's AI Diagnostic Orchestrator, paired with OpenAI o3, was tested on challenging cases drawn from the medical literature against physicians working without their usual tools and scored 85.5% versus 20% for the unaided physicians. Multi-agent frameworks more broadly have shown diagnostic accuracy gains of 7% to over 60% over single-agent baselines. The caveat is significant: 'unaided physicians' is the strong comparison (physicians work with reference tools in practice), and 'complex published case studies' is not the median clinical encounter. But the direction is clear — the unit of medical AI is shifting from single-model Q&A to orchestrated multi-agent workflows, and that shift is where the capability gains live. For health systems, this changes the deployment question from 'which single model do we license?' to 'which orchestration framework and tool-chain do we deploy?' — a software architecture problem, not a model selection problem. Confidence is medium because the test condition is favorable to the AI system.
