Coco surfaced a specific ROI-calculation error that's costing AI startups deals: enterprise buyers compare AI tool pricing to the legacy software the AI shares a workflow with, rather than to the labor the AI replaces. CAD seat license = $3K/yr; an AI that does the drafting itself = ?. The buyer benchmarks the AI against the seat and calls it overpriced; the right benchmark is the drafter's $80K+/yr salary plus overhead.

The reframe matters because of capacity multipliers: an AI-driven drafter can handle 20 projects in parallel where a manual drafter handles 1. So the ROI question isn't 'is this cheaper than CAD' but 'what does my team size look like at 20x output per FTE?' That changes the buyer from 'no budget' to 'rapid expansion.'

For AI sellers: the diagnostic question to ask enterprise prospects is which budget line item they're comparing your tool against. If they're comparing to software, you've already lost the framing. Force the conversation back to labor by asking 'how many hours per week does your team currently spend on this task?'
