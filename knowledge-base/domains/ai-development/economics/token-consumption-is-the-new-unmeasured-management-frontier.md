The keynote names a genuine management vacuum: organizations have adopted coding agents faster than they've developed any practice for governing token consumption. With ~1500 engineers, the speaker admits he can't measure performance, efficiency gain, or what 'normal' token usage even is. Decades of software-engineering management practice (story points, velocity, code review norms) were built for a human-paced world and don't transfer.

The concrete anchors: an intern reportedly used 61M tokens on day one — at market rates, more than the company paid him that day. A widely-cited heuristic (attributed to Jensen-style framing) says a generously-paid $500K Silicon Valley engineer should consume ~$250K in tokens; when first heard it sounds absurd, but the speaker's cross-company comparison found it 'roughly right.' Windsurf-adjacent data: engineers consuming ~$20K/month in tokens (~$250K/yr).

The speaker's management move is a forcing function, not a cap: he asks engineers to justify in writing why they use a given budget (e.g. why not use a provided CLI license) — not to scrutinize, but to make them think. He'll approve whatever they write; the value is the deliberate reasoning.

The actionable claim: this is genuinely early, and whoever cracks token-productivity measurement gains a real edge. For any org running coding agents at scale, build the measurement/attribution layer now (tokens-per-shipped-feature, token-ROI vs salary) rather than waiting for an industry standard. Note the productive tension with the panel source's 'companies measure ship speed, not tokens' — both are true at different altitudes: leadership doesn't police token cost per-head, but it does need an org-level practice to understand token economics, and that practice doesn't yet exist.
