Ubl frames the current moment as a live experiment in the price elasticity of the software market. There has always been a large body of software that 'should exist' but was never written because hardcoding all the business logic and edge cases made it uneconomical. Agents collapse that cost, so the unwritten portion of the Venn diagram of all-possible-software becomes viable to build. The thesis: the cheaper software gets, the more of it we make, and so far there are no signs of hitting the saturating S-curve.

The second-order effect is on labor. As more companies answer the build-vs-buy question with 'build' (the so-called SaaS 'copocalypse'), they generate more software work, not less. Even though each unit of software is faster to produce, the volume expansion dominates, so net demand for engineers rises. The actionable implication for engineers and founders is to treat agent-driven cost reduction as market expansion, not headcount elimination — and to position for the long tail of previously-uneconomical software that is now buildable.
