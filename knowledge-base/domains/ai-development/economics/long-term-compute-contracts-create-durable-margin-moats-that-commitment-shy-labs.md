Patel frames this as an Alchian-Allen-adjacent dynamic. The labs face a coordination-free prisoner's dilemma: committing long and early to compute looks reckless at the moment of signing, but the incremental chip coming online this quarter is priced to the value of frontier tokens, not to historical build cost. Anyone who locked in a five-year contract at 2022-2023 prices is transacting compute far below its current marginal value, while anyone scrambling now pays full markup.

Anthropic's 'principled' underbooking forced them to source late-stage capacity through Bedrock, Vertex, and Foundry revenue-share arrangements — effectively handing 50% of the margin to Amazon, Google, and Microsoft. OpenAI's apparent recklessness ('let's sign these crazy fucking deals' — when markets thought they couldn't pay) turned into a structural advantage once they raised $110B and validated the commitments. For four months during H2 2025, cloud vendors refused to sign with OpenAI; now they're begging. Dario's stated rationale (revenue inflection uncertainty) is logically inconsistent with his own claim that a data center of geniuses is 2-5 years away earning trillions.

The generalizable lesson: in markets where supply has a multi-year lag and the value of the output is rising exponentially, the correct move is to over-commit early even if it looks financially irresponsible, because waiting-and-seeing is priced by the market at a steep discount. 'Commitment issues' become a lasting margin handicap, not prudence.
