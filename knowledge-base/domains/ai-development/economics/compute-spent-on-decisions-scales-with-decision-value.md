Today, executive compensation is capped by what a single human brain can process and what talent markets will pay. Dwarkesh flips this: once role occupants are AI, the cost of a role is simply the compute it consumes, and the value comes from running massive simulations against every decision. A CEO considering an acquisition could run Monte Carlo simulations of five-year market dynamics, evaluate thousands of alternative strategies, and deeply analyze every technical system involved.

If a single strategic insight is worth billions, spending billions of compute to generate it is a rational trade. This inverts how we think about scarce abilities: future firms won't be constrained by what's rare in human skill distributions — they can optimize for whatever abilities matter most, spinning up thousands of world-class researchers or engineers on demand.

For investors and operators, this suggests inference compute becomes the new executive headcount — the bottleneck for strategic capacity — and compute providers capture economics previously flowing to rare human talent.
