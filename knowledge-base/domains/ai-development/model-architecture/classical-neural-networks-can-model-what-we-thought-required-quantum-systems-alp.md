Hassabis's structural observation: protein folding involves water-bond quantum effects, and one might naively think you'd need quantum computers to model it. But AlphaFold approaches optimal accuracy on a classical Turing machine (modern GPUs running neural networks). This may generalize: a lot of problems we think 'require' quantum systems may actually be modelable on classical computers if framed correctly with the right neural network.

The philosophical extension: Hassabis sees neural networks as approximate Turing machines and our brains as the same. Quantum computing remains valuable for specific problems (factoring, certain optimization), but the 'we need quantum computing to do AI' or 'we need quantum biology to model biology' arguments may be wrong. This has practical implications for capital allocation — billions are being invested in quantum computing partly on the assumption that classical AI will hit walls; if AlphaFold-style results generalize, the wall doesn't appear.

For Zuhn editorially: this is the most surprising under-discussed implication of AlphaFold. The first-order story is 'AI cures diseases' but the second-order story is 'classical computation may suffice for problems we thought needed exotic substrates.' Worth tracking as a structural input to long-term AI vs quantum capital allocation debates.
