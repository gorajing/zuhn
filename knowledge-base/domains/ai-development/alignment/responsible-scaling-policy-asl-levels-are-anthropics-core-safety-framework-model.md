Dario explains the ASL framework: ASL-2 (current level) means powerful models whose risks are comparable to other technologies. ASL-3 (which Anthropic's latest models are approaching) means models that could allow an unskilled person to do things requiring PhD-level expertise in dangerous domains like bioweapons — expanding the pool of potentially dangerous actors from tens of thousands to tens of millions.

At ASL-3, Anthropic must put in mitigations (preventing the model from assisting with narrow dangerous tasks) and security restrictions (preventing model theft). Dario emphasizes this is commercially viable: 'We believe we have a story for how to deploy those kinds of models safely by removing their ability to do this very narrow range of dangerous tasks without compromising their commercial viability.'

Critically, within months of Anthropic publishing their RSP, all major competitors followed suit. This pattern — Anthropic leads, industry follows — is a recurring theme in Dario's narrative and a powerful enterprise sales argument: buyers choosing Anthropic are choosing the company that sets the safety standard rather than following it.
