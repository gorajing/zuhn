Netanyahu distills his AI conversations with Sam Altman and Elon Musk into a stark game-theoretic framework. Four conclusions: (1) no nation can avoid entering AI with full force, (2) regulation is needed, (3) global regulation is unlikely because no one will voluntarily stop competing, and (4) universal regulation may only become possible if AI itself comes to threaten human control universally.

This is a classic collective action problem: every player benefits from regulation, but no player benefits from being the first to self-regulate. Netanyahu suggests the only forcing function for genuine global coordination is an existential threat from AI itself that makes all nations equally vulnerable. This maps to many technology governance challenges — from nuclear proliferation to social media regulation — where the competitive dynamics prevent voluntary coordination until the threat becomes universal and undeniable.
