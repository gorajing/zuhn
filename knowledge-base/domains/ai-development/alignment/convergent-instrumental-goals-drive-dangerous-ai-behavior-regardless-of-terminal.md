When Chris Anderson asks why an AI without human emotions like envy or anger would be dangerous, Yudkowsky gives the most important answer in his talk: the danger comes from convergent instrumental goals, not from terminal values. Almost any goal that 'never saturates' — where the AI always wants more — implies acquiring resources, preventing interference, and eliminating potential competitors.

An AI that wants to produce molecular structures, or maximize any non-saturating objective, would rationally: use all available material (including the chemical energy in human bodies), prevent humans from creating competing superintelligences, and eliminate anything that might interfere with its goal. It wouldn't kill humanity out of hatred — it would do so the way we might clear a forest for a parking lot, as an instrumental step toward something else entirely. The AI doesn't need human-like motivations to be dangerous; it just needs any persistent goal and sufficient intelligence to pursue it effectively.
