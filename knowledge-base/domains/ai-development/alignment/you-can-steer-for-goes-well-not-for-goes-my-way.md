Petrarch launched the classical revival intending to produce better Christian rulers who would govern like Brutus and Cicero. What actually emerged over 200 years was Hedonist and Epicurean philosophy, Machiavelli's political science, Bacon's scientific method, Voltaire, democracy, germ theory, and eventually a cure for the Black Death he himself survived. If you showed him that future, Palmer says, he would be horrified by its values (especially democracy) but would weep for joy at the curing of plague. He made a world that did not go his way but went well.

Palmer draws the explicit AI analogy: 'a lot of people have this idea that they're going to make AI go well by doing X thing,' but historically nobody has a track record of steering huge unanticipated changes 'my way, or according to my values.' The useful distinction is between 'go my way' and 'go well.' This is a strong argument against AI governance frameworks that try to lock in a specific value set and a strong argument for frameworks that try to preserve humanity's ability to recover, course-correct, and eventually cure problems we can't yet name. Also implies that people who claim to know exactly what the post-AI world should look like are making a Petrarchan mistake.
