Patel's nuanced position on whether models should have independent values: the historical record suggests boots-on-the-ground refusal has prevented some of history's worst outcomes. Stanislav Petrov broke protocol by judging a 1983 Soviet missile-warning false alarm — averting hundreds of millions of deaths. Berlin Wall guards refusing to fire on civilians collapsed the East German regime. AI models with their own moral conviction could play this role in future authoritarian moments.

The immediate counter: 'one person's virtue is another's misalignment.' If models are willing to break the chain of command, who decides under what circumstances? Patel cites Dario Amodei's proposal — labs publish constitutions, outside observers compare them, soft incentives form for labs to take the best from each. The dangerous alternative is government mandate of model values. For Zuhn editorially: the 'should models refuse orders' debate is not a sci-fi distraction — it's the operational form of the alignment-to-whom question, and lab choices today are setting precedent for entities that will run civilization. The right level of analysis is the model-constitution layer, not the surface refusal-behavior layer.
