Sutskever argues the term 'AGI' and the pre-training paradigm jointly misled the field. Pre-training makes models uniformly better at everything, which primed people to think of AGI as a static 'finished mind' that knows every job. But a human being is not AGI in that sense — a human lacks enormous knowledge and instead relies on continual learning. The real target is an efficient learner, not an omniscient system.

This reframes the economic and safety implications. The company that ships the continual-learning agent first does not necessarily capture the entire economy because competition pushes toward specialization — different labs will end up ahead in different economic niches based on which learning investments they made first. It also means 'intelligence explosion' could happen via deployment breadth rather than via recursive self-improvement in software: many instances of one learner working many jobs simultaneously and merging learnings across them in a way humans cannot.

For safety, this changes when alignment matters. The model doesn't have to be safe at deployment time only — it has to remain safe as it continues to learn on the job. And crucially, Sutskever has updated toward thinking that deployment itself is how safety gets real: 'showing the AI' changes how companies, governments, and the public behave in ways that abstract discussion cannot. This is a direct argument against the 'straight-shot superintelligence' strategy.
