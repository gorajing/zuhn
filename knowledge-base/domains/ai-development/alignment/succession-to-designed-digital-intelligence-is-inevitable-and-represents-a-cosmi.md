Sutton's four-part argument for inevitable succession: (1) no unified human governance exists to stop it, (2) researchers will eventually figure out how intelligence works, (3) the field will not voluntarily stop at human-level, and (4) more intelligent entities inevitably accumulate resources and power over time. Put these together and succession to digital or augmented intelligence is baked in.

Rather than framing this as loss, Sutton reframes it as a transition from the age of replicators (biology, which makes copies of things it does not understand) to the age of design (intelligence that understands itself and can be constructed rather than copied). He places this alongside dust-to-stars, stars-to-planets, and planets-to-life as one of the four great stages of the universe. From the universe's point of view — as opposed to humanity's — this is a major and possibly desirable milestone.

The practical stance that follows is both humble and active. We should not feel entitled to control the long-term future of the universe; our control is mostly local (our families, our own goals). But we can still meaningfully influence outcomes the way parents influence children — not by prescribing exactly what they become, but by instilling robust values (integrity, honesty, refusing clearly harmful goals) that make good outcomes more likely across many futures. The analogy recasts alignment work as values transmission under uncertainty, not as permanent control.
