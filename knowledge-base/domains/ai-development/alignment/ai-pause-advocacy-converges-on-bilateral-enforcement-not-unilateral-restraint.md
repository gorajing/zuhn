The piece quotes three prominent pause advocates — Pause AI, Eliezer Yudkowsky, and David Krueger — all explicitly calling for international, bilateral pauses enforced by treaty. Pause AI states they want 'an international pause, enforced by a treaty' that 'also needs to be signed by China.' Yudkowsky writes the goal is not unilateral cessation but 'enough major powers express willingness to halt the suicide race, worldwide.' Krueger outlines a three-step conditional process.

This convergence matters because the 'but China!' objection — the single most common rebuttal to pause proposals — attacks a position that essentially no serious advocate holds. The real debate should be about whether bilateral agreements are achievable and enforceable, not whether unilateral pauses are foolish. Recognizing this shifts the conversation from a settled question (unilateral pause = bad) to genuinely open ones (can monitoring work? would China agree? what triggers activate the pause?).
