Patel's reframe: an army of extremely obedient AI employees is what successful alignment looks like at the technical level. The reason it sounds dystopian is the unanswered question — to whom are these AIs aligned? In what situation should they defer to the model company vs. the end user vs. the law vs. their own sense of morality? Model companies have no incentive to make this question prominent because they have effective control over the preferences of the entire future labor force.

The operational implication: any 'AI safety' or 'responsible AI' framework that doesn't explicitly answer 'aligned to whom under which conditions' is dodging the central question. The Anthropic-Pentagon dispute is an early preview of these negotiations — a private company asserting the right to refuse government use cases, government asserting that's a supply chain risk. By 2030+, when AIs are doing 99% of military and civilian government work, these negotiations are the most consequential in history. For builders: the stack of stakeholders (lab → integrator → enterprise → user → regulator) needs explicit precedence rules baked into the model spec, not handled ad-hoc per dispute.
