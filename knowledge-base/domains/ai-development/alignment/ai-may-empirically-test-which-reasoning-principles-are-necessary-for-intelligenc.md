Graham notes that AI may serve as a form of 'alien life' that lets us determine precisely which truths are necessary for intelligence. If we find it impossible to build a system we'd recognize as intelligent without it using Occam's razor — preferring simpler explanations — that would constitute evidence that parsimony is a necessary feature of intelligence, not a human cultural preference. We might eventually prove it.

This reframes alignment and interpretability research as potentially bearing on deep philosophical questions. Understanding what any intelligence must believe isn't just useful for safety — it could settle longstanding questions about whether principles like induction, parsimony, and evidence-based updating are contingent or universal. The engineering problem and the philosophical problem may converge from very different directions.
