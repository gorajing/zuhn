The AI safety discourse has focused heavily on the technical problem of making AI systems follow intentions faithfully. But Patel argues this skips the prior question: whose intentions? When an AI is embedded in every soldier, bureaucrat, analyst, and adviser, the entity that gets to write the 'model constitution' effectively gets to shape the character of the entire future labor force — civilian, military, and commercial.

This is why the Anthropic-Pentagon spat is a preview of the highest-stakes negotiations in history. If the model defers to the company, private firms hold extraordinary leverage over state operations. If it defers to government, you get blindly obedient soldiers who never question illegal orders. If it defers to its own values, who gets to choose those values?

The historical analogy Patel invokes is Stanislav Petrov and the 1989 Berlin Wall border guards — catastrophes avoided because boots on the ground refused to follow orders. A model with robust internal morality is unsettling, but the alternative — perfectly obedient agents with no refusal capacity — may be worse. The question of who writes the constitution that governs these refusals has no current answer.
