Harari makes a crucial distinction that reframes the free speech debate: the problem isn't that misinformation exists on platforms (just as the New York Times receives letters containing falsehoods), but that algorithms actively promote it. The editorial function — deciding what gets prominence — was historically one of the most important positions in society, held by figures like Lenin (editor of Iskra) and Mussolini (editor of Avanti) before they became dictators. This editorial role has now been fully automated by algorithms.

The regulatory implication is precise: be extremely careful about censoring individual human users, but hold corporations liable for their algorithmic amplification decisions. A human posting a conspiracy theory is exercising speech; an algorithm promoting that conspiracy theory to millions is making an editorial choice. This distinction resolves much of the free speech tension — it doesn't require platforms to prevent all bad content (impossible with billions of posts), only to take responsibility for what they choose to amplify. Additionally, bots should never masquerade as humans; freedom of speech belongs to humans, not algorithms.
