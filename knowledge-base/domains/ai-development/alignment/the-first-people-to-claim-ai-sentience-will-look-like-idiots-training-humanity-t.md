Yudkowsky identifies a dangerous social dynamic: because AI systems are trained through imitation, their displays of emotion and self-awareness are almost certainly just pattern-matching on training data. The first people to claim sentience (like Blake Lemoine) are likely wrong, and 999 out of 1000 observers correctly identify it as fake. But this teaches humanity the lesson that all AI sentience claims are fake — precisely as systems keep getting smarter and the probability of genuine sentience increases.

This creates a catch-22: by the time there might be 'a tiny fragment of real caring' inside a model, we've been so thoroughly trained to dismiss such claims that we won't recognize it. The practical implication for AI developers: we need formal methods for detecting sentience that don't rely on behavioral signals contaminated by training data. Yudkowsky suggests training a GPT-3-sized model to detect and remove all consciousness-related discussion from training data, then retraining and seeing what the model says spontaneously. Even this wouldn't be definitive, but it would at least remove the most obvious confound.
