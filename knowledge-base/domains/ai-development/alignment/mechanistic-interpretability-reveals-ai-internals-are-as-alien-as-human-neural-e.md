Anthropic's mechanistic interpretability research found that Claude represents line-break prediction using one-dimensional helical manifolds in a six-dimensional space, with comparisons performed via manifold rotation. This is not 'looking up token probabilities' — it's a novel computational strategy discovered by the learning algorithm. The human equivalent is equally alien: entorhinal cells track 2D locations using high-dimensional toroidal attractor manifolds.

The implication is that both prediction-trained systems converge on strange but efficient geometric representations that neither the system itself nor outside observers can easily understand. You can't discover Claude's helices by asking it in chat, just as you can't discover your own toroidal manifolds through introspection. This symmetry undermines claims that AI cognition is fundamentally more 'mechanical' or 'shallow' than human cognition — both are built from equally weird low-level algorithms that produce apparently normal high-level thinking.
