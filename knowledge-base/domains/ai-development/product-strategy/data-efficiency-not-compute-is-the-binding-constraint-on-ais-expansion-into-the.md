The Spectors observe that LLMs have become extraordinary at a narrow set of tasks — search and coding — precisely because those tasks are exceptionally well-resourced with data: search has the entire internet, coding has a large fraction of it plus the ability to generate near-unlimited synthetic data. This abundance, not some special property of the tasks, is what drove the capability.

The rest of the economy looks nothing like this. Robotics requires elaborate effort just to generate training data, trading has only so much financial data, and scientific discovery has almost none — yet the upside there is unbounded. They use a deliberately absurd example (an 'end-to-end toaster supply chain') to make the structural point: the broad economy is composed of tens of thousands of poorly-resourced, long-tail tasks, not a handful of internet-scale ones.

The strategic implication is that the question 'can we get search/coding-level capability with 100,000x less data?' is the one that actually unlocks the broad deployment of AI. Humans reach coding competence on orders of magnitude less data than current models, so it should be possible — and whoever solves it captures the long tail that abundant-data approaches can never reach.
