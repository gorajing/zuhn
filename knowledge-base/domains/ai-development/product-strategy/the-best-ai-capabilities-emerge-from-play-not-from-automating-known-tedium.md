Wattrus warns that a new technology invites the trap of 'just automating the tedious things' — replicating known workflows faster. What excites him is emergence: behaviors that weren't possible before and that you didn't ask for. He traces this to play, drawing a parallel to the creative explosion when CSS3 and HTML5 arrived; after years of not playing, builders are playing again, and that's where the interesting capabilities come from.

His examples are unprompted and surprising. Dropping a JSON and a CSV file, the agent decided on its own to combine them because the data looked similar, then wrote a report flagging duplicates and suggested next steps — none of it requested. The most striking case: after giving agents a knowledge base of recorded customer calls and docs, an agent hit a problem it couldn't fix (missing employee IDs) and instead of failing, told the human exactly how to fix it — go to HR and have them generate the IDs. He wasn't expecting the agent to help the human do a job the agent couldn't do.

The product lesson is that you discover these form factors by playing and being curious, not by writing a PRD for a known task. He bakes the discoveries into products ('obvious') only after stumbling on them in exploration. The actionable stance: budget real time for undirected play with live models, because the capabilities worth shipping are the ones you couldn't have planned to build.
