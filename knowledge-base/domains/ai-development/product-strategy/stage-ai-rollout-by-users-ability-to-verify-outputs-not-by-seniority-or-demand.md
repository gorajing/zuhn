How a user interacts with an AI tool — their ability to verify outputs and give useful feedback — changes dramatically with their expertise. Northwestern Mutual sequenced its GenBI rollout crawl-walk-run by verification capacity: first to BI experts (who know what a correct answer looks like and just want it faster, like a GitHub Copilot), then to business managers (who can still spot wrong answers from daily exposure and are inclined to report them), and only eventually — perhaps never — to executives, who want concise answers they can trust blindly and would be poisoned by an inaccurate system. The counterintuitive part: the users with the most authority and demand are the wrong first audience, because they can neither catch errors nor tolerate them. Sequence by who can protect the system from its own mistakes.
