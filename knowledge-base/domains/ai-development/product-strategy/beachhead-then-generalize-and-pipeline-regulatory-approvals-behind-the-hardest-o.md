Asked how he decides when to go deep versus expand, Seo describes the strategy as 'beachhead and then expand,' with a critical constraint: build the core system generalizable enough that you are not making a one-off for motor cortex, then visual cortex, then auditory cortex. The same platform must transfer across regions of the brain. This keeps expansion cheap relative to the first product and prevents the company from accumulating specialized dead-ends.

The second move is regulatory pipelining. The binding bottlenecks Seo names are, in order, biology (hardest), then regulatory, payment, and market dynamics. Because the first approval is the slowest and most expensive — it must establish the clinical evidence of safety — the strategy is to invest preclinical and clinical resources across multiple products in parallel, timed so that once the first and hardest one clears, the others can point to it and ride a much faster pathway (PMA supplements, 510k). The first win is not just a product; it is the asset that de-risks the entire roadmap.

The generalizability discipline and the approval-sequencing discipline reinforce each other: a shared architecture is what lets a later product credibly claim equivalence to the first cleared one, which is what makes the faster regulatory route available. For any platform facing a steep, gated first approval, the lesson is to sequence the portfolio so the hardest clearance carries the rest.
