Meet-Ting's design choice to operate inside the inbox — where coordination already happens — rather than building a separate scheduling interface is a deliberate distribution strategy. Users connect calendars once and schedule through email, meaning the AI meets people where they already work rather than asking them to change behavior.

This principle applies broadly to AI product design: the highest-friction barrier to AI adoption is often not capability but workflow disruption. Products that augment existing tools (email, Slack, IDEs) face lower adoption resistance than those that require learning new interfaces. The tradeoff is less control over the experience, but the distribution advantage often outweighs it. Meet-Ting's viral growth — 600 calendars in two months — illustrates how embedded AI can achieve organic spread through the very workflows it enhances.
