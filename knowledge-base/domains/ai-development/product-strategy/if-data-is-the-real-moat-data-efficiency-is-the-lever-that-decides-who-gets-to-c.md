The third argument is structural and, as Asher frames it, philosophical: who gets to participate in the AI revolution. Very few companies can train frontier models today, and that concentration comes not only from compute centralization but from data centralization. The Spectors cite labs literally buying out distressed bookstores and combing rare libraries to assemble the niche data needed for new capabilities — an acquisition game only the largest players can afford to run.

They tie this directly to market belief: in a poll at the event, the most common answer to 'what is the moat in AI?' was data. If that is true, then data efficiency is precisely the thing that erodes the moat. A model that needs far less data removes the barrier that currently keeps participation narrow.

The takeaway for anyone reasoning about competitive dynamics in AI: data efficiency modulates the *shape* of the market, not just the cost of a single model. It determines whether AI capability stays concentrated among data-hoarding incumbents or spreads to companies that compete on algorithms and systems instead of on who can buy the most proprietary data.
