Shipper describes Every moving toward a 'demo culture': previously, to build something you had to write a memo or a deck and convince people it was worth the time; now you can vibe-code a prototype in a couple of hours that shows the thing itself. The key effect is psychological and organizational, not just speed — being able to 'feel' an idea lets a team pursue weirder concepts that would never survive an abstract pitch, and the low starting energy ('go do some research on this big refactor' while you work on something else) means far more experiments get attempted.

The strategic shift is that the unit of persuasion changes from argument to artifact. When the cost of a risky prototype drops near zero, the rational policy is to build-to-decide rather than debate-to-decide, which both surfaces non-obvious good ideas and kills bad ones faster than a review meeting would. The caveat is that demo culture rewards ideas that demo well, which is not identical to ideas that are durable or defensible — so it should govern exploration and early validation, not replace the harder judgment about what to ship and maintain.
