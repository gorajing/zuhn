The pre-AI product-design flow was sequential: PM writes a PRD describing what to build → UX produces wireframes/Figma → engineering implements. Each step was a hand-off, each hand-off introduced friction, and the loop took weeks per iteration.

The new pattern: PM uses an AI prototyping tool (AI Studio at Google, similar tools elsewhere) to produce 5 working prototypes in a single afternoon. The team meeting starts with all 5 in hand, picks the strongest direction, and iterates from working code rather than from wireframes. The decision happens earlier, on stronger evidence (a working prototype is more decision-shaping than a static mock), and the PM/UX/engineering boundary blurs because PMs are now shipping artifacts that historically belonged to UX and engineering.

The organizational implication is that decision authority and review responsibility need to be recalibrated. If a PM brings 5 prototypes, who's accountable for accessibility? Visual polish? Architectural fit? The pre-AI answer (UX, design lead, engineering tech lead respectively) doesn't cleanly map onto the new artifact. Teams that don't recalibrate end up either with bad prototypes shipping (no review check) or political friction (UX/engineering feeling bypassed).

This is the strongest example so far of where AI redistributes decision authority rather than just speeding up workflows.
