The team states their guiding principle for full-stack runtime: 'we don't want you to think about those details.' The user expresses intent ('I want a multiplayer app,' 'I have an e-commerce app') and the platform infers and provisions the necessary stack — Express, storage, a payment solution — abstracting infrastructure decisions entirely. They tie this to 'democratizing who can create things,' citing a non-engineer who started building after a YouTube video.

The strategic bet is that the next abstraction layer in software creation is intent, not configuration. Just as compilers hid assembly and cloud hid servers, intent-based platforms aim to hide the choice of framework, database, and service entirely. If correct, this reshapes who can build software and shifts product competition toward how accurately a tool intuits user intent rather than how many knobs it exposes. The risk is leaky abstractions, but the directional claim is that inferring the stack from intent, when it works, expands the builder population dramatically.
