Historically, the sheer cost and slowness of engineering forced teams to think hard before building, acting as a natural filter against shipping every feature request or whim. AI removes that filter: anyone, including a one-person team, can now ship the same feature set as an incumbent almost instantly, so the pendulum has swung toward shipping without thinking, producing convoluted software with confusing UX. Artman invokes Steve Jobs' framing that great products come from saying no to 999 things and yes to one. The strategic implication is that when output is commoditized, the scarce, defensible skill is curated restraint — grouping feature requests to find the real root-cause problem, then designing one excellent solution — rather than racing to ship everything. Tasteful, high-quality software becomes the moat precisely because AI made the alternative cheap for everyone.
