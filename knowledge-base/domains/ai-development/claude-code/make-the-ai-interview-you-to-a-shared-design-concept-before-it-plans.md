A common failure is 'the AI didn't do what I wanted' — there is a communication barrier, and talking to the AI is really the AI doing requirements gathering. Drawing on Brooks' The Design of Design, Pocock frames the goal as a shared design concept: the invisible, ephemeral theory of what you're building that floats between collaborators and cannot be captured in a markdown file. His viral 'Grill Me' skill is two lines — interview me relentlessly about every aspect of this plan until we reach a shared understanding, walking down each branch of the design tree and resolving dependencies one by one. It turns the AI into an adversary that asks 40–100 questions before it's satisfied; the resulting transcript becomes a PRD or issues. He argues this beats default plan mode, which is too eager to create an asset and start working rather than first converging on shared understanding.
