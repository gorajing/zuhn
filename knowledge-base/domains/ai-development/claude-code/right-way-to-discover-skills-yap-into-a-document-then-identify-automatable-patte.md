Pemberton's recommended skill-discovery method: yap into your phone for 20 minutes about every task, every workflow, every recurring activity in your work. Don't skip details. Get it in a document. Then ask AI to identify automatable patterns. Each automatable process becomes a candidate skill.

Why this works better than top-down skill design: you don't actually know what you do at the level of detail needed for a skill until you say it out loud. Top-down design produces obvious skills (email summary, calendar review) and misses the high-leverage ones that are specific to your workflow. The yap method captures the long tail of habitual workflows that have value precisely because they're routine to you and idiosyncratic to your role.

For builders: this is a useful diagnostic for any agent platform — does the discovery flow guide users toward this kind of self-observation, or does it dump them into a 'pick from these templates' menu? The first wins; the second produces shallow adoption. For Zuhn specifically: this maps onto the wake.ts / sleep.ts skill design — they capture session state via human dictation rather than predefined fields, which is the right shape for genuinely personal knowledge accumulation.
