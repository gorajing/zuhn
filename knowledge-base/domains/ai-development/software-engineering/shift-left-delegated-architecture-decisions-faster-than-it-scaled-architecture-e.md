Shift-left is celebrated for empowering developers to own more decisions, but the founders surface its darker side: 'architecture expertise and standards didn't scale with that empowerment.' Developers are making architectural choices whether the organization likes it or not, and the enterprise architecture team or architecture guild that is supposed to review those choices simply cannot keep up. The result is a structural bottleneck — guidance that is either too slow (gating) or absent (chaos).

This is a diagnosis worth holding independent of any product: whenever you push a class of decisions to a larger, less-specialized population, you create a guidance-scaling problem. The decision *volume* grows with the size of the population making decisions, but the *expertise* to inform those decisions stays concentrated in a small team that reviews on a fixed cadence (e.g., 'once every two weeks'). The gap between decision throughput and review throughput is where drift, redundant spend, and unmanaged risk accumulate.

The corollary is that the scaling lever for a centralized expertise function is no longer headcount or more meetings — it is the quality of guidance that function can encode into tools developers use in-workflow. The architecture team 'scales through the guidance they give to the AI,' not through reviewing more designs themselves.
