The study built a composite 'environment cleanliness index' (0-1) scoring tests, types, documentation, modularity, and code quality, and found a respectable ~0.40 R² between it and the productivity lift teams get from AI. The takeaway: invest in codebase hygiene to unlock AI gains. A clean codebase shifts more tasks into the 'AI can do most of the work' band; a dirty one pushes tasks into 'AI is not very useful.'

The dynamic is bidirectional and adversarial: using AI unchecked accelerates codebase entropy (tech debt), which degrades cleanliness and collapses future AI gains. Humans must actively push back on entropy to keep reaping the benefit — it's a treadmill, not a one-time setup. This connects hygiene investment directly to AI ROI rather than treating it as abstract craftsmanship, and explains why two teams with identical tools can get very different returns.
