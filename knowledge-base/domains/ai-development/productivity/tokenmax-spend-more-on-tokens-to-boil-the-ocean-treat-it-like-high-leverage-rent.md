Tan's central claim is that the old human constraint — settle for one source, do the minimum research, write the minimum tests — was a function of human time being expensive. With agents, you can 'boil the ocean': pull 20 sources instead of one, cross-reference where 13 agree and 7 disagree, feed all of it into the core prompt, and make a better-grounded decision than a human skimming a single headline. A month of human research becomes a $5–10 batch of Opus calls. The discipline is to ask 'what is the total-completionist version?' on any task and pay for it. He frames the spend the way YC frames San Francisco rent: it looks expensive in isolation, but it's the input with the highest return, so you should push hard on it rather than treating it like an office desk you economize on. The counter-discipline: tokenmaxxing only pays off when you're building the right thing — the human still supplies the agency and direction that says 'I need this.' Connects to the pattern that AI collapses multi-year projects into weekend-scale work, and that human attention, not agent capacity, is the binding constraint.
