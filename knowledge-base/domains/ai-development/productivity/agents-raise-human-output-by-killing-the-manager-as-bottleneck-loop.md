After the initial Figma-to-website success, swyx watched a Slack thread explode to 200+ replies as his Indonesia-based designer started prompting Devin directly — using red-line annotations on screenshots, with no instruction manual, simply 'how you would communicate with another human being.' The designer began initiating work on a throwaway project nobody asked for, purely because it was fun and the loop was instant.

The insight swyx draws is that the productivity gain is not primarily the agent's output — it's the human's. He is 'getting more productivity out of my humans' because the feedback cycle that used to block them (waiting on him as manager, or on a contracted developer) is gone. Once that wait disappears, people go from idea straight to execution, and they take on polish and experiments they never would have queued before.

The practical move for managers: stop being the routing bottleneck. Give non-technical team members their own direct agent access so their ideas don't have to wait in your queue — the unblocked, low-friction loop is what converts latent ideas into shipped work.
