When DX, DORA, and Google aggregate AI-adoption data, the averages are modest and positive: ~7.5% better documentation, ~3.4% better code quality, ~2.6% higher change confidence. But broken down per company, the same studies show extreme bimodal variability — some organizations gain 20% in change confidence while others lose 20%, and change failure rate ranges from improvement to a 2% increase (on a 4% benchmark, that's shipping ~50% more defects). The flat average is not the story; it is what hides the story.

The leadership implication is that benchmarking against 'the average AI productivity lift' is nearly useless for decision-making. The relevant question is not 'does AI help on average' but 'what separates the teams it helps from the teams it hurts' — because the same tool produces opposite outcomes depending on enablement, education, and how it's integrated. Treat high variance as the primary diagnostic: it signals that org-specific factors, not the tool, dominate the result, and those factors are exactly what leadership controls.
