The cloud transition expanded software TAM from ~$350B to ~$650B over 15 years, and that growth came almost entirely from new software products and existing-software-becoming-cloud. The AI transition unlocks something structurally different: not new software but services delivered by AI. Legal services, accounting, healthcare diagnostics, financial advice, customer support, talent acquisition, sales — all of these are services humans deliver today, and AI can now deliver them at substantially lower per-task cost. The aggregate addressable market is approximately $10T, fifteen times the entire software market. The strategic implication for builders: products that frame themselves as 'software for X' compete on the $650B software market; products that frame themselves as 'AI delivering the work that X does' compete on the $10T services market. The framing choice substantially affects valuation, GTM motion, and competitive set. The risk: most current AI products are still framed as software (per-seat pricing, feature-set comparison) when they should be framed as services (outcome pricing, work-completed metrics). Founders should explicitly test which framing fits their product and reprice accordingly.
