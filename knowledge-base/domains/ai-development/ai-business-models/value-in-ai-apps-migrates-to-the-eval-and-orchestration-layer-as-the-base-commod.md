swyx's 'aha moment,' credited to Ankur of Braintrust, is that the early parts of the LLM software development lifecycle are increasingly commodity. Raw model access, basic monitoring, and RAG are 'kind of free' — offered on free tiers across providers. The point where customers actually start paying, and where the work becomes 'real hard engineering,' is evals, security, and orchestration. This is why those became new conference tracks: they mark the frontier between demos and production.

For builders and investors, this reframes where to invest effort and look for defensibility. Differentiating on having an LLM, a vector store, or a dashboard is building on commoditizing ground. The defensible surface is the production-grade machinery: rigorous evaluation harnesses that tell you whether changes help, security and access control around tool use, and orchestration that reliably coordinates many calls. A product whose only moat is model access is exposed; one whose moat is its eval and orchestration discipline compounds.

The insight is tagged time-sensitive because which specific layers are 'free' will keep shifting upward as the stack matures — today's paid orchestration could be tomorrow's commodity. The durable principle is the direction of travel: value migrates up the stack away from whatever the foundation labs absorb.
