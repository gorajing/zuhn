Shipper claims a '10x difference' between an org where 90% of engineers use AI and one where 100% do. The mechanism is not the marginal productivity of the last 10% of people, but a coordination constraint: if even a tenth of the team works in a traditional code-editor flow, the org must 'lean all the way back over into that world,' preserving review norms, handoff rituals, and shared assumptions that block the genuinely new workflows (parallel agents, demo culture, cross-repo agent reading) the other 90% could otherwise adopt.

The practical implication is that AI adoption should be managed as a threshold to cross, not a percentage to gradually raise. Half-measures forfeit the discontinuous gains because the surviving minority sets the floor for everyone's process. The claim is from a single company (Every, 15 people) and conflates 'people using AI' with 'org redesigned around AI,' so it is best read as a directional argument for pushing hard to full adoption rather than a measured coefficient. It is marked time-sensitive because the size of the 90%-to-100% gap is a feature of this specific transition moment.
