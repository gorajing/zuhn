The speaker contrasts himself — a founder who read blogs nightly and ran 45 agents to capture every bit of incremental value — with a salaried engineer at the next desk typing code character-by-character 'like a caveman.' The usual explanations for the gap (the tools aren't good enough, people don't want to learn) miss the real driver: the founder captured the upside of working faster, the employee on base-salary-plus-bonus-plus-equity did not. Adoption tracks incentive structure, not access or aptitude. This reframes the 'why won't my team use AI' problem away from training, evangelism, and mandates toward the compensation question: does anyone on the team actually get richer by doing more with these tools? If the answer is no, low adoption is the system working as designed. The diagnostic move — treat a behavior gap as an incentive gap before assuming a capability gap — generalizes well beyond AI tooling.
