Across DX's organizational data, the clearest failure pattern in AI rollouts is the top-down mandate: leadership demands a target adoption percentage, and engineers respond with the minimum compliant behavior needed to hit the number while the underlying outcome metrics don't budge. Mandates convert a measurement problem into a gaming problem. The other common failures are the same shape: flipping the tech on with no education ('expecting everybody to just know the best ways to use it') and not knowing what to measure.

What actually moves the needle, per DORA's posterior-distribution analysis, are clear AI policies and — critically — time to learn: not just handing people materials but giving them protected space to experiment. The deeper reason traces to Google's Project Aristotle (2012), which found psychological safety, not talent or resources, was the dominant predictor of team performance. AI adoption is a behavior-change problem, and behavior change under fear and coercion produces theater; behavior change under safety and slack produces real practice. Leaders should replace adoption targets with enablement investments and reduce the fear that AI is coming for jobs.
