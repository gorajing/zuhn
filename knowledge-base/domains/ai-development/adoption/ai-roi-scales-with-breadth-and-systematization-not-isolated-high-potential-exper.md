A clear bifurcation is emerging between AI leaders and laggers, and the distinguishing trait of leaders is simply that they do more of it and think about it more comprehensively and systematically. They run multiple initiatives at once and treat adoption as a whole-organization strategy rather than scattered spot experiments. In the self-reported data, the relationship was nearly monotonic: the more use cases an organization submitted, the better the ROI it tended to report.

Crucially, leaders also escape the gravity of first-tier productivity wins. Instead of stopping at time savings, they deliberately pursue revenue growth, new capabilities, and new product lines — the categories with transformational upside. McKinsey's data corroborates the value of cross-organizational, cross-disciplinary thinking over isolated experiments.

The actionable implication is to resist the instinct to find the one perfect pilot and prove it before expanding. Breadth itself is a driver of return: a wide portfolio of use cases compounds organizational learning, surfaces non-obvious wins, and builds the muscle that single experiments never develop. Caveat: this is self-reported by a highly engaged, self-selected audience, so causality (does breadth cause ROI, or do capable orgs do both?) is unproven.
