Many expected this to be 'the year of agents' in the sense of mass workforce automation, and that didn't happen. But the quieter shift is more telling: full production agent deployment (not pilots, not experiments) among $1B+ enterprises in KPMG's pulse survey jumped from 11% in Q1 to 42% in Q3. Agents moved through the experimental phase faster than most predicted.

The consequence is that the emphasis has visibly moved from 'can the technology do it' to 'can the humans absorb it.' Organizations are now investing in upskilling, enablement work, and sandboxes where employees can interact with agents safely, and they report decreasing resistance as people actually engage with the tools rather than fearing them in the abstract.

The durable lesson — beyond the specific 2025 numbers — is that as a capability matures, the binding constraint migrates from the model to the surrounding human and organizational system. Builders and operators should plan for enablement, change management, and human-agent interaction design as first-class deliverables, not afterthoughts, because that is where the marginal blocker now lives.
