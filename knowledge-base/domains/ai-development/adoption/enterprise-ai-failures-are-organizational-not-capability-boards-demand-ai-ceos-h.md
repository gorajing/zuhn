Casado's reframe of the MIT stat: enterprise AI failure rates measure centralized initiatives, not actual usage. Individual employees are using ChatGPT and Claude effectively at massive scale; the failure is in organizations trying to centralize AI decisions through age-old processes for governance, compliance, data, and operations that were built over decades and don't fit AI's pace. The board says 'we need AI,' the CEO hires a consultant, a centralized project ships that nobody understands, and it fails.

Levie's complementary point: there's also paralysis from architectural uncertainty. Frontier labs leapfrog each other so quickly with different paradigms (agent harness in/out of computer, hosted/cloud, what tools, etc.) that enterprise architecture teams genuinely don't know what horse to back — and they remember being burned by past wrong bets. The result is a compounding lag: by the time the org consolidates around a paradigm, the paradigm has changed.

The operational implication for builders: enterprise AI products should bypass the centralized org and embed at the individual level (where adoption already works), then accumulate institutional usage from the bottom up. Top-down enterprise sales motions are fighting against organizational reality. For Zuhn editorially: the 'enterprise AI is failing' narrative is partially wrong — it's enterprise *centralization* of AI that's failing while individual usage thrives.
