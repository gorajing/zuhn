Shin Soo-jung's framing from Jang Gwang-myeong's book 'The Future Before': before AlphaGo, Go mastery required two things — native genius (you couldn't learn your way to top-tier without it) AND entering elite training (top academy, top master). The second requirement was an access gate: rural players, women without family Go-network access, and late starters were structurally locked out. AlphaGo and successor systems became BETTER masters than any human, available to anyone with internet access. The credential gate dissolved. This is the most significant democratizing effect of AI, and it's understated in mainstream productivity narratives that focus on '20% efficiency gains' for already-credentialed knowledge workers. The bigger story: people previously locked out of mastery (rural, female, non-elite, late-starting) can now reach top levels without needing the access. Shin extends this to office workers and entrepreneurs without elite networks: 'I don't have a good background, I didn't graduate from a good university, I haven't met a good mentor — you can solve most of these problems' with AI. The geopolitical implication: countries with strong AI literacy programs may see faster cross-class mobility than those still gating mastery through credential institutions. The corollary for incumbent elite institutions: their value proposition shifts from 'access to mastery' (now commoditized) to 'access to network' (which still matters, but is a much narrower offering than they currently sell).
