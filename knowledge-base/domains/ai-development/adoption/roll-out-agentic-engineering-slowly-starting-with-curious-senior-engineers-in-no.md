PFF's rollout was deliberate: pick the engineers with the deepest systems knowledge (the people others are told to 'go ask'), start in non-critical systems where bugs don't matter, prove out small features there, and only then move to the surface that gets 100M page views. Guardrails must be fully functional before entering an autonomous flow. The single most common failure mode Spitz cites is the opposite: giving everyone a coding assistant plus a hackathon and assuming they'll 'sort it out' — every org's style is different and that approach doesn't transfer.

The human dimension is blunt: 'not everyone can drive a sports car,' and that's acceptable. The engineer who thrives is the curious one who will spend time figuring out how something was built; the engineer who needs a highly prescriptive spec will struggle. There's also a scale asymmetry — this is far easier to roll out across 20 engineers than 1,000 — which gives small companies a structural advantage. But the countervailing pressure is real: because the gains compound, falling a few months behind today becomes six then twelve months behind later, so 'go slow' must be balanced against not being too conservative.
