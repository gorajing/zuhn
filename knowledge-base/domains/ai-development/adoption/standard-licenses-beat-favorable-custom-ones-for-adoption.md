A custom license, even one written to be commercial-friendly and permissive, is an adoption killer. The moment a legal department sees non-standard terms, it must run a procurement and review process that can stretch to 18 months — and frequently never resolves. The friction is not in the terms themselves but in the fact that they are unrecognized and must be evaluated from scratch.

A standard license (the speakers moved Gemma from a bespoke license to Apache 2.0) is something legal teams already understand and have pre-approved patterns for, so adoption clears legal in days instead of dying in review. For sovereign institutions and large enterprises — exactly the buyers who most value owning an open model — license standardization is often the difference between deployment and abandonment.

The general principle for anyone distributing software, models, or datasets: optimizing license terms for permissiveness is the wrong target if the license is non-standard. Recognizability and legal-team familiarity dominate marginal generosity in the terms. Pick the most standard license that meets your needs, not the most favorable custom one.
