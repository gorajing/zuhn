The question of whether AI deserves boardroom representation shifts the frame from AI-as-tool to AI-as-stakeholder. At the operational level, AI assists with execution — drafting, coding, analyzing. But at the strategic level, AI can synthesize competitive intelligence, model scenarios, and surface non-obvious patterns at a scale no human team can match. Boards that treat this as merely a technology procurement question may be underestimating the governance implications.

The practical tension is accountability: AI cannot be held responsible for decisions. But neither can a consultant, yet boards routinely engage external advisors with significant strategic influence. The more tractable framing may be: how do boards ensure AI-derived insights are properly represented in deliberations, without creating false authority or obscuring the human judgment that must ultimately bear accountability? This is less about giving AI a vote and more about building structured channels for AI-generated analysis to inform — not replace — strategic reasoning.
