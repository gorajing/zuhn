Early electric-motor factories simply dropped a more powerful motor where the single giant steam engine used to sit, keeping the surrounding 3D layout of conveyor belts unchanged — and got only incremental throughput gains. The real productivity unlock came two to three decades later when engineers redesigned the factory floor around the fact that electric motors can be small and distributed. Xin maps this directly onto AI: slapping agents onto an existing system without changing processes, tooling, and CI/CD gives some automation wins but not a massive gain.

The actionable corollary is that it is easier to build a new AI-native software factory from scratch than to retrofit a giant existing system, and reconfiguration of legacy systems is necessarily slow because you can't be too disruptive. For companies already far along, the move is to create new space — new teams, efforts, product lines, or organizations that are AI-native — rather than only retrofitting the legacy machine, which otherwise leaves a 'giant hole where the steam engine used to be.'
