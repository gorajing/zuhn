The closing piece of advice reframes agent-readiness as an organizational change-management opportunity. The practices the talk recommends — self-service, API-first interfaces, local-first tooling, structured documentation, machine-readable observability — were, in the speaker's words, 'always best practices.' Many teams simply never got buy-in to invest in them because other priorities won.

The leverage move is that AI is now a hot topic at every level of the organization, from the executive suite to individual contributors. That shared attention is a budget and a mandate that didn't exist before. So a platform owner who has been quietly pushing for cleaner APIs or real documentation can now attach those investments to the AI initiative everyone already wants to fund — using AI as the 'excuse to implement some best practices' that were justified on their own merits all along.

The insight is that the engineering case and the political case for good platform design are different problems, and AI temporarily solves the political one. This is marked time-sensitive because the leverage depends on AI being a peak-attention topic; the underlying best practices are evergreen, but the specific window where 'we need this for agents' unlocks resourcing will eventually close as AI tooling becomes routine.
