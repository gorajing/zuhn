The historical mechanism explains why AI 'hasn't made life easier' despite obvious capability. When electricity replaced steam power, the first move was to drop a giant electric generator into the same central spot the steam engine occupied — preserving the old factory layout (centralized power, belt-and-shaft distribution). That produced little productivity gain. The breakthrough came ~30 years later when factories were redesigned around electricity's actual advantage: distributed small motors at each workstation, with the floor plan rearranged around the work rather than around the power source.

The AI parallel: we're currently retrofitting AI into existing workflows — using it to do the old process slightly faster — which is the 'generator where the steam engine was' move. The large gains will come when work is redesigned AI-native: rethinking what the process even is, given that intelligence is now abundant. Until then, individuals feel busier but the org-level productivity step-change hasn't arrived.

This is the deepest available explanation for a pattern multiple sources in this corpus report (the from-agentic panel's 'redesign the org around agents,' Panel 2's enterprise conservatism, and the existing KB principle that organizational change is the binding constraint). It reframes the adoption bottleneck from 'people resist change' (true but shallow) to 'the productivity gain is structurally gated on workflow redesign, which takes time and imagination' (the mechanism).

The planning implication: don't expect AI ROI from bolting agents onto current processes. Budget for — and competitively, race toward — AI-native workflow redesign. And calibrate timeline expectations: if the electricity precedent holds even compressed, the economy-wide productivity step-change lags capability by years, not quarters. The edge goes to whoever redesigns fastest, not whoever adopts the most tools.
