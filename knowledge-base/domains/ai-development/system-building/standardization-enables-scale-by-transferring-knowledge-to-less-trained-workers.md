Grier traces how standardization was the critical enabler for scaling computation. Early nautical almanac calculations were done by individuals using idiosyncratic personal methods — one computer even had cramped handwriting that made his calculations nearly indecipherable to others. By the early 20th century, calculations were done on standardized graph paper with operations identified line by line.

The deeper principle is that standardization transfers complex processes to less-trained individuals. Weather data collection required people who hadn't attended college to perform arithmetic reliably — impossible without standardized methods. Herbert Hoover championed industrial standardization (even nuts and bolts weren't interchangeable across manufacturers), and computing inherited this imperative. Standard programming languages, standard algorithms, and standard interfaces all serve the same function: enabling people who don't understand the full system to reliably contribute their piece. This is directly analogous to how modern software abstractions and APIs enable scale.
