Joel David Hamkins advocates a 'mathematical multiverse' position: instead of one true set-theoretic universe, there are many equally valid mathematical universes corresponding to different axiomatic choices. The continuum hypothesis is true in some universes and false in others (Godel proved it's consistent with ZFC; Cohen proved its negation is also consistent). Some mathematicians work without the axiom of choice; others embrace large cardinal axioms that go far beyond ZFC. Hamkins sees these as genuinely different mathematical realities, not just different descriptions of one truth. For system builders, this is a profound architectural insight: foundations are design choices with tradeoffs, not discoveries of absolute truth. Just as choosing ZFC over constructive mathematics gives you different theorems, choosing a programming paradigm, type system, or AI architecture gives you different capabilities and blind spots. The multiverse view encourages maintaining multiple perspectives rather than seeking a single 'correct' foundation.
