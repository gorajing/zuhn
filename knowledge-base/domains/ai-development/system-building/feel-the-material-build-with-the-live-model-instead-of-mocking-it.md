Wattrus, a woodworker, frames AI design through physical-material analogies: before you build with a material you have to feel it — its properties, where it's smooth or rough, weak or strong. Traditional product design approached software 'through layers' — mockups, prototypes, click-throughs — trying to predict what would work. With generative tools (v0, Claude Code) good enough to produce working software quickly, designers can now feel the actual material: build the real thing, run it on real data, and see how the model behaves rather than guessing.

This dissolves long-standing role divides. When designers, product people, and engineers can all build together, you can 'get rid of mockups, get rid of click-through prototypes, and all the hand-wringing about whether the thing is worth the engineering effort.' The cost of finding out is now low enough that you find out by building.

The practical mandate: his new north star is 'creating an environment for these LLMs to shine' — finding the form factor that lets the model nail its assignment, stay aligned, and improve as models get better. Because an LLM is 'an intern with a PhD' you're putting 'in a box,' the box (the form factor) deserves real engineering effort, and the only way to design a good box is to build it and feel the material inside it.
