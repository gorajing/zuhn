Frebel explains that the Milky Way grew through hierarchical mergers: larger proto-galaxies gravitationally attracted and absorbed smaller neighbors. The oldest stars — made from just hydrogen and helium — ended up at the galaxy's outskirts, debris from ancient acquisitions that were never fully integrated into the new structure.

This is a powerful metaphor for organizational and platform growth: dominant systems grow by absorbing smaller ones, but the acquired entities often remain at the periphery, never fully integrated. The chemistry of those ancient stars tells the story of what the universe looked like before the merger — just as the culture and practices of acquired companies often preserve information about what the market looked like before consolidation. The systems that grow largest are not necessarily the most innovative — they're the ones with enough gravitational pull (capital, users, data) to absorb everything around them.
