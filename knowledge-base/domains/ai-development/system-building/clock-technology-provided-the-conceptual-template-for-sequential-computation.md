Grier identifies the escapement clock as the crucial technological metaphor and practical foundation for computation. While Babbage tried to build his Difference Engine using steam-engine-scale brass gears (and failed), a Swedish father and son later succeeded using clock technology — smaller, lower energy, with abundant standardized parts.

The clock's contribution to computing was the concept of a timed sequence controlling mechanical operations. The tick-tock of the escapement ratchet became a drumbeat that stepped through calculation mechanisms in a controlled, orderly way. Without this timing mechanism, machines would 'run wild.' This gave builders a technology to think with — 'we know this works for keeping time; how does it work for controlling additions?' The clock's influence is still embedded in every modern computer's clock cycle. The broader lesson: breakthroughs often come not from inventing something new but from recognizing that an existing technology (clocks) can serve as a template for an entirely different domain (computation).
