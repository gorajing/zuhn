Patel's distinction: people like Ben Thompson and Leopold Aschenbrenner argue that if AI is as transformative as nuclear weapons, the government would never let a private company control it. But that analogy fails because AI is not self-contained — it is more like the process of industrialization, with thousands of applications across every sector. If you applied the same logic to industrialization in the 1700s, the government would have had the right to requisition any factory and destroy any business that resisted. Free societies didn't handle industrialization that way; they regulated specific weaponizable use cases (chemical weapons treaties, environmental regulations, labor laws).

The operational principle: regulate AI by use case, not by ownership. Cyber attacks should be illegal whether human or AI launches them. Mass surveillance state-building should be illegal whether classical software or LLMs build it. Bioweapons design assistance should be banned at the use-case layer. The wrong move — handing the government a purpose-built regulatory apparatus 'for AI' — concentrates exactly the leverage that authoritarian-trending governments most want. The Pentagon's use of a 2018 anti-Huawei supply-chain authority to coerce Anthropic shows what happens when even ill-fitting authorities get repurposed; a purpose-built apparatus would be incomparably more dangerous.
