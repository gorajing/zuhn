Hao's analogy: 'If most of the climate scientists in the world were bankrolled by fossil fuel companies, would we get an accurate picture of the climate crisis?' The AI industry employs and bankrolls the majority of AI researchers globally, setting the research agenda via funding priorities — which types of capability research get done, which limitations get studied, which harms get measured.

The censorship side: Dr. Timnit Gebru was hired at Google specifically to critique Google's AI systems as co-lead of the Ethical AI team. When she co-wrote a research paper documenting harms of large language models, Google tried to stop publication, fired her, then fired her co-lead Margaret Mitchell. This wasn't an isolated case — Hao documents systematic quashing of research inconvenient to the empires' agenda.

The implication for anyone doing AI strategy: treat AI capability/limitation research published by lab-affiliated researchers with the same skepticism you'd apply to tobacco research from Philip Morris-funded labs. The independent voices exist but are chronically underfunded, and the loud consensus signals come disproportionately from people whose livelihoods depend on the narrative.
