Patel rejects the Leopold Aschenbrenner / Ben Thompson analogy that because nuclear weapons would never be left to a private company, AI shouldn't be either. The analogy fails because AI is not a self-contained weapon with one function — it's a general-purpose transformation of the entire economy, closer to industrialization than to a bomb.

If 17th-century Europeans had applied the nuclear-weapons logic to the industrial revolution, governments would have had the authority to seize factories and coerce any business that refused state demands. Free societies didn't handle industrialization that way. Instead, we banned specific weaponizable end uses (chemical weapons, indiscriminate aerial bombardment, illegal labor practices) while leaving the underlying productive capacity in private hands.

The applied recommendation: regulate AI-enabled cyber attacks, bioweapons synthesis, and government surveillance apparatus — things that would be illegal if a human did them — but don't build a regulatory framework over 'AI' as a category. This preserves private innovation at the substrate layer while drawing bright lines at specific harms, and it prevents the substrate from being captured by whichever party holds the presidency.
