Hao cites the most recent polling: 80% of Americans believe the AI industry needs to be regulated. For comparison, she asks when 80% of Americans last agreed on an issue. Dozens of protests against data centers have broken out across the US and globally — many stalling projects and some succeeding in banning data centers in their localities. Artists and writers are suing labs for IP infringement and creating public conversations about data rights. Parents like Megan Garcia are suing chatbot companies after child suicides caused by unregulated intimacy chatbots.

On the opposing side: frontier labs are spending hundreds of millions of dollars in the upcoming midterm elections to kill every possible piece of AI legislation and pass legislation that codifies their advantages (preemption of state laws, safe harbors on copyright, compute export rules favoring incumbents).

Hao's call to action for ordinary people: think about every way your life intersects with resources the AI industry needs, and withhold strategically. If you're a data donor, withhold data. If you're in a school or company debating AI adoption policy, don't make the adoption go flawlessly when OpenAI's internal revenue targets depend on flawless rollout. If a data center is being proposed near you, organize to oppose it. Build alternatives — the 'bicycles of AI.' The thesis: empires are only as powerful as the compliance they receive, and this is a rare moment where friction is both popular and effective.
