DeepMind's insistence on an AI ethics board as a pre-condition for Google's $500M acquisition in 2014 was one of the earliest examples of AI governance being treated as a structural requirement rather than an afterthought. This is notable because it embedded safety considerations into the organizational structure at the moment of maximum leverage—before the deal closed.

The pattern has broader applicability: governance mechanisms are most effective when established during transitions of power or ownership, not after. For startups building powerful AI systems, negotiating governance structures during funding or acquisition gives founders leverage they won't have later. The caveat is that the effectiveness of such boards depends entirely on their actual authority and independence—DeepMind's own ethics board has faced criticism for opacity.
