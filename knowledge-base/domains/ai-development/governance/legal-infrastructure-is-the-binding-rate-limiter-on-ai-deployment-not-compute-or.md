Nadella's argument: the entire modern economic and legal order is structured around humans owning property, holding rights, and being liable for actions. Delegating authority to AI systems requires that structure to evolve. Until it does, the most powerful AI will operate with delegated human authority, and 'AI did it' will not be an admissible defense in any court. This means the AI-takeoff concern is preceded by a courts-and-liability concern that will constrain deployment long before capability concerns do.

A second layer: democracies require return on labor, not just return on capital, or social stability breaks down. If AGI automates the 60% of labor David Autor studies, revaluation of human labor types becomes a political and legal precondition for deploying the technology at scale, not an afterthought. 'Meaning in work and dignity in work' is a rate limiter on deployment.

The application: when forecasting AI timelines, weight legal/regulatory/social adaptation speed more heavily than capability curves. Countries with more permissive liability structures may see faster deployment, but Nadella argues the world is small enough that 'rogue state' dynamics constrain even those outliers. This is the opposite of typical SF-coded AI timelines that treat capability as the binding constraint.
