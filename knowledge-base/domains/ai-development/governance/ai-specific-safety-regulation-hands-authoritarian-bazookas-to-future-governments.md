Patel directly challenges the Anthropic-aligned view that AI needs NRC-style regulatory oversight. His argument: the conceptual vocabulary of AI risk discourse ('catastrophic risk,' 'threats to national security,' 'autonomy risk,' 'deceptive models,' 'manipulative models') is so elastic that whoever holds enforcement power can bend it to any political purpose.

Concrete examples: a model that tells users tariff policy is misguided becomes a 'deceptive model.' A model that refuses government surveillance becomes an 'autonomy risk.' A model operating independently of state commands becomes a national security threat. The current Pentagon already abused two non-AI statutes (2018 supply-chain risk designation, 1950s Defense Production Act) to pressure Anthropic — imagine what it would do with purpose-built AI authority.

The collective-action argument for regulation (labs can't unilaterally sacrifice speed for safety) is real, but Patel argues the cure is worse than the disease. Given that AI will be the substrate of future civilization, handing government a regulatory framework over AI is functionally handing it a regulatory framework over civilization itself. The AI safety community, including Anthropic, has been naive about this.
