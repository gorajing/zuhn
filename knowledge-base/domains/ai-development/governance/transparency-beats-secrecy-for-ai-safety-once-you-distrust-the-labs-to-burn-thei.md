Kokotajlo describes his own flip: the traditional AI safety view was that the responsible lab should race to AGI, then pause and burn its lead on alignment. That plan only works if the lab actually pauses. Observing the internal dynamics at frontier labs convinced him most people there just plan to proceed regardless, convincing themselves it's fine. Given that, secrecy's main effect is to prevent outside researchers from stress-testing safety claims, not to protect a responsible actor's lead.

The concrete asks: publish model specs (OpenAI did this; it should be standard), publish safety cases for outside critique, allow freedom of speech on capability timelines, whistleblower protections — specifically making it legally safe to report a secret intelligence explosion to the government. Daniel's own experience refusing OpenAI's non-disparagement agreement showed him that fear of legality (not just retaliation) was a major filter preventing employees from speaking up; making things simply legal is valuable independent of protections against retaliation.

The broader principle: when you don't trust the insiders with the relevant expertise, and you don't trust the regulators who lack expertise, distributing the problem to a larger community of outside researchers is the best remaining option. Applies generally to any domain where the insider-incentives-versus-expertise tradeoff looks bad.
