Hao lists four OpenAI AGI definitions deployed to different audiences: (1) Sam Altman in Congress: AGI will cure cancer, solve climate change, cure poverty; (2) Altman to consumers: the most amazing digital assistant; (3) The OpenAI-Microsoft deal language: a system that generates $100 billion in revenue; (4) OpenAI's own website: highly autonomous systems that outperform humans in most economically valuable work. These are not the same technology.

This lack of definitional coherence is not accidental — it traces back to the founding of AI as a field in 1956 at Dartmouth, where John McCarthy named it 'artificial intelligence' (rejecting his earlier 'automata studies') specifically because pegging it to recreating human intelligence was ambitious-sounding. But human intelligence has no consensus definition in psychology, biology, or neurology, and historically every attempt to rank intelligence has been driven by nefarious motives. Without defined goalposts, AGI can be endlessly redefined based on which audience needs to be mobilized at that moment — regulators, consumers, investors, talent. The epistemic implication: predictions about 'when AGI arrives' are category errors; they conflate multiple incompatible goals.
