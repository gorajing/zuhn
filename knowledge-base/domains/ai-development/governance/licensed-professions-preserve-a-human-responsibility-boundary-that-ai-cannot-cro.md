The panel surfaced a structural feature of regulated professions that is often missed in AI capability discussions: the liability boundary is enforced by the license, not by the technology. A licensed architect stamps drawings; a licensed lawyer signs filings; a licensed structural engineer approves load calculations. AI can do all of the drafting/research/calculation that precedes the stamp, but cannot itself carry the liability.

This matters as a durability prediction: even if AI becomes 100x faster and more accurate than the human, the license-holder relationship persists because the regulatory system requires a human-recoverable accountability party when something fails. This is not a temporary regulatory lag — it is the function of professional licensing.

For AI startups in regulated verticals: build for the license-holder as the user, not as a gatekeeper. The license-holder's productivity (how many filings/drawings/diagnoses they can stamp per week) is the actual business metric, not 'AI replaces lawyers/architects/engineers.' Trying to disintermediate the license is fighting the wrong battle.

For general AI strategy: this is a strong argument against the 'AI replaces all knowledge work' narrative — the licensed knowledge professions are structurally protected, not just culturally protected.
