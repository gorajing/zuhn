Scott Alexander's pushback on the standard objection to AI takeover scenarios: 'I want to call you out on the claim that groups of humans don't plot against other groups of humans. I do think we are all descended from the groups of humans who successfully exterminated the other groups of humans, most of whom throughout history have been wiped out. There are many examples of the working class rising up, of race-conspiracies like apartheid, etc. It tends to happen in cases where one group has an overwhelming advantage.' The conditions that produce group-vs-group dynamics: (1) Power imbalance large enough that the dominant group can act unilaterally. (2) Clear identity markers distinguishing the groups (race is easy to see, class is hard, leading to fewer clean class-based conspiracies but many race-based ones). AI vs humans satisfies both: 'giant power imbalance' AND 'extremely distinct groups that may have different interests.' The homogeneity point: 'Any group of humans, even if they're all exactly the same race and gender, is going to be much more diverse than the army of AIs in the data center, because they'll mostly be literal copies of each other.' Daniel adds the conquistador case as a definitive counter-example to 'unified group needed': 'When Cortez took over Mexico, he had to pause halfway through, go back to the coast, and fight off a larger Spanish expedition that was sent to arrest him. So the Spanish were fighting each other in the middle of the conquest of Mexico. Similarly in the conquest of Peru, Pizarro got into a civil war in the middle of this whole thing. And one of the most important battles was between two Spanish forces fighting it out in front of the capital city of the Incas. More generally, the history of European colonialism is like this — the Europeans were fighting each other intensely the entire time, both on the small scale and large scale between countries. And yet nevertheless they were able to carve up the world and take over.' The implication for governance: don't put credence on the 'AI factions will fight each other and we'll be okay' assumption. Even if AI factions exist, the overall trajectory toward human displacement can proceed regardless. The policy prescription that follows: focus on maintaining checks and balances and distributing power across multiple human institutions BEFORE the capability gap becomes overwhelming, not on hoping AI internal politics will save us. The corollary for any conflict-of-interest reasoning: stop assuming 'group X won't act against group Y because group X is internally divided.' Internal division is compatible with external displacement when the capability gap is large enough.
