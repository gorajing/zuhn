Patel's counter-intuitive observation: people argue that the leverage problem (government can squeeze 3 frontier labs) goes away with diffusion. But from the government's perspective, diffusion is easier, not harder — there are now hundreds of vendors, all of whom can be told 'do this or we hurt your other business.' The federal government controls permitting for power generation (data centers), antitrust enforcement, contracts with all big tech for chips and funding, and can make implicit conditions on those contracts.

The Anthropic case demonstrates the leverage stack: even though prediction markets gave the supply-chain restriction a 74% chance of being backtracked, the Pentagon also had the Defense Production Act (a 1950s Korean War statute), and could make soft conditions on Amazon/Nvidia/Google contracts that those companies stop doing business with Anthropic. For builders and editorial coverage: the simplistic 'open source = freedom' equation misses that diffusion is not symmetric leverage — it expands the government's bargaining surface much faster than it shrinks it. The actual binding constraint on freedom is political norms, not market structure.
