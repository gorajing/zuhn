Professor Jiang's framework for the AI surveillance state: 'For the average person it means two things. It means digital ID and digital currency. What that will allow is for the government to basically monitor everything you do online and control all financial transactions. It can program your bank account so you can't buy cigarettes or you can't buy drugs. This will allow the government to categorize everyone and put everyone into a database to monitor that person. Once you have categorized everyone, you can use AI to predict their behavior.' The China model (per Jiang as a Chinese national in the diaspora): 'In China we all have digital ID. If you want to access anything in China you need your digital ID. Open a bank account, use e-payments, use a phone — all require digital ID. Everyone's part of this national database. The government can monitor everything — messages, payments, all interactions. Then they use AI to extrapolate your behavior and create incentives to guide and control behavior. They think this is progress because we're helping you be a better person — lose weight, avoid drugs, work harder.' The Western convergence: digital identity systems (eIDAS in Europe, ID.me in US for IRS access), real-time payment systems (FedNow, UPI, PIX), proposals for central bank digital currencies (digital euro, digital dollar exploration), and AI-driven AML/fraud detection that combines transaction analysis with identity verification. The framing differs (financial inclusion, fraud prevention, terrorism finance) but the architecture converges. The general principle: the question isn't whether AI surveillance state arrives — it's HOW it arrives. The Chinese model is overt and explicit. The Western model is gradual, opt-in-feeling, and framed as services rather than control. The control mechanisms are structurally similar regardless. The corollary for individuals: assume any transaction or interaction routed through identified systems will be permanently recorded and potentially used for behavior prediction. The space of pseudonymous/cash-based interaction is structurally shrinking. The corollary for civil-liberties advocates: the framing battle is more important than the technology battle. Once digital ID + digital currency are deployed (regardless of stated purpose), they can be repurposed. The implementation details that matter: (a) cryptographic separation of identity from transaction history, (b) limits on data retention, (c) procedural protections on government access, (d) prohibition on transaction-conditional restrictions. Battles for these specifics matter more than ideological positioning on AI generally. The corollary for entrepreneurs: there's a market for tools that preserve privacy/autonomy within the AI-surveillance-state framework — cryptographic identity, private compute, end-to-end encryption, jurisdictional arbitrage. These are increasing-value products in proportion to surveillance-state buildout. The corollary for businesses operating across jurisdictions: each major jurisdiction will have somewhat different AI-surveillance-state implementations. Multi-jurisdictional businesses need to navigate variable compliance regimes. The trend is convergence, but the path varies.
