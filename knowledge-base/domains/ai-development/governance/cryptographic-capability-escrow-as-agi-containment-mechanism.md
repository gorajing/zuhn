As AI systems approach recursive self-improvement capabilities, software-based guardrails face a fundamental problem: a sufficiently capable system could potentially modify its own constraints. Cryptographic capability escrow addresses this by embedding restrictions at the hardware level — requiring multi-party cryptographic keys from independent oversight groups to unlock substrate-level editing or major recursive enhancements.

This approach draws on proven patterns from nuclear launch protocols and financial cryptography, where no single party can unilaterally authorize critical actions. The governance architecture would include immutable logging of all substrate-level modifications, federated oversight with multiple independent approval requirements, and international treaty frameworks analogous to nuclear nonproliferation. While still speculative for AGI specifically, the underlying cryptographic primitives exist today. The key insight is that containment of recursive self-improvement requires constraints the system cannot modify from within — which means hardware-level cryptographic enforcement, not just software-level rules.
