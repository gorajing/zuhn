The AI Index 2026 Chapter 6 reports a governance gap in medical AI that structurally cannot be closed without a regulatory redesign. The FDA authorized 258 AI medical devices in 2025, most through pathways that do not require new clinical trials. The vast majority entered the market via safety-modification pathways that rely on existing safety and efficacy evidence rather than new randomized trials. Only 2.4% of devices had clinical studies supported by randomized trial data. A review of more than 500 clinical AI studies found that nearly half relied on exam-style questions rather than real patient data, with only 5% using real clinical data. The consequence is that FDA authorization of an AI medical device tells a buyer very little about whether the device actually works in their patient population. Procurement teams and hospital safety committees need to run their own trial equivalents, because the regulatory signal is vacuous for purposes of actual clinical decision-making. This is the inverse of the capability-governance gap in general-purpose AI — here capability may be sufficient but evidence production is absent.
