Victor Shih's reading of Ding Xuexiang's Davos speech — 'we need to invest in AI, but we can't go all out without knowing what the brakes are' — reveals the defining asymmetry between Chinese and American AI strategy. In the US, the private sector races toward AGI with minimal state oversight. In China, AGI sits under the Central Commission on Cybersecurity (headed nominally by Xi, day-to-day by Ding Xuexiang), and every major internet company, including DeepSeek, has an embedded team capable of halting the system if it generates content threatening to Party legitimacy (e.g., Falun Gong material proliferating beyond censor capacity).

This constraint is not a bolt-on: it shapes what AGI in China can become. Shih predicts institutional development will happen 'at the lower end' — designated human overseers placed inside every AI deployment across government and commerce — rather than a dedicated top-level AGI Leading Small Group. The practical implication is that China may be willing to accept a capability deficit relative to frontier US labs as long as controllability is preserved.

For anyone modeling the AGI race, this means the binding constraint on Chinese AI isn't GPUs or talent — it's the Party's unwillingness to let any system operate beyond its ability to shut it down. That constraint may cap Chinese AI at a level where human review of every generated artifact remains feasible.
