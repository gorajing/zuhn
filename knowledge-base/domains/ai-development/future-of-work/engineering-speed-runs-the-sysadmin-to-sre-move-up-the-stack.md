Scanlan grounds the disruption in lived precedent. As a former Unix sysadmin, he racked servers, cabled machines, and configured networks in data centers. Cloud came along and he 'moved up the stack' — sysadmins became SREs, the work shifted toward automation, and it became more impactful and higher-paid. He frames the AI transition as the same move, 'speed-running this 100 times faster on a full-industry scale.'

The operative principle for engineers: your job moves up the stack toward being a product builder who drives agents 'less and less' while moving higher up the food chain. Intercom encodes this in expectations — not adopting AI is explicitly 'not meeting expectations,' binary — and in a maturity ladder where engineers progress from using the agent for everything, to automating their work, to turning that into skills, to writing and improving skills, to optimizing the whole environment (architecture, docs) for agents. The reassurance for anxious engineers: this is a familiar transition, not an unprecedented threat — the abstraction layer rises, it doesn't disappear.
