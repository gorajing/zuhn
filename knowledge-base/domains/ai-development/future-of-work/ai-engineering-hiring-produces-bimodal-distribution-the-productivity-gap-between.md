Lewig's hiring observation: candidates split clearly into two groups. Group A 'really gets it' — they've invested the hours needed to learn AI tools (Claude Code, Cursor, agent orchestration) and are dramatically more productive. Group B hasn't invested. The productivity gap between the two is enormous and growing. Applied's interview process now selects explicitly for Group A — interviews are 'way harder than they've ever been' but candidates can use AI tools selectively to solve problems, which surfaces who actually knows how to use them well.

The operational implication for hiring managers: 'years of experience' has become a misleading signal. A 5-year-experience engineer who hasn't adapted is less productive than a 1-year-experience engineer who has. The new selection criteria: ability to ask the right questions, ability to tie together multiple AI tools, ability to validate AI output, and willingness to invest hours mastering each new tool generation.

For builders: redesign your interviews to surface AI fluency, not memorized algorithms. Ship a real sub-task with optional AI tool use; observe what they reach for and how skillfully they use it. For Zuhn editorially: this is the most concrete current data point on the 10x-engineer-becomes-1000x-engineer thesis (from Karpathy and Hassabis) — it's actually 1000x productivity gap manifesting in hiring outcomes at frontier engineering organizations.
