Noah Smith identifies three conditions under which keeping humans in the loop becomes un-economical: plentiful data, verifiable outputs, and no physical bottlenecks. Software development was the first major domain to meet all three, and biology research is rapidly approaching the same threshold. The key insight is that this isn't a choice any individual company makes—it's an emergent property of market competition. Any firm that maintains costly human oversight will be outcompeted by one that doesn't.

This frames AI safety as fundamentally an economic coordination problem, not a technical one. The parallel to overoptimized supply chains is precise: the same forces that drove just-in-time efficiency (eliminating costly inventory buffers) will drive human-out-of-the-loop automation (eliminating costly human oversight). COVID showed that efficiency-fragility tradeoffs are invisible until they catastrophically aren't. The question is whether we can build safety buffers before the next shock reveals how fragile our AI-dependent systems have become.
