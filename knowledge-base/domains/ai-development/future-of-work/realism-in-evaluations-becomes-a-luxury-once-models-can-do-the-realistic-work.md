Hume's original take-home worked precisely because it resembled real performance engineering work at Anthropic—optimizing kernels on a simulated accelerator with the same tooling patterns used on Trainium. That's the standard advice for interview design: make it look like the job. But by Opus 4.5, 'the job' was something Claude could do faster than most humans, which meant a realistic test rewarded candidates who simply watched Claude work.

His replacement went the opposite direction: Zachtronics-style puzzles that no performance engineer would ever actually write in production. He explicitly notes 'realism may be a luxury we no longer have. The original worked because it resembled real work. The replacement works because it simulates novel work.' This is a meaningful reframing of what evaluation design even means in the AI era.

The deeper implication: for tasks where models are competent, there is no longer a zero-sum relationship between job-likeness and discriminating power. Evaluations will increasingly need to measure orthogonal capacities—judgment, novel reasoning, taste—through proxies that don't look like the day-to-day work at all.
