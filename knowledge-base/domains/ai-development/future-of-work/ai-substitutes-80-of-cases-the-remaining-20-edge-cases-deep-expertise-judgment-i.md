Manuel Corpas's framing from his lecture: 'It will never be able to substitute human intelligence. It will substitute maybe 80% of the cases, but that will be for those people who decide not to go deeper and not to really explore the edge cases. You have to think about artificial intelligence as alien intelligence — powerful, but it's not going to think exactly the same. Rather than substituting it, you drive it like a conductor in an orchestra.' The structural argument: current LLMs are trained on the bulk of internet text, biomedical literature, code, and other human-generated content. They're excellent at the bulk distribution — common cases, well-documented procedures, standard analyses. Their performance falls off sharply on edge cases, novel combinations, and situations not well-represented in training data. The same falloff applies to all knowledge work. Implication 1 — for individuals: stop competing on the 80% (writing standard memos, doing standard analyses, generating standard code). Compete on the 20% — finding the edge cases nobody else sees, applying judgment about which model output to trust, integrating cross-domain expertise the model lacks. Implication 2 — for organizations: don't measure people on output volume (the 80% AI can now do faster). Measure them on judgment quality and ability to catch what AI misses. Implication 3 — for AI architecture: the 'verifiable filter' is the human's responsibility. Manuel's rule: 'It is your responsibility that whatever you produce, you are able to verify it. Because the lazy way will be caught very easily.' Anyone publishing AI output without verification is doing the work AI itself already did — they add no value and will get caught. The corollary: people most threatened by AI are mid-skill knowledge workers who compete in the 80% lane. People least threatened are deep specialists who operate in the 20% lane AND now have AI as a force-multiplier.
