Sholto's framing: think of the next several years as your individual leverage increasing by a large factor each year. Software engineers already report being 2-5x faster; that curve continues as the work mode shifts from 'AI is pairing with me' to 'I'm managing a small team of agents' to 'I'm running a division.' In most of the plausible trajectory, an individual will command resources that today require an entire company, so the binding constraint becomes taste, technical depth, and the ability to allocate and verify AI labor — not hand-coding.

This sharpens what education should emphasize: avoid subjects that prioritize rote memorization of facts; favor ways of thinking, mental models, and proximity to the frontier where real problems are visible. Dwarkesh reinforces: put yourself close to the frontier, because from there the problems are 'remarkably obvious' and you have a much better vantage point to act on. Use AI tools constantly so you build a calibrated sense of where they are and aren't good.

The practical instruction for someone at the start of their career: pick a technical domain with real fundamentals, learn it AI-natively, and aim to be the person capable of directing large amounts of machine labor toward targeted outcomes, because the economy is big, complex, and full of data-sparse contexts where human context still wins.
