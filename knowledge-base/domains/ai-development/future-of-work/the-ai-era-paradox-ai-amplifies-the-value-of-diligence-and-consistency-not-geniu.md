Shin Soo-jung's reframe of the standard 'AI replaces workers' anxiety: 'It is no longer genius that is needed; we have now entered an era where the power of consistency is required. Because AI needs to be able to receive consistent feedback, diligence has become even more important.' The mechanism: AI-amplified workflows aren't fully automated — they're a tight feedback loop between human and machine where the human curates inputs, validates outputs, identifies failure modes, refines prompts, and iterates. The marginal value of any single iteration is small, but the compounded value over months of daily iteration is enormous. The person who shows up to that loop 5 days a week for a year and tries 250 different prompt structures will outperform the person who shows up sporadically with a 'better idea.' This is the inverse of how genius dominated previous eras: when capability was scarce, a single brilliant insight could dominate. When capability is abundant (AI provides it), the scarce resource is the disciplined application of capability, which is diligence-shaped, not genius-shaped. Shin's term '엉덩이의 힘' (literally 'the power of the buttocks' — the ability to sit and work consistently) is the right Korean idiom for what's now scarce. The corollary for hiring: the AI era should make 'consistent, sincere, hardworking, learning-oriented' people more valuable, not less, even though intuition says the opposite.
