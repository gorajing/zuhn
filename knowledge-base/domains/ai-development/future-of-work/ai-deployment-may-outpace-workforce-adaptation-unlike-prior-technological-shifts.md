Dimon makes a nuanced argument: AI will definitely eliminate some jobs while creating many others (including in cybersecurity and AI itself), and there is already a workforce shortage for many well-paying white- and blue-collar jobs. However, the critical difference from prior transformations like electricity, the internet, or automobiles is speed. Those technologies 'took decades to roll out, but this implementation looks likely to accelerate over the next few years.'

The specific risk is a timing mismatch: 'In prior technological transformations, labor had time to adjust and retrain.' AI deployment may move faster than workforce adaptation. JPMorgan itself plans to have 'definitive plans on how we can support and redeploy our affected workforce,' suggesting even the most sophisticated employers recognize this isn't a self-correcting problem.

Dimon also emphasizes the unpredictability of second- and third-order effects, drawing analogies to how cars brought suburbs and shopping malls, agriculture enabled cities, and the internet (invented 1969) led to mobile phones and social media. The implication is that the most important consequences of AI are ones we cannot currently predict.
