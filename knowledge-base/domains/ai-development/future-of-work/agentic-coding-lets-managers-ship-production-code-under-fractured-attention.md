Shipper's 'favorite but also horror' observation is that managers — even the CEO — can now commit production code. The enabling shift is that AI lets engineers work with fractured attention: previously shipping anything required a 3-4 hour block of deep focus, but with a coding agent you can step out of a meeting, say 'investigate this bug,' go do something else, and return to a plan or root-cause fix you can turn into a PR. He stresses it is 'not magic' and 'not easy,' but it is genuinely possible, and it rewrites the maker-schedule/manager-schedule distinction that has long forced leaders out of the codebase.

The deeper point is that the deep-work block — the scarce resource that historically separated who could build from who could only manage — gets partially externalized to the agent, which holds context and does the focused execution asynchronously. This expands who can contribute (technical leaders re-enter the code) but carries the 'horror' Shipper names for his team: leaders dropping into the codebase can disrupt ownership and quality if the same plan/assess/codify discipline isn't applied. It is best read as a real expansion of contribution surface that must be paired with guardrails, not an unqualified good.
