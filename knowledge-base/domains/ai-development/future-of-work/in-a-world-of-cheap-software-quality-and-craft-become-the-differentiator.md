Before coding agents, nobody had enough time to do user research, consider design details, or think through architecture implications — implementation consumed most of the available effort, so teams shipped software they weren't proud of. Agents give that time back. The temptation is to spend it going faster and building a giant pile of the same mediocre software, but Appleton argues the real opportunity is to invest reclaimed time in rigorous critical thinking, deeper exploration, and stronger alignment during planning.

The strategic claim is that in a world of fast, cheap software, quality becomes the new differentiator: craftsmanship is what separates a product from vibe-coded slop, and the bar is rising. But craft still costs time and energy — it isn't free. To afford it you have to do fewer things better, which in turn demands strong alignment so the few things you pick are the right ones. The danger is the opposite: it's now trivially easy to prompt your way to the wrong thing and bolt on unnecessary features, so disciplined focus matters more than ever.
