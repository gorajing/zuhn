Levie's anecdote from a recent customer conversation: a large company famously incentivizes AI adoption by counting employee token consumption. The result: employees ('me and my co-workers') have agents do useless tasks just to hit the number. This is fake productivity producing potentially-problematic artifacts because the use is performative rather than productive.

The broader pattern: companies struggling to measure AI value default to measuring AI activity, which is much easier to count. But activity is a leading indicator of either value or theater, depending on culture and incentive design. The right metrics are downstream: did the work product improve? Did cycle times shorten? Did customer outcomes change? Activity-based metrics fail because employees optimize for the metric, not the underlying goal. For Zuhn editorially: this is the operational form of the Issue 2 thesis — when measurement frameworks lag behind the technology, organizations either over-credit or under-credit AI. The token-counting case is over-crediting; the lack-of-engagement-tracking-in-Salesforce case (from the Cursor episode) is under-crediting. Both are symptoms of the same disease: legacy KPIs don't fit the new substrate.
