Musk's framing — that AI and robotics make working 'optional' rather than impossible — is a precise and underappreciated distinction. It implies the economy doesn't collapse; it reorganizes. If time becomes abundant and goods become cheap, demand shifts toward things that help people create, learn, express, or connect — not things that save them time at work.

For founders, this is an orientation test: are you building a product that makes sense when most labor is automated, or are you optimizing for current friction? The highest-value products in an abundance economy will be those that help people do things they actually want to do — make music, raise children, understand the world — not those that shave hours off enterprise workflows.

This also implies that the most durable companies will be those that compound usefulness in domains humans care about intrinsically, not extrinsically.
