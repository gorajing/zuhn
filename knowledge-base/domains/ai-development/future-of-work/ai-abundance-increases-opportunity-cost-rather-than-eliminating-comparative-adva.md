A common objection to the comparative advantage argument is that compute will become so cheap and abundant that the constraint becomes irrelevant. This fundamentally misunderstands opportunity cost. More abundant AI produces more total value, which increases demand, which raises the opportunity cost of using AI for anything other than its most productive application.

This creates a self-reinforcing cycle: abundance → more value → more demand → higher opportunity cost → more tasks where humans are the rational choice. There is no number large enough to drown comparative advantage because the denominator (what AI could be doing instead) scales with the numerator (how much AI exists). This is why naming bigger and bigger compute numbers doesn't defeat the argument.

The formal economics model by Korinek and Suh confirms this: human wages never go to zero under AGI. They may drop sharply when AI takes the last exclusively-human task, but comparative advantage provides a permanent floor.
