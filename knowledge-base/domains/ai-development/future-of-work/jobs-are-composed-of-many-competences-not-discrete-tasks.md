Ege Erdil notes that popular automation estimates hinge on a wrong mental model: 'travel agents book flights, therefore automating flight-booking automates travel agents.' But the fraction of a travel agent's time spent on flight-booking is small; most of the work is client communication, problem-solving, negotiation, and handling exceptions. Even if AI can book flights perfectly by end of 2026, travel agent jobs don't disappear.

This is a key source of timeline disagreement between the Mechanize view (~2045) and San Francisco consensus (~2027). Bullish forecasters implicitly assume jobs are much simpler than they are, so they think fewer capability unlocks are needed for full automation. If jobs are bundles of dozens of loosely related competences, you need to unlock each of them, not just the most visible one.

For anyone reasoning about AI and labor: before claiming a role will be automated, enumerate the actual time allocation across tasks. Most 'automation' of a headline task leaves 70-90% of the job untouched.
