The keynote's careful distinction: AI automates knowledge work — the tasks — not knowledge workers — the people. Assume ~90% of today's tasks (whether you're a leader, manager, or IC) get automated. That doesn't mean the roles or jobs vanish; it means the nature of the work changes. The traditional 'individual contributor' job — heads-down execution of a task — disappears, because every IC is now directing AI and agents to do the task. By definition, everyone becomes a manager (of agents), even if the title/pay-grade 'IC' persists. He calls this the 'gold collar' era.

The corollary on capacity (the 'intensity' point): because agents don't sleep and you can run many in parallel (5, 50), individual output is no longer bound by the 24/7 ceiling — your throughput is set by how intensely and how well you direct agents, not by hours worked. So 'IC' increasingly means 'orchestrator of a small agent fleet.'

The skills this elevates: founder-mentality/agency (thinking across multiple functions, not one), curiosity, and especially critical thinking — because the parts that DON'T require critical thinking are exactly what AI now does, so the human value concentrates in the judgment AI can't supply. He's explicit that critical thinking was always rewarded, but the room for employees who lack it shrinks sharply going forward. And the most durable human job, if AI can do 'everything,' is evaluation — judging the AI's output: the design, the pace, whether it fulfills the need, whether there's business impact.

The self-directed takeaway: to stay competitive, treat yourself as a manager of agents now, and deliberately build the skills (agency, critical thinking, evaluation/taste) that survive task-automation — not the execution skills that don't. This is the role-level companion to the structural org-collapse insight.
