The conventional wisdom held that AI would first replace low-skilled physical labor, then low-skilled white-collar work, then high-skilled programming, and finally creative jobs. The actual sequence was reversed: copywriters, designers, customer service, legal drafting, and medical diagnosis were disrupted before warehouse workers or construction crews.

This inversion matters because it reveals a fundamental misunderstanding of what AI is good at. AI excels at pattern matching across massive datasets — which is exactly what creative and knowledge work requires. Physical manipulation in unstructured environments remains far harder for AI than generating text, images, or analysis. Career planning and workforce policy built on the old assumption are dangerously wrong.
