Huang rejects the standard 'AI will replace jobs' narrative with a specific mechanism: the first-order effect of productivity is prosperity, and prosperous companies hire more to expand into new areas. A company only shrinks its headcount after productivity gains if it has no more ideas — but most companies have many more ideas than they can resource. 'So long as there are more ideas in drug discovery, more ideas in transportation, more ideas in retail, more ideas in entertainment — the prosperity from productivity results in hiring more people.'

This echoes what Huang calls the 'Moritz corollary' to Moore's law: as compute gets cheaper and reaches more areas of the economy, addressable markets grow, not shrink. The falsifiable claim: AI-driven productivity gains will correlate with net job creation in the aggregate, even as specific workers lose jobs (often to other humans using AI, not to AI directly).

The actionable corollary for individuals: 'learn how to use AI.' The threat isn't losing your job to an AI — it's losing your job to someone who uses AI. The threat for companies is the symmetric version: not augmenting productivity means a competitor will hire more people, not fewer, while you stagnate.
