Appleton coins 'barefoot developers' by analogy to Mao's barefoot doctors. The category sits between two existing poles: regular 'end users of computers' who 'don't give a shit about programming and don't want to learn it' (Appleton argues we should stop harassing nurses and teachers to code) and professional developers who 'love writing code for its own sake.' The missing middle is people who are technically savvy and interested in solving problems for themselves and people around them, but don't want to become fully-fledged programmers. They currently live in Notion/Airtable/Excel. With the right tools, they could build the long-tail software that VC-backed companies will never build — simple CRUD apps with basic auth and a few API calls, solving very specific local problems. Appleton expects this population to grow exponentially faster than professional developers. Important because the current software industry is structured entirely around professional developers; a 10x or 100x expansion of who can build software changes which problems get solved, by whom, and for whose benefit.
