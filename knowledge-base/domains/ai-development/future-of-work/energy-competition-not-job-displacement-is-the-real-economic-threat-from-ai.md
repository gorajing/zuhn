The horse analogy reveals the true danger: horses didn't become obsolete because cars had comparative advantage — they were sent to glue factories because their upkeep competed for scarce urban land and human labor. The critical variable was a shared resource, not a producer-specific one. For humans, that shared resource is energy.

If AI becomes so valuable that its owners bid up energy prices astronomically, humans could be unable to afford fuel, electricity, manufactured goods, or food. This is the real dystopian scenario — not unemployment but resource starvation. Historical precedents exist: agribusiness consuming Colorado River water and cash crops exacerbating Indian famine show how market forces can allocate resources away from local populations toward distant wealth.

This reframes the policy question entirely. The debate shouldn't center on job retraining programs but on data center resource limits, energy allocation policy, and ensuring AI doesn't monopolize the inputs humans need to live. Some constraint on data center expansion — not necessarily Bernie Sanders' hard cap, but some form — may be the most important AI policy lever.
