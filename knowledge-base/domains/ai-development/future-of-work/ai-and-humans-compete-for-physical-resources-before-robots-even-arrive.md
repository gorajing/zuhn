The dominant narrative around AI economic disruption focuses on labor displacement — robots taking jobs. But there's a more immediate and less discussed mechanism: AI infrastructure directly competing with humans for physical resources. Data centers are already consuming exponentially rising fractions of US electricity. As AI scales, hyperscalers will outbid farmers for land, outbid residential developers for real estate, and strain water supplies for cooling.

This reframes the abundance agenda. Simply producing more supply doesn't help if data centers absorb the new capacity. The policy implication is resource reservation — zoning and land use policies that explicitly set aside electricity, land, and water for human consumption. This is a subtler but potentially more important intervention than labor market policies, because it addresses cost-of-living impacts that hit everyone, not just displaced workers.
