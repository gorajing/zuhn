The reassuring historical narrative about technological displacement (agriculture mechanization freed farmers to become factory workers, automation freed factory workers to become service workers, services displacement frees workers to become knowledge workers) has a structural assumption: there is a higher capability tier available for displaced workers to move into. AI breaks that assumption because it operates at the top tier (intellectual labor) and expands into adjacent tiers. A call-center worker displaced by AI cannot move up to analyst work if AI does that better and cheaper. The historical pattern produces a displacement-then-redeployment dynamic that took decades and produced net-positive employment. AI may produce displacement without redeployment because the redeployment targets are also being automated. This is not definitive — speculative future tiers (human creativity, human-AI collaboration specialists, care work requiring physical presence, artisanal craft) may absorb some displaced workers. But the default assumption of 'we'll find new jobs for people' has weaker justification than in previous transitions. Policy responses (UBI, retraining, tax shifts to AI-operated capital) should be designed assuming redeployment may not naturally occur at the speed or scale required.
