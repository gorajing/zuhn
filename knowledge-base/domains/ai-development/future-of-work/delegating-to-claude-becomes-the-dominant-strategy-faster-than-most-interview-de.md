One of the more striking data points: as early as Claude 3.7 Sonnet, Hume estimated more than half of candidates should have been delegating to Claude Code rather than coding themselves. The 'test is broken' moment didn't arrive with Opus 4; it arrived much earlier, and for most candidates. Opus 4 just made it true for the top candidates too.

The reason matters: humans typically spend the first half of a 2-hour take-home reading and understanding the problem. A human trying to steer Claude is therefore constantly behind—only understanding what Claude did after the fact. The dominant strategy collapses toward passive delegation, which provides zero signal about the candidate's own skill.

For any interview that permits AI use, the practical question is whether the problem rewards steering (human-in-the-loop judgment) or delegation (hands-off execution). If it rewards delegation, the interview has already broken—the candidate who ignores the problem and watches the model wins. Hume rejected the 'ban AI' option because it mismatches the real job, but his experience shows that 'allow AI' only works if problem depth stays ahead of model capability.
