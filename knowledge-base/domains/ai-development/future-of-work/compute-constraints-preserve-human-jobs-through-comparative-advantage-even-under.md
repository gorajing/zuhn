The standard fear about AI and jobs conflates competitive advantage (who does it better) with comparative advantage (who does it better relative to their other options). Even if AI outperforms humans at every task, compute is a producer-specific constraint that forces hard allocation choices. When AI could generate $2000/hour as an engineer but only $1000/hour as a doctor, the opportunity cost makes human doctors economically rational despite being 5x worse.

This mirrors the classic example of a venture capitalist who types faster than his secretary but still hires one — his time is better spent on deals. The key insight is that comparative advantage is inescapable: everyone always has one at something, and it doesn't go away with more abundance. More AI means more value means higher opportunity costs means more room for humans.

Critically, this only holds if the constraint is truly producer-specific. Compute satisfies this because humans don't need compute to work, and Rock's Law shows compute production costs double every four years from bottlenecks beyond just energy.
