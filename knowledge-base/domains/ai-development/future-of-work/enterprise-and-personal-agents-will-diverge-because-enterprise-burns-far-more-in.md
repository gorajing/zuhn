Solmaz draws an analogy to personal computing: the machine you used at home and the one you used at work became fairly similar over time. He predicts agents will break that pattern. At work, you will consume dramatically more inference than at home — running fleets of disposable agents on real tasks — which means the economics of enterprise and personal agents diverge rather than converge.

The strategic implication he draws is that enterprise is where the money concentrates, which is why he's betting on enterprise agent orchestration (disposable agents on Kubernetes, Slack-driven concierge agents, error-triage dispatch). For anyone choosing where to build, the claim is that the high-inference, high-parallelism enterprise workload is a structurally larger and more defensible market than the personal-agent space, even though personal agents are where most early consumer attention sits. It's a time-sensitive bet contingent on inference costs and enterprise adoption curves playing out as predicted.
