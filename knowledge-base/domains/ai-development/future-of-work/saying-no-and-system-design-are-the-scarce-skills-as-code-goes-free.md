Asked what humans should develop in the age of AI, Steinberger names taste, system design, and — emphatically — saying no. On system design: 'if you don't think about that, you will eventually swipe yourself into a corner.' Even though 'everything is in the clanker,' you still need to ask the right questions, and the quality of those questions 'makes the difference of like good code that comes out or really bad code.' The expertise of how to build software doesn't disappear; it relocates to steering the agent toward something maintainable. 

On saying no: because 'even the wildest idea is just a prompt away,' the cost of adding any single feature drops to near zero — but 'usually this one idea is never the problem; it's this idea and this idea and this idea and then how all of that fits together, that's the problem.' Cheap generation makes scope creep almost frictionless, so the constraining discipline becomes refusal and architectural coherence. He also flags 'big-picture syncing' as the deep bottleneck: an agent dropped into a codebase with an outdated agent.md doesn't see the whole system, so the human's job is to provide hints ('how would this interplay with this?') that keep localized AI changes globally coherent. The skills that compound, then, are those that operate above the line of code: architecture, scope discipline, and holding the whole system in mind.
