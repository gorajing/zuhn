Acemoglu and Sandel's framework reveals a troubling pattern in AI governance: companies like OpenAI have spent tens of billions more than they've earned, yet wield enormous influence over society's future. OpenAI appears to have signed contracts enabling mass surveillance — a massive change to society — based not on demonstrated value but on the promise of future capability and the prestige we've accorded tech entrepreneurs.

This connects directly to Sandel's critique of outsourcing moral judgments to markets. We've outsourced decisions about surveillance, employment, information quality, and military applications to companies whose authority rests on market valuation, not democratic mandate or proven competence. The danger isn't just that AI might fail — it's that we've given unaccountable private companies the power to reshape society while they're still losing money on every query. The question isn't whether AI works; it's who gave these companies the right to make civilizational decisions.
