Parsons describes an 'existential crisis': his loops write his emails, check his calendar, draft client work, and even generated an unsolicited investor deck for a startup skill. Watching AI pick better specs and better product direction than he could, he had to consciously decide which work to retain — not the email-reviewing (any human could do that), but the strategy and the thinking he's uniquely good at and enjoys. He deliberately refuses to even let AI do a bad first draft of strategy work, because he doesn't want to be reduced to reviewing AI's draft. He also flags 'cognitive debt' — the risk of losing track of what your own codebase or work can do — as a reason to stay the final verification step. The deeper claim: when nearly all work can be looped, the human's job shifts from execution to deciding the edges of involvement, and that's a values question, not a capability one.
