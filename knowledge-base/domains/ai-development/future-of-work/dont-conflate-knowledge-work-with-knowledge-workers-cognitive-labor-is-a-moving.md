Nadella's key distinction: knowledge work (the tasks) is different from the knowledge worker (the role). Today's knowledge work — email triage, meeting notes, slide creation, first-draft analysis — will be automated. But the second an AI handles that layer, a new cognitive labor task appears at a higher abstraction: reviewing the agent's selections, setting priorities, supervising multi-agent workflows, deciding which of the surfaced exceptions matter.

This reframes the 'will AI take jobs?' question. The historical pattern: humans have only valued 'cognitive labor' as defined today for roughly 200 years. What counts as high-value labor gets continuously revalued as tools change. Commoditized knowledge labor drops in price; new categories (physical therapy, care work, judgment-heavy oversight of autonomous systems) may get revalued upward. The horse analogy fails because cognition, unlike locomotion, has many non-fungible dimensions — humans retain cognitive agency even when powerful cognitive machines exist.

The practical takeaway: when thinking about career positioning or workforce planning in an AI-heavy world, ask what abstraction level you're operating at and whether you can climb faster than the automation frontier. Betting on any specific cognitive task remaining the human-held layer is fragile; betting on the supervisory/orchestration layer above whatever the current frontier is will be more durable.
