The naive reasoning is: if computer science and math are getting automated, study something fuzzy instead. Geoff rejects this explicitly, saying he is 'doubling down' on getting really good at math and CS, because 'if you actually know underlying how these large language models are actually working, you actually can wield them and prompt them better.' The people leading AI companies are described as math champions, not people who outsourced their technical understanding to the tools.

The deeper point is that automation of a skill's execution does not automate the judgment required to deploy it well; comprehension migrates from a production skill to a direction-and-evaluation skill. This complements the broader 'AI-maxing vs looks-maxing' framing in the conversation — AI-maxing reduces to maximizing IQ (technical depth to wield intelligence) and EQ (charisma and relationships) — but the specific, actionable claim is that technical foundations become a force multiplier on AI tools rather than a stranded asset. For anyone deciding what to study or invest learning time in under AI, the directive is to go deeper into fundamentals, not retreat from them.
