Classical economic theory predicts that productivity gains from automation suppress labor demand — you produce the same output with fewer workers. The current U.S. data superficially fits this pattern: GDP growth is healthy, prime-age employment rates are still elevated from prior peaks, but job creation has essentially stalled. Many commentators are concluding AI is beginning to eliminate jobs.

However, the mechanism matters. If productivity is rising because data centers are running 24/7 and capital utilization is intensifying, that's a different dynamic than AI replacing individual workers. Capital deepening tends to eventually create demand for maintenance workers, operators, and new service industries. The ambiguity in the current signal — are we seeing the first flicker of broad AI displacement, or a one-time capital boom? — is precisely what makes this a high-stakes interpretive question for anyone planning workforce or product strategy over a 3-5 year horizon.
