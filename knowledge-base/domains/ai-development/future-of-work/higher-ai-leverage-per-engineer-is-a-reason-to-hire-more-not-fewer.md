The default corporate reading of AI productivity gains is substitution: each engineer does more, so we need fewer engineers. Zapier inverted this. Having used agents to make new engineers effective in two weeks (versus an industry-good benchmark of a month and a typical 90 days), they concluded that if a single engineer now produces more value, the organization should hire more aggressively rather than hold headcount flat and squeeze individuals. More leverage per worker raised the return on every additional hire, turning faster hiring into a competitive edge.

This reframes AI from a cost-cutting lever into a growth lever, and it rests on a stance Reock states bluntly: AI augments engineers, it does not replace them (SWE-bench agents autonomously complete roughly a third of tasks, leaving two-thirds requiring humans). 'AI is not coming for your job, but somebody really good at AI might take your job.' Whether substitution or expansion is correct is genuinely contested and depends on whether a firm's market lets it convert added engineering capacity into more output — but for product companies with unmet demand, the expansion logic is a defensible and underused strategic move.
