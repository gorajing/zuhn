A common claim is that AI turns engineers into engineering managers. Orosz, a former EM, rejects this: management's actual burdens — interpersonal conflict, career drama, becoming removed from the product, and waiting six months to see the results of a decision made across ten people — are exactly what agents remove. Orchestrating agents is closer to a tech-lead or senior-mentor role, or DHH's 'mech suit' framing: you can do seven things at once, faster, while staying in control of the product.

The load-bearing distinction is the feedback loop. Human management decisions resolve over months; agent orchestration resolves in minutes, which changes the cognitive character of the work from delegation-and-wait to tight iterative steering. The orchestration patterns themselves remain personal and unsettled — some operators run many parallel agents, while practitioners like Mitchell Hashimoto deliberately keep just one or two ('two is enough for me'). The takeaway for role design: frame agent work as amplified individual contribution with fast iteration, not as a forced transition into people management — the latter mischaracterizes both the appeal and the actual skill required.
