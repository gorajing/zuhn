Shipper names the practice 'compounding engineering': in traditional engineering each new feature adds complexity that makes the next feature harder, whereas the goal here is to make each feature make the next easier. The loop has four steps — plan (write a detailed plan for the agent), delegate (hand it to the agent), assess (tests, manual trials, code review, agent review), and codify. Codify is 'the money step': you take the tacit knowledge engineers accumulate while planning, fixing bugs, and reviewing, and turn it into explicit prompts that live in CLAUDE.md files, sub-agents, and slash commands, then propagate that library across the organization.

The leverage is that knowledge normally trapped in individual heads becomes a shared, executable asset. Second-order effects he reports: new hires are productive on day one because environment setup and 'what a good PR looks like' are already encoded; expert freelancers can drop in for a day like a DJ playing a few bars; and tacit code-sharing across products gets cheap. The discipline that makes it work is treating codification as a first-class step rather than an afterthought — most teams do plan/delegate/assess but skip the compounding step that turns one-time learning into org-wide capability.
