The popular framing of AI engineering treats it as a software engineering specialization. Masad reframes it as model psychology: the highest-value skill is the rapid first-impression evaluation of a new model — what does it do well, what does it fumble, where does it lose coherence, what prompting style fits its training. This skill is largely tacit; it doesn't transfer through documentation; it accumulates through repeated exposure to many models in development. Replit treats this as core IP of its 'agent lab' and credits it for being able to ship state-of-the-art products on new models faster than the model labs themselves can. The career implication for engineers entering AI work: invest in the practice of evaluating models, not just integrating them. Build a personal library of test prompts and edge cases that surface model character quickly. The hiring implication for managers: an AI engineer who has hands-on experience evaluating 20+ models is dramatically more valuable than one who has integrated 1 model deeply. The systemic implication for AI labs: the tacit-knowledge gap between agent-lab teams and outside developers compounds — published benchmarks miss the kind of model-character knowledge that makes the difference between a working product and a brittle one.
