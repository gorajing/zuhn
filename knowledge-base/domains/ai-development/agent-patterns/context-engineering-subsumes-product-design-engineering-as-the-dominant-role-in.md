Tobi Lütke's prediction on the role that doesn't exist yet but will dominate in 5 years: 'There's something about context engineering which I had a hand in popularizing the term of. I think that will become more recognizably a role in companies that will subsume a bunch of other more specialized roles right now. People who have done engineer management and management generally are actually excellent AI programmers because they have been prompting intelligent agents for much longer than Claude Code existed. They are just good at communicating. So increasingly their best work is to prompt other people and AIs directly as sort of part of the same team. There's going to be some ascension path job that you can get into from product, from design, or from engineering — that's like a product builder role that involves the coordination of intelligent actors to build products. Good communicators are usually good thinkers because good communication is distillation of complex thoughts into unambiguous words. Even saying that, it sounds like context engineering.' The key insight: the meta-skill of 'prompting intelligent agents' has been called 'engineering management' for the past 20 years. The skill transfers directly to AI agents because both require: (a) Distilling complex intent into unambiguous specification. (b) Verifying that the agent's understanding matches yours. (c) Course-correcting when the agent's trajectory drifts. (d) Coordinating multiple agents toward a shared goal. (e) Managing context boundaries so each agent has what they need but not more. The surprise: engineering managers (often dismissed as 'no longer technical' or 'pushing process') turn out to have been training exactly the right skill all along. Their career path WIDENS, not narrows, as AI agents become common. The corollary for engineering managers: don't transition to IC roles to 'stay technical' as your default reflex. The management skill you've been building is the differentiated capability of the next decade. Lean into directing AI agents in addition to humans. The corollary for engineers worried about AI replacement: the path forward isn't 'become an even better engineer' (the IC ladder narrows). The path forward is upward into context engineering — coordinating, specifying, validating across multiple agents. The corollary for company structure: titles like 'staff engineer' and 'senior engineer' will increasingly mean 'context engineer who happens to know engineering' rather than 'individual contributor who happens to mentor.' Org charts will shift to coordinator-heavy structures with fewer pure IC slots. The corollary for hiring: when interviewing for senior technical roles, the right test isn't 'can you write this algorithm' but 'walk me through how you would direct a team of 3 AI agents to deliver this feature in 48 hours' — the answer reveals whether the candidate has internalized context engineering or is still in IC mode.
