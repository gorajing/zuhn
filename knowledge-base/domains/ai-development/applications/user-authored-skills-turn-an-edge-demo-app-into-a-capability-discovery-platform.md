Google's Gallery app is positioned not as a product but as a playground: every capability (audio scribe, ask-image, Wikipedia query, mood journaling, photo-to-music) ships with sample code, the whole app is open source and forkable, and users author their own skills inside the app and share them via a GitHub repo. The explicit goal is 'to inspire and motivate you to build your own experiences' — adoption through hands-on capability discovery rather than a fixed feature set.

The broader lesson, applicable beyond edge AI, is that for a new and unfamiliar capability surface, a forkable reference app with low-friction extensibility (write a skill without leaving the app) is a strong adoption mechanism. It lets developers calibrate what is actually possible by building small things, and the community-shared skills become a living catalog of use cases the vendor didn't have to imagine. Confidence is low because this is an asserted strategy whose adoption payoff isn't demonstrated with data in the talk.
