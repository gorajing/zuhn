Sandy Lee's hard-earned method for preventing AI slop in voice-driven content: don't start with a clever prompt; start with deep personal context. Her exact protocol: (1) Grab pen and paper, go to a quiet place. The physical writing matters — 'there's a connection with brain and handwriting' that surfaces deeper material than typing. (2) Work through the Ikigai framework (Japanese well-being concept, recently validated by Harvard Business School study): What do you love? What are you good at? What does the world need? What is the world willing to pay you for? (3) For each answer, go back 10-20 years and write concrete historical examples — real scenarios that happened, decisions you made, things that moved you. NOT what you do now in your job. (4) Now transcribe ALL of that to Claude as personal context. Use Whisper Flow if you prefer speaking. The interview specifically asks: 'Should I be detailed or brief?' Sandy: 'AS DETAILED AS POSSIBLE. The more you teach AI who you are, the better it's going to write in your style.' Why this matters: AI's default output is the centroid of its training corpus — generic, fluent, sloppy. To produce content that sounds like YOU, the AI needs more bits of context about YOU than the training centroid contains. Most personal-branding-AI failures aren't model failures — they're context starvation. The interviewer (Callum) confirms the same pattern: 'When I see AI slop on LinkedIn, it's not because the AI is bad. It's because the person gave it 50 words about themselves.' The corollary for any AI workflow with voice/style requirements: invest in upfront context, not iteration. 30 minutes of dense personal context produces content quality that 30 prompts of clever rewording can't match. This generalizes far beyond content: any AI agent representing you (email drafts, customer responses, code style, design choices) benefits from deeper upfront context.
