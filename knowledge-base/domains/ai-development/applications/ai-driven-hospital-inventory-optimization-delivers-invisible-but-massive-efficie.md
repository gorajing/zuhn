The pharmacy director described implementing an AI system to manage movement of 2,000 medical devices across 50 hospital locations, replacing manual report-based tracking. The algorithm reduced travel time for nurses retrieving devices by 50%. Notably, staff didn't consciously register the change — they just experienced less frustration and more productive time.

This exemplifies the 'invisible AI' pattern where the highest-impact enterprise AI applications are often unsexy logistics optimizations that remove friction rather than adding flashy new capabilities. The invisibility is actually a feature: workflow improvements that don't require behavior change face zero adoption resistance. The lesson for health-tech builders is that back-office operations optimization may be a faster path to hospital adoption than clinical decision support.
