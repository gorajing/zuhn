Masad's example pattern: a physical therapist with deep knowledge of fascia release built a sophisticated tracking app with body-scan and 3D range-of-motion features after spending hundreds of thousands of dollars on offshore developers and being unsatisfied. When she vibe-coded it herself on Replit, the result was 'one of the best health tech apps I've ever seen.' Same pattern: a mom in Korea building software to manage her child's rare condition; a pool-maintenance family business building vertical SaaS; a sports-club founder building software to replace MS-DOS legacy.

The structural reframe: when code is free, the binding constraint shifts entirely to domain understanding. The developer who doesn't understand fascia can write working software but can't decide what to build; the therapist who understands fascia knows what to build but couldn't write code — until now. The Silicon Valley blind spot is that vast swaths of the economy run on legacy software written by contractors who don't understand the domain. As vibe coding spreads, those domains get rewritten by the people who understand them.

For Zuhn editorially: this is the operational form of 'long-tail of vertical software gets unlocked.' The investment thesis follows: the next decade's verticalSaaS isn't built by SF technologists going horizontal-then-finding-vertical, it's built by domain experts going domain-then-becoming-technical. The deal flow looks completely different — most of these builders won't go through YC or even raise venture; they'll bootstrap to millions in revenue running their own domain businesses.
