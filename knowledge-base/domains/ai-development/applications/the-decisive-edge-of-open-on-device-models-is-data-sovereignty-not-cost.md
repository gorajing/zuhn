When Sanseviero enumerates where open models shine, the through-line is not price but control: offline use where there is no connectivity, 'offline use cases where you don't want your data to leave your servers,' on-device screen understanding via a Chrome extension, and sovereign-AI efforts where governments (India's Sarvam, AI Singapore) train national models on open bases. Specialized variants like ShieldGemma (safety/moderation) and MedGemma (radiology, chest X-ray) extend this into regulated verticals where sending data to a third-party API is often a non-starter.

The strategic reframe is that on-device open models compete on a different axis than frontier APIs. The honest division of labor the speaker draws is: use an API model like Gemini when you need the most raw intelligence, and use open on-device models when the constraint is sovereignty, privacy, latency, or offline availability. These are not the same customers trading quality for cost — they are workloads that an API fundamentally cannot capture at any price because the data or the connectivity precludes it.

For builders and investors, this argues for siting the value of open models in privacy-, regulation-, and sovereignty-bound markets rather than positioning them as a cheaper substitute for frontier APIs. The cancer-therapy-pathway result validated in a real lab shows the ceiling on these workloads is high, and the moat is structural: the data physically stays home.
