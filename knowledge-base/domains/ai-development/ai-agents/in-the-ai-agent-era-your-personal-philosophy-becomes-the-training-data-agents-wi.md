The documentary converges on a counterintuitive conclusion: in the age of AI agents, the most important investment is not in technology but in developing your own depth as a human. Kim Moon-jeong (elementary school teacher and AI content creator) articulates it precisely: 'An individual's philosophy is embedded in this agent. The data and concerns, thoughts, and experiences of many great figures — wouldn't these be truly important? Even if that technology improves significantly, if I don't have the data to use, it is nothing more than a useless shell.'

The agent era doesn't diminish the need for human substance — it amplifies it. The agent is a proxy whose quality depends entirely on the principal's depth. Stuart Russell (UC Berkeley AI pioneer) reinforces this: we should have defined AI not as 'building intelligent agents that pursue their objectives' but as 'building systems whose objective is to help human beings in whatever it is that humans want the future to be.' The human's job is to know what they want — and that requires philosophy, reading, experience, and sustained reflection on what matters.
