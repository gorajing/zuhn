A seismic shift has occurred inside software: the transition from co-pilots (suggestion and speed) to agents (autonomous outcomes). In the old model, AI features sat on top of existing workflows — autocomplete, summarize, recommend. In the new model, agents own the workflow itself: they read the brief, execute the task, and report completion. A legal co-pilot drafts a clause; a legal agent files the document.

This transition fundamentally changes how value is created, priced, and defended. Products that don't automate the outcome are now treated as features, not investable companies. The revenue model shifts accordingly — from seat-based pricing (monetizing access) to outcome-based pricing (monetizing results), tapping into services and labor budgets rather than just software budgets. However, today's 'automation arbitrage' of charging human-equivalent rates won't last as competition normalizes prices toward marginal compute costs. The durable winners will sustain margins through data leverage and workflow ownership.
