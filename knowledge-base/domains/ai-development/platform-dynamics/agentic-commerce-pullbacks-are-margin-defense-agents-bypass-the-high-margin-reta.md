The headlines show a contradiction: rapid agentic-commerce innovation (OpenAI × shopping platforms, Google, Meta) running alongside equally rapid pullbacks (Amazon blocking agents, Walmart rolling back its six-month OpenAI instant-checkout pilot). The naive read is 'the tech isn't ready.' The follow-the-money read is sharper: retail e-commerce is a low-margin business that retailers subsidize with their high-margin retail-media (ads) business. Amazon's ads are ~10% of revenue but contribute ~40% of operating income.

When a customer discovers, compares, and checks out entirely inside an LLM chat — never visiting the retailer's site — the retailer loses the surface where it monetizes attention through ads, sponsored placement, and data capture. So the agent doesn't just disintermediate the storefront; it disintermediates the profit center. That's an existential threat to the unit economics, not a UX concern, which is why pullbacks happen at the same pace as innovation.

The diagnostic this gives you: to predict whether a platform will embrace or block agents, look at where its operating income (not revenue) comes from. If agents route around the high-margin layer, expect resistance regardless of how good the agent experience is. The likely equilibrium the speaker predicts: discovery/comparison happens in the LLM, but cart assembly and payment get pulled back onto the retailer's platform so the retailer retains the monetizable surface.

This is the agentic-commerce instance of a general platform principle: control follows margin, and platforms defend the layer that pays for the rest.
