Miller corrects a common misconception: Moore's Law (transistor density doubling every ~2 years) is not a law of nature. It is an economic prediction that holds only because each shrinkage generation unlocks markets large enough to justify the billions in R&D. If the economics break, the technology immediately stalls.

AI has become the latest demand driver sustaining Moore's Law. ML-optimized chips have doubled in capability every two years for the past decade — exactly matching Moore's original 1965 prediction. Meanwhile, attempting to catch up to the cutting edge means chasing a target that races forward at this exponential rate. The 3D stacking of transistors (moving beyond planar 2D designs) is the current frontier, enabling more computing power per unit of space as 2D shrinkage hits physical limits.
