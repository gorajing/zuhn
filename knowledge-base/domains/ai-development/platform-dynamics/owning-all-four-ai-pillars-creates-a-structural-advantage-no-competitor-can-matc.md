Mapping the AI landscape by capability reveals a stark asymmetry. Nvidia has chips only. Microsoft has cloud and application surface but no frontier model and no chips. Amazon has cloud only (with some early chip efforts). Meta has applications but exited the frontier model race. OpenAI and Anthropic have models but no chips, no data centers, and depend on external capital. Apple has essentially none of these pillars. Google is the only company with credible scale in all four: Gemini as frontier model, TPUs at roughly Nvidia-comparable unit volume, Google Cloud as distribution, and Google Search, YouTube, Gmail, Maps, Android as consumer applications.

This matters because AI economics punish dependency: every layer you don't own is a layer where someone else captures margin and dictates terms. Model-only companies are dependent on Nvidia allocation and cloud credits. Chip-only companies depend on hyperscalers for distribution. Cloud-only companies pay Nvidia's 80% margin to offer GPUs. Google's stack lets it amortize training costs across trillions of inference tokens it generates itself, pay commodity margins on its own hardware, and deploy AI features into products with billions of existing users. The strategic question is whether this full-stack integration translates into product wins or whether organizational friction across the layers cancels the advantage.
