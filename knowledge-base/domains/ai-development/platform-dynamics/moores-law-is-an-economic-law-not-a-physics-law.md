Moore's Law is commonly misunderstood as a physical law about transistor miniaturization. Miller reframes it as an economic law: if you find ways to shrink transistors, you find larger markets, which funds further shrinking. The moment this economic logic breaks, the technology immediately stalls.

The current AI boom is renewing the cycle — surging demand for compute is driving massive new investment in semiconductors, making continued shrinking economically viable. For AI-optimized chips specifically, capabilities have been doubling every two years for the past decade, exactly tracking Moore's original 1965 prediction. The industry is also shifting from 2D to 3D transistor stacking to maintain the pace. Reports of Moore's Law's death are premature when viewed through this economic lens.
