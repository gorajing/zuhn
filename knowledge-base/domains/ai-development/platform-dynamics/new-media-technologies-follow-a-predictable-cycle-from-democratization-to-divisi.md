Noah Smith identifies a recurring historical pattern: new media technologies open entry to divisive voices (Charles Coughlin on radio in the 1930s, Rush Limbaugh on talk radio, Sean Hannity on cable TV, Drudge Report on the web), until consolidating forces emerge. In the mid-20th century, local newspaper monopolies and limited broadcast spectrum created the gatekeeping that produced the 'Cronkite era' of centrist, shared-reality media. Each subsequent technology that lowered barriers to entry reignited the cycle.

Social media represented the most extreme democratization yet, supercharging the divisive-entrepreneur model by giving anyone a platform. Smith argues that platform management cannot serve as the consolidating force — Bluesky's failed moderation attempts and Musk's limited algorithmic impact both demonstrate this. Instead, LLMs may serve as the natural consolidating technology: centralized systems trained on broad data that inherently push toward consensus. This framework suggests the 'Digital Cronkite' isn't a person or platform policy, but a technology whose architecture naturally favors epistemic convergence.
