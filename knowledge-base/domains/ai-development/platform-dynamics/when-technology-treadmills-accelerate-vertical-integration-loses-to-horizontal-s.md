Intel in 1975 did everything — transistor research, chip design, manufacturing, packaging, sales. By 2020, the industry had cleanly disaggregated into five layers: EDA tools (Synopsys, Cadence), IP cores (Arm), fabless designers (Nvidia, Apple), lithography equipment (ASML), and foundries (TSMC). Each layer is dominated by one or two companies that reinvest virtually all their profits into staying at the frontier of their single specialty. The treadmill — Moore's Law doubling transistor density every two years — forced this split: no single firm could keep pace across all five layers simultaneously, and the ones that tried (Intel) fell behind in every layer.

The same pattern is now playing out in AI. Foundation model training, inference infrastructure, fine-tuning tooling, agent frameworks, eval systems, and data labeling are each specializing into their own layer as the underlying capability treadmill accelerates. The firms that try to own all of them (arguably Google, arguably Meta) face the same structural problem Intel did: the pace of innovation at the frontier of any single layer outruns the attention any one CEO can give it. The lesson for founders is that horizontal specialization bets tend to win when the underlying treadmill is speeding up, and vertical integration bets tend to win when the treadmill is stabilizing.

Apply this when evaluating 'full-stack AI' plays: ask whether the rate of capability improvement at each layer is accelerating or stabilizing. If it's accelerating, the full-stack bet is probably going to lose to specialists who can pour their entire R&D budget into one layer.
