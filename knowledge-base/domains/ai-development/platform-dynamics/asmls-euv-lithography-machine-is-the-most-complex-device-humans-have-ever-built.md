Every advanced chip in existence requires ASML's extreme ultraviolet (EUV) lithography machines. These $350 million devices use a tin ball dropped through a vacuum, struck twice by the most powerful commercial laser ever deployed, creating plasma at 40 times the temperature of the sun's surface. This plasma emits light at exactly 13.5 nanometers, bounced off the flattest mirrors ever manufactured, to carve transistors into silicon.

This is the most complex and expensive machine humans have ever built, and ASML is the sole manufacturer. The geopolitical implications are enormous: whoever controls access to these machines controls who can produce advanced AI chips, smartphone processors, and military computing. Miller's insight extends to any industry: extreme precision requirements create natural monopolies where the machines that make the product are harder to build than the product itself.
