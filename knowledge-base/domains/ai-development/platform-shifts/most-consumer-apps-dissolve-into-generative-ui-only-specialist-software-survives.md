The talk's title thesis is that the app as a unit of consumer software is ending. Kitze rebuts the common objection ('my grandma will never vibe code') by reframing it: grandma won't write code, but she also won't need an app to accomplish a task. In a future agent OS she states what she wants and the interface materializes on demand — UI generated on the fly for the specific task, then discarded. The mental shift is from 'I need an app to do X' to simply 'do X.'

He carves out an exception: a small set of apps survives as professional tooling for specialists — color grading, movie-making, music production — where practitioners genuinely need dense, persistent software. But the long tail of consumer apps gets absorbed into the OS-level agent layer.

His wildcard prediction is that Apple (or a local-model platform) could win this, because on-device models are improving fast enough that a local Siri-class agent with tool access to your installed apps could do most consumer tasks with zero cloud credits and no data leaving the phone — he cites the Pixel already launching apps and ordering coffee in the background. The actionable read for builders: a generic consumer CRUD app is a depreciating asset, while either (a) deep specialist software or (b) the tool/skill layer agents call into is where durable value concentrates.
