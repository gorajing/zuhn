Kaku makes a fundamental philosophical point: digital computers compute on abstractions — zeros and ones. But the real world — molecules, diseases, chemical reactions — is not made of zeros and ones. It is made of electrons that can exist in multiple states simultaneously. Quantum computers compute on atoms and electrons directly, making them native to the substrate of reality.

This is why quantum computers could revolutionize medicine (modeling diseases at the molecular level instead of testing chemicals in Petri dishes), energy (stabilizing fusion reactions), and agriculture (unlocking nitrogen fixation for fertilizer). Digital computers are foreign to these problems — they must approximate molecular behavior through abstraction. Quantum computers operate at the same level as the phenomena they are modeling. The insight extends to any field: the most powerful tool is one that operates at the same level of abstraction as the problem.
