Hou traces a clean lineage: autocomplete (Copilot) became possible only when models got good at short-form completion; chat arrived with RLHF; agents arrived when models could plan and use tools. Each product paradigm was motivated by a specific change in model capability rather than a product insight that preceded the model. The corollary for builders is that 'the product is only ever as good as the models that power it' — chasing a paradigm the models can't yet support produces a demo, not a product.

The actionable inversion is to watch the capability frontier as the leading indicator of what to build, not the competitive landscape. Antigravity's four-category capability map (intelligence/reasoning, tool use, long-running tasks, multimodal) became the design brief: the team built the agent manager, browser, and artifacts specifically to exploit jumps in those four axes. The risk this guards against is product roadmaps anchored to last year's model — by the time they ship, the capability assumptions are stale in both directions (some constraints lifted, new affordances unexploited).
