As classical transistors shrink toward atomic scales, quantum tunneling causes electrons to leak through insulating barriers meant to be 'off,' increasing error rates and making it harder to distinguish 0s from 1s. This is the physics wall that constrains Moore's Law-style classical scaling.

Quantum computing begins at this exact constraint but takes the opposite approach: instead of fighting quantum behavior, it harnesses superposition, entanglement, and interference as computational primitives. This reframing — treating quantum effects as features rather than bugs — is a pattern that recurs across technology: the limitation of one paradigm often contains the seed of the next. However, it also means quantum and classical computing are complementary rather than sequential; quantum won't replace classical for general-purpose tasks.
