Palmer argues that what feels like 'ten tech revolutions in a row' (computer → PC → internet → cell phone → social media → LLMs) is really one revolution deepening and finding new application layers. She traces the same pattern for print: Gutenberg's press in 1450 wasn't economically sustainable until 1490; pamphlet distribution in the 1510s enabled Luther's Reformation the way cell phones enabled the Arab Spring; the newspaper arrived later as an accountability mechanism against unreliable pamphlets; the magazine arrived still later to fact-check newspapers. Each successive layer is as disruptive as the original.

The take-away for AI: treating 'LLMs' as its own revolution separable from 'the computer' is probably the wrong frame. LLMs are one application layer in an ongoing information revolution that started with computers in the 1940s and will keep producing successive revolutionary applications for decades. We should expect the equivalent of pamphlets, newspapers, and magazines — new institutional forms built to handle failures of the previous layer — rather than one stable endpoint. This reframes 'timing' questions: if you are early for the current layer, you may be arriving exactly when the next layer becomes possible.
