The common framing of voice is that it's a faster, more natural input modality — quicker and more interactive than typing, and more accessible to people with dyslexia or who struggle with keyboards. Harries pushes past that to the more strategic point: voice is omni-channel. The moment an agent gains voice, it unlocks entirely new interaction surfaces that text simply cannot occupy.

His examples are concrete: a product-analytics agent could join a Zoom call and correct you in real time if you cite wrong stats; a customer-support agent could answer an inbound phone line. These aren't the same interaction sped up — they're new contexts of deployment. This reframes the build/buy decision for voice: the ROI isn't 'users type less,' it's 'the agent can now exist where your users already are' — on calls, on phones, in ambient settings. For product teams, the implication is to evaluate voice by the channels it opens, not by marginal speed gains on the existing chat surface.
