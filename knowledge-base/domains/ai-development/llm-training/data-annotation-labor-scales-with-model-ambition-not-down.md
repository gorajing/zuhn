Hao has reported on data annotation for 7+ years and the labor requirement has increased, not decreased. The reason: models don't 'generally' become more capable by scaling alone — labs must pick specific capabilities (finance, law, medicine, coding, customer service) and hire tens or hundreds of thousands of human contractors to annotate examples that teach the model that specific capability. The capability roadmap is chosen by which industries can pay most, not by any natural intelligence gradient.

This falsifies two related narratives: (1) that models are becoming 'everything machines' — they are actually jagged-frontier narrow tools in each dimension; (2) that the horse has left the stable on training data — the appetite for new data expands because each new capability needs fresh annotation. Data annotation is now on LinkedIn's top-10 growing jobs list and is absorbing laid-off white-collar workers (marketers, lawyers, doctors, award-winning directors) who are being conscripted to train the models on their former jobs — a direct pipeline from 'career lost' to 'training its replacement,' which perpetuates further layoffs if the model acquires that skill.
