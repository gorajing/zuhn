Hao summarizes Sutskever's framework: brains are giant statistical models (Hinton's view), so building a bigger statistical engine than the brain will produce human-equivalent intelligence and eventually super-intelligence. Sutskever visualized this in a famous NeurIPS keynote showing brain size roughly linear with species intelligence, extrapolating to digital brains that exceed the human scale.

Critics — especially neuroscientists and psychologists outside AI — argue this framing is reductive: human brains are not merely statistical engines, and nothing in the science of actual human cognition has proven they are. Hinton's hypothesis is a hypothesis, not an established result. This matters enormously because the hypothesis drives trillion-dollar capital decisions: OpenAI's Stargate, Meta's Louisiana gigawatt facility, xAI's Colossus, Google's Hyperscaler investment — all justified by 'we need more compute to scale to AGI.'

The argument is important for anyone evaluating AI investment thesis: if the scaling-hypothesis is wrong, the marginal returns on the next 100x of compute may collapse, because specific capabilities require specific training data and specific annotation investment regardless of raw parameter count. The self-driving car example supports Hao's skepticism — self-driving has been 'imminent' for 10+ years, every new city requires complete retraining, and general transfer of skills is not happening.
