O'Leary draws a sharp line between 'using' AI and 'working with' it, and locates the actual gains entirely on the 'working with' side. He cites Armin Ronacher (creator of Flask) reporting a 30%+ daily time gain — but attributes that gain not to accepting more output, but to Armin knowing exactly what he can hand off and what he must keep for himself, and directing the work rather than blindly accepting every suggestion. The contrast case is the engineer who is just 'auto-completing their way through the day': they're using AI, but capturing little of its leverage.

The diagnostic that opens the talk reinforces this: most engineers can't articulate their real AI workflow — what they hand off, what they keep, how they decide in between — even though ~90% already use AI tools. That gap between using and being able to articulate working with AI is the gap between marginal and real gains. The practical implication is that getting good is a skill that requires reps to calibrate trust: you have to learn through repetition what you can trust the models to do and what you can't, which delegation boundaries hold, and which don't. The payoff senior engineers report isn't just speed but reclaiming attention for the harder engineering problems while tedious work is farmed out.
