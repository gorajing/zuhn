Naval takes a deliberately contrarian stance against the prompt engineering industry: he never bothers learning tips, tricks, or specialized AI workflows because their longevity is measured in weeks, not years. His argument is asymmetric adaptation — AI models are being trained on massive user data and tools are being built rapidly to make AI easier to use, so the system adapts to you faster than you can adapt to it.

The exception he carves out is narrow: if you're building at the bleeding edge right now in a competitive environment, ephemeral workflow knowledge might be worth it. But for everyone else, the optimal strategy is to speak naturally (what he calls 'boomer queries') and let the AI meet you where you are. This connects to a deeper principle about where to invest scarce human attention — on the problem you're solving, not on the intermediary tool that's rapidly self-improving.
