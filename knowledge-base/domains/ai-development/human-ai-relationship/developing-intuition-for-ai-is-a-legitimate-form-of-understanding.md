We tend to dismiss practical, intuitive knowledge of AI as inferior to understanding the technical architecture. Shipper argues this is the Socratic bias at work: if you cannot explicitly define the rules, you do not really know it.

But heavy ChatGPT users develop genuine intuition for what it is good at, when it might hallucinate, and how to prompt effectively. This is analogous to knowing when a friend is lying: you cannot articulate the rules, but you reliably detect the pattern. Treating this intuitive AI literacy as legitimate knowledge rather than a poor substitute for technical understanding is both more accurate and more useful for most people.
