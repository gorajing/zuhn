The mainstream framing of AI assistance for creative work treats AI as an augmentation: you bring the spark, AI polishes the output. Dan Koe's structural objection: creative discovery requires genuine surprise, and anticipation (which is what happens when you prompt an AI to be creative) precludes surprise. The mechanism is not that AI output is low quality — it is often high quality. The mechanism is that the author's creative faculty was never engaged, because the moment of surprise-to-self was bypassed. Over time this exhausts creativity rather than building it, because creativity is a practice that develops through repeated engagement with surprise, not through consumption of pre-anticipated output. Authors who use AI heavily for creative output show a specific pattern: high initial volume, declining novelty, increasing formulaic structure, and eventually difficulty producing anything interesting without AI. The counter-practice: produce the creative spark yourself from lived experience, use AI for mechanical polish only, and guard the surprise-to-self moment as the core creative asset.
