Po-Shen Loh describes his AI usage pattern: when curious about Nashville's country music performance scene, he didn't ask AI to write a report. He asked for links, background on specific venues, competitive dynamics of getting performance spots — all to build a richer mental simulation of that world. The AI fed his understanding; he drew his own conclusions.

He calls world-simulation 'the superpower that makes someone able to be a successful entrepreneur' because it lets you imagine products, strategies, and their consequences before committing resources. AI amplifies this superpower when used as an information-gathering tool that enriches your internal model, but destroys it when used as an output-generation tool that replaces your thinking.

The distinction is directional: AI-as-input (feeding your brain more data to simulate with) versus AI-as-output (replacing your brain's simulation entirely). The former makes you smarter over time; the latter makes you dependent. This maps directly to how the best AI-native workers will differentiate themselves — not by what they produce with AI, but by what they understand because of AI.
