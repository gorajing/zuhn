Origin: John McCarthy rejected his earlier name 'automata studies' for 'artificial intelligence' in 1956 specifically because pegging the field to recreating human intelligence was ambitious and mobilizing. Some of his colleagues were concerned about this choice precisely because human intelligence has no scientific definition.

Consequences today: every debate about AI capability gets trapped in the analogy. 'Models hallucinate' — well, humans hallucinate too (Bartlett's rhetorical move). 'Self-driving cars aren't intelligent' — but they drive safer than humans in specific locations. The intelligence framing keeps both critics and boosters arguing about an undefined concept rather than about what the outputs are and what costs produced them.

Hao's preferred framing: evaluate technology by its outputs and its costs. Does the self-driving car drive safer in a specific city? Okay, then use it there, but don't extrapolate that it's 'intelligent' or 'going to replace all drivers.' Does AlphaFold predict protein folding? Okay, then use it for drug discovery, but don't use that to justify scaling LLMs. This is an epistemic discipline argument: abandon the analogy, evaluate each deployment on its own measured capability and its own externalities. The anthropomorphic frame systematically hides the mismatch between broad claims and narrow realities.
