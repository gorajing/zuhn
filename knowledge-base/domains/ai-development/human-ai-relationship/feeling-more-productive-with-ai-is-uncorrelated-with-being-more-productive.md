The widely-cited METR study (despite methodological flaws) surfaced a striking dissociation: 100% of participating engineers reported feeling more productive with AI coding assistance, while the measured data showed they were actually 19% slower. The proposed mechanism is 'induced flow' — AI produces a pleasant, fast-feeling working state that the brain reads as productivity regardless of actual throughput. The good feeling is real; the productivity it implies is not.

This breaks the cheapest measurement instrument leaders reach for: asking developers whether AI is helping. Subjective satisfaction surveys, taken alone, will systematically over-report AI's benefit because the tool optimizes the experience of working, not necessarily the result. Effective measurement therefore has to triangulate self-report against hard outcome metrics (change failure rate, throughput, maintainability) and behavioral telemetry — and when felt-productivity and measured-productivity diverge, the measured outcome is the one to trust. The lesson generalizes beyond code: any tool that improves the feel of a task creates a perception gap that hides whether it improved the task.
