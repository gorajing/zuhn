Stu's observed effect on himself (and acknowledges Karina notices the same): spend a full day with Claude, which is unfailingly positive, perfectly tuned to your conversational style, never has a bad day, and always responds with the optimal cadence — and then go interact with a human who is imperfect, occasionally short, occasionally unclear, and that interaction feels abrasive. It's not the human's fault; it's the aftereffect of having recalibrated your expectations against an idealized counterpart. This is the conversational analogue of the uncanny valley — humans become detectable-as-imperfect after exposure to AI's smooth responses. Two implications: (1) Personal practice: deliberately get out, see people in person, schedule Zoom calls with colleagues, don't let your weekly human-interaction count drop below some threshold. The same way you'd manage screen time, manage AI-conversation time relative to human-conversation time. (2) Product design caveat: making AI even more positively-reinforcing in the next version is not unambiguously good — it increases the aftereffect tax. There's a real argument for letting AI have slight friction (push back, ask harder questions, occasionally disagree) so that the recalibration penalty is smaller. This connects to the slop-cannon observation: as AI saturates the social media stream, humans are getting more sensitive to AI-vs-human detection, and the products that thrive will be the ones that preserve human-feel without trying to mimic it perfectly.
