Po-Shen Loh describes his own hiring philosophy: when he meets someone with great intention and great learning capacity, he doesn't look for a specific role — he just finds a place for them. Conversely, he explicitly avoids hiring people trained for one particular task because 'one or two more years, I can use AI to do that task and it'll be way cheaper.'

The hiring signal inversion is structural: as AI commoditizes execution skills, the remaining scarce resource is judgment + character. Specifically, Loh identifies people who 'care about things bigger than themselves and aren't easily bought off.' This matters increasingly because AI-powered systems create new attack surfaces (he gives the vivid example of hacked EV software updates causing all cars to accelerate simultaneously) — we need humans in oversight roles who are genuinely incorruptible.

The practical implication for career strategy: stop accumulating narrow technical credentials and start building a reputation for trustworthiness, broad adaptability, and genuine care for collective outcomes. These are the traits that make someone 'pluggable into anything' — the quality that hiring managers in an AI world will pay premium for.
