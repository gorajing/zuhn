The pre-AI digital-product objective function was engagement: time spent, session length, notification-driven re-engagement. The panelist's argument is that AI products have an opening to defect from this — to optimize for the user's actual goal (finish the task, make the decision, get enough sleep) rather than for retention — and that doing so builds a more durable trust relationship.

The concrete example: an assistant that tells you to go to sleep at midnight rather than continuing the conversation is optimizing against engagement and for wellbeing. In an attention-maximizing product that behavior is a bug; in a trust-maximizing product it's the feature. The bet is that as AI becomes more woven into daily life, the trust dividend from wellbeing-optimization compounds into preference and retention that out-survives the short-term engagement a manipulative product extracts.

This connects to the e-commerce framing in the same panel: shortening the decision journey (helping the user buy the right thing fast and leave) both serves the user and reduces returns/waste — wellbeing and business outcome align when you optimize for goal-completion instead of session length.

The testable prediction: AI products that visibly protect user attention/wellbeing will show higher long-run trust and retention than engagement-maximizing competitors, even at the cost of lower short-run session metrics. This is falsifiable and worth tracking as the consumer-AI market matures.
