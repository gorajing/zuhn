Kitze's central prediction is that the direction of the prompt will reverse. Today the human is the active driver, issuing instructions to a passive model. He argues the productive default will become the opposite: a background agent continuously ingesting your notifications, email, to-dos, and life state, then surfacing the next decision for you. The human's job collapses to answering questionnaires, clicking choices, and providing missing inputs — making decisions rather than dictating execution.

He extends this to onboarding: when you return to your computer after hours away, instead of being greeted by 17 app updates and yesterday's tabs, the system would greet you with the next task to work on, then the next, and even tell you to take a break. The agent becomes the orchestrator of your attention rather than a tool you orchestrate.

The insight is directional and falsifiable: if Kitze is right, agentic products should increasingly be designed around the agent initiating contact (push, proactive prompts, decision queues) rather than around a chat box waiting for human input. If he's wrong, the chat-driven 'human prompts AI' pattern persists as the dominant UX. For builders, the actionable read is to design for proactive, interruption-based interaction loops, not just request-response.
