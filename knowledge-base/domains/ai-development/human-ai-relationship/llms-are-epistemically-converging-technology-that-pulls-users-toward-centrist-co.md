Unlike social media, which fragments reality by letting users self-select into echo chambers, LLMs present something closer to the average opinion of the full population. When a partisan user talks to an AI, they're indirectly engaging with viewpoints from across the spectrum — not just their curated social circle. Research from John Burn-Murdoch at the Financial Times confirms that all major chatbots (Grok, GPT, Gemini, DeepSeek) nudge users away from extreme positions toward more moderate, expert-aligned stances.

This positions LLMs as the first major epistemically converging technology since network television. Dylan Matthews draws the explicit parallel: just as broadcast TV built shared reality from the 1950s through 1990s, LLMs are centralized systems that behave similarly for everyone until prompted with specific context. The key mechanism is that AI persuasion is substantial — Chen et al. (2026) find recent LLMs are more persuasive than campaign advertisements — meaning this centrist nudge has real behavioral impact, not just theoretical appeal.
