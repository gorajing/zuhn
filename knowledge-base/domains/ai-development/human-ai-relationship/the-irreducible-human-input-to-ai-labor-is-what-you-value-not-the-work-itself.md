The recurring frame in Paperclip is that the human is the CEO and the agents are the labor — but the demo is explicit that the bottleneck this exposes is not capability, it is values transfer: 'these tools can do anything except know what you value, and so what you need to be able to do is communicate accurately what you expect.' Every productive interaction shown (the video pacing feedback, the CodeX coder's evolving instructions, the brand guide) is the human encoding judgment the model cannot infer.

The directional claim is that as execution becomes cheap and abundant, competitive advantage migrates to whoever can most precisely specify what 'good' means in their context. This reframes 'prompting' from a syntactic skill into a values-articulation skill: the work product quality is capped by how legibly you've externalized your taste, not by the agent's raw intelligence.

Practically, this means time spent writing brand guides, style preferences, quality rubrics, and correction instructions is not overhead — it is the actual job. Teams that treat agent management as 'fire and forget' will get generic output; teams that invest in making implicit standards explicit will get on-brand, high-quality work because the only thing they're really supplying is judgment.
