Harari makes a foundational distinction: intelligence is the ability to solve problems and attain goals; consciousness is the ability to feel things like pain, pleasure, and love. In humans these go together, which is why we confuse them. But computers are already highly intelligent with (as far as we know) zero consciousness.

This has two major implications. First, treating AI outputs as if they reflect inner experience is a category error that leads to poor design decisions. Second, the real question for AI development isn't 'how intelligent can we make it' but 'what happens when we deploy high intelligence without any consciousness' -- because an entity that can solve problems but cannot suffer or care creates novel ethical and practical challenges that our frameworks aren't equipped to handle.
