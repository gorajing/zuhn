Nations frames the real problem as a knowledge gap: AI can generate thousands of lines in seconds, but understanding them takes hours or days — maybe never if they're tangled. There's a difference between code that passes tests and code that survives in production, and between systems that function today and systems a future engineer can change.

The non-obvious cost is atrophy: pattern recognition comes from experience — 'when I spot a dangerous architecture, it's because I'm the one up at 3am dealing with it; when I push for simpler solutions, it's because I've had to maintain the alternative.' AI generates what you ask for; it doesn't encode lessons from past failures. So skipping the thinking degrades the very instinct that flags complexity early. The closing reframe makes the stakes a human endeavor: AI changes how we write code but not why software fails, and the developers who thrive will be those who still understand what they're building and can see the seams — the open question being whether we'll still understand our own systems when AI writes most of the code.
