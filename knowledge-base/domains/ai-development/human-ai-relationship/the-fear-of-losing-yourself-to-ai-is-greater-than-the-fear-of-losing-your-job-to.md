Kim Kyung-ah, a Korean designer, was confident in her work until her decision-maker started running every output through AI prompts. Instead of 10 pieces of normal feedback, she received 40 AI-generated revision requests. The boss would 'immediately turn on the prompt' whenever reviewing her work, giving infinite source code and telling her to fix everything exactly as prescribed.

The result wasn't job loss — it was identity erosion. 'My judgment was getting clouded. I thought if I really lost my mind like this, it would be a disaster. The fear of losing myself was greater than the fear of having my job taken away by AI.' She submitted her resignation voluntarily and began drawing by hand again, reading books by pre-digital authors, absorbing human philosophy to 'regain the senses I had unknowingly lost.' The documentary's broader message, echoed by educator Kim Moon-jeong: 'The most important value in post-AI is someone who can say: this is what I think.' Personal philosophy, not technical skill, becomes the critical human asset.
