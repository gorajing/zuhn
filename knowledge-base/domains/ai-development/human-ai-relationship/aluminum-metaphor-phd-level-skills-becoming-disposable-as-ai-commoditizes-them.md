The aluminum metaphor captures a specific phase transition: a resource that is structurally scarce becomes structurally abundant via a new production technology, and within a generation it goes from precious to disposable. Aluminum's pattern: precious-metal status (Washington Monument cap, Tiffany's display) → electrolysis invented → consumer commodity (foil, soda cans) → trash. Intelligence is following the same trajectory but faster. Skills that took decades to acquire (medical diagnosis, legal research, engineering analysis, financial modeling, language translation) are becoming AI-invocable at low cost. The implication for the careers built on those skills: the value capture shifts from possessing the skill to deciding when to apply it, which contexts to apply it in, and what to do with the output. The professionals who treated their skill as their identity face the hardest transition; the professionals who treated their skill as a tool they wielded for client outcomes have a smoother path. The implication for organizations: workflows that anchor on hiring scarce talent need to be redesigned around AI-invocable expertise; the binding constraint becomes orchestration and judgment, not skill availability.
