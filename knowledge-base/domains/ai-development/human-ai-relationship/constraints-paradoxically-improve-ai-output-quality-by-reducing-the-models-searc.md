Intuitively, adding constraints — word limits, tone requirements, vocabulary restrictions, audience specifications — feels like it should reduce the quality of AI output by limiting what the model can do. In practice, the opposite is true. Without constraints, models default to neutral, balanced, generic content because that's the safest response across the widest range of possible intents.

Constraints function as a focusing mechanism. 'Explain this to a high school student using only simple metaphors' forces the model to find genuinely creative analogies rather than falling back on jargon-laden explanations. 'Write a 50-word summary using only active voice and no jargon' demands precision that a 500-word unconstrained summary never achieves. This mirrors a well-known principle in creative work — constraints breed creativity — but it's especially potent with language models because their default mode is to maximize coverage rather than maximize impact.
