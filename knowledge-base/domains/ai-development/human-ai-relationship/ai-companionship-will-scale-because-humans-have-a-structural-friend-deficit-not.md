Rather than defending AI companionship on abstract 'people can decide for themselves' libertarian grounds, Zuckerberg anchors it in a specific unmet need. He cites that the average American has fewer than three friends but demand is closer to fifteen, with a natural ceiling around where people feel 'I just can't deal with more people.' The gap between three and fifteen is the addressable market for AI companions and therapists, and it exists regardless of whether the technology improves.

His product philosophy on this is notable: 'if you think something someone is doing is bad and they think it's really valuable, most of the time in my experience, they're right and you're wrong.' He's explicitly choosing to not be prescriptive upfront about which AI relationships are 'healthy,' because that framing systematically underestimates user value. This is the same revealed-preference argument Meta has used against content-moderation overreach, now applied to AI relationships.

The uncomfortable corollary for anyone building in this space: the ceiling is set less by model quality than by embodiment — today's AI companions are just an image plus rough animation. Zuckerberg points to Codec Avatars and always-on video chat as where this is headed, where more than half of communication (the nonverbal layer) finally becomes available. When companionship products can actually embody, expect the category to break out of its current stigma, the way online dating did between 2005 and 2015.
