CS50P teaches the fundamentals — variables, control flow, functions, OOP, testing, debugging — that many assume AI will make obsolete. But the opposite is true. When AI generates code, someone must verify it works correctly, debug it when it doesn't, and understand its behavior at the edges. This requires exactly the mental models CS50P builds: understanding how data flows through a program, what side effects functions can have, why certain patterns lead to bugs.

The verification bottleneck is already visible in practice: vibe-coded applications fail at scale because their creators couldn't evaluate the AI-generated code. The humans who thrive in AI-augmented development are those who can read a function and predict its behavior, spot off-by-one errors, and understand why a recursive solution might blow the stack. CS50P's emphasis on building these mental models — through progressive exposure to increasingly complex programs — is precisely the education that remains valuable. The course doesn't teach Python; it teaches how to think about computation, which is the skill AI cannot yet replace.
