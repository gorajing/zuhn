The AI Index 2026 Chapter 9 documents what Pew Research found to be the sharpest expert-public divide in their technology surveys: across nearly every topic, AI experts report substantially more optimism than the US public. On jobs, 73% of AI experts expect positive impact versus 23% of US adults — a 50-point gap. On the economy: 69% vs 21%. On medical care: 84% vs 44%. On K-12 education: 61% vs 24%. Only on arts/entertainment, elections, news, and personal relationships is optimism low for both groups. The Longitudinal Expert AI Panel (LEAP) data shows the same pattern on capability forecasts — across 68 forecast questions, experts predicted faster progress 71% of the time and were 16% more likely to predict faster outcomes. The structural implication is that AI policy is being made by a class (experts, researchers, founders) whose median expectation differs from the median public expectation by more than 50 points on the most consequential dimensions. Policy designed for 'the public good' will mean very different things depending on which median is used, and communication strategies that assume shared framing are miscalibrated by construction.
