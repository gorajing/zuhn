Bartlett's initial optimistic framing: AI is 'the first technology that might deliver on the promise of making us human and connected' because it takes over the spreadsheet work, freeing humans for IRL community, relationships, and Maslovian needs. He cites Gen Alpha plateauing social media use, rise of run clubs, disconnection from screens.

Hao accepts this is true — for a specific class. Business owners and leadership can actually make the decision about how to spend time once AI handles their tasks. The New York Magazine piece she references tracked the opposite pole: highly educated laid-off workers (college, PhD, law, medical, award-winning directors) absorbed into data annotation firms, competing against each other on speed and price. One woman interviewed: became a 'monster' screaming at her child because a project window opened and she needed to task furiously in an uncertain time window to put food on the table, unable to go to the bathroom or care for herself.

The data annotation industry is structurally designed to pit workers against each other because third-party firms compete on speed and cost to win contracts from OpenAI, Google, xAI. So the same technology that lets Bartlett become 'more human' by orchestrating agents is making the workers training those agents 'less human' at the same time. The implication for anyone reasoning about AI + wellbeing: the experience is bimodal, and aggregate 'AI makes us more human' claims hide the valley between the two modes.
