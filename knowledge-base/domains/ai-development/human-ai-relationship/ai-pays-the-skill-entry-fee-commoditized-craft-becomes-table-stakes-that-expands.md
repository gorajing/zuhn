The essay frames AI as 'the ultimate craftsman' that pays the skill entry fee for everyone. Rather than seeing this as pure loss, it argues this commoditization expands the creative surface — the total space of what can be attempted and expressed. When anyone can produce serviceable code, writing, or design, the bottleneck shifts from 'can you execute?' to 'do you have something original to say?'

The Picasso example is instructive: he spent years mastering academic realism before he could deliberately abandon it. With AI handling the realism phase, more people can operate at the Cubism phase — the expression layer where genuinely novel combinations emerge. The essay suggests the people who thrive won't be those clinging to craft mastery, but those who find commoditized execution 'almost uninteresting' because they're already working on new rules entirely.

Sasha Stiles at MoMA exemplifies this: using AI not as a replacement but as a collaborator to create self-rewriting poetry — a form that couldn't exist without AI but is deeply human in its conception.
