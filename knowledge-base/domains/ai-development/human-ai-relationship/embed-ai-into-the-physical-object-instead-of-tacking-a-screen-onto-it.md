Reflecting on museums that add big interactive tablets, Reeve's critique is that bolting a screen beside an artifact makes the technology feel tacked-on rather than a core part of the experience. The app version — photograph a statue, then talk to it through your phone — has the same flaw: a screen sits in the way.

His direction instead is invisible embodiment: commissioning a statue with the microphone, speaker, and phone built inside it so you talk directly to the object, and the red-phone-booth installation where you pick up a real handset to talk to an agent (e.g. Sir Michael Caine). The principle generalizes well beyond museums — he muses that elevators and other everyday objects will get voices, raising the genuinely new design question of what an inanimate object should sound like given its material origin, journey, and the accents it has lived among. As voice makes any object addressable, the design frontier shifts from screens to giving things native, embodied presence.
