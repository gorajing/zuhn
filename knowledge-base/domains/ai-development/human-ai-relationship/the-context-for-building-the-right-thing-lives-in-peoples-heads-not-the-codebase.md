Most of the context needed to build the right thing is not in the codebase. It's in people's heads: the business context and financial resources that determine what's correct to build, the political dynamics of who decides, the product vision from leaders, user research insights from designers, and the organization's history of what's been built before. Agents can never discover this on their own — it's tacit, social, and unwritten.

The implication is that effective agentic development requires a deliberate mechanism for humans to share this context early and naturally, without piling on process and overhead. This is why a shared, multiplayer environment matters more than a faster solo agent: when conversations around the code are captured alongside the work, both teammates and agents gain access to a 'social information fabric' that keeps everyone aligned. Practically, the lesson is to surface the why-and-what-for of work into a shared space before prompting, rather than expecting agents to infer intent from code.
