The AI Index 2026 Chapter 4 reports productivity gains from AI largest in structured, measurable work: 14-15% in customer support, 26% in software development, 73% in marketing. But it explicitly flags that 'recent evidence raises concerns that heavy AI reliance may carry long-term learning penalties that slow skill development over time.' This is the productivity-vs-learning tradeoff made measurable — the same AI tools that produce short-term gains may degrade the user's ability to develop tacit expertise, because expertise comes from struggle and iteration, not outcome copying. The long-term implication is that AI-assisted professionals may be less capable over a 5-10 year horizon than their non-AI-assisted counterparts, even if they outperform them quarter-to-quarter. Combined with the finding that entry-level workers are being displaced first, the picture is a labor market where AI raises current output while simultaneously eroding the skill pipeline that would produce next-decade output. Confidence is medium because the long-term studies are still early, but the mechanism is plausible and aligns with earlier findings about cognitive outsourcing.
