The argument rests on a physical asymmetry: a solar panel in orbit produces ~5x more power than on the ground (no atmospheric loss, no clouds, no seasonality, no night), and eliminates the battery cost entirely. Combined with ~10x cheaper solar cells in space (they can skip heavy glass and weather-resistant framing), Musk claims space becomes an order of magnitude cheaper than any terrestrial data center once Starship makes launch costs trivial.

The key insight is that Earth's electrical output is roughly flat outside China, while chip output grows exponentially. This creates a hard ceiling: you can build chips faster than you can turn them on. By late 2026, Musk predicts chips will pile up because clusters can't be powered. Space sidesteps the entire permitting, utility interconnect, and turbine-supply bottleneck by bypassing the grid altogether.

Treat this as a live prediction to track, not an established fact. If Starship reaches 10,000 launches/year and SpaceX becomes a hyperscaler launching more AI capacity annually than the cumulative Earth total, the compute map of the world redraws completely — and the key bottleneck shifts from electricity to chip fabrication.
