Younas's strategic frame: physical machines today are at the same fragmentation point phones were before iOS/Android. When Larry Page wanted to run Google products across phones, he discovered ~50 different operating systems and concluded the answer was building Android — a unified OS attractive enough to phone makers to actually consolidate the market. Applied Intuition is doing the same for physical machines: a unified OS that lets modern AI applications run across cars, trucks, drones, mining equipment, defense systems.

The market structure: physical AI lags consumer AI not because the models are worse, but because the deployment substrate is fragmented. Every OEM has its own OS, its own sensor stack, its own development tooling. Without consolidation, every AI capability has to be re-implemented per device. Applied's bet: be the consolidation layer, then participate in every AI application that runs on physical machines.

For builders: there's a parallel pattern in any fragmented vertical (industrial software, healthcare devices, financial back-office). The first company to build a credible OS-equivalent unifying that fragmentation captures the platform position. For Zuhn editorially: this is the operational form of why physical AI is potentially bigger than consumer AI but slower to ramp — the OS-consolidation prerequisite is structural, and only one or two companies per vertical will solve it.
