Gil's framing of the federal role: support long-term high-risk science (where ROI is uncertain or 30+ years out), build shared infrastructure that no single company can justify, and serve as a foundational anchor for the innovation ecosystem. This isn't a claim that government does science better than industry — it's a claim that specific market failures exist (long-horizon, public-good-shaped infrastructure, basic research with diffuse benefits) and federal funding is the right vehicle to address them. The Genesis Mission explicitly leverages this: connecting national lab infrastructure (federal) to industry partners (commercial) to academia (talent) in a way that combines all three appropriately.

Application: when arguing for or against federal science funding, the argument needs to be grounded in specific market failures that justify it. Diffuse claims about 'science needs more money' aren't credible to fiscal hawks. Specific claims like 'no private capital will fund a 50-year protein structure database, and yet that database produces multi-trillion-dollar drug discovery returns' are actionable and compelling.
