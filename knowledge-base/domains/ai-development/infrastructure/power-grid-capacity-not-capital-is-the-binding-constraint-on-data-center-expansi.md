The data center boom has hit a physical constraint that capital cannot solve on short timescales: electrical grid capacity. US disclosed pipeline reached 241 GW by end of 2025 (up 159% YoY), but only a third of projects are actively developing. Q4 2025 saw only 25 GW of new capacity added—half the Q3 pace—because utilities lack the grid and generating capacity to serve these massive new demand centers.

This creates a paradox: hyperscalers plan to spend ~$650B in 2026 (up $200B from 2025), but nominal CapEx records mean less than they appear because the constraint has shifted from financial to physical. Interconnection queues, permitting timelines, and natural gas availability in specific geographies (New Mexico, Indiana, Wyoming are growing fast for this reason) now determine buildout pace.

For investors and builders, this means the value shifts toward whoever controls energy access—not compute or capital. Multi-gigawatt portfolio deals between hyperscalers and energy developers are replacing single-asset PPAs, indicating the industry understands this. The rack power densification trend (higher cost per sq ft, lower cost per MW) also signals that operators are optimizing for energy density over floor space.
