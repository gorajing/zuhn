Solar production doubles roughly every 2-2.5 years, and each doubling cuts cost by ~40%, giving 15-20% annual price declines. Handmer argues this isn't a fluke — it's driven by ~10,000 manufacturing process engineers working full-time on an inherently easy-to-manufacture product with extreme standardization. The critical insight is that demand elasticity exceeds the learning rate, so cheaper panels unlock more than 40% additional demand per doubling, sustaining the flywheel indefinitely.

Conventional wisdom repeatedly predicts solar demand saturation 'this year' and is repeatedly wrong — adoption is not just continuing but accelerating, and the acceleration is itself accelerating. Gas turbines, transformers, and power stations don't have comparable learning rates because (1) banks won't finance 25-year gas turbine plants in a world where solar keeps getting cheaper, (2) Brayton cycles are inherently expensive ($35/MWh just for the spinning components), and (3) the supply chain is less fungible.

For anyone modeling AI compute buildouts beyond 2028, the base rate should be mostly-solar, not mostly-gas. Handmer predicts that by 2027, majority of newly groundbreaking data centers will be >90% solar. Gas is a bridge technology while solar supply ramps, not the destination.
