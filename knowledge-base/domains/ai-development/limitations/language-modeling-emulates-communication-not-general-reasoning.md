The argument draws on neuroscience that dissociates language production from general cognition — the brain regions responsible for fluent speech are distinct from those handling abstract reasoning and creativity. This means LLMs, which are fundamentally optimized to predict the next token in a sequence, may be learning the surface patterns of how humans communicate ideas rather than the deeper processes that generate those ideas.

This has practical implications for AI development strategy: rather than assuming pure scaling will eventually produce general intelligence, builders should expect diminishing returns on reasoning capability from language-only architectures. The path to more capable AI likely requires architectural innovations that model cognition directly, not just its linguistic output. For startups building on LLMs, this suggests hedging against the assumption that foundation models will solve arbitrary reasoning tasks through scale alone.
