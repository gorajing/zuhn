Tao reports that while roughly 50 Erdős problems have been solved with AI assistance, systematic studies show frontier models have a 1-2% success rate on any given problem. The appearance of breakthrough comes from running thousands of attempts across hundreds of problems and publicizing only the winners. Users who try the same tools on their favorite problem will experience the actual 1-2% rate and feel disappointed.

Furthermore, nearly all the Erdős problems AI solved were ones with essentially no prior literature — Erdős posed them once, nobody wrote anything up, and the solution was combining one obscure technique with a known result. That is real progress but it's the floor of hard problems, not the ceiling. The lesson for anyone evaluating AI-for-science claims: demand standardized benchmarks with disclosed negative results, not press releases. The field needs challenge problem sets that companies can't game by reporting only successes.
