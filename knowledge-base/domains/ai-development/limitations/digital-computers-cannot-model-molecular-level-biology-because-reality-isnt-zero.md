Kaku argues that the current model of drug discovery -- creating hundreds of chemicals and testing them one-by-one in Petri dishes -- persists because digital computers fundamentally cannot model molecular behavior. Molecules operate through smooth electron probability waves, not binary zeros and ones. Digital computers can approximate this but cannot natively simulate it.

Quantum computers, which compute using the same quantum mechanical principles that govern molecules, would enable molecular-level disease modeling directly in computer memory. This could transform drug discovery from slow empirical trial-and-error to rapid computational simulation. The insight is that the computational substrate matters -- certain problems are naturally suited to certain types of computation.
