Kaku explains that Moore's Law -- computer power doubling every 18 months -- is slowing because transistors are approaching atomic dimensions. At roughly 5 atoms across, electrons can quantum-tunnel through barriers, creating short circuits. This is a hard physical limit, not an engineering problem to be solved.

The economic consequence is significant: consumers won't upgrade computers that aren't meaningfully faster than the previous generation, potentially cratering demand. Kaku argues this is why every major tech company is racing toward quantum computing -- not just as an interesting research area but as an existential necessity. Silicon Valley's business model depends on exponential improvement, and without a successor technology, it faces stagnation.
