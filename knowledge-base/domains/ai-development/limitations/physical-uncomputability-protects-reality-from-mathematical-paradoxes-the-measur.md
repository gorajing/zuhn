Banach-Tarski is mathematically valid but physically unrealizable because the five pieces must be infinitely complex and detailed — more complex than any physical object can be. This suggests that the discrete, finite nature of physical reality isn't a limitation we should try to overcome but a structural safeguard.

This has implications for AI and computation. Systems that operate in continuous mathematical spaces can encounter pathologies (adversarial examples, undecidable problems, infinite loops) that discrete implementations avoid. The 'loss of precision' from discretization may actually be what makes computation tractable and reliable. When building AI systems, the temptation is always toward higher precision and more continuous representations — but the Banach-Tarski lesson suggests that some forms of coarseness are load-bearing.
