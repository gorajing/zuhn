Early scaling of language models produced dramatic leaps—from incoherent to fluent—because the models were acquiring fundamental language competence. However, once that baseline was established, further scaling yielded diminishing returns. Recent improvements manifest as better instruction-following, cleaner formatting, and longer context handling rather than genuinely new cognitive capabilities.

This has practical implications for AI strategy: organizations betting on 'just scale it up' as a path to transformative capability may be disappointed. The shift from discovery-phase gains to refinement-phase gains means that architectural innovation, not just size, will likely be required for the next genuine capability leap. Benchmark improvements can mask this reality since models optimize for test patterns without expanding underlying reasoning ability.
