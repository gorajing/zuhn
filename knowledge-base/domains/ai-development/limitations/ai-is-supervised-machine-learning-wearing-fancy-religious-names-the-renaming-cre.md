The lecturer's argument that names obscure mechanism: 'Supervised machine learning works like this. The algorithm has weights — for face recognition, eye, nose, chin — about a million weights. I don't know how much each one matters, so I let the computer figure out the weighting by back-propagation. I control the input. I control the output. The computer adjusts the weights until faces match correctly. That's it. To make it sound fancy, I call the weighting system a NEURON NETWORK — it's a brain, it's magic. Back-propagation I call DEEP LEARNING. The whole package I call AI.' The technical claim is correct: deep learning is back-propagation across many layers (1986 paper), neural networks are weighted matching (1958 perceptron), supervised learning is labeled-data training (1970s). The naming choices over the past decade have been deliberately religious/biological/occult — 'neural networks' borrows from neuroscience, 'deep learning' suggests profundity, 'AI' invokes the science-fiction connotation of general intelligence. The naming serves three purposes: (1) Investor/market psychology — fancier names → higher valuations; (2) Talent recruitment — engineers want to work on 'AI' not 'multivariate regression'; (3) Regulatory positioning — agencies don't know how to regulate 'AI' but understand how to regulate 'pattern matchers.' The lecturer's broader thesis: the renaming creates aura that obscures BOTH genuine capabilities AND structural limitations. Demystified — these systems can do impressive pattern matching at scale but cannot reason from first principles, cannot verify their own outputs, cannot handle edge cases their training data didn't contain. The corollary for builders and operators: when evaluating AI capabilities, mentally translate every marketing term to its technical underlying — 'agentic reasoning' → 'tool-use orchestrator', 'understanding' → 'pattern recognition', 'thinking' → 'multi-step token generation'. Treat the underlying mechanism with rational respect, not religious awe. For investors and capital allocators: be skeptical of pitches that use highly-religious-coded AI language (sentience, consciousness, alignment, AGI). The framing often signals the team is over-promising relative to the actual technical capabilities.
