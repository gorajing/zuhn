Advanced language, chess, Go, Jeopardy, and 100-digit arithmetic all emerged recently in evolutionary time and conferred small fitness gains when they did emerge. Evolution had little time or pressure to optimize for them, so humans find them hard and AI systems blast through them quickly. Meanwhile, sensorimotor skills and commonsense action that animals share were optimized over hundreds of millions of years and remain difficult for AI.

The deeper point is correlation decay: in humans, being great at competitive programming correlates strongly with being a great engineer, so we use such signals for hiring. In AI systems that correlation is much weaker—o3-mini-high may top competitive programming benchmarks while Claude wins actual coding revenue. Benchmarks that impress us are a narrow subset of what it takes to be an economically valuable agent.

Practically: when a new model smashes a benchmark humans find hard, update less on general competence than intuition suggests. Ask instead whether the model can sustain long-horizon, ill-specified, embodied, or culturally situated tasks animals/humans handle effortlessly.
