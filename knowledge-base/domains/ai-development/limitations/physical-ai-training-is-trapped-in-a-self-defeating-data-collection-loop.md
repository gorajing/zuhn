The panel surfaced a structural irony that the robotics field has not solved: physical AI's pitch is eliminating labor-intensive work, but the data required to train each robot to perform that work today is generated by even-more-labor-intensive human teleoperation. Brian Wong described visiting factories where human operators in VR headsets perform sorting and packaging motions on companion robots for thousands of hours per task to produce supervised training data.

What makes this self-defeating rather than just expensive: the data does not generalize. Change the hardware config, change the workspace lighting/layout, change the SKU — the dataset is discarded and the human teleoperator session restarts. So the per-deployment cost is roughly linear in number-of-environments × number-of-tasks, which is the opposite of the software-economics that physical-AI VCs are underwriting in their valuations.

Until the architecture breaks free of this loop (world foundation models that learn causality from passive observation rather than supervised teleoperation), the actual unit economics of physical AI look more like services than software. This is the strongest available evidence that current physical-AI valuations are racing ahead of the underlying data economics.
