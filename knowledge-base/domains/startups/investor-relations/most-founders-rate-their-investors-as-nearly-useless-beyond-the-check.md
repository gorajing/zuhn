The 'smart money' pitch — that VCs provide strategic guidance, network access, and operational support beyond the capital — is central to how venture firms market themselves to founders. But when 44% of funded startups give their investors the lowest possible helpfulness score, it suggests this value-add is more marketing than reality for most investors.

The implication for founders is that investor selection criteria should be weighted heavily toward conviction and fund fit, not promises of operational support. The practical value of a board seat is often negative (time cost) unless the investor has been genuinely helpful in prior companies in your specific stage and sector. This data also creates an opportunity for investors who actually deliver: genuine helpfulness is a real competitive moat precisely because it is so rare.
