10DS can pay market rates 'within reason' but not 'meta money.' Their thesis is that many high-performers will happily take pay cuts to work on consequential problems — they recruit from frontier labs, big tech, top research institutes, YC founders, and serial entrepreneurs, people who 'did not think they would be working in the civil service this time last year.' The pitch is that the decisions crossing a minister's desk are among the most important things one could work on, and the promise is an environment where people can do their best work.

The operative distinction is 'missionaries not mercenaries': pay matters, but it is a threshold (make it viable), not the lever (impact and autonomy are the lever). Mulgrew is explicit that 'a paycheck is not going to get you out of bed in the morning when stuff gets hard' — and the work is hard. The corollary is that mission-driven recruiting only works when paired with genuinely high-agency conditions (autonomy, a real mandate, the ability to ship), otherwise the mission claim is hollow. They also run a deliberately grueling, technically-targeted selection process with a 0.7-0.8% acceptance rate, treating selectivity itself as part of the value proposition.
