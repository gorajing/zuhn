When Dwarkesh ran a public search for a chief-of-staff/general-manager role, he leveraged his platform across multiple rounds and received close to 1,000 applications including many impressive people. Yet the person he hired — and several of the top finalists — came through mutual-friend references. He was explicitly stunned at how little his public reach helped for this specific type of hire. Sholto notes that hiring is relentlessly the number-one problem for startup CEOs, which compounds the lesson.

The contrast with his video editor hiring is instructive. For editors he successfully used open challenges and competitions to surface global talent (a farmer in Argentina, a freshman in Sri Lanka, a former Mr Beast editor). That worked because editing is a craft where 'willing to work hard and obsess' plus raw output quality are observable at low cost, and there's a real global wage arbitrage. Judgment-heavy generalist roles are different: the best candidates already have many options, rarely browse job postings, and are sourced through trust networks.

Practical rule: for craft/execution roles, run open public challenges that surface hidden global talent. For taste and judgment roles, assume inbound will disappoint and budget serious time for outbound and warm introductions — and treat every mutual-friend lead as higher priority than the application pile.
