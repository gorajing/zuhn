Graham's closing argument is that 'college is where faking stops working' — before this point, you can succeed by following rules and optimizing for metrics. After this point, in any environment that isn't 'like reverting to high school' (i.e., big companies), the only way forward is through genuine engagement with problems you actually care about.

This identifies a phase transition in career dynamics. Pre-college, the reward function is legible and gameable: study what's assigned, perform on tests, collect credentials. Post-college, the problems become open-ended enough that you can't fake your way through them. The implication for hiring and self-assessment: look for the transition point where someone stopped optimizing for external metrics and started following genuine curiosity. People who never made that transition — who kept 'faking' by chasing credentials and status markers — may struggle in environments that demand authentic problem-solving ability.
