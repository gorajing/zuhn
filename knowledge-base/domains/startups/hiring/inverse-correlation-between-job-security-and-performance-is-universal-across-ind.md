Graham identifies a universal pattern: actors, directors, junior professors, and professional athletes — all of whom can be fired easily — consistently deliver high performance. Auto workers, civil servants, and tenured schoolteachers — who are nearly impossible to fire — do not. This inverse correlation between security and performance is so clear he argues you'd have to be willfully blind to miss it.

For startups, rigid labor laws are especially damaging because startups have no redundancy — every person must perform. But the deeper problem isn't just that one bad hire is hard to remove; it's that the entire cultural attitude toward employment shifts when firing is difficult. Employment becomes a paternalistic relationship rather than an economic exchange, and that mindset makes people less likely to start companies in the first place. European soccer is the one domain where Europeans tolerate firing for performance, which Graham notes is at least a precedent.
