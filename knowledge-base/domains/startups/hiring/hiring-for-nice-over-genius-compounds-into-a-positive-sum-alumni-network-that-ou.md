Church's lab has spawned roughly 100 biotech companies — at one dinner Dwarkesh attended, 70-80% of founders had worked with Church. When asked how he spots talent, Church says his three criteria are (1) nice, (2) multidisciplinary, and (3) multidisciplinary again. He explicitly avoids selecting on genius: 'I'm not necessarily looking for geniuses. We end up with a lot of geniuses. But nice is highly predictive of how well you will do in the lab and afterwards.' The multidisciplinarity criterion is a learnability proxy — someone who has already mastered two unrelated skills has proven they can acquire a third, and will bridge between teammates.

The compounding mechanism is social: a lab of nice, multidisciplinary people generates an alumni network that stays cooperative even in a cutthroat field. Former lab members refer talent to each other, collaborate across companies, and surface opportunities for the next generation. This produces a positive feedback loop — early wins attract better candidates who generate more wins — that cannot be bootstrapped from scratch, because the reputation and network density have to accrete over decades. Boston's walkable biotech cluster amplifies the effect: spouses find jobs, collaborators live nearby, and knowledge circulates faster than in distributed geographies.

The counterintuitive lesson is that talent-maximalist hiring ('only geniuses') optimizes the wrong variable. In fields with long time horizons and network effects, cooperation across an alumni graph compounds faster than individual brilliance, and niceness is the load-bearing trait that keeps the graph connected. This directly contradicts the prevailing startup ethos that cultural fit is a luxury and raw talent is everything — Church's track record suggests the opposite.
