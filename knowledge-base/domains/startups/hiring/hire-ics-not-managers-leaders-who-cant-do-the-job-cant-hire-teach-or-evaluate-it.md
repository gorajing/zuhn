Lucy Guo's rule for hiring leadership across Scale AI and her current companies: 'When I hire leaders, I hire ICs. I don't believe in hiring a leader or manager and having to blow up teams. Everyone I look for can do the entire function themselves. I also think they hire better and are able to actually teach the future employees because it's very hard to determine whether your direct reports are doing a good job if you're not actually doing the job yourself. For example, let's say you're a customer support lead. How do you actually know your customer support team is answering tickets on time, answering them well, etc., unless you know how to answer tickets?' The three failure modes of pure-manager hiring: (1) Quality blindness — they can't tell good work from bad in their function. (2) Bad hiring — they hire for surface signals (good interview presentation, prestigious resume) rather than actual capability they can verify. (3) Bad teaching — new hires don't learn the craft because the manager can't model it. The compounding effect: a pure manager in a senior role hires more pure managers underneath, eventually creating a function full of people who manage process without doing work. Once this pattern sets in, the team's output quality is bounded by the most junior IC in the org (because they're the only ones actually doing the work, with no senior calibration). The corollary: when interviewing senior leaders, the right question isn't 'how would you manage this team?' but 'walk me through how YOU would solve this specific problem in this function.' If they can't answer at a craft level, they're a pure manager — pass.
