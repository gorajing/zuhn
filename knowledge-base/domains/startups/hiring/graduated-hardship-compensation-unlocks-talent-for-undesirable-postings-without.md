The US State Department uses a graduated pay system where foreign service officers receive 0-35% hardship bonuses (based on climate, healthcare quality, crime, pollution) plus 0-35% danger bonuses, stacking up to 70% additional pay for postings like Baghdad. The granularity is notable — even within a single country, Dubai gets 5% while Abu Dhabi gets 10%, reflecting micro-level difficulty assessment.

This approach solves the fundamental talent allocation problem: how do you get skilled people to voluntarily go where nobody wants to be? The answer is transparent, graduated pricing of difficulty. Startups face the same challenge when staffing unglamorous but critical roles, on-call rotations, legacy system maintenance, or difficult market expansions. Rather than mandating assignments or hoping for volunteers, explicitly pricing the difficulty converts sacrifice into a market transaction where people self-select based on their personal utility function.
