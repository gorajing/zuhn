Initially engineers wrote The Browser Company's prompts. After the team built prompt tools that let anyone iterate, a person on the strategy and ops team used them over a weekend to rewrite all the prompts, then dropped a Loom on Monday explaining what he did and why. Those prompts alone unlocked a new level of capability, quality, and experience—and led directly to the formation of the company's model-behavior team.

The lesson is to identify, agnostic of role, who at your company can shape your AI product and steer the model. It might not be an engineer—and the only way to find out is to give non-engineers self-serve access to the tools (which connects to building AI tooling into the product so non-engineers can use it).

For hiring and team design, this implies two things: don't assume prompt and behavior work requires an engineering background, and lower the barrier so latent talent across the org can surface itself. The democratization of tooling is what made this discovery possible in the first place.
