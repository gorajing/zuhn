Cliff Weitzman's articulation of why narrow specialists fail in AI-era teams: 'We had a situation where we hired a head of growth, 30 days in nothing, 60 days in nothing. I was like, all right, what's going on? He said, well, we're trying to hire the people. I gave the head of HR the brief. I was like — bro, source the people yourself, message the people yourself, get on the calls yourself. You've got 7 days. In those 7 days, I need to see you making ads in CapCut, editing in Figma or Photoshop or whatever tool, and buying the ads. If that doesn't happen, it's going to be very difficult to keep working together. It reminds me very much of Keith Rabois — we want to hire barrels, not ammunition. Someone who can go from 1 to 10 on their own. I don't want someone who can put the video on CapCut but can't do the editing. I want someone who can take the video, edit it, and post it. I do all of it.' Speechify's hiring criteria explicitly: '(1) Fire in the belly for the product. (2) High loyalty to the team. (3) Ability to learn fast and ship to production.' Plus the team rule: 'Respond to messages within 60 seconds. If you ship a PR to a repo your team is not responsible for, it gets fast-tracked.' The structural argument for why this matters more in AI era: pre-AI, narrow specialists could be productive because each specialist's contribution was sufficient input for the next. The handoff between specialist roles was efficient enough. Post-AI: the productivity of a full-stack executor compounds enormously because AI handles the parts the executor isn't expert in. A barrel + AI = full-team productivity. Ammunition + AI = still ammunition (the AI doesn't fix the handoff problem). The hiring math: 1 barrel + AI tools at $200K/year = output of 5-person team at pre-AI productivity. 5 ammunition at $400K/year combined = same output but worse quality, more coordination overhead. The ROI on barrels is approaching 5x that on ammunition. The corollary for hiring processes: design interview tasks that test full-stack execution. Not 'tell me about your strategy' (anyone can talk strategy) — 'show me you can execute this end-to-end with the tools you'd use on the job.' If candidates respond 'I'd delegate that part,' that's the wrong hire for an AI-era team. The corollary for ambitious operators: deliberately develop full-stack skills. Don't be 'the strategy person.' Be the person who can execute strategy AND ship code AND write copy AND analyze data. The breadth is the moat. The corollary for organizations restructuring around AI: collapse role specializations. The 'Head of X' that doesn't do X anymore is the most expensive hire in the AI era. Either the head does X, or the role is unnecessary.
