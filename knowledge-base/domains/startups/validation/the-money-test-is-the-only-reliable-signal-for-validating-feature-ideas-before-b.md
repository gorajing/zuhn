Shear identifies a specific failure mode in validation: asking users 'would you use this feature?' generates false positives because people default to 'yeah, that sounds great' out of politeness or genuine but shallow interest. The cure is asking for money. Getting someone to hand over their credit card upfront is, in Shear's words, 'amazing' — it clarifies genuine excitement versus polite agreement.

The five-dollar threshold is deliberately low. If someone isn't even five-dollars-excited about your idea, they're not going to change their behavior to adopt it. This test works best for B2B or paid consumer products. For free consumer products (like a student note-taking app), Shear acknowledges this is harder — you have to find proxy signals, like building the minimum hackable prototype (e.g., a browser extension) and measuring actual adoption rather than stated intent.
