IRC arbitrage was genuinely legal and conceptually sound — buying postal coupons cheap in weak-currency countries and redeeming them at higher value in the US. But the shipping costs wiped out margins, and there weren't enough IRCs in existence to sustain the operation. Ponzi's critical failure was refusing to accept that his idea, while valid in theory, was unworkable at scale. Instead of pivoting or abandoning the concept, he preserved the narrative and converted it into fraud. This pattern recurs in startups: founders who fall in love with their story rather than the economics. The pivot point — where a concept works in theory but fails in practice — is where intellectual honesty separates legitimate entrepreneurs from fraudsters.
