The biggest fear investors have with early-stage startups is that founders built something based on their own theories of what the world needs, but that no one will actually want. Sequoia calls this the 'proxy for demand' — what are people doing now, using inadequate tools, that shows they need what you're making? Specific user stories answer this directly.

The hierarchy of convincing demand evidence is: your own need (Apple, Microsoft, Yahoo, Google all grew from founders' needs), needs of people you know personally, and then observed behavior of strangers. Experienced investors recognize this pattern and weight it heavily. Another strong signal is when people pay a lot for something — a cheaper alternative to something popular is an easy sell if you preserve the qualities that made it popular. In all cases, concrete specifics about real people trump abstract market sizing.
