Graham and Lisa Randall argue that the experiment frame fundamentally changes the emotional economics of ambitious projects. Under the default 'bet' frame, failure is pure loss. Under the experiment frame, failure produces information—which is still a return on investment. This is especially powerful when the problem is sharply defined, because a definitive negative result (the theorem is false, the material fails under stress) is itself valuable knowledge.

The experiment frame also provides a natural exit strategy that doesn't feel like quitting: if the experiment conclusively rules something out, you move on. If it suggests a variation that might work, you iterate. This removes the sunk-cost pressure that traps people in failing projects. For founders, this means the psychological cost of trying something ambitious drops dramatically when you commit to learning rather than succeeding.
