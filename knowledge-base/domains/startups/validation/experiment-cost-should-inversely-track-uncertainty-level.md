Large companies habitually treat innovation as placing big bets — funding expensive pilot programs or building full prototypes before validating core assumptions. This approach is backwards: when uncertainty is highest, the probability of being wrong is also highest, so expensive experiments produce expensive failures.

The correct mapping is to invert cost against uncertainty. Early-stage ventures should run landing page tests, card-sort exercises, and customer interviews that cost nearly nothing. Only after these cheap signals converge on a pattern should you graduate to prototypes, then pilot studies, then market launches. This creates an investment staircase where each step is justified by evidence from the previous one, and the total cost of being wrong at any stage remains bounded.
