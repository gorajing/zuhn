The biggest fear investors have with early-stage startups is that you've built something based on a priori theories rather than real need. Sequoia calls it the 'proxy for demand' — what are people doing right now, with inadequate tools, that proves they need what you're making? Specific user stories directly counter this fear in a way that market size figures never can.

Graham ranks the evidence hierarchy: best are stories about your own needs (Apple, Microsoft, Yahoo, Google all grew from founder needs), next are needs of people you know personally. Both carry authenticity that abstract personas or TAM calculations lack. Another strong signal is when people already pay a lot for inferior alternatives — it's easy to convince investors of demand for a cheaper version of something popular.

This reflects a broader principle: concrete, specific evidence always persuades more than abstract, general claims. A single vivid user story creates more conviction than a slide full of market research numbers.
