Emmett Shear describes how Twitch's user research divided subjects into three groups: existing Justin TV broadcasters, broadcasters on competing platforms, and non-broadcasters (gamers who never streamed). Each group revealed completely different priorities. Existing users asked for chat features and UI polish — problems they tolerated anyway. Competitor users revealed dealbreakers like revenue sharing and video stability — problems so severe they refused to switch. Non-users revealed market expansion blockers like hardware limitations and fear of strategy exposure.

The strategic implication is that most startups only talk to their existing users and get a distorted picture. Competitor users tell you what to build to grow short-term (what convinced Twitch to focus on stability and monetization). Non-users tell you what to build to expand the total market (what led Twitch to integrate broadcasting into Xbox and PlayStation). Shear explicitly notes that Twitch focused on competitor users first because converting existing behavior is easier than creating new behavior, especially when under pressure to show 25% month-over-month growth.
