Investors' biggest fear with early-stage startups is that founders have built something based on a priori theories about what the world needs, but that no one will actually want. The antidote is what Sequoia's Greg McAdoo calls the 'proxy for demand' — evidence of what people are doing right now, using inadequate tools, that demonstrates they need what you're making.

This works because it shifts the argument from speculation to observation. Instead of projecting future demand, you're pointing at existing behavior. Related signals include people paying a lot for something (making a cheaper alternative obvious) and founders solving their own problems (Apple, Microsoft, Yahoo, Google all grew from founder needs). Stories about specific users with specific problems are always more persuasive than abstract market analysis, and the most credible stories are about needs you've personally experienced.
