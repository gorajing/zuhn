DoorDash's first version was a static landing page with PDF menus and a personal phone number — no app, no backend, no dispatch system. Yet orders grew from 1 to 10+ per day organically. Stanley Tang's key realization was that when people willingly tolerate a terrible user experience (calling a phone number, browsing PDF menus), it proves the underlying need is so strong that execution details become secondary.

This inverts the common startup instinct to polish before launching. The friction itself becomes a filter: only genuinely motivated users will push through, giving you a higher-quality signal about real demand. If people won't use your product even when it's ugly and broken, no amount of design polish will save it. Conversely, if they use it despite everything being wrong, you've found something worth building properly.
