Hormozi argues that free content serves as a reliable signal for paid content quality: if someone provides high-value, tactical information for free, the probability that their paid offering is worse is low. This creates a practical due diligence framework: follow multiple educators in a space, compare the density and applicability of their free content over seven days, then purchase from the one whose style and substance resonate most.

The counter-intuitive element is his advice to then 'buy them all' — treat each purchase as one class in a self-directed entrepreneurial degree rather than expecting any single program to be transformative. This reframes the risk: instead of needing one $2,000 course to be perfect, you spread $2,000/month across multiple sources, extracting the best ideas from each. The aggregate knowledge from multiple perspectives often exceeds what any single program offers, similar to how investors diversify portfolios to reduce idiosyncratic risk.
