DoorDash's first version was a static landing page with PDF menus and a phone number — users had to call in orders manually. Despite this terrible experience, orders grew from 1 to 2 to 5 to 10 daily through pure word of mouth. Stanley Tang identifies this willingness to tolerate friction as the moment they knew they had real demand.

This inverts the common startup advice to build a great user experience first. The insight is that friction acts as a natural filter — users who push through it have genuine need, not casual curiosity. If people won't use your product when it's ugly and manual, a beautiful version won't save you. If they will, you've found something real. This applies beyond MVPs: any time you can measure user tolerance for friction, you're measuring depth of need.
