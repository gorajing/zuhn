Internet Made Coder's team gave their productivity app to friends who responded with encouraging words and feature requests ('if it just had this one thing, I'd definitely use it'). The team built each requested feature, creating a massive feature list — but analytics showed users dropped off after the first day regardless. The friends' requests were social politeness, not genuine demand.

This is a devastating counterweight to 'get feedback early.' The quality of feedback matters more than the quantity. Friend feedback is structurally corrupted: the social relationship incentivizes positive signals that have zero correlation with purchase or retention intent. The only feedback that matters is behavioral — do users come back day 2, day 7, day 30? If not, no amount of verbal encouragement or feature requests changes the underlying lack of need. The real metric is retention, not reaction.
