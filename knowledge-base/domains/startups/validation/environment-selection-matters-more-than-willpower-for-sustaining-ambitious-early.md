Willpower-based approaches to pushing through discouraging early work ('just tell yourself to press on') are unreliable and get harder with age as standards rise. The more robust strategy is environmental: surround yourself with people who can actually judge early work accurately — distinguishing ugly ducklings from baby swans. Generic encouragement doesn't work because you learn to discount it. What works is colleagues on similar ambitious projects who understand the gap between early and finished work.

This is why university departments, research labs, and startup ecosystems like Silicon Valley function so well — they create 'eddies in the social headwind' where unusual early-stage work gets evaluated by its trajectory rather than its current state. The practical implication is that actively seeking out others attempting new things is not optional social networking but a critical infrastructure decision for ambitious work.
