Paul Graham draws a sharp distinction between environments where gaming works (school, large companies) and startups where it doesn't. In school, you optimize for exam questions rather than mastering material. In big companies, you can succeed by sending emails late at night or sucking up. But startups eliminate the intermediary — there's no boss to impress, no system to game. Users are 'like sharks: too stupid to fool. It's meat or no meat.'

This insight is especially dangerous for high-achievers who have spent 20 years perfecting system-gaming as their primary skill. Their first instinct in a startup is to find 'the trick' — what are the extracurricular activities of startups? What growth hacks exist? Graham argues these founders confuse fundraising (which can be gamed temporarily) with actual success (which cannot). Someone who knows zero about fundraising but has made something users love will have an easier time than someone who knows every trick but has a flat usage graph.

The liberating corollary: if you're someone who hates gaming systems, startups are one of the few domains where authentic work is directly rewarded.
