There is a natural human instinct to polish before presenting — to make things look finished before exposing them to scrutiny. In innovation contexts, this instinct actively works against learning. A low-fidelity prototype (a sketch, a paper mockup, a landing page with no backend) can answer critical questions about desirability at near-zero cost. A high-fidelity prototype answers the same questions at 10-100x the cost, but no more accurately.

The discipline is to match fidelity to your current evidence level. When you know almost nothing, a rough sketch is appropriate. When you've confirmed basic desirability through early tests, a clickable prototype makes sense. When you've validated willingness to pay, an MVP is warranted. Jumping to high fidelity too early doesn't accelerate learning — it just burns resources answering questions you could have answered cheaply. The progression from low to high fidelity should be earned through evidence, not assumed based on internal enthusiasm.
