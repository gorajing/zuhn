The Rashmi Sinha analogy — founding vision as scientific hypothesis — isn't just motivational framing. It imports a specific epistemic discipline: hypotheses must be falsifiable. A vision that can't be broken into testable claims isn't bold, it's unfalsifiable, and unfalsifiable beliefs are the most dangerous thing a founder can carry.

This reframe changes the founder's relationship with their idea from emotional conviction to structured uncertainty. You still believe in the vision, but you've specified the conditions under which you'd update or abandon it. That's not weakness — it's the mechanism by which you convert uncertainty into knowledge as cheaply as possible. The scientific method's power comes precisely from its willingness to be wrong, and startups that adopt this stance waste less capital discovering what doesn't work.
