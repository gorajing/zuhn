Investors' biggest fear with early-stage startups is that founders built something based on a priori theories rather than real demand. The antidote is what Sequoia calls a 'proxy for demand': concrete evidence of people using inadequate tools to solve the problem you're addressing. This is fundamentally stronger than citing market size numbers because it shows revealed preference—people are already spending time and money on inferior solutions.

The hierarchy of demand evidence is clear: best is when founders built for their own need (Apple, Microsoft, Yahoo, Google all started this way), next is needs of people you know personally, and after that comes observed behavior of strangers. Each step down the ladder loses credibility because the observer's understanding of the problem becomes more theoretical. This framework also explains why cheaper alternatives to expensive popular products are easy pitches—the existing spending is undeniable proof of demand.
