RuBisCo evolved when atmospheric oxygen was negligible. As plants themselves raised oxygen levels, RuBisCo began mistakenly fixing O2 instead of CO2 roughly half the time, producing a toxic byproduct. Yet 450 million years later, every plant on Earth still uses it — there are 40 billion tons of it at any moment. Plants compensate by overproducing RuBisCo and evolving detoxification pathways rather than replacing the enzyme.

This is a powerful analogy for technology and business lock-in. Once an ecosystem builds deeply around a flawed core component, the cost of replacement exceeds the cost of endless workarounds. Startups should recognize that capturing ecosystem dependency early — even with an imperfect product — creates moats that survive competitors with technically superior alternatives. Conversely, founders attacking entrenched incumbents must understand they're not just competing on quality; they're competing against the entire workaround infrastructure built around the incumbent's flaws.
