The 'Build' in Build-Measure-Learn is widely misinterpreted as 'build a smaller version of the final product.' In practice, a landing page measuring click-through rates or a card-sort exercise ranking customer jobs-to-be-done can generate more useful signal at a fraction of the cost. The goal of early experiments is not to demonstrate technical feasibility but to reduce uncertainty about customer behavior and willingness to pay.

Strategyzer's work with consumer goods teams illustrates this: before investing in expensive technology prototypes, teams built web landing pages and ran prioritization exercises with customers. These experiments cost almost nothing but produced actionable evidence about which jobs-to-be-done actually mattered. The discipline is to ask 'what do I need to learn?' before asking 'what can I build?' — and then design the cheapest possible experiment that answers the learning question.
