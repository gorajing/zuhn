Shear makes the counterintuitive argument that non-users are often the most important people to interview. For most new products, the vast majority of potential users aren't using any solution — yours or competitors'. If you only talk to current users and competitor users, you optimize for a tiny slice of the total addressable market and never learn what prevents the rest from participating.

For Twitch, non-user interviews revealed that computers weren't fast enough, broadcasting software was too complex, and professional gamers feared strategy leaks. None of these problems would ever surface from talking to active broadcasters. This led Twitch to invest in areas no user ever requested: partnering with broadcasting software companies, building broadcasting directly into Xbox and PlayStation, and buying computers for potential broadcasters. These infrastructure investments expanded the total market rather than just shuffling users between competitors.
