Three counterintuitive signals that an idea is actually good: First, it's hard to get started (Paul Graham's 'schlep blindness'). Stripe was obvious to thousands of developers who knew payment integration sucked, but none tried because you had to get a special bank deal and learn credit card infrastructure. This scared everyone off and left a $100B opportunity on the table.

Second, boring spaces. Gusto (payroll software) is boring, but boring ideas have much higher hit rates than fun ideas because they get left on the table for years. And the day-to-day of running any startup is mostly the same — writing code, fixing bugs, talking to users — so initial excitement has little correlation with actual enjoyment.

Third, existing competitors. Dropbox was the 20th cloud storage company, which naively looked like a terrible market. But most people used none of those 20 products, which actually proved demand existed but hadn't been served well. The key was Drew's technical insight that syncing directly with the OS was the step-function UX improvement all competitors missed.
