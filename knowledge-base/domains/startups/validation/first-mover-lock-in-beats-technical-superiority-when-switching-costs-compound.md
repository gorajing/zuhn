RuBisCo is objectively bad at its job — it confuses oxygen for CO2 roughly half the time, creating toxic byproducts that plants must spend energy cleaning up. Yet it remains the most abundant protein on Earth (40 billion tons) because it was the first enzyme to fix carbon, and all plant life evolved around it.

This mirrors startup dynamics where first movers with adequate-but-imperfect solutions become so deeply embedded in ecosystems that replacement becomes structurally impossible. The lesson isn't that quality doesn't matter — it's that once dependencies compound, the switching cost eclipses the performance gap. Founders should recognize that being 'good enough first' often beats being 'perfect later,' and that their own early technical choices may similarly become permanent whether they like it or not.
