Cheung describes a 'honesty curve' where feedback quality varies by relationship distance and whether the product is paid. For free products, your mom lies the most (proud of you regardless), friends are moderately honest, and strangers don't care enough to give useful feedback. But for paid products, the curve inverts at the stranger end — people who paid money and feel it wasn't worth it will tell you exactly why.

This has a practical implication: the common startup advice to 'get users first, monetize later' actively delays your access to the most honest feedback signal. The faster you charge, the faster you learn what actually needs fixing. This doesn't mean charging on day one, but it means the path to charging should be compressed — not deferred until you have product-market fit, because charging IS how you discover product-market fit.
