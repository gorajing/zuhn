Investors evaluating early-stage startups are primarily screening for one failure mode: founders who built something based on untested theories about what the world needs. Abstract market-size arguments don't counter this fear—specific stories about specific users do.

Sequoia looks for 'proxy for demand': what are people doing right now with inadequate tools that signals they need what you're building? Another strong signal is when people pay a lot for existing solutions, because a cheaper alternative to something popular is an easy sell. The most compelling stories are about your own needs—Apple, Microsoft, Yahoo, and Google all grew from founder needs. Experienced investors know this pattern, so founder-as-user stories get outsized attention. The hierarchy is: your own need > needs of people you know personally > abstract market research.
