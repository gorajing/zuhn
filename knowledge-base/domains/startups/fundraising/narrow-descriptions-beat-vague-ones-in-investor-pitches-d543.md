Founders resist concise descriptions because early-stage startups have many possibilities, and a tight description feels misleadingly narrow. But Graham argues this instinct is backwards: as you approach a description of something that could be anything, the content approaches zero. Investors will discard vague language like 'collaboratively leverage the value of information' as meaningless boilerplate.

The better approach mirrors incremental development: start with a description that's gripping but perhaps overly narrow, then flesh it out. At every point you need a 'working description' in the investor's head—a concrete mental model they can evaluate and remember. The calculus metaphor is precise: you're converging on the right answer through successive approximations, not trying to capture the entire solution space at once.
