Graham argues that top VC firms like Sequoia deliberately take artificially low ownership percentages, not because they can't negotiate more, but to keep founders feeling ownership. At 30%, Sequoia needs to improve outcomes by just 43% to break even for founders. Graham claims the signaling value alone—being able to say you're Sequoia-funded—likely exceeds that threshold for average startups.

The catch is selection: Sequoia funds roughly 20 out of 6,000 annual applicants (1 in 300), so the companies that get this deal are far from average. This creates an interesting paradox where the deal is most valuable to those least likely to get it, and those who do get it may have succeeded regardless. The insight is that VC brand premium is a real, quantifiable asset that should factor into dilution math.
