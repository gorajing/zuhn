Marc Andreessen describes a fundamental tension in venture investing: the default approach is to check boxes (good founder, good idea, good product, good customers) and invest when everything looks reasonable. But these checkbox deals rarely produce outlier returns. The companies that generate 97% of all venture returns — roughly 15 per year out of 4,000 — almost always have one dimension of extreme strength paired with serious, visible flaws.

This has a direct implication for founders: if every investor says your company looks 'solid' but nobody is wildly enthusiastic about one specific dimension, you may have a checkbox company. The counterintuitive advice is that having a glaring weakness is acceptable — even expected — as long as you have something that makes you genuinely remarkable along one important axis. Andreessen explicitly states that ruling out companies with serious flaws would have excluded almost every major winner in VC history.
