A VC speaking at YC described investing in a company whose business model he knew was wrong — and expected it to change three times before they got it right. The founders were experienced repeat entrepreneurs who'd just raised millions, and even their model was bad. This is the norm, not the exception.

Smart investors know this. So when you spend your precious few minutes on revenue projections and pricing strategy, you're talking about guesses in an area that's probably not your expertise. Worse, if the model sounds spectacularly wrong, it becomes the thing investors remember — not your elegant solution to an important problem. The opportunity cost is enormous: you could be demonstrating deep domain knowledge about the problem space, which is the thing that actually differentiates strong founders from weak ones.
