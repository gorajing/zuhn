Investors' primary fear at the early stage isn't that the market is too small — it's that the founders built something from a priori theory that no one actually wants. Specific user stories directly counter this fear by demonstrating observed demand rather than projected demand. Sequoia calls this the 'proxy for demand': what are people doing now, with inadequate tools, that proves the need exists?

Graham identifies a hierarchy of story credibility: stories about your own needs are strongest (Apple, Microsoft, Google all started this way), followed by needs of people you know personally. This hierarchy tracks trust — investors can verify whether founders genuinely experience a problem far more easily than whether an abstract market segment does. The practical implication is that founders should mine their own experience and immediate network for the most visceral, specific pain points rather than constructing top-down market narratives.
