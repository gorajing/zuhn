Graham describes how most investors rely on herd signals—they invest if others want in, and pass if others pass. As founders gain leverage, they can increasingly punish this behavior by giving priority to investors who commit unilaterally. Herd-followers get last place in line, which in oversubscribed rounds means missing the deal entirely.

The counterintuitive insight is that removing this crutch may actually improve investor returns. Chasing hot deals doesn't improve selection quality—it just provides emotional comfort. Investors forced to evaluate startups independently will develop better judgment. This maps to a broader principle: removing information shortcuts that feel useful but are actually noise leads to better decisions.
