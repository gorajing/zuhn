The VC math is brutal: raise $20M, give away 50%, sell for $30M. First return $20M to investors, split the remaining $10M with investors (they own 50%), then split your share with co-founders and team equity. After 10 years of founder-level stress, you might net six figures — less than you'd have earned at a regular job. But if you bootstrap and sell for $30M, you keep all of it.

This is the most powerful counterweight to the 'raise money to grow fast' narrative. The author learned this the hard way — she raised money, built product, hired team, then realized her TAM could never support a billion-dollar outcome. Even hypothetical 100% market capture wouldn't reach $1B. She returned the money, fired everyone, and started over. The key insight: VCs want to invest in companies that don't need money (because those have working products). If you need their money, it's a red flag for them too. The fundraising game is fundamentally about creating the illusion that you don't need capital.
