Graham argues that detailed business model discussion in early pitches has negative expected value for two reasons: first, any business model at this stage is probably wrong (even experienced founders with top-tier VC backing typically pivot their model multiple times). Second, a spectacularly wrong business model pushes out the things you want investors to remember — they'll recall you as 'the company with the boneheaded monetization plan' rather than 'the company that solved that important problem.'

The key insight is about attention allocation under time constraints. You sound smarter talking about the problem you deeply understand than guessing about business dynamics that aren't your expertise yet. Briefly acknowledging you've thought about revenue shows responsibility; diving deep into it wastes your strongest minutes on your weakest material.

This generalizes to any persuasion context: lead with your strongest, most differentiated content and minimize time on areas where you have no defensible advantage.
