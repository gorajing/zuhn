In 2015, SVB CEO Greg Becker actively lobbied lawmakers to exempt banks with under $250 billion in assets from the enhanced supervision and stress-testing requirements of the Dodd-Frank Act — regulations specifically designed after the 2008 crisis to prevent bank failures. In 2018, the bill passed. Five years later, SVB collapsed from precisely the kind of interest rate and liquidity risk that stress tests are designed to surface.

The pattern is a reliable signal: when regulated entities successfully lobby to weaken their own oversight, it reveals both the intent to take on more risk and the removal of the mechanism that would force course correction. Cornell law professor Saule Omarova, who testified against the 2018 relaxation, was proven right. For startup founders choosing banking partners, and for investors evaluating systemic risk, active regulatory lobbying by financial institutions should be treated as a red flag, not a sign of business savvy.
