Graham notes that charismatic candidates tend to have more skeletons than 'squeaky clean dullards,' yet this rarely costs them elections — they just say they've reformed and stonewall the details. The same dynamic plays out in fundraising and hiring: a compelling founder who owns a past failure with confidence often gets more benefit-of-the-doubt than a cautious founder with a clean record but flat delivery. This suggests that parties (and startups) shouldn't over-index on sanitized backgrounds when selecting leaders; the charisma that attracts controversy is often the same quality that wins support. The risk, of course, is that this tolerance can enable genuinely bad actors.
