Graham makes a striking claim: 'Birds fly; fish swim; deals fall through.' This isn't just colorful language—it reflects a structural feature of startup investing. Investors making high-risk bets are prone to extreme buyer's remorse. They don't fully grasp the risk they're taking until the deal is about to close, and then they panic. This happens at every level, from individual angels ghosting founders to billion-dollar acquisitions collapsing.

The compounding problem is self-deception. Founders want the deal to close, so they start believing it will, creating a dangerous gap between perceived and actual probability. This leads to the cardinal sin of fundraising: stopping the pursuit of alternatives when one investor says yes. The practical rule is to never consider a deal done until money is in the bank, and to maintain a pipeline of alternatives as insurance—not because you're pessimistic, but because you understand the base rate.
