The standard VC evaluation framework — growth curves, retention metrics, market share — breaks down completely for stealth startups. Smart investors replace these with four proxy signals: team execution history (have they shipped before?), technical defensibility (is there a genuine IP wedge?), stealth rationale clarity (can they articulate why silence is strategic, not evasive?), and network signal (have credible people quietly joined the cap table?).

This creates an interesting selection effect. Stealth fundraising heavily favors repeat founders with strong networks, because the evaluation proxies all depend on pre-existing reputation. First-time founders with no network are explicitly called out as poor candidates for stealth mode — not because their ideas are worse, but because the trust infrastructure required to raise without proof doesn't exist yet. The article's red flags for 'fake stealth' — vague milestones, no prototype, defensiveness around basic questions — are essentially tests for whether stealth is earned strategy or laziness disguised as mystery.
