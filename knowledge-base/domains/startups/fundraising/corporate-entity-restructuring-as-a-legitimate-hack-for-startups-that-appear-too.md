The founders relay advice from Silicon Valley investors: founders who spent years consulting, pivoting, and testing before finding product-market fit often face investor skepticism because their company looks 'too old' relative to its stage. The hack is straightforward — incorporate a new company, transfer the relevant resources and team, and pitch the new entity with a fresh incorporation date while leveraging all prior learnings.

This challenges the assumption that company age is a fixed constraint in fundraising. It's a legitimate restructuring that many successful founders have used, particularly those whose path to PMF was non-linear. The key nuance is that this isn't deceptive — the new entity genuinely represents a focused, refined version of what was learned. Investors care about trajectory and momentum, not the legal entity's age per se, so reframing the timeline around when the current product/approach crystallized is both honest and strategically sound.
