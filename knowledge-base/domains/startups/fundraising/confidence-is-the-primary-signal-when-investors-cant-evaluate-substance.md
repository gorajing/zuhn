Graham identifies an asymmetry in investor pitches: founders who are bad at explaining talk to investors who are bad at understanding. Given brief time and limited technical background, investors fall back on meta-signals—chiefly, whether the founders themselves seem genuinely impressed by what they've built.

Critically, this confidence must be implicit. Saying 'we're passionate' or 'our product is great' gets ignored or triggers bullshit detectors. The test Graham proposes is existential: if you don't genuinely believe investors would be doing themselves a favor by investing, why are you investing your own time? This reframe shifts confidence from a performance skill to an authenticity check—fake confidence fails, but real conviction about the problem you're solving reads naturally.
