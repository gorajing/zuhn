Both Sacks and Flint emphasize a mental model shift that separates survivors from casualties: stop assuming the next round is 12-18 months away. Flint's Trulia raised a $15M Series B in Q2 2008 and didn't get an upround for over three years—they had to go public to get one, despite doubling revenue year over year. The fundraising market can stay closed far longer than founders expect.

Sacks recommends 8-10 quarters (2-2.5 years) of runway because the math demands it: 2-3 quarters to adapt and restructure, potentially a year to pivot to a new model, another year to prove the new model works with real growth data, then time to actually run a fundraising process. The dot-com bust lasted two years of gradual erosion, and companies that held out hope of a quick recovery 'one by one fell by the wayside.' The PayPal case is instructive—with $40M and $10M/month burn (4 months of runway), they were forced to charge for a product they'd promised would be free, and it worked. The constraint of 'there is no Plan B' produced the revenue model that made PayPal viable.
