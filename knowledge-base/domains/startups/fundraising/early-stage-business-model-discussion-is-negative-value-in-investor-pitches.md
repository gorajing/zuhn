A top VC told Y Combinator founders that even experienced repeat founders with millions in funding had a business model that was 'crap' and would change three times. He invested anyway because he expected it to be wrong at that stage. Smart investors know this.

The danger is asymmetric: spending time on your business model is at best neutral (showing you care about revenue) and at worst catastrophic (investors remember you as 'the company with the boneheaded monetization plan' instead of 'the company solving that important problem'). Time spent on speculative guesses about areas outside your expertise directly displaces time you could spend on solid, interesting things you actually know — the problem and what you've built.
