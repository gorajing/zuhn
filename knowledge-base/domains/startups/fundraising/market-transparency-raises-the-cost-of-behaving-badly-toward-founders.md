Graham observes that investor behavior toward founders has improved materially over the past two decades, and attributes this to structural changes — not moral evolution. As the startup world became more connected (via YC, Twitter, founder communities), information about investor conduct aggregates faster and spreads wider. An investor who mistreats a founder may not face a press story, but loses deal access as the signal circulates through founder networks.

YC is specifically cited as an aggregator of this data: because it sees behavior across thousands of startups, it has a comprehensive picture of which investors are trustworthy. This institutionalizes reputation accountability in a way that didn't exist when deals were more siloed.

The practical implication: behavior that was previously private (how an investor treats a founder in a down round, how they behave on a board) is now semi-public within the ecosystem. This shifts the incentive structure for investors and creates natural selection pressure toward better behavior — not because people became nicer, but because the market now prices character.
