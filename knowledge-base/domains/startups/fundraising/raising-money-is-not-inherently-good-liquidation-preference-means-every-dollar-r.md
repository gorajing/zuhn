Elliot explains a critical nuance most first-time founders miss: raising money isn't automatically good. Ideally, you use profits to grow. When you can't, you sell equity — but investors get liquidation preference (typically 1x). This means in any acquisition, invested capital gets returned first before equity splits.

Concrete example: raise $10M, sell for $15M. Investors get their $10M back first. The remaining $5M is then split by ownership percentages. For founders who raised at high valuations, this can mean seeing almost nothing on a moderate exit. The strategic implication: every dollar you raise increases the minimum exit threshold at which founders actually benefit. This makes the decision of how much to raise — not just whether to raise — one of the most consequential financial decisions a startup makes.
