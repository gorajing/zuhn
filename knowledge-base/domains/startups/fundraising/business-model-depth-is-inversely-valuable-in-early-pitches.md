Graham relays a VC who invested in a company while openly acknowledging their business model was wrong and would change three times. The founders were experienced repeat entrepreneurs with millions in funding, and even their model was 'crap.' The investor expected this.

This reveals an asymmetry in what investors actually evaluate versus what founders think they evaluate. Time spent on business model details is doubly wasteful: it consumes minutes that could showcase the problem and solution (where founders have genuine expertise), and if the model seems spectacularly wrong, it becomes the thing investors remember—displacing the actually compelling parts of your pitch. The business model is guesses about stuff that's probably not your area of expertise; the problem you're solving is where you sound smart.
