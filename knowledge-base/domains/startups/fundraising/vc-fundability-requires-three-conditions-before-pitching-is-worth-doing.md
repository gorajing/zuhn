Venture capital has a specific investment thesis: they need 10x-100x returns on a portfolio basis, which means they only fund companies that can grow very large very fast. A business that is high-quality but not scalable (e.g. a services firm, a niche product) will be rejected regardless of deck quality. Pitching it anyway trains investors to mentally file you under 'not VC-backable.'

The three checks — scalability, large addressable market, and positive customer signals — are not just VC requirements; they're filters for whether the venture model is the right funding path at all. Many founders who fail to raise VC should instead be bootstrapping or raising from angels.

This is a classic case of confusing activity (pitching) with progress (securing the right funding). Pre-qualifying removes wasted cycles and protects founder credibility in a small, networked community where reputation compounds.
