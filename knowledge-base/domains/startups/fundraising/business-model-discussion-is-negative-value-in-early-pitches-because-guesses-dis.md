Graham makes a stronger claim than 'don't waste time on business models' — he argues the discussion is actively negative-value. A VC who spoke at YC described investing in experienced founders whose business model was wrong and would probably change three times. If seasoned repeat founders with millions in funding have bad business models, early-stage founders' models are certainly wrong too.

The damage is twofold. First, it's an opportunity cost: minutes spent on monetization guesses are minutes not spent on the problem and product, where founders sound genuinely smart. Second, a spectacularly wrong business model creates a negative halo effect — investors remember you as 'the company with the boneheaded monetization plan' rather than 'the company that solved that important problem.' The business model crowds out the substance in investor memory.
