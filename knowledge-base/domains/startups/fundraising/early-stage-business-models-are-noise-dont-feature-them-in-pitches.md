A VC speaking at YC described investing in a company whose business model he knew was wrong and would change three times. He invested anyway because at the early stage, the model is just guesses about stuff outside the founders' expertise. The real signal is whether you've found an important problem and built something compelling.

This creates a counterintuitive failure mode: if your business model seems spectacularly wrong, it pushes out everything else from investors' memory. They'll remember you as 'the company with the boneheaded monetization plan' rather than 'the company that solved that important problem.' The takeaway is ruthless prioritization of pitch minutes toward your strengths (problem, product, traction) and away from your weaknesses (revenue projections, market sizing).
