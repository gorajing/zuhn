A VC Graham cites invested in a company whose business model 'would probably change three times before they got it right' — and they knew this when writing the check. At the early stage, business model is a set of guesses in an area that's probably outside the founders' expertise. Talking about it at length signals poor prioritization of scarce pitch time.

Worse, if the business model seems badly wrong, it crowds out the things investors actually want to remember: the problem you're solving and what you've built. They'll remember you as the company with the bad monetization plan, not as the team that identified an important unsolved problem.

The optimal use of limited pitch time is to establish problem importance and product existence — areas where founders have actual knowledge and evidence. Business model deserves a brief mention to show you've thought about it, then move on.
