Investors have heard thousands of pitches. Explicit claims of quality or passion have been conditioned into meaninglessness through overuse. Worse, stating them can mark you as naive about investor norms or, more charitably, as someone who relies on assertion rather than demonstration.

The mechanism PG describes is that investors are often evaluating without enough technical context to judge the product directly. In that vacuum, founder demeanor becomes the primary signal. A founder who presents nervously or apologetically implies, at a subconscious level, that they don't fully believe in what they've built.

The reframe that makes this actionable: if you've genuinely built something valuable, presenting to investors is doing them a favor. You're the one with something worth having. If you don't feel that way, it may be a signal about the product itself — not a presentation coaching problem.
