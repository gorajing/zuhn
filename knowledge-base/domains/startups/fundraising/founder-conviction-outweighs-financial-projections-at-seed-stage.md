The unicorn deck analysis reveals that the companies that became massive looked almost nothing like what their pitch decks described. Uber pitched as a luxury black car service. Airbnb's revenue projections were wildly wrong. Facebook's deck was barely a fundraising document. Yet all raised successfully.

This means the actual content of seed decks — the TAM numbers, the revenue projections, the competitive landscape — matters far less than the founder's conviction about an insight they've seen that others haven't. Coinbase pitched when Bitcoin was $6.25, and the deck reads like a missionary manifesto. The implication is that seed investors are pattern-matching on founder conviction and clarity of thinking, not on the accuracy of early-stage financial models that everyone knows are fiction.
