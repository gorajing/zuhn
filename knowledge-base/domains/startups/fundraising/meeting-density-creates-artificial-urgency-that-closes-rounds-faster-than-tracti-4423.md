Graham argues that fundraising grinds founder-only companies to a complete halt for ~6 weeks, making the cost of slow investors enormous. A reputable investor offering $100k with a 24-hour decision window would see every good deal first, because founders would rationally approach the fastest money before slower alternatives.

This inverts the conventional wisdom that access to deals comes from brand, network, or check size. Instead, the binding constraint is speed. Investors notorious for slow decisions or heavy negotiation get pushed to the back of the queue — and for the best startups, 'last' becomes 'never.' The insight generalizes: in any market where supplier time is scarce, the fastest buyer wins.
