Graham recounts a VC who invested in a company despite knowing their business model was wrong — he expected it to be wrong at that stage, even though the founders were experienced repeat entrepreneurs. The implication is stark: if even seasoned founders get business models wrong early on, spending your limited pitch time on one is doubly wasteful.

The opportunity cost matters most. Every minute spent on speculative revenue guesses is a minute not spent on your actual strengths: the problem you understand deeply and the solution you've built. Worse, a spectacularly wrong business model can actively push the memorable parts out of investors' minds, leaving them remembering you as 'the company with the boneheaded monetization plan' rather than 'the company that solved that important problem.'
