Andy Rachleff's onion theory frames a startup as a stack of risks on day one: founding team risk, product risk, technical risk, launch risk, market acceptance risk, revenue risk, cost-of-sale risk, viral growth risk. Each funding round should peel away specific layers. Seed money eliminates founding team and initial product risk. The A round tackles deeper product risk, recruiting risk, and first customer risk. The B round addresses the next set.

The practical application is that founders should pitch investors by narrating their risk-peeling journey: here's what the seed eliminated, here's what the A eliminated, here's what the B will target, and here's the state I'll be in for the C. This is especially critical for capital-intensive businesses where imprecise fundraising — raising too much or too little per round — compounds into fatal cap table problems or insufficient runway to reach the next milestone. The framework replaces the naive 'raise as much as possible' approach with surgical capital deployment.
