Graham distinguishes between legitimate and manufactured urgency in fundraising. If you're genuinely progressing toward an offer from investor B but prefer investor A, telling A creates real pressure without manipulation. However, investors are oversensitive to fabricated urgency because founders attempt it constantly.

The practical protocol: share that competing interest exists but never reveal which specific firms. VCs will ask, but disclosing names lets them coordinate or undermines your position. The deflection is simple — you wouldn't want them sharing your conversations either. This framework applies broadly to any multi-party negotiation: truthful competitive signals accelerate decisions, but the moment a signal is perceived as manufactured, it destroys more trust than the urgency was worth.
