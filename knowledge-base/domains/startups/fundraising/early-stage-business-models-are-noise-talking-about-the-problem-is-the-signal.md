Graham relays a VC who invested in a company whose business model he expected to change three times. The founders were experienced and well-funded, yet their business model was still wrong. This reveals that sophisticated investors discount early business models entirely—they're evaluating the team's understanding of the problem space, not their revenue projections.

Worse, a spectacularly wrong business model becomes the memorable thing, crowding out what you actually want investors to remember. The information asymmetry works against you: you're guessing about business dynamics (not your expertise) when you could be demonstrating deep problem understanding (your actual expertise). This is a form of Gresham's Law applied to attention—bad content drives out good.
