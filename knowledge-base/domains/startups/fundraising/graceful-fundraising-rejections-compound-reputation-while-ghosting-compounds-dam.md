When Sequoia passes on a deal, they invest real effort in explaining why — sometimes writing detailed memos, sometimes offering specific suggestions for what to focus on. Alfred Lin experienced this firsthand running Zappos: Sequoia passed 2-3 times with thoughtful feedback each round, and when Zappos reached the right stage, they partnered.

This practice works because founder networks are dense and reputation compounds in both directions. A thoughtful rejection signals respect and genuine engagement, making founders more likely to return later or refer others. Ghosting — the default behavior at most firms — saves time per interaction but destroys reputation at the network level. The asymmetry is stark: the cost of a graceful pass is thirty minutes of writing, while the cost of ghosting is unmeasurable lost deal flow over years.
