Ron Conway's dominance in early-stage investing stems not from superior pattern-matching or capital, but from being authentically good — a trait that generated referrals from Google, Facebook, and Twitter founders. Paul Graham argues this is not incidental: in a transparent market, bad behavior leaks out and costs deals, so reputation built on genuine character compounds while reputation built on performance degrades.

The key insight is that unpredictability amplifies this effect. When you cannot predict who the next hot founder will be, you must treat everyone well — and only people who are genuinely good can sustain that consistently. Strategic niceness requires knowing who to target; authentic goodness requires no calculation. This makes character a durable moat in high-uncertainty, high-connectivity domains.

The implication for founders evaluating investors — and for anyone building a career in a networked field — is that the 'nice' investors who close deals fastest are often the most successful long-term, not despite their character but because of it. Character is not a soft factor; it's a structural advantage in information-rich environments.
