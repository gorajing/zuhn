Graham cites a VC who invested in a company whose business model he knew was 'crap' — expecting it to change three times before they got it right. The founders were experienced repeat entrepreneurs with millions in funding, and even their model was wrong. This is the norm, not the exception.

The implication is that business model slides are a trap for founders. They shift conversation to your weakest area (guesses about monetization) and away from your strongest (the problem you've lived with and the solution you've built). Worse, a spectacularly wrong business model can become the thing investors remember about you, displacing the actual substance. The meta-lesson: in any high-stakes communication, spend your limited time on your strongest signals, not on expected-to-be-wrong projections.
