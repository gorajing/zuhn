Founders resist narrow descriptions because they fear underselling — the product could be so much more than its current form. But as a description approaches 'everything,' its information content approaches zero. 'A system to collaboratively leverage the value of information' conveys nothing; 'an easy web-based database' is immediately graspable.

Graham frames this as an incremental development analogy: start with a working prototype (a gripping, possibly narrow description), then flesh it out. The goal at pitch time isn't to capture the full vision — it's to convince investors you're worth a follow-up conversation. A compelling narrow description achieves that; a vague expansive one doesn't.

This principle generalizes beyond fundraising: the inability to describe your startup in one clear phrase is often a signal that the plans themselves lack focus. Writing the phrase is as much a diagnostic tool for founders as a communication tactic for investors.
