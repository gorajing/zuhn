Every generation has produced major bootstrapped companies: Microsoft raised one pre-IPO round for board-seat reasons, not capital. Dell never raised money. Yahoo and eBay raised rounds they never touched. Instagram was extremely lean. Midjourney is completely bootstrapped. The question isn't 'should I raise?' but 'does my business model require capital to win the market faster?'

Gil identifies a geographic bias: Silicon Valley companies bootstrap too little (raising VC for things that could be great cash-flow businesses), while companies outside Silicon Valley raise too little (staying small when capital could unlock massive growth). The decision framework: if customers won't pay early, or you need heavy sales/product investment, raise. If you can charge early and get high leverage, consider bootstrapping. Parker Conrad at Rippling is the counter-example — amazing execution backed by aggressive fundraising specifically to win a market. Both styles work, but only for certain business types.
