Most VCs sell themselves on qualitative claims — 'we're founder-friendly,' 'we have the best network,' 'we add real value beyond capital.' But these claims are nearly impossible to verify before signing a term sheet. The hard questions that actually differentiate great VCs from mediocre ones involve measurable outcomes: fund-level return multiples, follow-on rates of portfolio companies, median time to next round, and concrete examples of value delivered.

Founders who treat VC selection as their own due diligence process — rather than feeling grateful for any term sheet — make better partnership decisions. This is especially critical because the VC-founder relationship typically lasts 7-10 years, longer than most marriages. Asking uncomfortable questions about track record before committing is not adversarial; it's responsible capital allocation from the founder's side.
