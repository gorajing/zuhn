A VC speaking at Y Combinator described investing in experienced founders whose business model he knew was wrong—and expected it to change three times before they got it right. He invested anyway because at the early stage, the business model is just a bunch of guesses about stuff that's probably not the founders' area of expertise.

The implication for pitch time allocation is clear: mention that you've thought about monetization (it shows you care), but don't go deep. If you're solving an important problem, you'll sound much smarter talking about that than speculating about revenue models. Worse, a spectacularly wrong business model can push the things you want investors to remember out of their heads—they'll remember you as 'the company with the boneheaded plan' rather than 'the company that solved that important problem.'
