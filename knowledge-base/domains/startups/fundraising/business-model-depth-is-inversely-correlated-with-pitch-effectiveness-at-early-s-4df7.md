Graham recounts a VC who invested in experienced founders despite their business model being 'crap' — because he expected it to be crap at that stage. The model would change three times before they got it right. This reveals a fundamental asymmetry: your problem understanding is your area of genuine expertise, while your business model is just guesses about domains you probably don't know well.

Worse, a spectacularly wrong business model actively displaces what you want investors to remember. They'll categorize you as 'the company with the boneheaded monetization plan' rather than 'the company solving that important problem.' The optimal strategy is to mention monetization briefly to show you've thought about it, then spend your limited time on solid, interesting things you actually know about.
