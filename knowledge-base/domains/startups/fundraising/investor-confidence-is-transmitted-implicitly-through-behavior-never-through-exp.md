When investors can't fully evaluate early-stage technology due to time constraints and limited technical background, they rely heavily on founder confidence as a proxy signal. But this confidence must be demonstrated, not declared. Saying 'we're passionate' triggers the same skepticism as a restaurant with a sign reading 'GOOD FOOD'—if it were true, you wouldn't need to say it. The audience either ignores the claim or actively marks you as a bullshitter.

The flip side is equally important: nervousness and apologetic behavior are devastating because they signal that even you aren't convinced. Graham frames this as a litmus test—if you don't genuinely believe investors would be doing themselves a favor by investing, you should reconsider what your company is doing. This connects to a deeper truth about persuasion: the most convincing people aren't performing confidence, they're channeling genuine conviction about their work.
