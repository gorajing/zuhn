Once a startup has raised initial money, it is literally more valuable — same company plus cash in the bank, plus reduced risk of failure. This economically justifies a higher valuation for subsequent investors. However, Graham warns that investors react emotionally to price increases, sometimes to the point of acting against their own interests.

He cites an investor who passed on a $5M cap because a friend got in at $3M, only to later invest at $15M. The tactical advice is clear: only raise price on investors you're comfortable losing. This reveals a broader principle — in negotiation contexts where relationships matter, being economically right doesn't protect you from emotionally-driven counterparty decisions.
