By Series A, the cap table transforms from a historical ownership record into a real-time signal about how the founder thinks and operates. VCs reverse-engineer dilution history, forecast exit returns, and test whether the structure is fundable and scalable — all from a single document. Multiple convertible notes with varying caps and discounts, fully vested founders with no re-vesting schedule, equal equity splits that no longer reflect contribution, and absent governance structures are all red flags that compound. Each one individually might be explainable, but together they paint a picture of a founder who doesn't understand their business as an investable asset. The strategic move is to consolidate convertible instruments before the raise, reintroduce founder vesting (standard 4-year with 1-year cliff), establish a formal board with clear voting thresholds, and build a basic waterfall model showing returns across multiple exit scenarios. This preparation isn't just about passing diligence — it's about entering negotiations with leverage.
