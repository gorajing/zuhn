Synthesia's journey illustrates how founder conviction functions as a compounding asset. After being rejected 100 times and turning down acquisition offers from Adobe and Meta worth up to $3 billion, the company raised $180 million to continue building independently. The decision to reject guaranteed outcomes in favor of long-term vision is a pattern seen in many breakout companies.

This pattern matters for founders facing the acquisition-vs-independence decision. Accepting an acquisition often feels like the rational choice, especially after repeated rejections. But Synthesia's case shows that conviction — when backed by genuine product-market traction — can unlock significantly larger outcomes. The 100 rejections weren't evidence of a bad business; they were the cost of finding investors aligned with the founder's ambition level.
