Investors processing hundreds of decks develop heuristic checklists — scalable business, large market, customer validation, clear team, defined ask. These boxes must be checked quickly and legibly. A deck that buries its traction on slide 11 or explains its market size in dense prose fails not because the business is weak but because the presentation doesn't serve the reader's scanning behavior.

The implication is counterintuitive: novelty in structure or format works against you. Standard slide order (problem, solution, market, traction, team, ask) exists precisely because it maps to the investor's checklist. Deviating from it creates friction.

This is a specific instance of the broader principle that communication design should serve the audience's cognitive workflow, not express the creator's creativity. Clarity and predictability are features when attention is scarce.
