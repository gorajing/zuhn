Stripe offered $1.2B to acquire Airwallex when it had roughly $2M in revenue — a 600x revenue multiple that would seem irrational to decline. But the offer valued the business on current output, not on the compounding infrastructure being built. A decade later, Airwallex is worth $8B with $1B in revenue.

The information asymmetry here is structural: acquirers can see revenue but struggle to price the nonlinear compounding of regulatory licenses, direct payment connections, and network effects that infrastructure companies accumulate. Zhang and his co-founders understood that their real asset wasn't $2M in revenue but dozens of jurisdiction-specific financial licenses and direct connections to domestic payment systems — assets that would take any competitor years to replicate. This suggests that founder conviction about declining acquisition offers should be evaluated based on whether the company's core asset is a compounding moat or a feature advantage.
