Graham's observation is counterintuitive but validated by behavior: the VC example he cites invested *despite* knowing the business model was wrong, because at early stage, model correctness is not the signal being evaluated. What's being evaluated is whether you understand an important problem and have built something that addresses it.

Detailed business model discussion carries double risk: it wastes scarce presentation time on guesswork, and if the model seems obviously wrong, it displaces memory of your actual value proposition. Investors remember you as 'the company with the bad monetization plan' rather than 'the company solving X.'

The right move is one brief slide showing you've thought about monetization — enough to signal commercial awareness — then returning immediately to what you know deeply: the problem and what you've built.
