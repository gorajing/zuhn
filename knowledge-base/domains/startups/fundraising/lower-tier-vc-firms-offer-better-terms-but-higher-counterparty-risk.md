The VC industry has extreme power-law performance: of roughly 1,000 US VC funds, only about 50 consistently make money. This creates a two-tier dynamic. Top-tier firms get the best deal flow through reputation (success is self-perpetuating), while lower-tier firms are left with rejected deals. However, their desperation makes them more founder-friendly on terms—better valuations, more founder control, and willingness to let founders stay as CEO.

The trap is counterparty risk. Lower-tier firms have term sheet break rates as high as 50%, because their biggest fear is that a top-tier firm will swoop in and take the deal. If you accept their term sheet, tell other VCs you're committed, and the deal falls through, you become 'damaged goods' in the VC ecosystem. Graham's tactical advice: ask any VC offering a term sheet how many of their last 10 term sheets converted to deals—forcing them to either reveal their track record or lie outright.
