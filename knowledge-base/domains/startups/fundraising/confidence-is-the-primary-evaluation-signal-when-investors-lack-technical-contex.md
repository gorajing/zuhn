Graham identifies a structural problem in startup pitching: people who are bad at explaining talk to people who are bad at understanding. Given brief time slots and non-technical audiences, investors often can't properly evaluate the product itself. In this information vacuum, founder confidence becomes the dominant signal.

Critically, this must be shown rather than told. Saying 'we're passionate' or 'our product is great' gets ignored or marks you as a bullshitter. The confidence must be implicit — conveyed through how you present, not what you claim. Graham frames it as a logical test: if you've truly made something good, you're doing investors a favor by telling them about it. If you don't believe that, you should change what your company is doing, not fake enthusiasm.
