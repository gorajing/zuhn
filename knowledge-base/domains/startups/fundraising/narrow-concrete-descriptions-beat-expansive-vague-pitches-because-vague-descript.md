Paul Graham frames pitch descriptions as an information theory problem: as your description approaches something that 'could be anything,' its actual content approaches zero. Investors discard sentences like 'a system to collaboratively leverage the value of information' as meaningless boilerplate.

The fix is to treat your pitch like incremental development — start with a description that's gripping but perhaps overly narrow, then flesh it out. A 'web-based database' is more memorable and evaluable than a 'collaboration platform.' The narrowness gives investors a concrete mental model they can build on, whereas vagueness gives them nothing to hold onto. This applies beyond pitching to any persuasive communication: specificity is the vehicle of credibility.
