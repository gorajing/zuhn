A veteran investor argues that YC has shifted from selecting founders with deep problem-first quests to favoring consensus-friendly prompts that reflect current market trends. This creates what's termed a 'Consensus Capital Machine' — a system that produces startups optimized for legibility to investors rather than genuine market insight.

The antidote is belief-driven founding: pursuing missions rooted in personal conviction and unique insight rather than pattern-matching to what's currently fundable. Founders who escape this hyperlegible sameness tend to build more defensible companies because their conviction carries them through the inevitable periods when consensus shifts away from their category. This applies beyond YC to any ecosystem where social proof drives allocation decisions.
