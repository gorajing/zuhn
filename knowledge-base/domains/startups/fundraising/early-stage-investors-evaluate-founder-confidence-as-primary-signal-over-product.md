At the earliest stages, most investors can't deeply evaluate the technical merits of what you've built — they lack both the domain expertise and the time. So they rely heavily on proxy signals, and founder confidence ranks highest. But this must be shown, not told: saying 'we're passionate' or 'our product is great' gets ignored or triggers bullshit detectors.

The deeper insight is Graham's reframe: if you've truly built something good, you're doing investors a favor by telling them about it. If you don't believe that, you should change what your company is doing. This turns confidence from a performance trick into a genuine test of founder conviction — the presentation becomes a mirror for whether you actually believe in what you're building.

This principle extends to any high-stakes persuasion: implicit confidence conveyed through deep knowledge and enthusiasm always outperforms explicit self-promotion.
