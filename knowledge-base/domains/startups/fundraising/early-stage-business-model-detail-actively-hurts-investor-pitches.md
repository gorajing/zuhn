Graham notes that even experienced founders with millions in VC backing have business models that are 'crap' at the early stage — and sophisticated investors expect this. A spectacularly wrong business model doesn't just waste time; it actively displaces the strong signals (problem importance, product quality) from investors' memory.

The practical implication is asymmetric: your problem understanding and product are areas of genuine expertise that make you sound smart, while business model guesses expose your weakest knowledge. Investors evaluate early-stage teams on talent and traction, not revenue projections. Acknowledging you care about monetization is sufficient; detailing it is counterproductive.
