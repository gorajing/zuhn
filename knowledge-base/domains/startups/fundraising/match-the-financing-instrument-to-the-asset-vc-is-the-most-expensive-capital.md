Cooper makes two linked capital-allocation arguments. First, financing is a toolbox, not a hammer: infra startups under-utilize debt. Railway raises hardware-secured debt — secured against the servers, at roughly prime plus a spread, refinanceable as rates fall — which is categorically different from venture debt and far cheaper than equity for buying a depreciating-but-collateralizable asset. The general move is to be deliberate about which instrument fits which need: debt for hardware, equity for advantage, cloud burst for elasticity, rather than reflexively reaching for VC for everything.

Second, he reframes how to think about venture capital itself: most founders try to raise as much as possible from the best available investor, but the right question is what unfair advantage you can buy with that equity — because, assuming your company keeps improving, the equity you give away today is the most expensive equity you'll ever part with. Railway deliberately matched each round to the capability gap of that stage: early operators who'd leave them alone, then partners who knew how to scale a business, then enterprise-oriented investors as they moved upmarket. The transferable principle: treat every financing event as buying a specific scarce capability (a collateralized asset, an operator, an enterprise network) at a known cost of capital, and refuse to let one default instrument price every decision.
