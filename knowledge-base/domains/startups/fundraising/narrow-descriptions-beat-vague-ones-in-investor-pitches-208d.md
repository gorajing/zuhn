When founders resist calling their product something specific because it could be so much more, they fall into a trap: a description that could mean anything means nothing. Paul Graham frames this mathematically — as you approach a description of something that could be anything, the content approaches zero. Investors will discard vague language as meaningless boilerplate.

The solution is to treat your pitch like incremental development: start with a gripping but perhaps overly narrow description, then flesh it out. At every point you should have a 'working description' in the investor's head, just as you should always have working code. The primary goal isn't to describe everything your system might become, but to convince investors you're worth a follow-up conversation.
