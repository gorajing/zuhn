The framing from OnlyCFO — 'VC money makes you dumb' — captures a structural problem: when capital is abundant, founders lose the forcing function that makes hard choices obvious. Bootstrapped companies can't afford to run three experiments simultaneously, build features with no revenue path, or hire ahead of need. That constraint is a feature, not a bug — it aligns action with what actually matters.

The practical heuristic is to periodically apply bootstrapped-company logic even when flush with VC capital: would you build this feature if it had to pay for itself? Would you make this hire if runway were six months? These questions act as a filter against the diffuse spending patterns that VC abundance enables. Founders who internalize this discipline tend to hit profitability faster and build more defensible cost structures than those who treat capital as a substitute for prioritization.
