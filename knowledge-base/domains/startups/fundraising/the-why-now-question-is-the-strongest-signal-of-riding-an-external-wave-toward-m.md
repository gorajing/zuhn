Graham observes that every company that achieves massive scale is 'lucky' in the sense of riding some external wave. The 'why now' question forces founders to identify that wave explicitly: what changed recently that makes this idea viable, and why hasn't anyone else noticed yet? The ideal answer is that some shift — technological, regulatory, behavioral — has just created an opening.

This is more than a pitch tactic; it's a strategic filter. If you can't articulate a convincing 'why now,' your startup may be a good idea in the abstract but lack the external tailwind needed to reach the scale investors require. Microsoft's 'why now' was microcomputers becoming powerful enough to support real software — a wave that turned out far bigger than anyone predicted in 1975.
