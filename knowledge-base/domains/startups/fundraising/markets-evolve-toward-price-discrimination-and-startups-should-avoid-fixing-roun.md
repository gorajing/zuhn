Graham argues it's a mistake for startups to fix upon a specific fundraising number because the optimal amount depends on investor reactions — which are impossible to predict. If investors are easily convinced, raise more now; if skeptical, take less and use it to reach a more convincing state. This flexibility principle applies broadly: any negotiation where you must commit to terms before knowing counterparty demand puts you at a disadvantage.

The deeper insight is that markets always evolve toward higher resolution — more granular pricing that reflects the actual value each party brings. Fixed-size rounds with uniform terms were an unthinking imitation of Series A structure, where a single lead investor makes uniform pricing sensible. But multi-investor angel rounds have fundamentally different dynamics, and the deal structure should reflect that different investors bring different value through timing, expertise, or network effects.
