Judy Faulkner's aversion to outside capital wasn't ideological—it was observational. In the 1970s and 80s, the venture playbook explicitly involved bringing in experienced executives to replace technical founders once a company reached scale. Her Microsoft comparison is instructive: both started within four years of each other, both had generational-programmer founders, both refused primary venture capital. Both founders got to run their companies for multiple decades and imprint them completely.

The causal chain is underappreciated. Raising capital creates a board with fiduciary duty to maximize returns, which creates pressure to professionalize management, which creates pressure to replace the founder once the company 'outgrows' them. Bootstrapping severs that chain at the root. Epic's 47-year founder continuity let Judy imbue every layer of the org—hiring, training, software methodology, customer relationships, the Verona campus aesthetic—with a coherent worldview that would be impossible under serial CEO turnover.

For founders whose edge is a long-horizon, idiosyncratic vision (especially in vertical software where markets develop slowly), the math of bootstrapping vs. VC is not really about dilution or control clauses. It's about whether you'll still be in the chair in year 20, when the compounding actually happens. Judy won by being able to run a small business for a full decade ($1.5M revenue after ten years) without anyone demanding a pivot or an exit.
