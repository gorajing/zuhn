The traditional follow-on problem assumed a closed system: you either had reserves in your fund or you didn't. SPVs break this constraint by creating single-purpose investment entities that raise external capital for specific deals. When a portfolio company raises a Series C that would require $2M to maintain pro-rata, a micro-VC can create an SPV on platforms like AngelList, rally external backers, and participate without touching core fund reserves. This is a structural innovation that changes the competitive dynamics of venture — it means fund size no longer determines follow-on capacity, only deal access and founder relationships do. The broader pattern is that lightweight financial structures (SPVs, rolling funds, syndicates) are unbundling the traditional VC fund model, allowing smaller players to compete on access and judgment rather than capital scale.
