Parker Conrad and Ron Conway both independently confirm that board votes almost never matter in practice. Conrad observes that when things go well, founders have virtually unlimited power regardless of board composition or covenant structure — investors want to ride the rocket ship and will defer. When things go badly, no amount of legal protection saves the founder because they need to return to investors for more money, at which point all prior terms get renegotiated from a position of weakness.

Conway reinforces this with 20 years of board experience across public and private companies: he has never been in a board vote that mattered. Decisions are always clear by the end of discussion, reaching unanimous or near-unanimous consensus. This means founders should spend less time optimizing board structure and protective provisions, and more time ensuring the business performs well — because performance is the only real source of founder power. The corollary is that the most important board-related decision is choosing investors you'd want involved even if they didn't bring capital, since the intangible relationship quality matters far more than structural protections.
