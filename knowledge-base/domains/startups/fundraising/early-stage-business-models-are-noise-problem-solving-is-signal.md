A VC speaking at YC described investing in a company whose business model he knew was wrong — expecting it to change three times before they got it right. The founders were experienced repeat entrepreneurs who'd just raised millions, and even their model was crap. He invested anyway because at this stage, business models are just guesses about things outside the founders' expertise.

This reveals an asymmetry in what founders think investors want versus what they actually evaluate. Founders feel pressure to show commercial viability, but investors at the early stage are evaluating problem importance and founder capability. A spectacularly wrong business model doesn't just waste time — it actively pushes out the things you want investors to remember, replacing 'company that solved an important problem' with 'company with the boneheaded revenue plan.'
