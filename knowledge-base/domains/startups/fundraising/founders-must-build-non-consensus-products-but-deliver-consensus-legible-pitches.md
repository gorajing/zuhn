Most VCs don't invest in ideas—they invest in narratives that sound like other winners. This creates a paradox where founders need genuine product differentiation (something new, strange, unfair) but must translate that vision into the language of consensus to survive the fundraising gauntlet of partners, investment committees, and LP reporting.

The tactical implication splits by founder type: if building a fast-follow in a hot market, stack the logos, maximize the round, and protect optionality. But if building something weird, slow, or culturally misaligned with consensus capital, stay close to smaller high-conviction funds that won't outbid each other but also won't rewrite your roadmap.

Notion exemplifies the optimal strategy—they ignored consensus for years, built product depth until profitable, then entered the capital market from a position of strength at $2B valuation on their own terms. The lesson: knowing when to step into consensus and when to stay outside it is itself a strategic competency.
