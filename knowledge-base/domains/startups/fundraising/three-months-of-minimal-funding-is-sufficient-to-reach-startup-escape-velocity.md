Y Combinator's first batch proved that $20,000 or less was enough for teams to build functional prototypes within 10 weeks. Seven of eight groups had demos ready, and one (Reddit) had already launched a live product. This contradicts the prevailing assumption that startups need hundreds of thousands in seed capital before they can show results.

The constraint of limited funding and a fixed timeline actually accelerated progress by forcing teams to focus on building rather than planning. The demo day deadline created a natural forcing function. This model — small checks, short timelines, working product — became the template for modern accelerators and suggests that founders raising large seed rounds before building may be optimizing for the wrong thing.
