In a brief pitch to a mostly non-technical audience, investors often cannot independently verify whether the technical work is sound or the market thesis is correct. In that vacuum, the founder's confidence fills the gap — it becomes a proxy for 'these people have thought harder about this than I have and believe in it.'

Graham distinguishes showing confidence from claiming it: saying 'we're passionate' or 'our product is great' triggers skepticism. Real confidence is demonstrated through clear, unhesitant delivery, specific numbers, and the implicit posture that you're doing investors a favor by letting them in — not the other way around.

This maps to a broader pattern: when evaluators lack domain expertise, they use meta-signals (confidence, clarity, credibility of the presenter) as substitutes for object-level evaluation. Understanding this dynamic helps founders focus on what actually moves decisions, not just what feels most honest or thorough.
