The question of when and how to prepare for an IPO is fundamentally about operational maturity, not market timing. Companies that successfully transition to public markets typically begin building the infrastructure — audited financials, SOX compliance, board composition, investor relations capabilities, and predictable revenue forecasting — years before filing an S-1.

This preparation period serves a dual purpose: it stress-tests whether the company can operate with the transparency and discipline public markets demand, and it reveals whether the business model truly supports the predictability investors require. Companies that rush IPO preparation often face painful post-IPO corrections when the gap between private-market storytelling and public-market scrutiny becomes apparent. The preparation process itself is a valuable signal — if building these systems feels impossibly burdensome, the company may not be ready regardless of its revenue scale.
