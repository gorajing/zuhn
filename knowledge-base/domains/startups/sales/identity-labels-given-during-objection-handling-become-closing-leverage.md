When a salesperson says 'that's a really smart question' or 'you must be a great family man' during the associate phase, they're not just building rapport — they're creating an identity label the prospect will try to live up to. This is a deliberate setup for the close: if earlier you labeled someone as a 'smart decision-maker,' you can later frame the purchase as what smart decision-makers do.

Hormozi gives the example of a spouse objection: by acknowledging the prospect as a family man early on, you can later tie the purchase to being a responsible family man. The labels pair the prospect's own obstacles with identity commitments that later resolve in favor of buying. This technique converts objections from barriers into closing leverage — each obstacle becomes an opportunity to plant an identity label that serves the close.
