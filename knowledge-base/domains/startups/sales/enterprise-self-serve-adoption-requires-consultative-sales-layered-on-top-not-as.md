Levie initially feared enterprise sales as an 'intermediary' blocking the power of the internet, but learned the relationship is complementary. In a user-led enterprise model, individual users adopt the product bottom-up (like Box's freemium model attracting hundreds of thousands of signups monthly). But when an organization decides to standardize, they need help navigating security requirements, competitive alternatives, and deployment across thousands of employees.

The key distinction is that sales in this model is consultative, not transactional. Salespeople aren't cold-calling to create demand — demand already exists from user adoption. Instead, they help customers through the organizational buying process, which involves different stakeholders (IT, security, procurement) than the original users. This is the Mixpanel pattern Levie references: developers adopt the product, then inside sales helps convert the organization. The product must remain the center — sales is never a 'handicap' for not building a great product, but rather an accelerant for organizational adoption that's already started.
