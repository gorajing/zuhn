ServiceNow's whole pitch is that 20th-century enterprises are organized into functional silos (Finance, HR, Sales, Engineering, Customer Service) each with its own systems of record, and that real business processes (order-to-cash, procure-to-pay, hire-to-retire) run horizontally across them. That means the person who can authorize a platform-level solution must have a broad Enterprise view — and structurally only the CEO does.

McDermott's hardest sales problem isn't the product story (which he describes as 'common sense'), it's getting into the corner office. Every other executive is well-meaning but compromised: the CFO defends the ERP, the CHRO defends the HRIS, the CIO has a portfolio of entrenched vendor relationships to protect, and none of them are paid to see the whole picture. Sell to any of them and you get a departmental deployment at best, and a silo-specific feature comparison war at worst — which you'll lose to the vertical incumbent.

The implication for founders building horizontal platforms: your sales motion, brand strategy, and executive relationships must all be designed around CEO access. Departmental entry is a fallback, not a plan — and if you build the departmental muscle first, you'll find it's the wrong muscle when you try to elevate the relationship later.
