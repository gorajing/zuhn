Epstein introduces the concept of 'uncommon commonalities' -- things that are genuinely unique connections between you and the recipient, not obvious shared traits. 'We're both men' is too common. 'We both took classes in Van Munching Hall at UMD' is uncommon and immediately creates a sense of kinship.

He shows a LinkedIn message that worked on him: 'Hope you don't mind the cold reach out but saw you also spent time in Van Munching Hall.' Instantly, Epstein feels a connection -- this person likely went to his university, shares his experience. Then the ask: 'Help a fellow Terp review my YC app.' Combined with credibility ('we're both ex-Googlers') and a low-friction CTA ('what's the best email to send you the doc?'), this was irresistible.

For enterprise outbound: the research required to find uncommon commonalities -- reading their blog posts, using their product, finding shared connections, checking their conference appearances -- takes time. But one deeply researched email to the right person outperforms 50 generic templates. This is where being small is your advantage: you can give personalized attention that big companies' SDR armies cannot.
