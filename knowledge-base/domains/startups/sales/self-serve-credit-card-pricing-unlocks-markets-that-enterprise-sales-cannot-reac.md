Before AWS, procuring infrastructure meant a proposal, financial approval, RFP, vendor selection, contract negotiation, and often a data center visit. AWS made a credit card the only required artifact. This wasn't just a nicer UX — it created a market segment (weekend hackers, 48-hour hackathon projects, pre-seed startups building MVPs) that enterprise sales motions literally cannot serve, because the cost of a sales call exceeds the lifetime value of most of those users.

The strategic genius is that this 'unserveable' segment turns out to include the next generation of Fortune 500 customers. Dropbox, Instagram, Airbnb, Uber, Zynga — all started on AWS precisely because no enterprise vendor would take their business. By the time they were large enough to matter, they were already locked in by data gravity and architectural assumptions. Incumbents serving 'real customers' with 'real contracts' missed the entire seed corn of the next decade.

When building infrastructure, developer tools, or API products, a credit-card tier isn't a nice-to-have — it's how you acquire your future enterprise customers before anyone else even knows they exist.
