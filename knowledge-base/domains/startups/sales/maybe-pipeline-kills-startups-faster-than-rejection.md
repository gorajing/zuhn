Bosmeny argues that the most dangerous state for an early startup is having a large pipeline of 'maybe' prospects. A 'no' is actually better than a 'maybe' because it frees you to focus on someone who might say yes. Your time is your only resource as a founder.

This requires what he calls 'superhuman followup' — the second deal Clever ever signed required dozens of touchpoints including multiple ignored emails. But the goal of that persistence isn't to harass; it's to force a decision. Founders who proudly report '100 people who expressed interest' without converting them to commitments are actually in a worse position than those with 10 clear nos and 5 yeses.
