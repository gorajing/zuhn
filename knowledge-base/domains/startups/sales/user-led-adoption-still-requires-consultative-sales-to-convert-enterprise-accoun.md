Levie corrects a common startup misconception: that building a great product with consumer DNA means you don't need salespeople. The reality is more nuanced. In the modern enterprise, users (not IT departments) bring technology into organizations through personal adoption on sales teams, marketing teams, and finance teams. This bottom-up, user-led adoption is the entry mechanism—and it requires genuinely great UX.

However, converting individual user adoption into enterprise-wide deployment requires salespeople. The key shift is in what those salespeople do: they're not cold-calling CIOs or running RFP processes. They're consultative experts who help customers navigate deployment, understand the competitive landscape, and get enabled across the organization. The product sells itself into the company through users; sales helps the company buy it officially. Box exemplified this: hundreds of thousands of free users adopted the product, and then sales engaged when organizations wanted enterprise security, compliance, and administrative control. The product must remain the center—sales cannot be a substitute for a bad product.
