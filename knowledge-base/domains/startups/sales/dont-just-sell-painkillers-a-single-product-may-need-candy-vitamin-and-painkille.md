Sudheesh challenges one of sales' most sacred axioms: 'sell painkillers, not vitamins.' He points out the US painkiller market is ~$7B while vitamins are ~$36B and candy is ~$70-80B. The axiom is reductive.

Each product type has distinct characteristics. Candy: makes me happy, targets users directly, sold through packaging/look/smell, marketed through stories. Vitamins: make me stronger, recommended by friends/influencers, sold through word-of-mouth and brand reputation, marketed through benefits. Painkillers: make me better, prescribed by doctors, sold through prescriptions/authority, marketed through facts and data.

The insight: a single product from a single vendor for a single use case in a single opportunity may require all three motions simultaneously. The end user might respond to candy messaging (delight, beautiful UI, magical experience). The evaluator responds to vitamin messaging (peer recommendations, community proof). The executive responds to painkiller messaging (ROI data, competitive risk, prescriptive urgency).

For AI sales, Claude can be positioned as candy (delightful to use, surprisingly creative), vitamin (makes your team stronger, recommended by peers), or painkiller (competitive necessity, cost savings, risk mitigation) — and the best deals deploy all three framings to different stakeholders.
