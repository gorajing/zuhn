The Starbucks cashier metaphor perfectly captures how pricing should be delivered. When you order a latte, the cashier says 'that'll be $7' and turns around to do something else. No negotiation energy, no apologetic tone, no 'is that okay?' You scramble for your card because the assumption of payment is absolute.

Shelby argues that if you anticipate an objection when presenting price, that anticipation shows in your energy and actually creates the objection. The prospect mirrors your uncertainty. Instead, present pricing as the natural conclusion: 'You have these problems. Here's the clear solution and game plan. Here's the investment to get you there.' Problem, solution, price -- delivered with total certainty.

For enterprise sales, this applies to proposal delivery and commercial conversations. When you present a $200K annual contract, the energy should be: 'Based on the $2M problem we've quantified together, here's the investment.' Not: 'I know this is a lot, but...' Flinching on price teaches the prospect to negotiate harder.
