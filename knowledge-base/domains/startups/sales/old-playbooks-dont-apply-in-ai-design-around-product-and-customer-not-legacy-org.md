Maggie shares one of OpenAI's boldest organizational decisions: they initially built separate go-to-market teams for the API and ChatGPT Enterprise products, recognizing each had a distinct sales motion. The dedicated ChatGPT Enterprise team helped launch what they believe was the fastest-growing enterprise application in history.

But they then collapsed both orgs into a single unified go-to-market team of 500 people — most of whom had only been at the company for months. Everyone got new roles, new workflows, new managers, and had to learn the product they hadn't been selling. It was painful but drove 'faster execution, less duplication, and most importantly, a better customer experience.'

The meta-lesson: 'Old playbooks don't always apply in AI. You need to design around orgs and product, not around legacy structures.' For new AI products, the traditional approach (train everyone, bolt it on) may be inferior to creating a dedicated tiger team to launch fast, then merging back once the motion is proven.

Maggie applies Amazon's one-way vs two-way door framework: most decisions (like reorgs) are two-way doors — painful to reverse but doable. Don't delay change to avoid discomfort. Sales leaders who wait until fiscal year-end to reorganize in AI are already too late.
