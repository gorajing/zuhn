Whyte breaks decision criteria into three clear dimensions. Technical criteria: can the solution do what the customer needs technically? This extends beyond features to infrastructure, security, interface, and integration requirements. Economic criteria: does the business case stack up? Is the ROI sufficient? But critically, it's not just about money — it's about resources. Does implementing your solution require so much effort that even a great ROI isn't worth the organizational cost? Does the business case place you at the top of their priorities list? Relationship criteria: does the customer want to work with you? Are you a good partner? How strong is your roadmap, your industry presence, your partner ecosystem?

The complexity: different people in the decision process weight these criteria differently. A CTO cares most about technical criteria. A CFO cares most about economic criteria. A CRO or business sponsor might weight relationship criteria heavily. You must map each stakeholder to their primary criteria dimension.

Whyte's most tactical advice: when customers don't have a formal decision criteria (which is most opportunities outside of RFPs), it's your job to create it — or at minimum, heavily influence it toward your strengths. This is the highest-leverage competitive move in enterprise sales.

For AI sales: Anthropic's decision criteria advantages span all three — technical (model capabilities, context window, reliability), economic (API pricing, implementation efficiency), and relationship (safety brand, responsible scaling, enterprise support partnership).
