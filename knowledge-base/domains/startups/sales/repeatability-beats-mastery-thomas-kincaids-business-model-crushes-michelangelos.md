Sudheesh's Michelangelo vs Thomas Kincaid analogy is a powerful framework for evaluating deal strategy. The Michelangelo trap: a marquee customer gets all your resources, bespoke pricing, complex agreements, 12 months of legal wrestling, and planets aligning to close. The result is a masterpiece you can talk about forever — but nothing about it is repeatable.

Kincaid sits at home painting picture after picture with mail-order distribution, standard pricing, no negotiation, and flexible use cases (you can put it in your bathroom or your den — you don't put a Michelangelo in the bathroom). Kincaid's model creates leverage: predictable unit economics, standard processes, and reference selling (see a painting in someone's home, buy it from the website).

The trap for startups: a whale customer arrives with a multi-million dollar ACV opportunity. You throw your entire resources at it. The deal closes, but you've built nothing repeatable. One day you find yourself in no man's land because your direction was shaped by one customer's bespoke needs.

For AI companies like Anthropic, this means resisting the temptation to over-customize deployments for large enterprises. The API-based model is inherently Kincaid-like — standard pricing, self-serve onboarding, scalable. The challenge is maintaining that repeatability even as enterprise customers demand bespoke solutions.
