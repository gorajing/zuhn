Behavioral psychology principle: deprivation creates motivation. If you're hungry, you're motivated to eat. In sales, expanding the prospect's awareness of their deprivation -- what they've tried, what failed, how much they've spent, and the ongoing daily cost of inaction -- creates urgency that product features never can.

Hormozi's pain cycle questions include: What have you done so far? How much have you spent? What happened? The answers always reveal gaps in past solutions, which you then bridge with your offering. For example, with Weight Watchers: they had nutrition and accountability but not fitness, so results were temporary. Your product completes the missing leg of the stool.

This is the 'three-pillar pitch' -- every successful solution needs three interdependent components. Past failures always had one or two but not all three. You identify the missing pillar and position your solution as the completion. This works in enterprise sales when asking about failed implementations of competitor products: what was missing, what went wrong, why are you still looking?
