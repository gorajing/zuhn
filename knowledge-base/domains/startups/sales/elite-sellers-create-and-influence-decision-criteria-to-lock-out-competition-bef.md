Whyte identifies decision criteria as the single most game-changing element of MEDDPICC for individual sellers. Most buyers, especially in self-generated opportunities, don't have written decision criteria. This vacuum is an enormous opportunity: you can become the trusted advisor who helps define what they should evaluate, naturally weaving in your unique capabilities as requirements.

The process: do deep discovery, document what you think their criteria should be, then send a follow-up: 'Have I heard this right? It sounds like your decision criteria are X, Y, Z. And it also sounded like any solution absolutely must have [your unique capability].' This reframes your differentiator as a table-stakes requirement.

Whyte shares how this transforms even procurement negotiations. When procurement says 'you're twice the price of the other quote,' you can respond: 'We could make it cheaper by removing these capabilities — but it sounded like you needed those.' When procurement can clearly see that only you meet the criteria the organization defined, the pricing conversation shifts entirely.

For AI sales at Anthropic, this could mean establishing criteria like 'constitutional AI safety alignment,' 'enterprise-grade context windows,' or 'responsible scaling policy compliance' as must-haves in customer evaluation frameworks before competitors even know the deal exists.
