Maggie's most revealing hiring story: her first OpenAI hire was an SDR. She wanted someone with years of experience and an MBA, so she scouted Stanford and Harvard. She talked to 50 candidates. 49 said 'I don't want to be an SDR at OpenAI.' One — Molly, with 8 years at Google and top of her Stanford GSB class — said 'Is being an SDR ideal? No. But I believe in myself and will crush it.' Within months, Molly was leading a global sales dev org. Now she's one of OpenAI's top sellers globally.

The 49 who said no had ego. The 1 who said yes had conviction. Over 40 of those 49 have since reached out wanting to join OpenAI.

Maggie's hiring filter for this trait: 'The single most important thing any of us can do is be the most helpful person possible, to have no ego, and to be helpful.' She looks for people who volunteer for unglamorous work — on her first day at Slack (during a massive hack), she volunteered to be the first phone call for every hacked Fortune 10 company. The exec team loved her for it, and she had an incredible career there.

In interviews, she probes: when they describe a lost deal, do they blame others or own it? Are they running TOWARD something (Maggie got promoted to senior director and quit 3 days later to join OpenAI) or AWAY from something? She knows within about a week if a hire is going to work.
