In SMB, finding an enthusiastic champion who will test your product and show leadership what they've built is a winning strategy. In enterprise, the same approach can backfire catastrophically. The video describes a scenario where an ambitious mid-level person runs a demo, builds a proof of concept on their own, then goes to their CTO at a 40,000-person company and says 'I've been working on this for two months.' The CTO's response: 'Why were you working on this instead of your assigned projects? This wasn't approved by security and compliance. Stay in your lane.'

The champion is now embarrassed and your deal is dead -- worse, it's dead with negative political residue. Warning signs of a false champion: they won't bring other stakeholders in, they insist on a trial before involving anyone else, they don't know their own organization's buying process, and they've never purchased software at this company before.

For enterprise AEs: champion vetting questions include: 'Have you led a software purchase here before? What did that process look like? Who else would need to weigh in? What's your organization's typical evaluation timeline?' If the answers are vague or unrealistic, you need to either coach them through the process or find additional sponsors.
