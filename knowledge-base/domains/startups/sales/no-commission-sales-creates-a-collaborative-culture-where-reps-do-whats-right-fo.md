Maggie articulates the logic cleanly: with commission, every hour closing a deal is money in your pocket, and every hour onboarding someone, testing a new strategy, or running a project is money you're NOT making. This creates a direct financial incentive against company-serving behavior. At OpenAI, they reorg constantly, pivot strategies, and shift teams — commission would make every change a comp fight.

The model: full salary (equivalent to what a product manager or engineer earns) plus equity for early hires. Companies that pioneered this include Slack, OpenAI, Stripe, AirTable, Figma, and New Relic — notably, all are known for having the best and brightest sales talent. The correlation isn't coincidental: the no-commission filter selects for builders over coin-operated sellers.

Maggie recommends Management by Objectives (MBOs) as an intermediate alternative: 'you close 10 logos, you get this amount.' This drives specific behaviors without creating the perverse incentives of percentage-based commission. When commission is eventually introduced (as companies scale), she recommends using it to drive specific behaviors — Slack centered 75% of comp on new logos vs. existing upsells, and new logo acquisition surged.

The cautionary tale: one unnamed company had a rep making $4M per quarter because the commission plan was so aggressive — that's not scalable and nearly impossible to reverse. Better to start with a collaborative structure and evolve intentionally.
