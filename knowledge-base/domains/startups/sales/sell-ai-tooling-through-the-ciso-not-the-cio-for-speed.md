From years of selling at Arthur AI, Dickerson notes that CISOs are 'typically willing to write checks — especially smaller checks for startups — more quickly and with less overhead than a CIO would.' CIOs run bigger orgs with far more process; CISOs tend to be scrappier and more willing to trial tools. This is a structural difference in buyer behavior, not a one-off.

This is why guardrail products — hallucination detection, prompt injection defense — first found enterprise traction by entering through the CISO's office and signing deals there, before the agentic wave. The same capability framed as a 'security risk and opportunity' moves through a faster, lower-overhead buying process than the same capability framed as IT infrastructure.

Actionable takeaway for AI startups: identify which C-suite persona experiences your product as their problem, and bias toward the one with the shortest path to a signed check. For many AI-safety and evaluation tools, that persona is the CISO. Map the same feature to multiple buyer narratives (security vs. governance vs. infrastructure) and lead with the one that compresses sales cycle time.
