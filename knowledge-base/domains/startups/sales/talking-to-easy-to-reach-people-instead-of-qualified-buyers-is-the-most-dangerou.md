Kazanjy identifies two specific anti-patterns: (1) selling enterprise software to startups because startups are easy to talk to, even though they don't have the problems your product solves at scale, and (2) going bottom-up with a product that requires top-down adoption (e.g., talking to individual doctors when you need the hospital CIO to sign off).

The danger isn't just wasted time -- it's that feedback from wrong-fit prospects is actively counterproductive. They'll give you product suggestions that pull you away from what your real customers need. The illusion of pipeline activity masks the absence of actual progress toward revenue.

For enterprise sales professionals: ruthless qualification is a career skill. Every hour spent with a non-buyer is an hour not spent with a real prospect. The discipline to say 'this isn't the right fit' early -- even when your pipeline is thin -- is what separates quota-crushers from perennial 80%-of-plan reps.
