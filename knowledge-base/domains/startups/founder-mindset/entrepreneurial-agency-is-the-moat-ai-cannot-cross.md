Naval frames entrepreneurial agency as diametrically opposed to job-holding: entrepreneurs operate in unknown domains with extreme self-direction, making any helpful AI an ally rather than a threat. The key insight is structural — AI lacks its own desires, survival instinct, and replication drive, which means it cannot independently identify what problems to solve or why they matter.

This extends beyond entrepreneurs to scientists exploring unknown domains and true artists creating what has never existed. In all these roles, the work is so difficult and self-directed that AI augmentation only levels you up. Naval's test is revealing: unless you can pull the plug on an AI and it lives in mortal fear of being turned off, it doesn't have genuine agency. The implication for career strategy is clear — moving toward roles requiring extreme agency and authentic creative direction is the most AI-proof positioning, not because AI can't do the tasks, but because it can't generate the motivation and direction.
