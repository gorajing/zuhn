Closing the talk, the Spectors make an explicit recruiting pitch tied to the nature of their work. Because they are trying to change the paradigm of how AI is trained — not replicate the established frontier-model playbook — they deliberately seek out creative people with unconventional backgrounds, alongside the veterans who have trained big models before.

They ground this in observation: their experience watching the companies Ben incubated at Prod was that creative people with unconventional backgrounds 'can actually do amazing things and change the world.' Their own team embodies it — a PhD who spent three years 'in the kernel mines' writing low-level GPU systems, a co-founder who super-commuted for three years between college and the lab, a Thiel fellow.

The principle for founders: the hiring profile should match the kind of work. Executing a proven approach rewards conventional pattern-matching on credentials; attempting something genuinely new rewards people whose unusual paths gave them non-standard primitives to think with. This is offered with appropriate humility — it is a stated belief and recruiting stance, not a proven law — but it captures a real tension between hiring for safe competence and hiring for paradigm-breaking creativity.
