The avoider can't look at their bank account. Research from Sages & Grayel shows people scoring high in financial avoidance save less, carry more high-interest debt, delay retirement planning, and experience higher financial stress. Saying 'finances aren't my thing' is self-handicapping — an ego protector, not an honest assessment.

Codie Sanchez couldn't look at her credit card debt when starting her first business. She made minimum payments and tried to forget her login. When she finally sat down and looked at the actual numbers, the plan wasn't that hard — she'd just delayed it. The fear of looking is always worse than the reality. The remedy: a 5-minute truth session daily. Open bank account, credit card statement, and last month's spending. Write one sentence about what to do next. By day seven, avoidance dissolves. Tell someone you trust your actual number — avoiders avoid alone, but accountability breaks the pattern.
