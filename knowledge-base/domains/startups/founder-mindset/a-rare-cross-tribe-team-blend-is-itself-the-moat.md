The founders close the talk by highlighting that their team is 'a little unusual': a subset of people deeply expert in LLMs—who have worked on Claude, Gemini, and Grok—put together with experts in chip design. They frame this 'very great mix' not as a staffing footnote but as central to why the company can do what it does, given that their entire thesis is co-designing AI and the silicon it runs on.

The underlying principle is that for problems sitting at the seam between two deep disciplines, the scarce resource is not talent in either field but a working team that spans both. Each tribe has tacit knowledge, vocabulary, and intuitions the other lacks, and incumbents are usually organized around one tribe. A startup that assembles the rare blend gains a capability that is hard to copy precisely because hiring across these gaps is socially and organizationally difficult—the founders' decade of working together across Google Brain, Anthropic, DeepMind, and Stanford is what made the blend assemblable. Founders attacking interdisciplinary problems should treat the cross-tribe team composition as a first-class part of the strategy, not an afterthought.
