The essay cites Frank Herbert's Dune on how the mind leans under stress being 'strongly influenced by training,' and NFX's explicit advice is to 'practice optimism in good times so that you can still access it when everything goes wrong.' This reframes optimism from a character trait (you either have it or don't) to a skill with a training regimen.

The implication for founders is concrete: the daily practice of finding upside in small setbacks, reframing obstacles as information, and vocally expressing confidence in the team's ability to adapt — these aren't naive positivity, they're reps. When a true crisis hits (funding dries up, a key customer churns, a co-founder leaves), the founder who has been training optimism can access it under cortisol load. The one who hasn't will default to catastrophizing, which cascades through the organization because teams mirror founder psychology. This is why CEO psychology is called the 'greatest point of leverage' — it's a multiplier on every other principle in the list.
