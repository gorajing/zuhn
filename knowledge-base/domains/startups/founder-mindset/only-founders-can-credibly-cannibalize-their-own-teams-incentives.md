When Amazon launched Marketplace in 2000, Bezos made a radical organizational decision: if a third-party seller offered the same product cheaper, they'd win the buy-box on Amazon's product page. Category managers suddenly had competitors inside their own castle walls, and sales routed to third-party sellers didn't count toward their P&L. This created immediate political pain—managers whose bonuses depended on sales numbers watched them get siphoned off to a different team.

A hired CEO—especially one brought in during a downturn (which Amazon was in)—almost certainly could not have pushed this through. The political cost was enormous, the board was already skittish about Bezos after the Joe Galli incident, and the move looked structurally self-destructive in the short term. Only a founder with unambiguous moral authority and a long time horizon could absorb the backlash and keep people marching. Today Marketplace is >50% of Amazon GMV.

The general lesson: when a strategic shift requires rewriting who gets credit and comp inside the organization, founder authority is doing load-bearing work that can't be substituted. Professional CEOs can execute plans where everyone's incentives align with the new strategy; they struggle to execute plans that make half the company's leadership look bad in the short term. This is one of the under-appreciated reasons founder-led companies outperform during discontinuities.
