Nvidia's stock lost 80% in 2008 during the financial crisis as Wall Street punished them for pouring money into CUDA with no revenue attached. It lost 50% in 2011 after another earnings whiff. It lost another 50% in 2018 during the crypto winter. Each of these would have been a natural point to shop the company to Goldman, pivot away from general-purpose GPU computing, or at least scale back the CUDA investment to appease shareholders. Almost any other public-company CEO would have done so — the pressure from the street at each crash was enormous.

Jensen did none of those things. He kept pouring resources into CUDA through the 2008 crash even though it had no revenue. He stayed on the machine learning narrative in 2011 even as analysts thought he sounded crazy. This looks in retrospect like strategic brilliance, but it was strategically indistinguishable at the time from stubborn delusion. The thing that made it work wasn't prescience — Jensen did not foresee AlexNet — it was endurance: the willingness to sit in pain for years while building something whose payoff was uncertain.

This is a specific psychological capability that most founders (and all professional managers) lack. The competitive advantage it creates is subtle: you outlast everyone who pivoted away, and when the market finally arrives, you are the only one still holding the platform you spent a decade building. It suggests that when evaluating long-duration platform bets, the single most important variable may be the founder's capacity to sit through drawdowns without changing course — and that capacity is best judged by how they behaved during prior crises, not by how they describe their strategy.
