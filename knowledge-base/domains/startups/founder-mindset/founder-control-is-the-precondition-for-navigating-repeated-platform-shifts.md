In 2005, the norm for a VC-backed series A was that the investor took board control and could fire the founder. Peter Thiel's 2004 angel investment, and Accel's 2005 $98M series A where Zuckerberg kept board control, were both unprecedented — Accel even had LPs drop out over it. Every subsequent existential bet Meta made — going public into a mobile transition where desktop revenue was about to implode, buying Instagram for $1B during the IPO quiet period, spending $70B+ on Reality Labs with no payback horizon, pivoting to Reels to blunt TikTok — would almost certainly have been blocked by a conventional board that optimized for quarterly results.

The mechanism is not that founders are always right, but that founders are uniquely willing to hold through periods where the company looks obviously broken to outsiders. Every Meta existential moment had public evidence that the board should have intervened: the 2012 IPO crash, the Cambridge Analytica fallout, the metaverse pivot. A non-founder-controlled company would have capitulated or fired leadership at each of these. The lesson for both founders and investors is that if you expect a business to traverse multiple technology generations, founder control is not a vanity clause — it is a structural necessity, and the investor willing to grant it gets the compounding reward.
