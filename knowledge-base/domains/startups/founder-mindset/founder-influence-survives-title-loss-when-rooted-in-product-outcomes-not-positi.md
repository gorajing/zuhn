When Larry Page was sidelined in 2001, he didn't fight for his title back. Instead, he redirected his energy into championing transformative product bets — Gmail, Google Maps, Chrome, Android, YouTube — all from a position without formal operational authority. Each success compounded his credibility until his return was inevitable.

This pattern suggests that for founders, influence rooted in product outcomes is more resilient than influence rooted in org chart position. Schmidt's parting tweet — 'day-to-day adult supervision no longer needed' — confirmed that Page's track record had made the case no boardroom argument ever could. The implication for founders facing investor pressure to step aside: channel energy into outcomes rather than political battles over titles.
