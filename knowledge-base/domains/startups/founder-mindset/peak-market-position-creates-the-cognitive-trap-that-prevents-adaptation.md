In 1997, Yahoo held 90% of internet searches and was worth $125B. From that vantage point, two graduate students in a garage with an algorithm were literally invisible as a competitive threat. Yahoo's leadership believed users came for the 'Yahoo experience' — news, chat, email — not for search quality. This framing made Google's superiority in search seem irrelevant rather than existential.

This is the peak-position trap: dominance creates a self-reinforcing narrative about why you're winning that blinds you to the actual mechanism of value creation. Yahoo thought they were winning because of their ecosystem breadth, but users actually stayed because Yahoo was the best way to find things online. Once that function was served better elsewhere, the ecosystem advantages evaporated. The trap is especially dangerous because the signals of decline are initially invisible — user engagement metrics stay strong right up until the moment users discover something better.
