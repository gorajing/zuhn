Lucy Guo's track record: ClassPass-for-Clubbing → pivoted quickly. Health-care 'find the best doctors' → applied to YC with this, pivoted to API-for-humans. API-for-humans → pivoted to data labeling for AI when a YC investor noted 'with enough labeled data you can train AI.' Each pivot happened in roughly 3 weeks. Her stated rule: 'I generally make pivots like 3 weeks in because too many founders fall into sunk cost fallacy. It's really hard not to because you've invested so many resources you just want to make the thing work. That's the number one trap a founder can have. It's incredibly important to be real with yourself and be like — this does not have the logarithmic scale we needed to have. So it's time to pivot.' The diagnostic isn't 'is the current idea making any money?' (it usually is, in small amounts that feel reassuring). The diagnostic is 'will this scale logarithmically?' — meaning revenue and impact grow non-linearly with effort. Linear-growth businesses can be fine lifestyle outcomes but trap founders in misallocated effort. The 3-week window is calibrated to (a) be long enough to actually test market response with real prospects, (b) short enough that you haven't accumulated investor obligations, large team, or branded reputation around the wrong direction. Founders who push past 3 months on a non-logarithmic idea typically can't pivot at all because the accumulated commitments make it psychologically and operationally too expensive.
