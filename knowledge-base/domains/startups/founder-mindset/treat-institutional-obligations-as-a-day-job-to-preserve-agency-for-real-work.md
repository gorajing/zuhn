Graham's 'day job' reframe is a psychological technique for maintaining agency within constraining systems. A musician who waits tables doesn't identify as a waiter; similarly, treating school (or any institutional obligation) as a day job means doing it competently without letting it define you. This orthogonal positioning — neither rebellion nor obedience — preserves psychological resources for genuine work.

The insight generalizes beyond school: founders in corporate jobs, artists with day jobs, and researchers in bureaucratic institutions all face the same trap. Rebellion is as identity-consuming as obedience because both are defined by the institution. The orthogonal vector — doing what's required without emotional investment, then pouring real energy into self-directed projects — is the only approach that preserves autonomy. This is practically actionable: identify what institutional demands you're over-investing identity in, reframe them as maintenance tasks, and redirect that psychological energy.
