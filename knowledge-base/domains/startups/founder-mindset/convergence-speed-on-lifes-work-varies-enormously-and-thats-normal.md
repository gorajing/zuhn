Graham dismantles the movie-plot version of career discovery where everyone has a calling they just need to find. He contrasts Mozart (early convergence) with Newton (restless exploration across math, physics, alchemy, and theology), arguing that attributing a single calling to Newton is hindsight bias. For people whose lives don't converge quickly, figuring out what to work on isn't a prelude to hard work but an ongoing, integral part of it.

This has practical implications for how non-convergers should evaluate their progress. Instead of the two-variable system (how hard am I working? how well am I doing?), they need a three-variable system that also asks 'should I keep working in this field or switch?' The difficulty is calibrating when to persist versus when to pivot — being too determined can work against you, like an immune system attacking itself. The honest signal is interest: if the work is genuinely interesting to you, it's likely worth continuing.
