Sandy Lee's hard-won distinction after 5 years failing with original content and 5 months succeeding with format-copying: 'There's a main difference between being original and being authentic. If you're just pushing for original, it's going to take forever. You're going to stay in that cocoon phase forever. You're copying what's actually working, the framework — but changing everything into your own style — and then you become authentic.' Her 2018-2023 channel was original (Korean to Spanish-speakers — nobody else doing it) and grew slowly to 550K across three platforms WITHOUT generating revenue, because the format didn't fit any algorithm pattern. Her 2026 channel grew 200 → 12,000 subscribers in one month and generated $5.5K/month + $3K sponsorship by deliberately copying outlier videos from her niche (then putting her own spin: her personal story, her examples, her voice). The contrarian observation she shares: even Mr. Beast (top YouTuber globally) copies other creators' formats. 'That's what the YouTube strategists recommend.' Why originality fails: (a) Algorithms reward format consistency because they classify content into successful clusters. Original formats don't fit clusters and get under-served. (b) Audiences need cognitive scaffolding — a familiar format reduces the cognitive load of consumption, letting them attend to the actual content. (c) Producers learn faster by iterating on a working format than by repeatedly inventing new ones. The same logic generalizes to startups: don't invent a brand-new business model when a proven one + your authentic differentiation works. Don't invent a brand-new sales motion when copying Clay-style PLG and adding your domain expertise works. Don't invent a novel app UX when copying Notion-style or Linear-style and adding your vertical specialization works. Reserve originality for places where formats DON'T work (rare). Apply authenticity everywhere format works (almost always).
