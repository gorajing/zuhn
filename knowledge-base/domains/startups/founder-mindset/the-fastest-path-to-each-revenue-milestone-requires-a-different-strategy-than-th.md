Hormozi uses the slot machine analogy: three machines cost the same effort but pay out $100K, $10M, and $1B respectively — with the catch that higher payouts take longer to materialize. Most people line up at the $100K machine because it pays out immediately. The rational choice is the $1B machine, but human time-preference bias makes this psychologically nearly impossible.

He extends this with the 'sharpening the axe' metaphor: a service business with no foundation (brand, product, regulatory moat) is like hacking with a dull axe at a mile-wide tree. Someone who spends a year building foundation (turning the dull axe into a lightsaber) catches up by year two and dominates by year ten. The practical trap is that founders operate on employee time horizons — family asks 'are you making money yet?' after two weeks, applying paycheck-cycle expectations to venture-building timelines. Changing the measuring stick is prerequisite to building anything large.
