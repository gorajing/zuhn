Nearly every successful startup receives acquisition offers en route to its full potential. For money-motivated founders, these offers are rationally compelling — a guaranteed large payout versus continued risk. Founders driven by benevolent purpose have a structural reason to decline: the acquisition would end their ability to pursue the change they care about.

This creates a selection effect where the highest outcomes disproportionately accrue to mission-driven founders, not because benevolence is rewarded by the market directly, but because it provides the psychological fuel to keep going when rational financial analysis says to stop. The implication is that genuine purpose isn't just nice to have — it's a competitive advantage disguised as a personality trait.
