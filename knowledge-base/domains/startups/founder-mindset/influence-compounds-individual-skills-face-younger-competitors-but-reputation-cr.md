Developers who focus exclusively on individual technical mastery face a structural disadvantage over time: younger competitors with fresher skills enter the market constantly. Whether you choose a management track or stay as an individual contributor, you need influence to be recognized and advance.

Influence starts with reputation — becoming someone others want to work with. Early-career professionals naturally chase opportunities through resumes and applications. Mid-to-late career, those with strong reputation and influence don't seek opportunities; opportunities are thrown at them through their network. The key shift is from 'I increase my own output' to 'my presence increases the output and capability of those around me.' People who get trapped in the expertise pursuit — sitting in front of a computer coding exclusively — see their communication skills atrophy and growth opportunities dry up. The regret people feel as they age is overwhelmingly about opportunities they didn't take, not ones they did.
