When Hormozi sold his gyms and invested the proceeds into his next venture, he lost everything. But he immediately generated $100,000 the following month by maxing out a credit card and applying his skills. This moment crystallized a key insight: he thought the gyms were what he'd built, but the skills were the actual asset. The gyms were a temporary vehicle; the skills were permanent and portable.

This reframes the entire risk calculus of entrepreneurship. Most people view skill-building years as sunk cost if the business fails. But if skills are the durable output — not the business itself — then no effort invested in becoming better is truly wasted. Hormozi frames skills as 'income insurance': unlike stock market retirement plans that depend on external conditions, skills guarantee you can always trade value for money regardless of currency, market, or circumstance. This makes the downside of ambitious risk-taking much smaller than it appears.
