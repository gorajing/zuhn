Amazon's track record is a graveyard of failed initiatives: Amazon Auctions, zShops, the Fire Phone, the A9 search engine, BlockView, sijo.com partnerships, pets.com investments, home grocer, webvan-era bets. Joy Covey said it looked 'like Jeff fired an arrow and then followed that arc'—but the arrow was philosophical (customer obsession, long-term, reinvestment) while the tactical path was random exploration. Bezos himself wrote in the 1997 letter: 'we will make bold rather than timid investment decisions where we see a sufficient probability of gaining market leadership... some of these investments will pay off, others will not, and we will have learned another valuable lesson in either case.'

The contrast with Elon Musk is instructive: nearly every Musk venture works (Tesla, SpaceX, PayPal). Amazon had dozens of dead ends. The difference is that Amazon built an institutional capacity to try bold things, learn fast, and back out of failures without cultural damage. The key is keeping tactical failures decoupled from philosophical identity—experiments are disposable, principles are not.

This matters most in markets where no one knows the right answer. The internet in 1997 was such a market. So is most of AI today. In those conditions, betting on a brilliant strategist is worse than betting on a cheap-experimentation machine that can run many concurrent bets and compound learnings. The meta-skill is learning, not planning.
