Sandel argues meritocracy has a dark side that persists even when chances are truly equal. The successful 'inhale too deeply of their own success,' forgetting two forms of luck: first, having the talents you have is not your doing; second, living in a society that happens to prize your particular talents is historical contingency. Had LeBron James lived during the Italian Renaissance, his athletic gifts would earn him nothing — they cared about fresco painters.

For tech founders, this is directly applicable: programming skill is rewarded now, but a generation ago legal expertise paid more, and a generation before that, physical strength dominated. The current moment values a specific narrow band of capabilities, and the people who happen to possess them claim the rewards as earned desert. Acemoglu adds that this isn't just unfair — it's corrosive. The conceit of superiority through success poisons society regardless of how much the winners are taxed. The attitude, not just the money, is the problem.
