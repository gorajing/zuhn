Bezos wanted to be a theoretical physicist at Princeton but realized he'd be mediocre at it — there were people who could effortlessly solve problems that took him hours. His key insight wasn't defeat but redirection: 'there are a thousand ways to be smart.' His intelligence was in invention — generating a hundred atypical solutions where 99 don't survive scrutiny but one might work.

This is a meta-insight about career strategy. Rather than grinding on a path where you're merely good, find the specific way your brain is wired to be brilliant. Bezos describes going around meeting people and 'always looking for the way that they're smart' — because everyone has unique cognitive strengths. IQ is not a single dimension. The Yosanta story (who could solve differential equations by pattern-matching to problems from years ago) illustrates what elite talent looks like in one dimension — and how recognizing you're not that in a given domain is a feature, not a failure.
