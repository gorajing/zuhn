Seo reframes 'Elon time' — the famously aggressive schedules — as a rigorous engineering instrument rather than arbitrary pressure. The technique he names is the 'all green light schedule': imagine every single light is green, with no administrative burden, no shipping delays, no legacy bottlenecks, and ask how fast you could actually manufacture the chip or design and test the robot. That number becomes the physically-possible baseline against which all real-world delay is measured.

The striking claim is the magnitude: when you strip away man-made bottlenecks, Seo estimates at least 80-90% of the things you assumed were fixed turn out to be that way only because nobody questioned them from a first-principles perspective. The schedule is valuable not because the team hits the green-light date, but because the gap between that date and reality makes every artificial constraint visible and attackable.

Seo is explicit that this is not free: it is not for everyone, it shifts priorities constantly, generates intense stress, and has real cultural ramifications. The framework is a thinking tool with a cost, best applied deliberately in atom-heavy domains where the physical baseline is meaningfully below the conventional one — not a default operating mode to impose everywhere.
