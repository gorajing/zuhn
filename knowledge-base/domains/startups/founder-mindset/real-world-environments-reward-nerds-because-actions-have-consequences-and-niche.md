Graham identifies two structural differences that make the real world hospitable to nerds: actions have real effects (making correct answers valuable), and the world is large enough for small minorities to achieve critical mass. In school, intelligence is irrelevant because nothing has real consequences. In business, Bill Gates's social awkwardness is irrelevant because revenue is the real test.

For founders, this is both validation and strategy. The same traits that made someone unpopular in school — obsessive focus on getting right answers, indifference to social games, deep specialization — become competitive advantages in markets where outcomes matter. The critical mass insight is equally important: niche communities that seem impossibly small in a school of 500 become viable businesses in a market of millions. Founders should seek environments where their specific form of intelligence is the most important thing, rather than trying to succeed in generalist popularity contests.
