After a year of no growth and a binder full of maxed-out credit cards, the Airbnb founders kept going because they had personally experienced something transformative as the first hosts on their own platform. They'd rented out airbeds during a design convention purely for rent money, but discovered that both they and their guests genuinely enjoyed it. That firsthand experience gave them a conviction that couldn't be shaken by investor rejection or market conditions.

This distinguishes experience-based conviction from thesis-based conviction. Many founders believe their idea should work based on market analysis; the Airbnbs knew it worked because they'd lived it. Paul Graham notes that 'people reveal their nature under extreme conditions' — and what the extreme conditions revealed about the Airbnbs was that they weren't doing it for money. They'd seen the future and couldn't let it go. For founders choosing what to work on, this suggests building something you've personally needed and experienced the value of creates a resilience moat that no amount of market research can replicate.
