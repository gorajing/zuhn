Graham uses Google circa 2006 as his example: zero startups were coming out of Google because people liked working there too much to leave. He frames this as a local maximum problem—you're optimizing for comfort rather than for lifetime outcome. The activation energy metaphor is precise: starting a startup requires a threshold of discomfort or dissatisfaction that a good employer deliberately suppresses.

The tactical implication is counterintuitive: the best pre-startup employment is at another startup, not at a comfortable large company. Startups end quickly one way or another (you get rich, it gets acquired and becomes unpleasant, or it fails), so they don't create the local maximum trap. Graham suggests that if you plan to eventually start a company, you should deliberately avoid employers whose comfort level might prevent you from ever leaving.
