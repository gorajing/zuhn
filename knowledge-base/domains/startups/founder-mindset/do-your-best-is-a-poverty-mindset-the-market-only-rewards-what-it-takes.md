'I tried my best' is a self-soothing narrative that the marketplace completely ignores. Customers, bosses, and competitive markets care about whether the required outcome was delivered, not about the effort curve that produced it. This creates an uncomfortable truth: if your best isn't sufficient, the only productive response is to raise your capacity until your best exceeds what is required.

This insight connects to Hormozi's broader framework of tolerating nothing less than excellence — from yourself first, then from others. Leaders who hold themselves to a standard above what they demand from their teams earn the moral authority to enforce that standard. The practical shift is from effort-based self-evaluation ('did I try hard?') to outcome-based self-evaluation ('did I produce what was required, and if not, what capability gap do I need to close?').
