The article's most persistent theme is that endurance — not speed, intelligence, or fundraising ability — is the primary differentiator between founders who achieve large outcomes and those who don't. The 10-year map shows that every founder hits at least one existential crisis where quitting is the rational choice. The ones who build $250M+ companies are simply the ones who kept going through periods where continuing felt irrational.

This has a non-obvious implication for founder selection and self-assessment: the question is not 'am I smart enough to build this?' but 'am I constitutionally capable of enduring 3-5 years of grinding with no external validation?' This reframes founder-market fit from domain expertise to emotional architecture. It also suggests that the most valuable skill a founder can develop is not product intuition but a sustainable relationship with uncertainty and discomfort.
