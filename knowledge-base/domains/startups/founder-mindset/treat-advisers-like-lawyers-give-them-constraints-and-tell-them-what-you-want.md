Michael draws a direct parallel between working with lawyers and working with advisers: tell them what you want and give them constraints, then ask for advice. Without constraints, lawyers go way out of scope, red-lining documents and raising extraneous points. Advisers do the same — completely open-ended questions like 'what should I do with my startup?' produce extremely random advice. The founder is the boss of the adviser, just as they're the boss of the lawyer, even though the adviser has done this 100 times. This takes work — it requires the founder to do prep before the meeting, define their agenda, and ring-fence what kind of advice they're looking for. 'Hey, do you think right now is a good time to fundraise?' is a well-constrained question for an investor. 'Tell me things that could help my startup' is not.
