Hormozi argues that individual skills have limited earning potential, but combinations of skills create exponential value. Someone who knows sales alone might earn well, but someone who knows sales AND finance can raise a billion-dollar fund. Someone who can code AND market can build software and distribute it to millions without depending on co-founders for the missing half.

This is a human capital version of the compounding argument. Unlike financial assets, skills don't depreciate and can't be taken away — 'you are the permanent hold.' The implication for career strategy is that after reaching competence in one domain, the highest-leverage move isn't going deeper but rather acquiring a complementary skill that unlocks a new combination. This explains why generalist founders often outperform specialists — they can execute the full value chain.
