Kinoshita presented his x-ray lithography results in 1986 and the audience 'refused to believe it,' calling it a 'big fish story.' Hawryluk, after presenting at a conference, was 'literally laughed off the stage' by every person he looked up to in the field. He told his boss 'I will never speak of it again.' Three days later, the Executive VP of AT&T called and invited him to present at Bell Labs.

This pattern — expert consensus declaring something impossible, followed by ridicule of the proposer, followed by eventual vindication — appears repeatedly in paradigm-shifting technologies. The key insight isn't that experts are always wrong, but that in domains where the solution requires cross-disciplinary convergence, domain experts systematically underestimate feasibility because they can't see the full picture. Kinoshita and Hawryluk had conviction rooted in first-principles understanding. The experts' skepticism was rooted in the limitations of their specific sub-field.
