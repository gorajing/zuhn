When pitching potential projects to Ivan Sutherland (the father of computer graphics), Raibert deliberately sandwiched his real idea — the pogo stick robot — between two intentionally boring alternatives. Sutherland immediately spotted the interesting one and funded it. This $3,000 led to a $250,000 DARPA grant, which became the foundation of 40 years of robotics.

This is a subtle persuasion technique for fundraising and pitching: rather than arguing for your idea directly, present it alongside clearly inferior alternatives and let the audience's intelligence guide them to the right choice. Smart people want to feel they discovered the opportunity rather than being sold on it. The decoy framing makes the real idea shine by contrast while giving the decision-maker agency.
