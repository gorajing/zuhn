The directional claim matters. Most corporate values statements are retrospective reconstructions: a company succeeds, then leaders write down the characteristics they believe explained the success, then those characteristics become the official 'values.' Berkshire explicitly inverts this: 'values are not the result of our success, but the reason for it.' The difference is operational — if values are retrospective, they are descriptive and can bend to accommodate new conditions; if values are prospective, they are constraints that make some decisions off-limits even when they would increase short-term returns. Berkshire is claiming that decades of decisions were made by REFUSING to violate values, not by discovering values after the fact. This is almost impossible to verify from outside the company, but Abel's phrasing suggests it's meant as the defining operating doctrine for the post-Warren era. The general lesson: companies that treat values as constraints on action produce different long-run outcomes than companies that treat values as marketing copy.
