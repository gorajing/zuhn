The personal finance internet is dominated by investment optimization content — index funds vs. ETFs, tax-loss harvesting, Roth conversion ladders. Hormozi argues this is exactly backwards for people with little savings. If you have $5,000 invested, the difference between 7% and 10% annual returns is $150/year — completely irrelevant compared to the $12,000-$50,000+ annual impact of focused income growth.

This insight has a sharp corollary: time spent learning about investment optimization at the sub-$10K level is actually negative ROI because it displaces time that could be spent building skills, starting side projects, or negotiating raises. The optimal financial strategy at low wealth levels is radically simple: earn more, spend less, don't touch the investment settings. Complexity becomes valuable only after the base is large enough for allocation to matter — roughly when investment returns exceed what you could earn by working an extra few hours per week.
