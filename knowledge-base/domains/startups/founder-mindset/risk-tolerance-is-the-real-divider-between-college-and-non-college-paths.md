Hormozi observes that the people who succeed without college aren't necessarily smarter — they're more risk-tolerant. College serves as a risk-reduction mechanism: it provides structure, credential insurance, and a socially acceptable path. Choosing to skip it requires comfort with ambiguity and potential failure. This same risk tolerance is precisely what entrepreneurship demands. The correlation between 'skipped college' and 'built successful businesses' isn't because college is bad — it's because both decisions are downstream of the same personality trait. This has implications for talent evaluation: when hiring for entrepreneurial roles, someone who took an unconventional path may signal the exact risk profile you want, independent of their formal education.
