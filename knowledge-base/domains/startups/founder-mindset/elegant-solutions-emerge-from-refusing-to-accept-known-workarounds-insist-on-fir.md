The Klein-Gordon equation was a perfectly functional relativistic wave equation that other physicists accepted. It made reasonable predictions. But Dirac rejected it because its second-order time derivative treated time differently from space — violating the symmetry that relativity demands. His colleagues thought he was being needlessly picky.

By insisting on mathematical beauty rather than pragmatic adequacy, Dirac was forced to invent a completely new approach: a first-order equation using 4x4 matrices instead of simple numbers. This 'unnecessary' pursuit of elegance didn't just produce a prettier equation — it revealed the existence of antimatter, one of the most profound discoveries in physics. The four-component wave function contained two 'extra' solutions that described positrons, particles no one had imagined.

The startup parallel: when existing solutions work but feel architecturally wrong, the instinct to accept 'good enough' is strong. But the founders who insist on first-principles correctness — who refuse to build on shaky abstractions — sometimes stumble into entirely new categories. The ugly workaround gets you to market; the elegant solution gets you to a breakthrough.
