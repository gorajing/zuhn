Burns contrasts two approaches to winning the battle of ideas. Ayn Rand was a purist who demanded total alignment from followers, broke relationships over disagreement, and became angrier over time. Milton Friedman was 'the happy warrior' who debated with a smile, accepted half a loaf, and could say 'I was wrong' — even expressing doubts about globalization near the end of his life.

Rand had deeper psychological impact on individuals (people read her and changed careers, got divorces, felt they understood the whole world). But Friedman had broader policy impact because he could work within the system. The insight for founders and builders: philosophical purity inspires devoted followers but limits real-world influence. Pragmatic empiricism — being willing to test ideas against reality and adjust — creates more lasting change. The 'happy warrior' wins more debates than the angry absolutist.
