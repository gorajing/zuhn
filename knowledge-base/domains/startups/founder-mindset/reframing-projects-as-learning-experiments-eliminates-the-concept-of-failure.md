Graham identifies experiment-framing as one of the most effective psychological techniques for undertaking risky work. If the problem is sharply defined enough, failure itself becomes knowledge — a disproven theorem or a structural member that breaks under stress both teach you something concrete, even if it's not what you hoped to learn.

This reframe works because it changes the payoff matrix. Under the 'make something great' frame, the project is binary: succeed or waste your time. Under the 'learn something' frame, every outcome has positive expected value. Graham notes that curiosity is a particularly powerful variant of this — starting projects 'just to see how they'll turn out' sustained him through years of work on Bel. The technique is especially valuable for younger people who have more time blocks available and whose standards haven't yet risen to the point where early work feels intolerable.
