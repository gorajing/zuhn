Richter describes a 'psychological fallacy' where founders must switch off their logical brain because they're working on problems nobody else sees. This isn't recklessness—it's a deliberate cognitive strategy. Rational analysis of novel markets will always conclude 'this won't work' because the evidence doesn't exist yet.

The historical context matters: Richter grew up seeing the Berlin Wall fall and the Cold War end—events that seemed impossible until they happened. This shaped his understanding that the consensus view of what's possible is often wrong. For founders, the lesson is that the most valuable skill isn't rational analysis—it's the ability to override rational analysis when your pattern recognition sees something the data can't yet support.
