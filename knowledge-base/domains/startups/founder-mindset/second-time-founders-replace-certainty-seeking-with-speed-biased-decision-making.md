First-time founders get trapped in advice-seeking loops, trying to de-risk every decision before committing. Second-time founders flip this: they bias toward action because experience has taught them that most decisions are reversible, and the ones that aren't are rarely the ones you agonize over. This isn't recklessness — it's calibrated confidence from having felt both failure and success. The practical implication is that speed of iteration, not quality of initial decisions, is what compounds in early-stage startups. Founders who can make 10 decent decisions in the time it takes a first-timer to make 3 perfect ones will outpace them every time.
