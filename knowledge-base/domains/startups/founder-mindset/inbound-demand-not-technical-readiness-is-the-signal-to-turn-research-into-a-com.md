Park had the vision for human-behavior simulation as early as 2020-2022 (the Social Simulacra and foundation-model work), but the conviction to start a company arrived about half a year after the Generative Agents paper — and it arrived from outside. The paper generated heavy inbound: first social scientists wanting to run RCTs on the platform, then Fortune 500 CEOs and board members visiting Stanford and asking whether the market-research questions they couldn't answer could be run in simulation. That inbound 'showed a clear line towards real-world impact for research, which is not always the case.'

The signal that mattered was not that the models had crossed some capability threshold (he is explicit that human-simulation fidelity has plateaued and still needs a new frontier). It was that serious buyers were independently pulling the technology toward concrete, high-value problems. Only then did the team go validate accuracy (the ~85%-of-self-replication study) and decide it was responsible to offer as a product.

The transferable rule: a research result is ready to become a company when the market reaches toward you with specific, fundable problems — not when you personally feel the tech is mature. Self-assessed readiness is easy to fool yourself about; unsolicited, specific demand from buyers who control budgets is far harder to fake and is the better commercialization trigger.
