When asked about his source of strength for persevering through seemingly impossible engineering challenges, Musk rejected the framing entirely: 'I don't need a source of strength. Quitting is not in my nature.' This reveals a fundamentally different approach to persistence than the typical advice of finding motivation or inspiration.

The distinction matters psychologically. If persistence requires ongoing motivation, it's fragile — motivation fluctuates. But if quitting is simply not in your decision space, persistence becomes the default state that requires no energy to maintain. This echoes the concept of identity-based habits: you don't need willpower to not smoke if you identify as a non-smoker. Similarly, if 'quitter' is not part of your identity, you don't spend cognitive resources debating whether to continue. The risk is obvious — this can lead to destructive persistence on doomed projects — but for genuinely important problems where physics allows success, removing the quit option eliminates a major source of decision fatigue.
