Stauch is blunt that 'more and more of the moat for any startup is the customer insight, the empathy of actually understanding what they want,' and that a product advantage 'is copyable overnight.' The logic follows directly from cheap, fast AI implementation: if anyone can build the feature in days, the feature can't be the moat. What can't be copied overnight is a differentiated, lived understanding of the customer's real problems.

He earns that insight through full immersion rather than a North Star metric — he's in every one of his ~100 customers' Slack channels and most of them get a message from him every single day. He admits it's overwhelming and he sometimes wonders if he's wasting his time, but it gives him 'his finger on the pulse,' and he sees no substitute for it. The signal-collection method is qualitative and anecdotal-synthesized, not a single dashboard number.

This connects to how product direction now flows: Serval's forward-deployed engineers sit between customers and the product, and improvements emerge from that loop ('gradient descent for product improvements'). The founder's job shifts from authoring product direction to ensuring the organization stays saturated in customer reality — because in a world where building is easy, the scarce input is knowing precisely what to build.
