Perfectly calibrated self-assessment sounds optimal but fails in practice because ambitious projects face compounding discouragement from two directions: other people's skepticism and your own harsh judgment of early results. Slight overconfidence acts as armor against both forces simultaneously. The error in self-assessment compensates for errors in the opposite direction, making the net expected-value calculation roughly correct even though both components are wrong.

This explains why the most successful people across many fields tend to be slightly overconfident rather than perfectly calibrated. It's not a cognitive defect but an adaptive strategy. For founders specifically, this means cultivating belief slightly beyond what evidence warrants is not self-deception—it's a rational response to a systematically hostile evaluation environment.
