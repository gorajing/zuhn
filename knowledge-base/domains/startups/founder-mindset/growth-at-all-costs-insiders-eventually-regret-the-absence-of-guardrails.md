Chamath's first-person account from inside Facebook is unusually candid: 'We did what we did to grow at all costs and beat the competition. We all wish we had been more careful.' This is a powerful data point against the self-regulation argument. Even people who understood the risks and had good intentions couldn't resist the competitive pressure to exploit an ungoverned landscape.

This pattern has broad implications beyond AI regulation. It suggests that in any high-stakes, fast-moving market, the rational individual response to an absence of rules is to push boundaries — and that this remains true even when actors know they'll regret it later. The structural solution isn't better founders or stronger ethics; it's external constraints that remove the competitive penalty for being careful. For AI founders specifically, this argues that advocating for regulation isn't altruistic self-sacrifice — it's a rational strategy to prevent a race to the bottom that harms everyone, including the winners.
