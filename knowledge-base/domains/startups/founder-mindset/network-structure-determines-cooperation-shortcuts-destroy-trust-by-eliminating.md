Watts and Strogatz simulated the prisoner's dilemma on different network structures. In a clustered network (local communities), cooperation spread naturally -- exactly as Axelrod's tournament predicted. But when they added just a few shortcut connections (making it a small-world network), cooperators were crushed and the world filled with defectors.

The mechanism: cooperation requires repeated interactions within stable groups. When shortcuts connect you to distant strangers you'll never see again, defection becomes rational. Strogatz explains: 'The world of the internet, where anyone can get on Twitter and badmouth anyone else, tends to discourage cooperation. You don't have communities.' However, real-world experiments showed that when players could choose their connections, cooperation returned. The actionable insight: curate your network deliberately. The structure of who you interact with shapes your behavior more than your character does.
