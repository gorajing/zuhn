Park draws a sharp algorithmic distinction between the two institutions he has lived in. A research lab is optimized for breadth-first search: a roomful of smart people each owning a small slice of thesis, exploring widely, and surfacing surprising results — but 'we're not necessarily known for finishing our job' and rarely carry the work to real-world impact. A company is optimized for depth-first search: you form a conviction about one area, pick a single hill to climb, and assemble people and resources to pursue that singular vision 'without hesitation.'

The practical consequence is that the skills and instincts that make great research are actively dangerous in a startup. The exploratory, hedge-many-bets posture that surfaces good papers is the same posture that prevents a company from concentrating force on one outcome. Founders coming out of research (or any exploratory background) should expect to deliberately suppress their breadth instinct and over-index on depth and finishing.

This is a reusable lens for any builder, not just academics: when you notice yourself keeping many options open 'to learn more,' ask whether you are still in search mode when the task actually requires committing depth-first to one path and driving it to completion.
