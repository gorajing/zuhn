Robinhood's GameStop crisis crystallized a fundamental asymmetry in crisis communication: 'Robinhood is stealing from the poor and giving to the rich' is a simple lie that writes itself and takes seconds to spread. The truth — involving DTCC collateral requirements, T+2 settlement mechanics, and Dodd-Frank solvency rules — takes an order of magnitude more time to explain than the lie takes to produce.

Vlad Tenev spent 2-3 years rebuilding trust afterward. He identifies that the simple antidote would have been to 'point the finger at someone' — other brokers deflected blame to their clearing firms — but Robinhood's instinct to take responsibility for everything, even things not their fault, extended the damage. The NPS didn't recover to pre-GameStop levels for over two years. The practical lesson: in a crisis, having a simple counter-narrative ready is more important than having the truth. Brand trust is lost in a day and recovered over years.
