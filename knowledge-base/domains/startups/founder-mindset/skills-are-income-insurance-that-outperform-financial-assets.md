After losing his entire nest egg from selling his gym chain, Hormozi immediately generated $100K the next month by maxing out a credit card and deploying his skills. This experience crystallized a key insight: the gyms weren't the real asset he'd built — his skills were. Financial assets can go to zero, but competence in sales, marketing, operations, and leadership persists through any market condition.

This reframes the conventional wisdom about building wealth through asset accumulation. While stock portfolios and real estate are subject to market risk, developed skills function as a perpetual income stream. The implication for early-career entrepreneurs is to prioritize skill acquisition over financial accumulation — even if it means accepting lower short-term income. The compounding returns on skills (which improve with use) may exceed the compounding returns on capital (which are subject to market volatility).
