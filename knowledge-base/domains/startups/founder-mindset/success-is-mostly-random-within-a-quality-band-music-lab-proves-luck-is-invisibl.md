Columbia sociologist Duncan Watts created an artificial music market with 14,000 participants and 48 songs by unknown bands. In the independent control group, people rated songs purely on quality. But in socially-influenced groups where download counts were visible, the same song could rank anywhere from #1 to #40 depending on random early momentum.

The implication for founders: quality creates a floor (you must be competent) and a ceiling (there is a maximum), but within that band, who succeeds is largely random — driven by timing, who noticed first, compounding early advantage. From inside success, luck becomes invisible because the brain is a press office, not an Oval Office — it constructs meritocratic narratives after the fact. This does not mean effort is pointless, but it means formulas extracted from survivors are retrospective illusions, not replicable blueprints.
