Any experienced early-stage investor's first instinct on a plan containing multiple sequential 'and then a miracle happens' steps is to pass. This heuristic is correct the vast majority of the time: path-dependent bets compound failure probability, and most founders underestimate how many independent near-misses they need. The Qualcomm story a priori looks exactly like this: digitize cellular, invent a new multiple-access scheme, get a US standards body to allow non-mandatory alternatives, convince carriers, solve the near-far problem, bet on GPS, forecast Moore's Law, bootstrap handsets, bootstrap base stations, fabless-before-fabless-was-a-thing, and so on. Any VC would pass on that pitch in 1985.

The asymmetric risk is that the Qualcomm team wasn't pattern-matching 'and then a miracle' — they had done the actual information-theory math under Claude Shannon himself, and each step was a calculated inference rather than a hope. The distinguishing signal isn't the shape of the plan (path-dependent and improbable) but the calibration of the team's confidence at each step, and whether that confidence is grounded in first-principles analysis or hand-waving.

Practical heuristic: when you hear a multi-miracle plan, the diagnostic is not 'how many miracles' but 'can the team explain, at first-principles depth, why each individual step is higher probability than a third party would assume?' If yes — and it will feel uncomfortable, because the plan still looks crazy — the expected value can be extraordinary. This is the class of deal venture capital exists for, and pattern-matching it away is one of the most expensive mistakes an investor can make.
