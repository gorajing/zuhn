Wen draws a clear contrast between research labs that commercialized successfully versus those that didn't. OpenAI succeeded because despite being a research lab, it needed to find revenue to fund its compute-intensive research — creating genuine pressure to build products people would pay for. Meanwhile, Google Brain and DeepMind did 'fantastic research' but didn't commercialize effectively because Google's existing revenue made commercialization optional rather than existential.

This pattern — that having too many resources is 'actually a curse rather than a blessing' — echoes a broader startup principle. The constraint of needing to make money forces focus on user problems rather than interesting research problems. It also explains why many corporate innovation labs produce papers but not products: without survival pressure, the incentive structure rewards academic output over market impact. For founders, this suggests that lean operations aren't just a financial necessity but an innovation advantage.
