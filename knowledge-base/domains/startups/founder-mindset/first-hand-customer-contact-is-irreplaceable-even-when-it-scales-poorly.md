Every time a finding passes through another person — a researcher, a consultant, a product manager, an executive summary — it gets filtered, summarized, and reframed. Nuance collapses. Contradictions get resolved prematurely. The emotional texture of what a customer actually said disappears. By the time a finding reaches the person who needs to act on it, it has been shaped to fit existing mental models rather than challenge them.

This is why Osterwalder insists that leaders searching for a value proposition or business model must do the customer work themselves, even though it doesn't scale. The loss of signal fidelity from outsourcing this work is not a small tax — it can invert the conclusion entirely. A leader who sits in on ten customer interviews will make fundamentally different decisions than one who reads a synthesis of those same interviews. The former experiences confusion and surprise; the latter receives a cleaned-up narrative.
