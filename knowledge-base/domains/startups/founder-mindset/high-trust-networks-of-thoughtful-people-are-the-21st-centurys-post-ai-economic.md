Loh — a network theorist by training — proposes that high-trust personal networks will replace centralized credentialing systems as the primary economic infrastructure. His mechanism: pair people from developed and developing countries (US/Korea/China with Rwanda/Ethiopia) through his coaching program. Over 5-10 years, they build genuine trust through shared work. When the developed-country partner starts a company, they hire the person they know and trust from the developing country via remote work.

The economic arbitrage makes this self-sustaining: compensation that's modest by US standards is transformative in developing economies. 'If you split the difference, both sides win massively.' But the critical ingredient is direct trust — not institutional credentials. The network 'shortcuts' traditional aid flows where 'somebody is professional at receiving resources' and channels them directly to proven individuals.

This model challenges the conventional VC-backed startup paradigm by suggesting the most important infrastructure being built right now isn't software platforms but trust relationships between capable people across economic boundaries. For entrepreneurs, the implication is: invest in being known and trusted by people in different economic contexts — that network IS your future competitive advantage.
