The Yahoo episode is often retold as 'Zuck had vision,' but he tells it differently: 'I hadn't done a good job communicating the long-term vision, because I didn't — I wasn't thinking about that at the time.' The board and management team rationally interpreted 'great project, lots of users' as a signal to take the billion. After they tried to fire him, his reaction was: 'I don't want to get fired from my own company for wanting to build it, so let's set up a governance structure that makes it somewhat harder to do that.'

The lesson is not 'always refuse acquisitions' or 'always hold super-voting shares' — it's that founders who plan to operate on a 20-year horizon need governance that can survive a 2-year offer. Without it, the rational behavior of every other stakeholder (LPs returning capital, employees with vesting stock, board members with fiduciary duty) compounds into an outcome the founder can't override. The structure is the only durable defense.

The trap on the other side: super-voting shares concentrate accountability in someone who may not deserve it for the full duration. Zuckerberg's case works because he kept being right (or at least, kept controlling the narrative long enough to look right); the same structure in less capable hands has produced disasters. The honest framing: this protects long-horizon vision *and* removes a check on bad long-horizon vision, and you have to be willing to accept both halves of that trade.
