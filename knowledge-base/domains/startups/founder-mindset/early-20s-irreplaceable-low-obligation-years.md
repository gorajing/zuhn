The conventional "go to big tech first, then start a company" plan
has a hidden cost: golden handcuffs trap you, you learn nothing
novel for startups (fixing Jira tickets is not founder training),
and you waste irreplaceable low-obligation years. Your early 20s
— before mortgage, kids, lifestyle inflation — are the only time
you can take maximum career risk with minimum personal downside.
The only real value of big tech is the logo on your resume, and
that value is declining as startups become more respected. Max's
argument is not anti-big-tech per se, but anti-delay: if you know
you want to build something, the opportunity cost of "getting
experience first" is measured in years you cannot recover, not in
skills you cannot learn elsewhere.
