Shin Soo-jung's correction to her own earlier book: she originally wrote 'Accumulation followed by Divergence' (the traditional Korean advice that you should quietly build expertise for years and recognition will follow), but after observing how the modern world actually rewards effort she updated to 'Accumulation AND Divergence' — the two are simultaneous, not sequential. Her concrete proof: when she joined SK Fielders at <10 employees and got her CISP certification abroad, she posted how to acquire it on Facebook. Many people in cybersecurity saw the post; without any sales work, they became aware of her company's services and bought them. The small seed of sharing her credential journey compounded into inbound customers. The mechanism: in the pre-internet era, the feedback loop from sharing to recognition to opportunity took decades (work at one company for 30 years, get promoted). The internet shortened it to years; AI to weeks. So the optimal strategy shifted from 'accumulate first, then radiate when you're ready' to 'radiate continuously starting day one, even when you're not ready, because the act of radiating produces feedback that accelerates accumulation.' The contrarian version of this for builders: don't be 'sincere, kind, hardworking, and consistent' in private — be those things publicly, where the radiation can find people who reward them. The corollary: people who are still using the 'quiet accumulation' strategy are falling behind those who don't even have much to share yet but actively express what they have.
