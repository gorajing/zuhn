This is the contrarian thesis that justifies the company's existence against the obvious objection 'why won't the foundation labs just own this?' Frontier labs' P0 is AGI — one generic model that's ever more capable at coding and math, where the playbook is clear: more pre-training, bigger models, more data, more RL, more inference compute. Memory and continual learning are real to them but live as a product effort, not the central bet.

Engram's wager is that much of what individuals and organizations want to learn is private (never appears in any post-training set) or outright conflicting (my way of doing a task contradicts yours). That data resists the clean-supervision, ground-truth-reward, well-defined-environment recipe ML has used for decades — real-world tasks are ambiguous and hard to score. So the natural form factor is millions of divergent small models, each serving a different person or team, rather than convergence on one model.

The founder lesson generalizes beyond AI: a defensible startup against incumbents with overwhelming resources comes from a structurally different worldview about where value and data live — here, that ownership of the model and proximity to private data is the wedge incumbents are not optimized to capture, not a feature race they'd win.
