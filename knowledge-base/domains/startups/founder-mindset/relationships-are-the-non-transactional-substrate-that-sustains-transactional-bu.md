Itzler notes that when his business had a 'down tick,' customers who had been treated transactionally churned, but customers embedded in genuine relationships stayed. The asymmetry matters because most revenue-smoothing assets (cash, credit, contracts) are expensive to maintain, while relational capital accrues as a byproduct of showing up well. The practical implication is that founder relational work isn't discretionary overhead — it's cheap insurance against the inevitable bad quarter. This argues against the pure 'product-led growth' frame: even in SaaS, the human layer is what gets invoked when the product has an outage or the market turns.
