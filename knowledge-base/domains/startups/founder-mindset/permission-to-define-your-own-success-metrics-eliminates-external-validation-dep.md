Startup culture is saturated with externally defined success metrics: ARR milestones, fundraising rounds, headcount, TechCrunch coverage. Optimistic nihilism applied to entrepreneurship suggests these are arbitrary constructs, and founders who explicitly define their own success criteria are more resilient to the inevitable periods where external metrics look bad.

This isn't about ignoring market feedback — it's about separating signal (customer behavior, unit economics) from noise (social comparison, prestige metrics). A founder who has consciously decided what success means to them personally can weather a down round without an identity crisis. The practical application is to write down your actual success criteria before you start, and revisit them when external pressure tempts you to optimize for someone else's scoreboard.
