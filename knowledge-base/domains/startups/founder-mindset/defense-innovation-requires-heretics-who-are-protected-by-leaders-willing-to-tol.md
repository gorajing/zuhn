Shyam Sankar catalogs historical defense innovations and finds a universal pattern: every breakthrough — the Higgins boat, the F-16, nuclear submarines, Project Maven — was heretical. The institution fought it. The bureaucracy tried to kill it. What made these innovations survive was not just the heretic's persistence but the existence of a protector — a senior leader who recognized something special and shielded them.

This maps directly to startups. The founder is the heretic by definition. But even heretics need protectors — investors, board members, early customers — who absorb institutional friction so the founder can keep building. John Boyd's three-star Air Force general, Bernard Shriver re-hiring Edward Hall after firing him — these are the defense equivalents of a board member backing a controversial pivot. The lesson: if you want breakthrough outcomes, you need both the difficult genius AND someone with enough institutional power to keep them alive.
