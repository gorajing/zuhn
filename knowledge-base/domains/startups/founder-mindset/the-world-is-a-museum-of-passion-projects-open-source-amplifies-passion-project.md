    Kieran Kunhya's quote of John Collison: 'My favorite quote on this topic is
    John Collison's — the world is a museum of passion projects. Everything out
    there is a passion project. And open source multimedia and open source in
    general — you can just do that so much faster. There's such a faster network
    effect. I can open a cafe and that can be my passion project, but I have to
    get building codes, I have to build a building, I have to find a location, I
    have to do all sorts of things. Well, in the software world, that passion
    project can move quickly, it can be amplified by the network effect, and
    that amplification can be more than the sum of the parts.' Examples from the
    conversation that support the framing: FFmpeg was started in 2000 by Fabrice
    Bellard as a passion project; today powers most video on the internet. VLC
    was a French engineering school student project from the mid-1990s; today
    downloaded 6B+ times. Linux started as Linus Torvalds's passion project;
    today powers most servers globally. Git started as Linus's tool for managing
    Linux kernel patches; today is universal version control. Anthropic, OpenAI,
    even Google all started as passion projects (or compound-passion-projects)
    of obsessed individuals. The structural argument: most enduring institutions
    exist because someone cared enough to start them without external
    validation. Career-track work that's optimized for promotion, market
    opportunity, or institutional approval rarely produces enduring impact.
    Passion projects optimized for genuine love of the work do. The software
    amplification: a physical passion project (a cafe, a book, a workshop)
    compounds linearly with effort. A software passion project compounds with
    network effects — each new user makes the project more valuable to the next
    user. VLC's 6 billion downloads weren't from advertising or marketing
    budgets; they were from word-of-mouth and search-engine-organic compounding
    over 25 years. The corollary for time allocation: examine your time. How
    much is going to passion projects with network-effect potential vs to
    career-track work without it? Most people spend 90%+ on the latter, 10% on
    the former. The reverse allocation (90% passion projects with network
    effects, 10% career maintenance) is what produces unexpectedly large impact.
    The corollary for ambitious founders: when choosing what to build, prefer
    ideas you'd build for free if no one paid you. Those have passion-project
    sustainability. Ideas you'd only build for the money tend to lose engagement
    when the money is slow to materialize. The corollary for AI-era career
    planning: AI dramatically lowers the cost of building. The marginal effort
    required to start a passion project is approaching zero. The marginal
    returns to existing career-track work are also declining as AI eats the
    assembly-of-known-patterns tier. The relative ROI of passion projects has
    never been higher. The corollary for organizations: 20% time and similar
    policies (Google's famous, now-defunct policy) acknowledged this. Companies
    that systematically allocate engineer time to passion projects often produce
    their most impactful products from those efforts (Gmail, AdSense, Google
    Maps all came from 20% time). The companies that killed 20% time have
    systematically lost edge.

  movements started as someone's passion project — but software passion projects
  compound enormously faster than physical-world equivalents because the network

  for ambitious operators is on passion projects that have network-effect
