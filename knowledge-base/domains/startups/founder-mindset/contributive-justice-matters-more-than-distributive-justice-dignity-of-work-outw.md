Sandel distinguishes distributive justice (fair allocation of income/wealth) from contributive justice (recognition and esteem for contributions to common good). We could achieve perfect redistribution through taxation and still have a corrosive problem: a hierarchy of prestige where tech entrepreneurs and hedge fund managers sit at the top while care workers, teachers, and delivery workers are overlooked.

The pandemic briefly exposed this misalignment — we celebrated essential workers, then forgot them when things returned to normal. Sandel argues the populist backlash against elites isn't primarily about money; it's about the sense among working people that their contributions are devalued. People want work to be not just a living but a source of dignity and social recognition. When tech founders accumulate not just wealth but outsized prestige, honor, and political influence, it creates a hierarchy of regard that poisons democratic life. The market serves as a default — and unreflective — arbiter of contribution value, but market prices are not moral truths.
