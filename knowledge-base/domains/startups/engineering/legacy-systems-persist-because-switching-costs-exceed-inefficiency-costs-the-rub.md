RuBisCo evolved when atmospheric CO2 was abundant and oxygen scarce. When oxygen levels rose, RuBisCo began mistakenly fixing O2 instead of CO2 roughly half the time, producing a toxic byproduct. Yet plants never evolved a replacement — they went all-in, producing ~40 billion tons of RuBisCo globally and building secondary pathways to neutralize the toxic waste.

This mirrors how organizations retain legacy systems. The decision to keep a bad-but-working system is rational when: (1) the system is deeply coupled to everything else, (2) workarounds are cheaper than rewrites, and (3) the system still delivers net-positive value despite inefficiency. The lesson isn't that legacy is good — it's that the threshold for replacement is far higher than engineers typically estimate. Before proposing a rewrite, compare the true cost of scaling compensatory workarounds against the cost and risk of replacement.
