Graham uses a thought experiment: if a language blocked your preferred expression but forced you into something shorter, it wouldn't feel restrictive — it would feel like a shortcut. The frustration of restrictiveness comes specifically from forced detours that make programs longer than the version in your head.

This reframes language evaluation. Instead of asking whether a language has specific features, ask whether it forces unnecessary verbosity. Features like macros, higher-order functions, and dynamic typing aren't valuable in themselves — they're valuable because they eliminate forced detours. This also explains why experienced programmers in powerful languages find it 'unbearably restrictive' to return to verbose ones: they can conceive compact solutions they're no longer allowed to express.
