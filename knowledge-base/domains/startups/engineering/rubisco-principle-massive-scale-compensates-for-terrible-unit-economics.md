RuBisCo is the most abundant protein on Earth (~40 billion tons) despite being spectacularly bad at its job — it confuses oxygen for CO2 roughly half the time, producing a toxic byproduct plants must then clean up. Plants never evolved a better enzyme because they had already 'gone all-in' on the RuBisCo strategy.

This mirrors a pattern in engineering and business: when a core system is deeply embedded, organizations scale around its flaws rather than replace it. Legacy codebases, dominant-but-inefficient platforms, and entrenched business processes all exhibit this. The lesson isn't that inefficiency is acceptable — it's that the cost of switching can exceed the cost of compensating, and recognizing which situation you're in determines the right strategy.

The corollary is that competitors who start fresh without the legacy burden can sometimes leapfrog — but only if the switching cost for the incumbent's ecosystem is lower than the performance gap. In RuBisCo's case, 450 million years later, no plant has managed the switch.
