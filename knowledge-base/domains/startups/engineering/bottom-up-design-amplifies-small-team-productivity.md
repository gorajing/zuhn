Frederick Brooks observed that programmer productivity decreases as team size grows due to communication overhead. Paul Graham reframes this: small teams using bottom-up design don't just lose less to coordination costs — they gain a compounding advantage because each abstraction they build reduces the remaining problem size.

This has direct implications for startup engineering. Instead of hiring more people to handle complexity, a small team can invest in building the right abstractions first. The resulting codebase has fewer components, fewer inter-component connections, and fewer places for bugs to hide. The constraint of a small team becomes an advantage when paired with bottom-up methodology.
