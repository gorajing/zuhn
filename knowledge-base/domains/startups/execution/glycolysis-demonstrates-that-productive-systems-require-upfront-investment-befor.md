Glycolysis requires the cell to spend 2 ATP molecules before the process can generate 4 ATP, yielding a net profit of 2 ATP. This 'you gotta spend money to make money' principle operates at the most fundamental level of biology — even single cells cannot generate energy without first investing energy.

This has implications for how we think about startup economics and system bootstrapping. The pattern where initial investment is mandatory before any returns are possible is not just a feature of human economic systems — it's baked into the physics of energy transformation. Systems that try to skip the investment phase (extracting returns without upfront cost) are working against a principle that biology solved billions of years ago. The key insight is that the investment-to-return ratio matters more than avoiding investment entirely: glycolysis's 2:4 ratio is modest, but it enables the downstream stages that produce 36 more ATP.
