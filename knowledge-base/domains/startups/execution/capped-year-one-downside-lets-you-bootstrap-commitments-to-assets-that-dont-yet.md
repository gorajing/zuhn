The IPL launch involved an interlocking web of commitments — $1B in media rights from WSG/Sony, $724M in franchise fees, jersey sponsorships, Bollywood star ownership — for a league that hadn't played a single ball. The whole structure would have collapsed if any party had to write a large check up front.

Modi's insight was to structure every deal so year-one cash exposure was the smallest possible amount that signaled real commitment, with the bulk of the obligation deferred to years 2-10. Sony only had to pay $60M of its $1B media commitment in year one. Franchise owners only paid $5M of their $50-100M+ bids in year one. Shah Rukh Khan's $5M year-one franchise payment was offset exactly by the $5M Nokia jersey sponsorship Modi had pre-arranged for him. WSG took on the $1B notional but only paid $60M cash with an implicit gentleman's agreement that Modi would dissolve the league if year one failed (and they could cover their costs by sub-licensing to Sony).

Each party effectively had a free year-one option: minimal cash out, asymmetric upside if it worked, and an implied exit if it didn't. This is how Modi got billion-dollar commitments to a non-existent product. The general principle: when you need to coordinate multiple counterparties into committing to something that doesn't exist yet, the right design isn't to pitch the steady-state economics — it's to engineer the year-one financials so that nobody is meaningfully on the hook even though their notional commitments are enormous. Once year one proves the thesis, the deferred obligations become real, but by then there's a working business and everyone wants in.
