Calculus 1 introduces marginal cost as the derivative of total cost — the cost of producing one more unit. This seemingly simple application carries a profound business insight: optimal production quantity is determined not by average costs or total revenue, but by the relationship between marginal cost and marginal revenue at the boundary. You maximize profit precisely where MC = MR, which is often far from where intuition would place it.

This has direct implications for startup execution and pricing. Founders often think in aggregates ('our average cost per user is X') when they should think in marginals ('what does the next user cost us?'). The marginal framework explains why scaling often breaks: marginal costs can spike non-linearly even when averages look healthy. It also explains why some seemingly unprofitable businesses are actually well-optimized — their marginal economics are sound even if total numbers look concerning early on.
