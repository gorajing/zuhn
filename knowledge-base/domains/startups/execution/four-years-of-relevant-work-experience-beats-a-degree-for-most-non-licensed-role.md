Hormozi speaks from the employer side of the hiring table: across his portfolio of companies employing nearly a thousand people, no one checks where candidates went to school, what they majored in, or their GPA. The only things that matter are relevant experience and the likelihood of performing well in the role. Given a choice between a four-year philosophy graduate and someone with four years of sales experience for a sales role, the experienced candidate wins every time.

This creates an asymmetric opportunity cost. The degree-holder spent four years and $200K+ learning content largely irrelevant to their eventual job, while the career-starter spent four years building directly applicable skills and getting paid. The exception is clearly delineated: licensed professions (doctor, lawyer, accountant) and roles in science/technology where formal credentials create genuine value. For everything else — sales, marketing, operations, trades, home services — the degree is pure overhead. Many of today's most in-demand roles didn't even exist when current degree programs were designed.
