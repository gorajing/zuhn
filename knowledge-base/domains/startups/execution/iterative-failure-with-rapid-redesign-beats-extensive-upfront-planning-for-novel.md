Rather than spending months modeling stress distributions and material properties, the Mad Batter team took a fundamentally iterative approach: spin up, break something, diagnose the failure, redesign, and try again. Wooden bat snapped at the stress concentration point? Switch to aluminum. Aluminum bat flew off the shaft? Drill pins through the handles. Machine vibrated dangerously? Add a wobble switch kill relay.

This mirrors the lean startup methodology but in physical engineering: each failure provided precise diagnostic information (tension break location, grip failure mode, imbalance effects) that no amount of upfront simulation would have revealed as efficiently. The key insight is that for truly novel problems — where you lack prior data — controlled failure is the fastest path to the information you need. The prerequisite is having safety margins that let you fail without catastrophe, which is the engineering equivalent of limiting downside risk.
