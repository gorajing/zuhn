Hormozi uses the metaphor of a bridge with 30 bricks: you must lay all 30 before the first dollar can cross. At 28 bricks, you've invested 93% of the effort but captured 0% of the return. Many people abandon at this stage and start a new bridge, accumulating half-built bridges that each represent sunk costs with zero return.

The critical insight is that returns in skill-building and business are highly nonlinear — the last 10% of effort often captures 100% of the value. This maps to the concept of threshold effects in career development: a mediocre copywriter who also knows media buying, branding, and management becomes a CMO. Each additional skill below the threshold adds little; crossing the threshold unlocks disproportionate returns. The discipline to finish one bridge completely before starting another is the primary differentiator.
