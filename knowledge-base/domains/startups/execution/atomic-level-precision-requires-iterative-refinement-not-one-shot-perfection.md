To create the roundest object ever made, scientists started with a sphere 2mm larger than needed and progressively ground it finer using increasingly delicate abrasives until they were 'massaging atoms.' The final sphere's surface deviations are so small that if scaled to Earth size, the highest mountain to lowest valley would be just 14 meters apart.

This manufacturing process is a direct analogy for how exceptional quality is achieved in any domain: you don't try to nail perfection on the first pass. You start with rough approximation and iteratively refine. Each pass uses finer-grained tools appropriate to the current level of precision. Early passes remove large errors; later passes address subtler imperfections.

The 14-meter-on-Earth comparison also illustrates how human intuition fails at extreme precision — we can't perceive the scale of quality difference between 'good enough' and 'world class.' This perception gap means most people stop refining far too early because they can't see the remaining imperfections.
