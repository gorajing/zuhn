Graham's Florence argument is striking: fifteenth-century Florence had Brunelleschi, Ghiberti, Donatello, Masaccio, Botticelli, Leonardo, and Michelangelo. Milan was equally large but produced almost no comparable artists. With roughly 1,000x Florence's population, the modern US should statistically contain a thousand Leonardos — yet we don't see proportional output. The variable isn't DNA but environment.

This explains why great work clusters: the Bauhaus, Manhattan Project, Xerox PARC, Lockheed's Skunk Works. For founders and builders, the implication is that choosing where to work and who to work alongside is not a lifestyle decision but a strategic one. Being too far removed from a center of excellence in your field makes it nearly impossible to do great work, regardless of individual ability. The talent-density network effect compounds — each great person raises the bar and provides feedback for others.
