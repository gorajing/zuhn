Cruise ships face a seasonal demand problem: Caribbean routes peak November through April, then demand shifts to Europe. Moving a fleet of ships across the Atlantic empty would be enormously expensive. Instead, lines sell repositioning cruises — one-way, two-week voyages from Florida to Amsterdam at discounted rates. The ship is always making money, and the moment one cruise ends, the next begins.

This principle — monetizing what would otherwise be dead transit time — applies broadly to capital-intensive businesses. The turnaround day itself is another example of extreme utilization: in under half a day, 6,000 passengers exit, the entire ship is cleaned, restocked with 700,000 eggs and 15,000 pounds of potatoes, and 6,000 new passengers board. The ship departs at 5 PM whether you're on it or not, because every hour in port is an hour the casino isn't running. The underlying insight is that asset utilization rate, not asset quality, often determines profitability in capital-heavy industries.
