Graham identifies a specific linguistic pattern — 'We're doing X, but we're going to keep working on the startup' — as a near-perfect predictor of failure. The mechanism is twofold: first, the distraction itself diverts scarce founder attention; second, and more insidiously, having an alternative identity ('grad student who also does a startup') provides a face-saving narrative for failure that makes quitting psychologically cheaper.

This connects to the broader commitment-device argument: startups succeed partly because founders have no escape route. Grad school, consulting projects, and day jobs all create escape routes. Graham specifically flags graduate school as the most dangerous distraction because it provides both an alternative time sink and an alternative identity. The practical test is simple: if the startup is not your sole occupation, you've already de-risked your psychology in a way that de-risks failure along with it.
