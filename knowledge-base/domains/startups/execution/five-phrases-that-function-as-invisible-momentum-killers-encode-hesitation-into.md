Language shapes organizational behavior more than most founders realize. Five seemingly innocent phrases—'let's revisit this later,' 'we're waiting for more data,' 'let's explore other options,' 'it's not hurting anyone,' and 'we'll get to it after X'—each sound responsible but encode specific pathologies: indefinite deferral, analysis paralysis, commitment avoidance, tolerance of clutter, and backlog accumulation.

The fix isn't banning these phrases but replacing them with time-boxed alternatives that preserve forward motion. 'Revisit later' becomes 'decide by Friday.' 'Waiting for data' becomes 'what's the fastest test we can run this week?' 'Keep it live' becomes 'if it's not compounding value, retire it by end of sprint.' Each replacement converts an open-ended stall into a bounded action. Over time, this linguistic discipline shapes a culture where motion is the default and hesitation requires explicit justification.
