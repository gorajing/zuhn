Naval reframes Gladwell's 10,000 hours concept: it's directionally correct but misleading because it implies passive time accumulation leads to mastery. The actual driver is the number of complete iteration cycles: try something, test the result against a free market or physics, ask what worked, make a creative guess for improvement, and repeat. The faster you can rotate through this loop, the steeper your learning curve.

This has direct implications for team composition and company culture. Great people distill insights from every iteration, meaning they extract maximum learning per cycle. It also explains why startups can outrun large companies — not because they work harder, but because they iterate faster. A startup deep in the 'idea maze' has explored and eliminated thousands of dead ends that a copying competitor will still need to discover independently, giving the startup a compounding knowledge advantage that looks trivially copyable from outside but isn't.
