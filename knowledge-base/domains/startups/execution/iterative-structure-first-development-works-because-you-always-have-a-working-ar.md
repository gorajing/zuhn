Graham draws an explicit parallel between writing lists and iterative software development: you think of N/2 points in five minutes, giving you a complete but low-resolution structure, then progressively refine. He compares this to both programming (ship version 1 fast, then iterate) and painting (start with a blurry sketch, crank up resolution over a week).

This is a powerful mental model for any creative or building work. The key insight is that having a 'valid artifact at every stage' dramatically reduces the risk of dead ends. In traditional essay writing (or waterfall development), you might invest hours only to discover your train of thought doesn't lead anywhere. The iterative structure-first approach eliminates this risk by ensuring you always have something that works, even if it's rough. The trade-off is that you sacrifice the serendipitous discovery that comes from following an unstructured train of thought.
