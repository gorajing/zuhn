The Calvin Cycle fixes three CO2 molecules per turn, consuming 9 ATP and 6 NADPH to produce 6 G3P molecules. But 5 of those 6 G3P units are immediately consumed to regenerate the 3 RuBP molecules needed to start the next cycle. Only 1 G3P is 'net output.' The cycle has an 83% overhead ratio.

This pattern — where most output is consumed maintaining the system rather than producing value — appears widely in organizations: teams where most work is coordination, codebases where most effort is maintenance, funds where most returns are reinvested to maintain positions. The gross output looks impressive; the net is much smaller.

The strategic question in any cyclical system is: what is the actual net extraction rate, and can the overhead be reduced? In photosynthesis, C4 plants evolved a pre-concentration mechanism that feeds RuBisCo more CO2, reducing wasted cycles. Analogously, organizations that reduce coordination overhead (fewer meetings, better tooling, clearer ownership) improve their effective throughput without increasing gross activity.
