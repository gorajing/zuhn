Ballmer's pattern: every time Microsoft needed to build a business that would disrupt an existing franchise, they pulled it out of the franchise's org. Windows NT was incubated away from Windows 9x. Azure was incubated under Ozzie, not under Server & Tools, even though Azure was literally the future of Server & Tools. The incumbent team has too much self-preservation instinct, too much installed pattern, and too much short-term revenue pressure to nurture a business that competes with their own P&L.

The constraint: incubation only works if leadership is willing to let the incubated thing eventually subsume the franchise. Ballmer notes that even Azure was 'constrained by Windows' at launch (platform-as-a-service tied to .NET/Windows rather than generic infrastructure-as-a-service), and that constraint was a drag — a half-measure between genuine independence and full integration.

The takeaway: organizational distance matters more than technology. If a new bet threatens an existing business, its org chart must place it where the threatened team's leadership cannot slow it down or fold it back in. Give the new team its own budget, its own hiring, and its own goals — even if the overlap with the existing business seems wasteful.
