Kim frames culture as a 'superorganism' where the collection is greater than the individuals combined (1+1=3). Culture operates like muscle memory — habitual, slow to change, and impossible to rewire through top-down declarations. Saying 'today we define our culture as XYZ' doesn't work because culture is a collection of people's habits that evolves through nudging and iterative feedback loops.

His counter-example is devastating: at a previous company with 100 employees, he watched leadership disconnect from the field, good people leave first, while toxic employees who were skilled at 'selling to their senior bosses' gained power. The culture rot killed execution. The lesson: culture is the operating system of a company. Day-to-day decision making and execution are dictated by culture, not by checklists. Start with mission, core values, and workshops from day one — but know that maintaining culture requires constant, daily reinforcement.
