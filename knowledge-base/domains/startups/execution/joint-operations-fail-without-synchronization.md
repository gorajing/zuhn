At Gallipoli, the British Navy spent two months trying to run the Dardanelles alone before the army showed up. That gave the Ottomans ample warning to fortify the cliffs, and the landing stalemated in three days. The campaign ground on for eight months and cost 190,000 casualties. Normandy was the inverse — years of materiel buildup, active disinformation pointing the Germans at Pas-de-Calais, and every service launching on a single day. It worked.

This generalizes cleanly to startups and product launches: when engineering, marketing, sales, and customer success all move in sequence instead of in lockstep, the competition has time to adapt between each phase. The 'joint' operation — where all functions commit on the same day after shared buildup — is a force multiplier precisely because it denies the adversary (or the market) reaction time. Pre-announcing one piece of a strategy weeks before the rest is the startup equivalent of running the Dardanelles alone.
