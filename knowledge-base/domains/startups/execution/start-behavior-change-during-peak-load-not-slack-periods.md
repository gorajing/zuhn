The conventional wisdom is to wait for a calm period to introduce new behaviors, but this creates a fragile habit that collapses the moment conditions worsen. If a behavior change is contingent on having free time, then the moment you get busy again, the habit stops. This is the 'when-then fallacy' — believing you need conditions X before you can do Y, when actually doing Y under current conditions is what creates durable change.

The parallel to startups and systems is direct: stress-test processes under load, not in ideal conditions. A deployment pipeline that only works when traffic is low isn't reliable. A sales script that only works with warm leads isn't robust. The same applies to personal execution — if you can maintain the behavior when you're at maximum capacity, it will survive any future load. Additionally, if you're busy and not making progress, the first intervention isn't addition but elimination: you're doing the wrong things, and removing low-value activities frees more capacity than optimizing scheduling ever will.
