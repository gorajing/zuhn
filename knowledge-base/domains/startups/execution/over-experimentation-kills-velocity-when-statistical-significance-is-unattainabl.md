Verna identifies over-experimentation as a growth anti-pattern: teams that insist on A/B testing everything, including low-traffic scenarios where statistical significance is mathematically impossible within reasonable timeframes. This creates analysis paralysis disguised as rigor.

The practical heuristic is a one-month threshold: if you can't get statistical significance within a month, switch to pre/post analysis. Trust intuition more when you have low traffic/conversion volumes, the change is low-risk, you need to move quickly, or the impact is obvious. This isn't anti-data — it's recognizing that the cost of perfect information often exceeds the cost of being slightly wrong. Micro-optimizations like button colors, single one-off emails, and adding auth providers rarely move the needle regardless of what the test says.
