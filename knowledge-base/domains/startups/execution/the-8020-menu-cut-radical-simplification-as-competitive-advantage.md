By 1948, the McDonald brothers had a thriving restaurant with 20 waitresses, 100+ cars on weekends, and enough money for a mansion. Most entrepreneurs would optimize what was working. Instead, they analyzed their receipts, found 80% of revenue came from hamburgers, and made the radical decision to shut down entirely — cutting ribs, pork sandwiches, hot dogs, and waitress service. They then physically traced their kitchen layout on tennis courts, timing and measuring every step to create the Speedy Service System.

The result: 15-second order fulfillment, no need for skilled cooks, lower costs, cleaner restaurants, and a replicable system. This wasn't just efficiency — it was a new category. The key insight is that simplification isn't about doing less, it's about creating room for a fundamentally different operating model. Their competitors couldn't copy just the menu reduction; they had to copy the entire system that simplification enabled. This pattern recurs in tech (Apple's product line under Jobs, Google's homepage) — radical subtraction creates space for radical innovation in what remains.
