Prieto frames the lack of startup playbooks in 1999 as potentially a gift — it forced eDreams to derive strategy from direct observation of customer behavior rather than adopting borrowed frameworks. Their innovations (service fee model, multi-carrier aggregation, hybrid OTA-Meta) emerged from watching what customers actually did, not from following prescribed methodologies.

This suggests a tension in the modern startup ecosystem: the proliferation of frameworks (lean startup, jobs-to-be-done, blitzscaling) can create a false sense of certainty that substitutes for genuine customer observation. Frameworks are useful guardrails but can become cognitive cages when founders optimize for framework compliance rather than reality. The practical implication is that founders should treat playbooks as hypothesis generators, not instruction manuals — and should regularly ask whether their strategy derives from customer reality or from framework orthodoxy.
