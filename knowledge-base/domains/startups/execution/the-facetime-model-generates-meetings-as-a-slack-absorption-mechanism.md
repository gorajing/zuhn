Graham identifies a causal chain: companies can't measure knowledge worker productivity, so they enforce physical presence as a proxy. This creates hours of neither-working-nor-fun time. Meetings become the primary mechanism for absorbing this slack — they count as work but require minimal effort, functioning like 'an opiate with a network effect.'

The deeper problem is that people pretending to work interrupt those actually working, fragmenting productive time into unusable chunks. Graham's proposed experiment — a 'Work Day' where meetings are forbidden — highlights how radical it would feel to simply let people work. The fix isn't better meetings but eliminating the facetime model entirely and measuring output directly, as his own startup did with no fixed hours.
