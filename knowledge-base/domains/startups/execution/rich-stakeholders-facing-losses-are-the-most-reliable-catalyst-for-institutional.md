The Articles of Confederation had obvious structural problems for years, but reform only materialized after Shays' Rebellion threatened property rights and government bondholders realized they might never get repaid. As Green notes: 'When rich people feel like something has to be done, something is usually done.' The Annapolis Convention of 1786 attracted only six states, but after the financial threat crystallized, the Philadelphia Convention drew 55 delegates — most of them wealthy, over half college-educated.

This pattern recurs in every institutional context. Abstract problems (bad governance, technical debt, organizational dysfunction) can persist indefinitely. Concrete financial threats to powerful stakeholders trigger rapid, decisive action. For startup founders, this means that if you need institutional change — a pivot, a restructuring, a governance overhaul — you're more likely to get buy-in by connecting the change to investors' or board members' direct financial interests than by arguing about structural elegance. The catalyst for the US Constitution wasn't a better idea about governance; it was rich people's money being at risk.
