Horowitz challenges the narrative that startups succeed through one big bet or pivotal moment. Instead, he argues that outcomes are the compound result of getting many small decisions right—each feeling insignificant in isolation but devastating in aggregate when consistently wrong. This reframes founder work from 'find the one big insight' to 'build a system for making many good small calls.'

This has practical implications for how founders spend their time. Instead of agonizing over a few 'big' strategic decisions, the highest-ROI activity is improving the quality and speed of the hundreds of daily micro-decisions. It also explains why execution-oriented founders often outperform vision-oriented ones—they're optimizing the right variable. The compounding nature means early decision quality matters disproportionately, as each correct choice creates better context for the next.
