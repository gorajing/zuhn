RuBisCo is described explicitly as 'not particularly good at its job'—slow, promiscuous, prone to misfiring. But at the time of its emergence, it was categorically better than the alternative (waiting for chemically formed organic carbon), so single-celled organisms simply produced it in huge quantities to compensate for its poor per-unit performance. That strategy was sufficient to achieve planetary dominance.

For founders and operators: the instinct to delay shipping until a solution is optimal is often wrong. A mediocre solution deployed at scale—with resources poured into compensating for its weaknesses—will frequently outperform a theoretically superior solution that hasn't shipped. Iteration speed and volume of attempts matter more than per-attempt quality in most early-stage competitive environments. The biological record suggests this strategy is not just viable but world-conquering.
