Most founders delay releases because they intuitively know the stress level will spike once real users are involved. Graham argues this stress is the point — it's a feature, not a bug. The shift from 'interesting problem' to 'alarming problem' is what drives the velocity startups need to survive.

This reframes the 'release early' advice from a tactical product decision to a psychological lever. The minimal release also acts as a shakedown cruise that surfaces fatal problems — bad ideas, cofounder conflicts, broken architectures — before they become deeply embedded. Wufoo released their form builder before the database even worked, got 83,000 users, and discovered a critical Flash dependency they could fix early. The cost of waiting is not just lost time but lost signal.
