The panel repeatedly located the agentic-AI adoption bottleneck not in the technology but in human and organizational inertia. The argument: Fortune 1000 process structures are built around humans — handoffs, sign-offs, approval chains across legal/purchasing/finance/accounting. Dropping an agent on top of a human-shaped process doesn't work, because the process itself assumes human actors at each node. You have to re-engineer the business process for agents, which large organizations are structurally unwilling to do.

The corollary: young companies have an advantage that's nothing to do with technology — a CEO who can decide in the break room to redesign the organization around the agent rather than around humans. They design org-around-agent from scratch; incumbents must retrofit agent-into-org, which fights every existing structure.

This converges with multiple sources in this corpus (Panel 2's enterprise-conservatism, PM-Google's note that the tools outpace team coordination) and with the existing KB principle that AI's adoption bottleneck is organizational change, not model capability. The accumulating evidence is strong enough to treat as a planning assumption: when forecasting AI adoption in a given org, weight its willingness/ability to redesign process far more heavily than the capability of the models available to it.

The sharp sales implication (developed in the companion 'meet customers where they are' insight): since you can't easily change the org, either sell to orgs structurally able to change, or retrofit into the existing process rather than demanding redesign.
