Graham argues that organizations historically dampened variation in returns for performance — your prestige was your institution's prestige, and working outside institutions was nearly impossible. Technology has eroded this: ambitious projects now require less initial funding, and individuals can learn, earn, find collaborators, and reach audiences without institutional backing.

The consequence is double-edged. The democratization is authentic — many new winners will be people institutions would never have admitted. But without institutional dampening, variation in outcomes increases: those who do well do even better, those who do badly do worse. This means pursuing superlinear returns is not for everyone. It's rational for those confident in their ability or young enough to afford the risk of finding out. The key insight is that this shift is accelerating and structural, not cyclical — driven by technology reducing the cost of ambition.
