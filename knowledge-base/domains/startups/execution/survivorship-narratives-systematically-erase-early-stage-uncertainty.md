Graham observes that when PR people and journalists recount startup histories, they always underestimate initial uncertainty. Looking at Google, it seems impossible they were ever vulnerable — but Graham argues that just six months of the wrong focus by two employees could have killed the company. The 'railroad tracks of destiny' framing is a cognitive distortion that emerges after the fact.

This has practical consequences for founders and investors alike. Founders who believe the survivorship narrative may underestimate the criticality of early decisions and focus. Investors who internalize it may undervalue the role of early-stage guidance. The insight also explains why early-stage startup risk is so poorly priced by outsiders: the true fragility is invisible in retrospect, which is precisely why the rewards for those who navigate it successfully are disproportionately large.
