During the 2008 financial crisis, Maersk faced collapsing demand and surging fuel costs. A working group proposed simply going slower — running ships at 14-18 knots instead of the designed 22. The industry had long assumed this would damage engines, but Maersk proved that belief false. The result was 10-25% fuel savings per journey, and slow steaming became standard practice industry-wide.

The pattern here is that pre-crisis operations often carry inherited assumptions that nobody questions during good times ('ships must run at designed cruising speed'). Crisis strips away the luxury of those assumptions, forcing experiments that would otherwise never be tried. When those experiments succeed, the innovation persists long after the crisis ends. This applies to startups facing cash crunches, teams under headcount freezes, and any organization where resource constraints force a reexamination of unquestioned defaults.
