The emotional arc of any new venture follows a predictable pattern: uninformed optimism (everything looks amazing), informed pessimism (you discover the hidden work), and the valley of despair (the work feels overwhelming relative to results). Most people bail at the valley and jump to a new opportunity, resetting to uninformed optimism. This creates a lifetime of half-built bridges.

The antidote is Hormozi's duration test: do something for so long that it would be unreasonable for you to be bad at it. This reframes persistence not as blind stubbornness but as a statistical inevitability — given enough reps, competence emerges. The serial starter's fundamental error is confusing the discomfort of the learning curve with a signal that the opportunity is wrong, when in reality every worthwhile pursuit has this exact same valley.
