Clair Patterson's journey illustrates how investment in measurement infrastructure compounds in unexpected ways. His original goal was narrow: measure the ratio of uranium to lead in meteorites to determine Earth's age. But his lead measurements were contaminated by environmental lead from decades of leaded gasoline use. Rather than work around the problem, he built extreme infrastructure — tearing out lead-soldered electrical cables, cleaning daily with ammonia, requiring plastic bunny suits, essentially inventing the cleanroom.

This 'overkill' precision for his original problem (dating meteorites) created a platform for a far more consequential discovery. Once he could measure lead with extreme accuracy, he turned the same tools on ocean water at different depths, ice cores spanning millennia, and ancient bones vs. modern bones. Each measurement demolished the industry claim that environmental lead was natural.

The pattern applies broadly to execution strategy: building measurement and tooling infrastructure that exceeds your immediate needs creates optionality. Patterson's cleanroom didn't just help him date the Earth at 4.55 billion years — it gave him the precision to prove an entire industry was poisoning civilization, ultimately saving over a million lives per year after the global leaded fuel ban.
