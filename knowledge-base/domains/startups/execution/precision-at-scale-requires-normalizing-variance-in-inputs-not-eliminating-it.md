Byron Ferguson's ability to hit an aspirin tablet mid-flight seems like magic but rests on rigorous input normalization. He spine-tests every arrow, measuring its stiffness under a standardized 2-pound weight at 26 inches. Arrows outside tolerance go into a reject box — he simply won't shoot them. This ensures every arrow wobbles identically, making the wobble predictable rather than random.

This principle applies directly to any precision operation: manufacturing, software deployment, content creation, sales processes. The instinct is to eliminate variance entirely (make a perfectly rigid arrow), but that's often impossible or counterproductive. The superior approach is to measure variance rigorously, set tolerance bands, and reject inputs that fall outside them. This transforms unpredictable variance into predictable, manageable behavior — the foundation of repeatable excellence.
