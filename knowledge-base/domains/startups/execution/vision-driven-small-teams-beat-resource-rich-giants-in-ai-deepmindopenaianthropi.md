Daniel Kokotajlo's empirical case against the 'random discovery wins' hypothesis: 'Think about the recent history of AGI. There's DeepMind, there's various other AI companies, then there's OpenAI and there's Anthropic — there's just this repeated story of big bloated company with tons of money, tons of smart researchers, flailing around trying a ton of different things at different points. Smaller startup with a vision of we're going to build AGI and overall working towards that vision more coherently with a few cracked engineers and researchers. And then they crush the giant company. Even though they have less compute, even though they have less researchers, they're able to do fewer experiments.' Why this matters: if random fortuitous discovery dominated, the giant companies with zillions of researchers running zillions of experiments would be going proportionally faster than tiny startups with vision. That basically doesn't happen — it's rare. This is direct empirical evidence that research TASTE matters more than experimental volume. Some humans/teams are better at picking which 1000 things to NOT try and which 1 thing to try carefully. The corollary: 'You get more lottery tickets for these discoveries when you are intelligent, when you have good technology, when you're doing good work. It's not randomly distributed across everyone in the world.' Scott Alexander adds: when 'random fortuitous discoveries' happen, they happen to extremely smart professors who've been working on adjacent things for years. The Ozempic discovery from Gila monster venom looks random — but it required someone smart enough to look at the venom in the right way. The implication for builders: focus on hiring research taste, not experiment-running capacity. One person with good taste running 10 experiments will out-produce 100 mediocre people running 1000 experiments. The implication for capital allocators in AI: small vision-aligned teams with cracked engineers are higher EV than well-funded giants on average. The metric to optimize when evaluating an AI lab isn't 'how much compute do they have' but 'how good is their research taste, and how coherent is their direction'.
