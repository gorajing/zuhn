Hormozi credits his gym success partly to having signed a lease with all his savings — he literally couldn't quit when the passion faded. Phil Knight's principle reinforces this: 'You're not out of business when your bank runs out; you're out when there are padlocks on your door.' The constraint wasn't psychological but structural.

This maps to commitment device theory in behavioral economics: humans are poor at self-regulation over long time horizons, so engineering external constraints (financial commitments, public declarations, contractual obligations) produces more reliable execution than willpower alone. The implication for founders is to deliberately create situations where quitting is harder than continuing — not out of recklessness, but as a structural support for the consistency that passion alone won't provide.
