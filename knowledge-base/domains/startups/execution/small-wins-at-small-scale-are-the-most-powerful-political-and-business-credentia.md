Bukele's path from Nuevo Cuscatlán mayor to president follows a pattern that maps directly to startup scaling: prove it works small, then use that proof to earn the right to go bigger. His tiny town became a living case study — journalists couldn't find a single critic, literacy went from 15% to near-universal, and the homicide rate dropped to near zero. These weren't projections or promises; they were observable, verifiable results.

The startup parallel is the 'do things that don't scale' principle applied to credibility-building. Bukele forfeited his salary, personally branded every project, and invested disproportionate resources into a small town — none of which would scale to a country. But that wasn't the point. The point was to create an undeniable proof point. For founders, this maps to the power of a single reference customer, a single market, a single use case executed perfectly. The constraint of small scale is actually an advantage: it's easier to show transformative results when the denominator is small.
