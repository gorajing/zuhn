Rather than evolving a more efficient carbon-fixation enzyme, plants simply manufacture enormous quantities of RuBisCo — an estimated 40 billion tons exist on the planet at any time. This brute-force approach works because the cost of producing more enzyme is lower than the cost of re-architecting the entire photosynthetic pathway.

This pattern appears frequently in startups: when a core system (database, algorithm, API) has known inefficiencies but is deeply integrated, the pragmatic response is often to throw more resources at it (more servers, more workers, more instances) rather than undertake a risky rewrite. The key insight is recognizing when scaling around a problem is genuinely cheaper than fixing it — and that this can be a permanent, viable strategy, not just a temporary hack.
