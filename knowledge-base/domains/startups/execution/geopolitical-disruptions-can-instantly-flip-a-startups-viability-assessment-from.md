Northern Pacific Airways planned to create a connecting hub in Anchorage for trans-Pacific travel — essentially an Iceland Air model for the Pacific. Before the sanctions, experts dismissed it because trans-Pacific fares were already low due to fierce Asian airline competition, and direct routes over Russia made Anchorage a detour. Within weeks of the airspace closures, Delta's Detroit-Seoul flight had to reroute from 13 to 14 hours via a southern path, while Northern Pacific's Anchorage hub route could actually beat Delta by an hour on Boston-Seoul itineraries.

The insight isn't that geopolitical disruption helps startups — it's that business models dismissed as structurally unviable can be sitting on hidden optionality tied to regime changes in the operating environment. Northern Pacific's bet wasn't on Russia invading Ukraine; it was on a geographic position that would become valuable under multiple disruption scenarios. The startup equivalent: building capabilities that are marginal today but become critical under plausible future states is a form of strategic optionality, even if the market currently prices that optionality at zero.
