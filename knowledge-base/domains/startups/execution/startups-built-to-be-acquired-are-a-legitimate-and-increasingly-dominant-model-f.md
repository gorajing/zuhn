Graham describes a then-emerging shift in what 'startup' meant: from a small company hoping to grow big, to a vehicle for developing technology on spec for acquisition. His own company Viaweb was explicitly built this way, designed from the start to slot easily into a larger company.

The logic is sound from both sides. For big companies, acquiring startups means paying more but bearing less risk — they only pay for technology that actually works. For founders, it creates direct accountability: you only get paid if you build the winner. The innovative atmosphere of startups produces better technology faster than the bureaucratic atmosphere of large companies. This model has become the dominant pattern in tech — most successful startups today are acquired rather than IPO'd, and large companies increasingly treat acquisitions as their primary R&D channel.
