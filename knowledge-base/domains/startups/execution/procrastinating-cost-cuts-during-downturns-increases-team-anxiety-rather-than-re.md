David Sacks observes that founders who procrastinate on cost cuts believe they are protecting morale, but the opposite occurs. Employees at peer companies are already cutting, and your team knows the axe will fall—the uncertainty of when is worse than the event itself. The pattern Sacks describes is that the best founders make one deep cut, communicate the go-forward plan immediately, and explicitly tell remaining employees they are the chosen team for the mission ahead. Pete Flint corroborates this from Trulia, where they called a management meeting on Sunday and presented the full plan Monday morning, converting each team's role into a specific metric target so people could see exactly how they contributed to survival.

The practical implication is that delayed cuts force a company into a slow death spiral: burning cash at the old rate while revenues decline, which shortens the actual runway available for the eventual pivot. Each month of delay is a month of runway permanently lost. The counter-intuitive insight is that the 'kind' thing—waiting—is actually the cruel thing, because it reduces the company's odds of survival for everyone.
