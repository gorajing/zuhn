Graham identifies a practical two-part technique: first, deliberately frame new work as a lesser category (a sketch, a hack, a prototype) to lower the standard you judge it by; second, choose media and tools that allow fast iteration with minimal upfront commitment. The combination is powerful because it attacks the early-work problem from both the psychological and the practical side simultaneously. Psychologically, calling something 'just a hack' gives you permission to produce ugly output. Practically, working in a notebook rather than carving stone means you get initial results faster, which lets you focus on rate of improvement rather than absolute quality. Patrick Collison extends this further — making the tenuousness itself a feature, leaning into the ridiculousness of the attempt, which paradoxically frees you to push the naive approach further than anyone expected.
