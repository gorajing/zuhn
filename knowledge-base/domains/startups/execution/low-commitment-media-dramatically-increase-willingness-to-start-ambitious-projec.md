Graham identifies that the fear of making something lame is the primary force preventing great work — not lack of skill, resources, or ideas. This means anything that reduces the psychological barrier to starting has outsized value. Low-commitment media serve this purpose: it's easier to convince yourself something is 'just a sketch' when drawing in a notebook than carving stone.

John Carmack's observation about game mods illustrates this perfectly: Quake's crude technical limitations meant quick hacks weren't far from the official game, spawning many careers. When commercial quality rose, modding became a full-time job and innovation dropped. Minecraft and Roblox reversed this by making crudeness the aesthetic, letting innovative gameplay concepts become the overriding value. The principle generalizes: digital media's advantage over physical isn't primarily the software — it's that you can start something new with little upfront commitment. The 'just a hack' framing is a legitimate psychological technique, not intellectual dishonesty.
