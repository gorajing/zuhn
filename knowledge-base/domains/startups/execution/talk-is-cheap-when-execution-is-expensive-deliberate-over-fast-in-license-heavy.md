Daniel Ek explains the contrarian Spotify culture: while most tech companies worship 'move fast and break things,' Spotify tells employees that talk is the cheapest resource they have. The reason is structural — every meaningful product change required convincing the entire music industry, and licenses could take one or two years to negotiate. When iteration cycles are that expensive, the cost of being wrong dwarfs the cost of debate.

The lesson generalizes to any business with high-friction dependencies: regulated industries, hardware companies, marketplaces with two-sided dynamics, or anyone whose product requires partner buy-in. In these domains, the standard Silicon Valley advice ('ship and iterate') actively destroys value because each shipped iteration burns external goodwill or lock-in. The right move is to do most of the iteration in conversation — game theory the rollouts, model the second-order effects, and only act when conviction is high.

This also reframes deliberation as a competitive advantage rather than slowness. If your competitors are fast-iterating in a slow-iteration market, they're paying compound costs that you avoid by thinking longer. 'We're probably going to be more right when we actually do something' becomes a moat.
