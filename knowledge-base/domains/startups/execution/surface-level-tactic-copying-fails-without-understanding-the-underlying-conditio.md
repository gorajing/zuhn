A common pattern among early-stage founders is observing what worked for a successful company and replicating the visible tactic — the pricing model, the onboarding flow, the go-to-market motion — without examining the structural conditions that made it work. The growth loop that scaled Dropbox required a product with inherent viral mechanics; the freemium model that worked for Slack required a product that became more valuable with team adoption.

This is a form of survivorship-biased cargo culting. The article advocates grounding in first principles rather than trends, because first-principles thinking forces you to examine whether the conditions that enabled a tactic exist in your specific context. The resources it recommends (Paul Graham's essays, NFX founder essays) share a common trait: they teach frameworks for evaluating conditions, not prescriptions for copying tactics.
