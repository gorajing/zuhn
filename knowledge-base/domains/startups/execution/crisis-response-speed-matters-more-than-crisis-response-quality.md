Levine emphasizes that hesitation during a crisis is itself a decision — one that systematically reduces your options. Each week of inaction during a cash crisis burns runway that could have been preserved, and each week of inaction during a PMF crisis lets competitors solidify their position in the shifting market.

The counterintuitive insight is that the quality of your crisis response matters less than its speed. A 70% correct decision made in week one preserves more optionality than a 95% correct decision made in month three, because the option space has contracted dramatically by then. This also has a leadership dimension: teams sense hesitation, and prolonged uncertainty erodes the trust and motivation needed to execute any recovery plan.
