Hormozi advocates a systems-based approach to personal finance: automate the investment allocation so money moves to a separate account before you can spend it, then make spending deliberately harder (cash instead of cards, pre-approved shopping lists only, no recreational shopping). The key insight is asymmetric friction — lubricate the path toward investing and add resistance to spending.

This maps directly to behavioral design principles used in product development. Rather than relying on daily willpower decisions (which deplete), you make the default action the desired one. Checking your bank account every morning creates an observability loop — similar to how monitoring dashboards change engineering behavior. The daily confrontation with reality, while initially painful, creates the feedback loop necessary for behavioral change.
