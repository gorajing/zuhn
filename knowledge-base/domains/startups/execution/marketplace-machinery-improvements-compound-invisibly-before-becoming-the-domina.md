When asked what levers Uber has if it doesn't set prices (the marketplace does), Dara pointed to ML-driven matching and pricing improvements that lift throughput roughly 5% per year, on top of an increasingly large base. This is free growth that scales with the data advantage—Uber runs 2 billion transactions per quarter, giving them experimentation capacity no competitor can match.

The key insight is that this is the kind of advantage that's genuinely invisible for the first several years of investment. When Dara talks about Uber's recent outperformance of Lyft, he traces it to operational machinery built 2-3 years earlier: merging rides and Eats teams after Eats hit sufficient scale, building the experimentation infrastructure for cross-app promotion, etc. Inputs and outputs are separated by years.

The generalizable pattern: in any business where the core economic engine is a learning system (recommendation, matching, pricing, routing), the best time to invest in the machinery is when it looks like it's not paying off. By the time the compounding becomes obvious in financials, the advantage is already structural and competitors are at least 3 years behind.
