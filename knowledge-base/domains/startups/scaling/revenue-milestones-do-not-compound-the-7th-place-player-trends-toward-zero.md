Founders often comfort themselves with the belief that if the market leader does $500M and they do $50M, they're simply worth a tenth of the winner. Dalton argues this reasoning is almost never supported by evidence — the 7th place player in most software markets trends toward zero revenue, not proportional revenue. This is a power-law dynamic where winners capture disproportionate value.

This misconception also appears at the milestone level: founders believe that reaching $1M in revenue proves they can reach $100M or $1B. But examining public companies reveals that what generated the first million is frequently not what generates the majority of revenue later (Google's early monetization vs. search ads, Microsoft's evolution). The path to $1M and the path to $1B are often fundamentally different businesses. Series B companies still fail at 80-90% rates, which shocks founders who assume later stages guarantee success.
