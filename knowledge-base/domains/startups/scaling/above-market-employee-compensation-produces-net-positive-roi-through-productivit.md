The dominant retail strategy treats labor as a cost to minimize. Costco pays its 245,000 workers an average of $21/hour—double the U.S. retail average—plus superior health insurance and retirement benefits. The result is employees who are three times more productive than industry benchmarks.

This creates a counterintuitive economic advantage: higher per-worker costs produce lower per-unit-of-output costs. The mechanism works through reduced turnover (saving constant hiring and training expenses), higher service quality (driving membership renewals), and operational efficiency (experienced workers handle the warehouse-to-shelf logistics that make the no-backroom model possible). The broader lesson is that labor cost analysis must account for productivity multipliers, not just hourly rates—a principle that applies equally to startups deciding between fewer expensive engineers versus many junior ones.
