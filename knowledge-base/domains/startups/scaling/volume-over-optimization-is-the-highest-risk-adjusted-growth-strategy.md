Hormozi frames growth as three strategies — more, better, or new — and argues that 'more' is consistently the highest risk-adjusted return. Hiring three more salespeople closing at 30% gives a 300% improvement, while trying to optimize one salesperson from 30% to 40% gives only a 25% improvement with real risk of degrading to 20%. The key insight: when you change anything, you guarantee you incur the cost of change but do not guarantee the benefit.

This is the 'one to N' problem vs the 'zero to one' problem. Entrepreneurs love zero-to-one (getting something to work) but resist one-to-N (doing it many more times). What presents as volatility — inconsistent sales, unpredictable results — is typically a symptom of low volume, not a broken system. Hormozi's mentor tested with 5,000 flyers per day for 30 days (150,000 total) while Hormozi tested with 300 total. The system wasn't broken — the sample size was. The practical rule: before optimizing, ask whether you can simply do 3-10x more of what's already working.
