Costco pays an average of $21/hour — double the US retail average — plus better health insurance and retirement benefits. The result is employees who are three times more productive than the industry norm. This isn't altruism; it's a calculated investment where the productivity multiplier more than offsets the wage premium.

This challenges the common startup instinct to minimize labor costs, especially in operations-heavy businesses. The mechanism works through multiple channels: lower turnover reduces hiring and training costs, experienced employees handle more volume with fewer errors, and worker satisfaction translates to better customer experience. The lesson is that employee compensation should be evaluated on output-per-dollar, not cost-per-hour — and that above-market pay can be the cheaper option when second-order effects are accounted for.
