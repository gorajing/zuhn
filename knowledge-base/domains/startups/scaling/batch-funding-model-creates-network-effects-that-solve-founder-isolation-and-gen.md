The YC batch model was born from ignorance — Graham and his co-founders wanted to 'practice being investors' and thought funding many startups simultaneously was the fastest way to learn. They even framed the first Summer Founders Program as semi-fake: 'We wouldn't feel guilty for being in a sense fake investors, because they would in a similar sense be fake founders.' But this accidental structure created three compounding advantages.

First, it solved founder isolation — 'Now you not only had colleagues, but colleagues who understood the problems you were facing and could tell you how they were solving them.' Second, alumni formed a tight community dedicated to helping current batches. Third, startups became each other's customers, creating what Graham calls the 'YC GDP.' This is a classic example of emergent network effects: the batch structure created density of interaction that serial investments couldn't, and that density generated value that grew superlinearly with each new batch.
