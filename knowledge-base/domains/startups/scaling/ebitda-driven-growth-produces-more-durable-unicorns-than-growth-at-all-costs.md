While the 2000s-2010s mantra was 'growth at all costs,' eDreams made EBITDA their north star — driving project prioritization, engineering allocation, and incentive structures. They reached $550M+ revenue and $150M+ EBITDA before their $1.5B IPO. Crucially, their focus on profitability didn't actually slow growth; it just made growth sustainable.

This is a powerful counter-narrative to the dominant VC playbook. Profitability-first doesn't mean growth-averse — it means every growth initiative must prove its unit economics. The practical advantage is control: profitable companies aren't forced into unfavorable funding rounds or desperate pivots. When eDreams reached unicorn status, it was backed by real cash flows, not a story about future monetization. For founders, the takeaway is that EBITDA as a north star is a decision-making simplifier — it creates a single filter for prioritization that aligns the entire organization.
