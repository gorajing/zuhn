While both sides of the CLTV/CAC ratio matter, the CLTV side offers three compounding levers — retention (longer customer lifespans), upsells/expansions (higher revenue per customer), and product stickiness (deeper usage driving loyalty) — that reinforce each other over time. A customer who stays longer is more likely to expand; a stickier product drives both retention and expansion.

CAC reduction, by contrast, faces natural floors: ad costs are set by auction markets, sales salaries have market rates, and content production has baseline costs. You can optimize CAC, but you can't compound it downward indefinitely. This asymmetry means that startups focused primarily on CLTV improvement through product and retention investment tend to build more durable competitive advantages than those focused purely on acquisition efficiency.
