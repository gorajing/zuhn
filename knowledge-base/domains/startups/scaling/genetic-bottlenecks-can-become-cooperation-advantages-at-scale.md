When a few Argentine ant queens were transported globally, the resulting colonies had extremely low genetic diversity. This meant new colonies didn't diverge enough to become rivals — they cooperated as one supercolony instead of fragmenting into warring factions as they do in their native range.

This is a provocative parallel for startups: strong cultural homogeneity (a 'bottleneck' in diversity of thought) can accelerate scaling by reducing internal friction and coordination costs. Early-stage companies with tight founder-culture alignment scale faster than those with diverse-but-conflicting values. The tradeoff is real though — the Argentine ants' low diversity also makes them vulnerable to novel threats, just as culturally homogeneous companies develop blind spots. The lesson isn't 'stay homogeneous forever' but rather that reduced variance can be a temporary scaling superpower.
