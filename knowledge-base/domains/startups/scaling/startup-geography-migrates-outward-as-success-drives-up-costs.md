Graham notes that University Ave in Palo Alto would have been the Valley's heart 10 years prior, but rising costs pushed startups to Mountain View's Castro Street instead. Palo Alto became a place startups visit to meet investors, not where they operate day-to-day. Old Palo Alto houses at $1000/sqft became 'post-exit Silicon Valley.'

This is a predictable lifecycle: success breeds cost inflation, which pushes the frontier of innovation to cheaper adjacent neighborhoods. The pattern has since repeated with startups moving to SF's SoMa, then to Oakland and remote-first. Understanding this migration pattern matters for founders choosing locations and for investors looking at where the next generation of startups will cluster. The active center of gravity always moves toward where young, cash-poor founders can afford to operate.
