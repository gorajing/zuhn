Naval describes Slack as inheriting email's worst property — the ability to asymmetrically waste other people's time. One message to 50 people takes seconds to send but collectively consumes hours of attention as everyone sifts through noise for signal. Over time, group messaging degenerates into entertainment, politicking, and non-germane conversation with a terrible signal-to-noise ratio.

By forcing communication through direct text messages or in-person conversations, you raise the barrier to interruption. People must think carefully about whether their question is worth tracking someone down. This intentional friction serves a strategic purpose: it limits the company's ability to scale, which is exactly the point for an early-stage team. Small teams can't hide underperformers, allow deeper curation by founders, and enable the uninterrupted free time that creativity requires. Naval frames boredom as productive — letting people manage their own time between deep creative bursts rather than keeping them perpetually busy with make-work.
