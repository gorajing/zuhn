Park describes a deliberate land-and-expand motion. Customer journeys 'start with a very concrete use case' — concept testing is the big, straightforward one: a customer has a new product or market message and wants fast reactions. The immediately legible promise is scale: instead of testing five to ten ideas a month, test thousands of ideas across thousands of sub-populations instantly. That concrete, quantifiable improvement is what closes the initial engagement.

Only after landing do customers pull themselves toward the visionary capabilities: product testing with a temporal dimension ('experience this product for 10 minutes and tell us what you saw'), multi-agent simulations (CEOs routinely ask Simile to simulate their earnings calls and audience reactions), and second-order market effects (how launching an EV reshapes perception of the rest of the product line). These are the things that have 'no way to test for' today — but they are the expansion, not the entry point.

The go-to-market lesson is to resist leading with the most ambitious framing even when it is the true source of long-term value. Enter on a problem the buyer already understands and can price (here, faster/cheaper concept testing at scale), prove the platform, and let the customer's own imagination escalate the relationship into the harder, higher-value, more defensible use cases.
