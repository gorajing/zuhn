The conventional Western business wisdom since the 1980s has favored focus over diversification, with conglomerates trading at a 'conglomerate discount.' Tata Group challenges this by operating in 100+ countries across steel, automotive, IT services, beverages, chemicals, communications, and hospitality — while achieving world-leading positions in several: largest wholesale voice carrier, top 5 commercial vehicle manufacturer, top 10 steel maker, second largest tea company globally.

The key differentiator appears to be that Tata doesn't diversify for diversification's sake. Each division competes to be best-in-class. This suggests the conglomerate discount may apply specifically to unfocused conglomerates, not to diversified groups where each unit maintains genuine competitive excellence. The model may work particularly well in emerging economies where institutional voids (weak capital markets, limited talent pools) make internal resource allocation more efficient than market-based allocation.
