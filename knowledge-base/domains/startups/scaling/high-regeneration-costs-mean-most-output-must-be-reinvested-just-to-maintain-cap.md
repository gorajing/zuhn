The Calvin Cycle's economics are striking: for every 3 CO2 molecules fixed, the cycle produces 6 molecules of G3P (the useful sugar precursor), but 5 of those 6 must be immediately recycled to regenerate the 3 RuBP molecules needed to restart the cycle. The net yield is just 1 G3P per full turn — an 83% reinvestment rate.

This maps directly to startup scaling: many businesses appear to generate substantial gross output but must reinvest most of it to maintain the machinery of production (customer acquisition costs, infrastructure, team overhead). A company doing $10M in revenue but spending $8.3M just to sustain its capacity to generate that revenue has Calvin Cycle economics. The actionable insight is to explicitly calculate your regeneration ratio — what fraction of output must cycle back to maintain current capacity — before celebrating gross numbers. Businesses with lower regeneration ratios have fundamentally better unit economics and compound faster.
