Costco's self-imposed rule that no item can be marked up more than 15% (vs Walmart's 24% and Home Depot's 35%) seems like leaving money on the table. But the constraint serves multiple functions: it forces continuous operational efficiency (warehouse layout, bulk purchasing, minimal decor), it builds deep customer trust, and it makes every pricing decision trivially simple.

The insight generalizes beyond retail. When a company voluntarily constrains its extraction rate, it signals aligned incentives to customers, which compounds through retention and word-of-mouth. The short-term revenue sacrifice (a few cents per item) is dwarfed by the long-term value of membership renewal ($60-120/year). This is the same logic behind loss-leader open-source strategies in developer tools.
