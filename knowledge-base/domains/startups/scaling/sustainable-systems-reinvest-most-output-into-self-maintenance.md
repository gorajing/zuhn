In the Calvin Cycle's third phase, 3 molecules of RuBP are converted into 6 molecules of G3P, but 5 of those 6 must be fed back into the cycle to regenerate the original 3 RuBP molecules. Only one G3P — roughly 17% of total output — leaves as net production. This isn't inefficiency; it's the mathematical requirement for sustainability.

This ratio maps directly onto business scaling. A SaaS company spending 80% of revenue on maintaining existing customers (support, infrastructure, retention) and only 20% on growth isn't failing — it's sustainable. Startups that try to minimize reinvestment to maximize apparent output (growth metrics) often collapse because the regeneration machinery breaks down. The Calvin Cycle teaches that the ratio between gross production and net output is a fundamental constraint, not an optimization target.

The uncomfortable truth for growth-obsessed operators is that the 'overhead' of self-maintenance isn't waste — it's the engine. Companies that gut their regeneration capacity (institutional knowledge, culture, infrastructure maintenance) to boost short-term output are spending down their RuBP reserves.
