The Panama Canal's lock width of 168 feet doesn't just limit ships — it defines them. Fifty percent of ships using the old locks were built to exactly the maximum width, meaning the canal's physical constraints became the ship design specification for a massive portion of global trade. This is a powerful pattern: when a single chokepoint gates access to a critical resource or market, the entire upstream industry reshapes itself around that bottleneck's exact parameters. The same dynamic appears in startups — app store review policies, API rate limits, or platform TOS become de facto product specifications. The danger is that when the constraint shifts (as when Panama expanded its locks), every participant built to the old boundary faces sudden obsolescence or expensive retrofitting, while those who anticipated the shift gain disproportionate advantage.
