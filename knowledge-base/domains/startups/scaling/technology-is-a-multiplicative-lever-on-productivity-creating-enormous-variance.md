Graham reframes the Bubble-era claim that technology would increase productivity. The analysts were right about the principle but wrong about it being uniform. Technology doesn't add a constant — it multiplies what's already there. This means the range of productivity outcomes expands dramatically rather than shifting upward uniformly.

The practical consequence is that companies of the future may be surprisingly small in headcount but enormous in revenue. As Fred Brooks established, internal friction in a group grows as the square of its size, so small teams with high leverage can outperform large organizations. Graham suggests that a ten-person company outsourcing everything except product development could achieve revenues that would surprise most people. The mistake companies make is loosening their belts as revenue grows — the question isn't whether you can afford more salaries, but whether you can afford the productivity loss from a larger organization.
