After the dot-com crash forced Netflix to lay off a third of their employees, something counterintuitive happened: the company became more productive. By keeping only their star players, the team became leaner, more efficient, more focused, and more decisive. Reed Hastings built this into Netflix's permanent culture through the 'talent density' concept — the idea that one rockstar employee outperforms 10 average ones by 10-100x. Netflix enforces this through the 'keeper test' (would you fight to keep this person?), top-of-market salaries, and generous severance for anyone who's merely adequate. The tradeoff is real: this creates a pressure-cooker environment that some describe as a 'culture of fear.' But the organizational design philosophy is coherent — high talent density enables less bureaucracy, fewer rules, unlimited vacation, transparent financials, and radical honesty, because you can trust top performers with freedom that would be abused by average ones.
