Before agriculture, survival demanded superb physical and mental abilities across all domains from every individual. Hunter-gatherers were arguably superior to modern humans as individual generalists — their average brain volume may have even been larger. But generalist excellence has a ceiling.

Agriculture created predictable food surplus, which meant individuals could increasingly rely on others' skills. Some could specialize in tools, crops, or invention. This division of labor compounded: villages became cities, cities became kingdoms, and progress went exponential. The pattern maps directly to startup scaling — a founding team must be generalist until product-market fit creates reliable 'surplus,' at which point specialization becomes the unlock for exponential growth.
