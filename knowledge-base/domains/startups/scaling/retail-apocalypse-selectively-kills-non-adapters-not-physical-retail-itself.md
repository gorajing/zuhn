The popular narrative frames Amazon as the executioner of physical retail, but the data tells a more nuanced story. The companies that died—Sears (tried to do everything poorly), Toys-R-Us (dismissed the internet), J.C. Penney (lost touch with customers)—share a common trait: internal failure to adapt, not external competitive pressure. Meanwhile, Costco, Aldi, Ross, Dollar General, and Lush are all growing.

This pattern repeats across every industry disruption: the narrative blames the new entrant, but incumbents actually die from their own rigidity. The lesson for founders is that disruption creates as many opportunities as it destroys. When competitors die, they leave behind cheap real estate (Spirit Halloween's entire model depends on this), abandoned customer bases, and proven demand. The question is never 'is this industry dying?' but 'which specific players are dying, and why?'
