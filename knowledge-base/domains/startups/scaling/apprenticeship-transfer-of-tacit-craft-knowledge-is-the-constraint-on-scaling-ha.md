Hermès uses the word 'savoir-faire' 133 times in a single annual report, which is almost comic but accurately reflects the business constraint. A new artisan trains for roughly two years before being allowed to produce, then additional years before being trusted with Birkin and Kelly construction. Even the sixth-generation CEO apprenticed for five years making bags by hand as a teenager. This structure exists because the knowledge is not in manuals; it's in the pricking-iron muscle memory, the ability to screen-print 20 ink layers in perfect alignment by hand, the judgment of when leather is matched well enough from one hide. You cannot hire around this constraint, you cannot consult your way past it, and you cannot even automate it away at the current quality bar — the current artistic director has said explicitly that the day machine stitching matches hand stitching, they will switch, and that day has not come. For any business whose product quality depends on tacit expertise, growth is ultimately governed by master-to-apprentice bandwidth, not by capital or demand. Plan around the training pipeline, not the order book.
