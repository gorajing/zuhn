The Calvin Cycle's economics look terrible on paper: for every 3 turns of the cycle consuming 9 ATP and 6 NADPH, only one G3P molecule escapes as net product. The other five must be recycled to regenerate the 3 RuBP molecules needed to restart the cycle. That's an 83% reinvestment rate.

Yet this 'inefficient' system powers the entire biosphere. The key insight is that sustainability matters more than efficiency when operating at scale over long time horizons. Many successful businesses operate similarly — Amazon reinvested nearly all revenue for decades, SaaS companies spend 80%+ of revenue on growth and infrastructure. The viability test isn't the overhead ratio itself but whether the net output, however small, compounds meaningfully. A system producing 17% net output that runs continuously for 450 million years will outperform a theoretically efficient system that can't sustain itself.
