After eight years of hiring at PolyAI, Shawn Wen observed a clear pattern: their best performers were candidates where at least one interviewer had a strong, specific reason to hire them ('I really wanted to hire this person because of X'), rather than candidates who received average-good scores across all interviewers. The 'nice person nobody fell in love with' pattern correlates with mediocre performance.

This has practical implications for interview panel design. Rather than averaging scores and hiring the statistically safest candidate, teams should actively look for champions — interviewers who have a strong positive signal about a specific capability. This mirrors the 'spike' hiring philosophy: in fast-moving technical environments, one exceptional capability matters more than well-roundedness. The approach requires interviewers to articulate specific strengths rather than general impressions, and the hiring committee to weight passionate advocacy above consensus comfort.
