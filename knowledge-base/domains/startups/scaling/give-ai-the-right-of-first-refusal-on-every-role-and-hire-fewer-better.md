Serval questions whether every role and department needs to exist, starting from the assumption that 'maybe this doesn't need a person anymore — maybe this could be AI.' AI gets the 'right of first refusal' on every job. Concretely they run with no SDRs and no solutions engineers: reps aren't more technical than past generations, but Serval answers product questions instantly and builds decks, one-pagers, and battle cards mid-call. They also deliberately delay hires (enablement, RevOps) and discover how far they get before a role is genuinely needed — often concluding the department is real but smaller than expected.

The hiring mantra is 'fewer better,' repeated deliberately. The reasoning chains: cost down + capabilities up = accelerating change; surviving accelerating change requires agility; and the best way to be agile is the smallest possible number of the best possible people. Stauch argues the returns to talent density have never been higher, and that people are the only moat left — 'having better people in the room is really the only thing that will keep you ahead.' Notably, hiring remains his single biggest worry even though AI is supposed to reduce headcount needs; every AI company he talks to is hiring more than ever.

The second-order benefit is steerability: a small, dense team can change go-to-market or rebuild the product 'almost overnight,' whereas convincing thousands of people of a new direction is nearly impossible. So 'fewer better' is not just a cost or quality stance — it's an agility stance, deliberately preserving the org's ability to reinvent itself.
