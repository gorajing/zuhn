RuBisCo, the enzyme responsible for carbon fixation in photosynthesis, misidentifies oxygen as CO2 roughly half the time, creating toxic byproducts plants must actively neutralize. Despite this catastrophic error rate, plants never evolved a replacement — they simply produce staggering quantities of the enzyme (an estimated 40 billion tons on Earth at any moment) and built elaborate cleanup pathways around its failures.

This mirrors a common startup pattern: the first system that works at all tends to win, not because it's elegant but because it ships and scales. Companies like Craigslist, PHP-era Facebook, and early Amazon all ran on famously ugly infrastructure they never fully replaced. The lesson isn't that quality doesn't matter — it's that the window for replacing a working-but-flawed core narrows rapidly once an organization has scaled around it. The cost of the workarounds becomes the cost of doing business, and the competitive advantage of being first and big enough often outweighs the drag of technical debt.
