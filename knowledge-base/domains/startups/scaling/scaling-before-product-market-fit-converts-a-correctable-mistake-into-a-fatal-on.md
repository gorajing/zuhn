Webvan is the canonical premature-scaling failure: they built out warehouse infrastructure across multiple cities before validating that customer acquisition costs and logistics were manageable at scale. Each new city locked in fixed costs that required a customer density that hadn't been proven. When the model didn't work, there was no graceful contraction — only bankruptcy.

The error is structural. Early-stage companies are experiments; scaling is a commitment. When you scale, you convert variable costs into fixed ones, transform reversible decisions into irreversible ones, and create organizational complexity that slows adaptation. This is exactly the wrong profile for a company that hasn't yet confirmed it has a working model.

The practical rule that falls out of this: treat unit economics and product-market fit as hard gates on scaling decisions. If you can't demonstrate repeatable, profitable customer acquisition in one market, geographic or product expansion won't fix the underlying model — it will just make the flaw more expensive.
