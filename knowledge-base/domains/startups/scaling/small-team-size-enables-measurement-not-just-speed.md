Graham's galley metaphor reveals why large companies structurally cannot reward exceptional performance: when 1,000 rowers contribute to the same boat, no individual's extra effort is visible or attributable. The averaging effect means a top-1% performer gets compensated at roughly the average rate. A 10-person team puts you within a factor of 10 of measuring individual contribution.

But the deeper insight is that startups are not merely 10 random people — they are self-selecting groups of ambitious people who already know each other. This means the 'averaging' that does occur is with other high performers, not with the general population. Steve Jobs's claim that a startup's fate depends on its first five employees follows directly: those first hires define the quality of the average your work gets blended with.
