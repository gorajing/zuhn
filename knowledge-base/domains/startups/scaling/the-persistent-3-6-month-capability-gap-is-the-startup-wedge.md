The founders concede that for high-stakes domains — math, coding, cyber — the 'magic of training' will land in the frontier labs' hands. But they argue there is consistently a three-to-six-month lead where bespoke tasks people are actively exploring are not yet well-served by the base models. The models will eventually get there, but if you can autonomously learn in a lightweight way, you capture value during that window.

The non-obvious part is that this is not a one-time gap that closes — it is a moving frontier that keeps reappearing as new bespoke needs emerge faster than generic models absorb them. That makes 'be 3-6 months ahead on your specific context' a renewable wedge rather than a fragile head start that evaporates at the next model release.

The motivating contrast: an individual who quit their job to 24/7 engineer context and skills for a frontier model would barely move the needle — they'd be better off just waiting for the next model version. The bet is to invert that, so that time spent actually compounds into performance on the domains you care about. For founders, the lesson is to build where effort accrues to you in a window incumbents structurally lag, not where you're racing the base model head-on.
