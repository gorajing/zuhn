In-N-Out bakes buns daily, uses 100% dairy milkshakes, and operates zero freezers or microwaves. This means every location must be within a single day's drive (~500 miles) of a distribution center. Rather than compromising quality to remove this bottleneck, they treat it as a feature — each new distribution center unlocks a geographic region methodically.

This is a counter-intuitive growth strategy: the constraint forces disciplined expansion and ensures quality never degrades. Compare this to Subway, which at its peak opened five restaurants per day and eventually suffered massive quality inconsistency and franchisee closures. The lesson generalizes beyond food — any business where quality depends on operational proximity (consulting, handcrafted goods, high-touch services) can use supply chain constraints as a natural governor against over-expansion.
