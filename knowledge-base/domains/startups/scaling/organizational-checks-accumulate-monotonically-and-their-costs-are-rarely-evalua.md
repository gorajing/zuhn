Organizations naturally accumulate checks and approval processes in response to past disasters—a supplier goes bankrupt, so now all suppliers must prove solvency. This learning process is rational at the individual-check level but pathological in aggregate because no one accounts for the cumulative cost.

The key asymmetry is that the benefit of a check (preventing a known disaster) is vivid and concrete, while its cost (the best supplier who doesn't bid, the feature that never ships) is invisible. Graham suggests a meta-check: anyone proposing a new process should have to explain not just the benefit but the cost. Even a crude cost analysis would force the organization to acknowledge that checks aren't free, shifting the default from 'add it to be safe' to 'justify the overhead.'
