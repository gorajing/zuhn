The square-cube law in biology shows that scaling doesn't just amplify existing challenges — it fundamentally reorders which forces matter. An ant is immune to falls but can drown in a water droplet. An elephant is immune to surface tension but destroyed by gravity. Neither animal's survival strategy would work for the other.

Startups exhibit the same categorical force rebalancing. At seed stage, the dominant threat is building something nobody wants (surface tension — small forces that trap you). At scale, the dominant threat is coordination overhead and structural collapse under your own weight (gravity). Companies that try to maintain seed-stage practices at scale — flat hierarchy, no process, move fast — are the organizational equivalent of an elephant trying to survive a fall by being light. The insight is that scaling requires not just more of the same solution but fundamentally different engineering for the forces that now dominate.
