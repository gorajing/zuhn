Nick Lane presents a striking biological fact: bacteria have had 4 billion years of evolution, access to metagenomes larger than the human genome, and populations orders of magnitude greater than eukaryotes — yet they never evolved complexity beyond biofilms. The bottleneck wasn't information. E. coli's metagenome exceeds 30,000 genes (more than humans' 20,000), yet bacteria remain bacteria.

The breakthrough required endosymbiosis — a mitochondrion that stripped away 99% of its own genome to become a pure power pack, enabling a 100,000-fold increase in energy available per gene. This maps directly to organizational scaling: companies that try to grow by simply adding more people, more data, or more lateral knowledge transfer (the bacterial metagenome approach) will plateau. Real scaling breakthroughs require structural reorganization — stripping overhead from support functions (like mitochondria shedding genes) so core capabilities can expand dramatically. The lesson is that the constraint is rarely knowledge; it's the energy architecture that determines what level of complexity is achievable.
