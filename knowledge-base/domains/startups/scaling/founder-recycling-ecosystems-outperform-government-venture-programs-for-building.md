Research on Sweden's outsized unicorn production reveals that the ecosystem's engine is founder recycling — alumni from Spotify, Klarna, and Skype taking their exits, knowledge, and networks and reinvesting them into the next generation of startups. This creates a compounding flywheel: each successful exit produces multiple experienced founders and angel investors who de-risk the next cohort.

Critically, the research found that tax incentives and cultural norms around entrepreneurship mattered more than government-backed venture funds. This challenges the common policy playbook of creating public venture programs to stimulate startup ecosystems. The implication is that ecosystem development is better served by removing friction from the recycling loop — favorable capital gains treatment, cultural celebration of serial entrepreneurship, and dense networks — rather than trying to inject capital from the top down.
