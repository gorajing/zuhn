Seo argues the most underappreciated thing about Neuralink is that scale was designed in from founding — not just the implant, but the surgical robots, the factories, and the deployment pipeline to eventually do a LASIK-like procedure for billions. This required heavy capital and boldness up front and made the company look slow to outsiders for years. The payoff is a compounding iteration loop: vertical integration across the entire stack (design, develop, deploy) lets the best talent move fast without external bottlenecks.

The mental model Maguire offers is the iceberg: when you build for scale from the beginning, almost all the work happens under the waterline and progress is invisible. Once the structure breaches the surface, it scales massively and quickly, surprising observers who only saw the slow phase. The implication for founders is that 'apparent slowness' in a scale-first company is not a warning sign but the cost of building the machine that builds the product.

The caveat is domain-specific: this applies most strongly in the 'world of atoms,' which Seo repeatedly calls unforgiving. Physical products take real time, and the muscle and hard lessons from building them are non-transferable and slow to acquire — which is precisely why early infrastructure investment, not late retrofitting, is the right sequence.
