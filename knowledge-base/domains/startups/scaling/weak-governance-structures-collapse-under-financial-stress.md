The Articles of Confederation deliberately created a weak central government as a reaction to British tyranny. Congress could declare war and make treaties but could not collect taxes — it had to ask states politely for money. This design worked during wartime unity but collapsed when peacetime financial stress hit: states racked up debt, trade became nightmarish without unified tariffs, and farmers revolted (Shays' Rebellion).

The startup parallel is direct: early-stage organizations often resist strong governance structures because founders fear bureaucracy. But as soon as the organization faces real financial pressure — a down round, a cash crunch, a major customer loss — the lack of enforceable decision-making authority becomes existential. The lesson isn't that you need heavy governance from day one, but that governance structures must scale ahead of the crises they'll need to handle. The Articles lasted exactly 10 years before reality forced a complete rewrite.
