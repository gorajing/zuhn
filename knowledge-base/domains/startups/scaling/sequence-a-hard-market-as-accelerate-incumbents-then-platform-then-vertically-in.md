Recursive sequences its ambition into three phases. Phase one sells speed to incumbents who already have hundreds of chip-design experts, attacking the two 'long poles' (physical design and verification) that each take up to a year. Phase two turns the accelerated tooling into a platform that lets any company with a sufficiently large workload design custom chips without a thousand-person team. Phase three is vertical integration: with cheap, fast chip design in hand, build and co-evolve their own chips and models to 'serve intelligence at a price or capability that would be impossible to match.'

The pattern is a disciplined answer to a recurring founder tension: how to pursue a massive end-state without dying in a market guarded by deep expertise and incumbent tools. Each phase funds and de-risks the next, the early phase generates revenue and credibility from buyers who would never hand over a full project, and the platform phase widens the customer base before the capital-intensive integration play. Naming the phases also disciplines what to say no to now, so the team isn't tempted to skip to the glamorous vertically-integrated end-state before the wedge is proven.
