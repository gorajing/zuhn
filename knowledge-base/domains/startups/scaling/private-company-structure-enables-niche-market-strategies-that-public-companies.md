Sculley emphasized that Obi's ambitions were 'quite different than some of the large public companies' because Obi was private. They didn't need massive market share to build an attractive business — they just needed a viable slice of a high-growth market.

This highlights a structural advantage of private companies: they can rationally pursue opportunities that public companies must ignore. A $100M revenue smartphone business targeting emerging markets would be transformative for a private company but irrelevant to Apple's $300B+ revenue base. Public companies face institutional pressure to pursue only opportunities that materially impact their top line, creating systematic blind spots that private companies can exploit. This is why large incumbents often cede emerging or niche markets to smaller players even when they have the capability to compete.
