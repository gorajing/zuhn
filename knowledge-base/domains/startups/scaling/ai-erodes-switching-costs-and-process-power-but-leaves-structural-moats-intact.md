Applying Hamilton Helmer's Seven Powers framework, Cherny argues AI redistributes which moats matter rather than destroying all of them at once. Switching costs collapse because a model can simply port a user from one tool to another. Process power — moats built on proprietary workflows and operational know-how — erodes because Claude is 'getting really good at figuring out process'; with 4.7 you can give it a target and it will hill-climb until done. But the powers rooted in structure rather than friction or know-how — network effects, scale economies, cornered resources — are 'not really changing with AI.'

For founders, this is a moat audit. If your defensibility rests on how painful you are to leave, or on workflow complexity competitors cannot replicate, AI is actively dissolving it. If it rests on multi-sided networks, cost advantages from scale, or exclusive access to a resource, it survives. The strategic implication is to stop investing in friction-based lock-in and structure-light process moats, and to anchor strategy in the durable structural powers that AI does not touch.
