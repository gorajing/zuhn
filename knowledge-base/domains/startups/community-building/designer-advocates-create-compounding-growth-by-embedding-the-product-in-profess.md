Figma's designer advocate program worked because it made the product part of how designers presented themselves professionally — on Twitter, at meetups, in job listings. This is qualitatively different from a referral program: advocates were not incentivized to share, they shared because sharing affirmed their own professional identity and expertise.

The compound effect is that each advocate creates a small gravity well that pulls other designers toward the product. New designers encounter Figma as the obvious professional standard rather than one option among many. This is how products win categories without winning individual sales contests. The strategic implication is that community investment pays off most in categories where professional identity is strong — design, engineering, data science — and less so in categories where the work is invisible to peers.
