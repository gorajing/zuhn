At TI, Morris Chang fought every year to raise R&D from 4.8% to 5.5% of revenue and was denied every time. When he returned as TSMC CEO in 2009, he simply set R&D at 8% of revenue regardless of recession, eliminating the annual bargaining ritual between the R&D director and the CEO. The head of R&D later told him this was the single best thing the company ever did for R&D.

The deeper lesson is about attention and incentive. When R&D must justify itself annually, leaders waste cycles building defensive cases instead of making technical bets, and the budget becomes a proxy for whichever side has political momentum that year. A constant percentage converts R&D into an operating cost of being in the business, not a discretionary line item. It also forces counter-cyclical investment: when revenue drops in a downturn, spending drops in absolute terms but the commitment to future nodes never wavers.

This pairs with the 28nm decision directly. Once R&D knew their budget was stable, they started thinking bigger — that is when they identified 28nm as 'the sweet spot' and pushed to ramp capex threefold. Stable inputs unlock ambitious planning; volatile inputs produce conservative planning.
