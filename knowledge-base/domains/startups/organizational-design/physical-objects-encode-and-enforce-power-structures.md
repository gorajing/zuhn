Peter Fenton explains they deliberately rejected two default table shapes. Rectangular tables embed a power structure — there is a head, which automatically produces a dominant participant. Circular tables solve hierarchy but atomize the group into disconnected sidebar conversations. Their custom-designed table was built so that one conversation is physically enforced: you cannot whisper to your neighbor without everyone else hearing, so the group stays tuned to a single thread whether the news is good, bad, or hard.

The underlying principle is that culture claims ("we're non-hierarchical," "we all discuss everything together") are cheap without physical or procedural enforcement. Benchmark pairs the table with a related rule — no pre-selling deals in the hallway before partner meetings — because both habits (sidebar chatter and pre-selling) emerge naturally in the absence of structural constraint. Groups that want flat, single-thread conversation should audit their environment for every surface or process that subtly permits the opposite. You cannot will a culture into existence against the grain of your furniture.
