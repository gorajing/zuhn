Dee Hock's core innovation at Visa was not technology or branding — it was a legal structure that made defection impossible. Ownership in Visa could not be sold because there was no stock to sell; your 'ownership' was your participation volume, and it was non-transferable. Member banks also agreed to operate exclusively within the network (no side-chains allowed), and to be bound by future rule changes passed by supermajority vote, much like a constitution they couldn't exit.

This matters because cooperatives between competitors usually collapse once the network becomes valuable: the largest members try to monetize their equity, the most ambitious ones fork off to build competing systems, and the system unravels. Visa's structure front-loaded all the hard conversations — you either agreed to the rules in perpetuity or you stayed out. Once signed up, your best strategy was to make the network more valuable, because that was the only way your own participation increased in worth.

Any founder trying to build a standards body, industry consortium, or multi-stakeholder platform should recognize that voluntary cooperation decays under success. If you want a coalition to survive its own value creation, the legal wrapper has to make exit structurally painful, not just economically suboptimal.
