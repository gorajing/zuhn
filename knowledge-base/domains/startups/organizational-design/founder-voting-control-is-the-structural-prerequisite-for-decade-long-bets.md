The formative governance decision at Facebook came from a near-firing. In 2006 Yahoo offered $1 billion, the entire management team wanted to take it, and the board moved to oust Zuckerberg when he refused. He admits he hadn't done the work to communicate a long-term vision because he wasn't yet thinking in terms of building a company. After surviving that attempt, he restructured governance to make it 'somewhat harder to fire me from my own company for wanting to build it.'

That structural decision is what made everything since possible. Reality Labs has consumed roughly $50 billion with no near-term return. The pivot to AI infrastructure during the 2022 recession coincided with an 80% market cap drawdown. Mark explicitly says he might not be able to convince public investors to keep funding Reality Labs — but he doesn't have to, because supervoting shares mean the case only needs to convince him. The investor pressure exists; the binding constraint doesn't.

The principle generalizes: any CEO dependent on quarterly investor approval is structurally incapable of making bets whose payoff is longer than investors' patience. If the bet you want to make has a 10-year horizon and the market's horizon is 18 months, the only way to fund it is to remove the market's veto power. For founders building anything that requires capital + time + conviction, governance design upfront is more important than any subsequent strategic decision — because every later decision is filtered through whoever can fire you.
