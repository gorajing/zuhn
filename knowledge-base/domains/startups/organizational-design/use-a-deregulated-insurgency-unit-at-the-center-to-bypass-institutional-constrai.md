10DS could not 'turn the oil tanker' of a 400,000-person bureaucracy directly, so instead they negotiated to 'take the shackles off' — a small insurgent team at the center with a No. 10 mandate, unusual autonomy, the ability to pay market rates, and freedom from standard civil-service recruitment. The explicit logic is that this is 'a hack to get around the system' that functions as a pilot: prove the model works, then push to make it business-as-usual across the rest of government.

The pattern generalizes to any large, slow organization: a protected unit with explicit top-level sponsorship and exemption from the constraints that throttle the core can move 10-100x faster on the same problems. The key design choices are concentration (small and elite), insulation (different rules), and a clear mandate that lets it enter other departments and act. The risk Mulgrew flags is that the insurgency alone cannot scale the whole system — it needs to eventually convert its exceptions into the new norm via 'strategic intervention,' or it remains a permanent workaround rather than a transformation.
