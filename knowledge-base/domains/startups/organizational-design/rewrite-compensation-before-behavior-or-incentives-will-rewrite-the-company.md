When Dimon inherited JP Morgan in 2006 he removed the 20%-of-profit-pool side deals, private bonus arrangements, and leverage-linked comp that senior bankers had negotiated. His reasoning: with 30x leverage and 20% of the profits, a banker's rational move is to push to 40x — 'it will literally add 25% to your bonus.' Culture talks can't override that math. He lost people in the process and considered that acceptable cost.

The principle applies beyond banking: any comp plan that rewards a single metric (deal volume, GMV, growth) will bend behavior toward gaming that metric at the expense of unmeasured risks. The corrective is to (a) tie compensation to firm-wide outcomes rather than siloed P&Ls, (b) remove special side deals even if they were used to attract star talent, and (c) accept that changing incentives will trigger departures — those departures are often the exact people whose behavior most needed to change.

Operator framing: if your incentive plan rewards behavior you'd veto in a one-on-one conversation, the plan is broken regardless of what the culture doc says.
