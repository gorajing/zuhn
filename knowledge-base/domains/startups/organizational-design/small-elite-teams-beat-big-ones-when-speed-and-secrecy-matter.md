Skunk Works delivered the P-80 Shooting Star in 143 days and the U-2 spy plane in 18 months for $3.5M total — feats that larger, more resourced aerospace divisions could not match. Kelly Johnson's third rule was explicit: 'The number of people having any connection with the project must be restricted in an almost vicious manner — use a small number of good people, 10 to 25.' The pattern recurs in the original iPhone, iPod, and Xerox PARC teams: six to ten full-stack people who cross disciplines beat hundreds of specialists.

The mechanism is communication overhead and the game of telephone. Every additional person multiplies coordination cost and dilutes conviction. Combined with Rule 4 — 'the Skunk Works manager must be delegated practically complete control' — you get a single auteur vision executed by people who can all fit in one room. For any project where the environment is hostile (war, existential competition, fast-moving market), this architecture is non-negotiable.

The practical corollary: if your team is bigger than 25 and you are still moving slowly, you probably have a coordination problem, not a capacity problem. Adding people will make it worse.
