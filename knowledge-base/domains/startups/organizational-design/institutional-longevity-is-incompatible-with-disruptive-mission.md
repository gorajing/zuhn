Peter Fenton observes that Benchmark is 'collectively aligned around being anti-authoritarian' and wants 'no part of this firm to become the incumbent.' This is operationalized through what he calls 'violent rejuvenation with a common culture of collective joy in serving entrepreneurs' — every partner who has left the firm in its history voluntarily fired themselves the moment they felt they would take more than they gave. Fenton claims this self-firing ethic is unprecedented in his study of the industry and is the firm's primary defense against calcifying into the kind of incumbent it was built to destroy.

The structural insight is that mission-longevity and institutional-longevity can be in direct tension. A firm whose work requires betting against complacent incumbents cannot safely let itself age into one, because the same organizational reflexes that produce durability (accumulated relationships, scaled teams, franchise branding, stable compensation) also produce the blind spots they exploit in others. The Benchmark answer is to cap the firm at five equal partners, refuse growth capital, and make the exit decision a peer-driven norm rather than a retirement contract. Any firm whose value depends on being an outsider should ask what mechanism prevents it from becoming the thing it disrupts — and whether that mechanism is cultural aspiration or structural enforcement.
