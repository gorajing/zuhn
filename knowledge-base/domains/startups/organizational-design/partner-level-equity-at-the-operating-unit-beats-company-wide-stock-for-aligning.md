Walmart's first hires as multi-store managers weren't salaried employees with stock options; they were partners in the specific store they ran. They put up real dollars. Their upside was tied to the P&L of their unit, which they directly controlled, not to some aggregate company performance they had no influence over. Existing managers were also allowed to invest in new store partnerships as they opened, which created both cross-store alignment and a form of internal GP commit on every new launch.

Modern stock options try to simulate this but fail on two axes: (1) the employee didn't put dollars in, so psychologically it's treated as a lottery ticket or gift, and (2) the employee has zero line of sight to how their individual work affects the company-wide equity value. A Walmart store manager had both — they felt the cost of the investment and they could watch their specific store's numbers move daily.

The practical lesson isn't necessarily 'make people buy in.' It's that incentive scope should match control scope. Give people equity-like upside in the thing they actually run, not the aggregate thing they're 0.01% of. Most companies get this wrong by default because company-wide options are administratively easier, not because they align better.
