Two different CEOs at TSMC tried to split the company into divisions — Don Brooks in 1996 and Rick Tsai in 2005. Both proposed dividing by fab or by advanced vs mainstream technology. Chang blocked Brooks (after having McKinsey validate the functional recommendation) but let Tsai try and fail. His principle: 'sometimes you have to let the CEO make his own mistakes and learn from them.' When Chang returned as CEO in 2009, he immediately recombined the operations groups and consolidated business development into a single 80-person team.

The logic: TSMC's top customers — Qualcomm, Apple, NVIDIA — all use both the leading edge AND mainstream nodes. An iPhone contains an A-series chip on the newest node and dozens of legacy-node chips. If you split the organization by node or fab, the same customer now has multiple TSMC account teams, duplicated handoffs, and inconsistent pricing. You've created internal coordination problems that solve nothing for the customer. When McKinsey was asked for an example of a big functionally-structured company, they cited Boeing — which Chang noted was actually wrong, since Boeing is functional across commercial/government but does not have separate divisions per aircraft model.

The generalizable test: draw the customer-to-product matrix. If most customers buy multiple products, a functional structure preserves the account relationship; a divisional structure destroys it. Divisional structures only work when customers genuinely cluster into non-overlapping segments — which is rare in platform businesses.
