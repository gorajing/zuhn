The structural argument links two collapses. Horizontal collapse: pre-AI, organizations had one specialist per function (one person does X well, another does Y well); AI lets a single person perform multiple functions competently, so the count of distinct specialized roles shrinks. Vertical collapse follows mechanically: middle management exists largely to coordinate across many specialists and many ICs; when each person spans multiple functions and there are fewer people, there's less cross-coordination to manage, so management layers compress.

The cited evidence: Meta's ~8,000-person layoff notably eliminated managers — many manager jobs were cut or converted back to IC. The speaker reads this as representative of the future, not a one-off cost cut. He also notes a caveat that complicates pure AI-attribution (companion insight): layoff decisions track 'where the dollar shifts' more than pure AI efficiency — companies tolerated inefficiency for years before AI, so AI is partly a convenient closing function/excuse for delayering decisions made for other reasons.

The career implication he draws: the IC-vs-manager question many Meta engineers asked him is somewhat moot — practically everyone becomes a manager (of agents) by definition (see the gold-collar insight), so the title distinction matters less than the reality that coordination work is being redistributed and delayered.

Confidence is medium because the trend is early and entangled with macro cost-cutting. The falsifiable core: if true, expect sustained compression of middle-management ratios (more ICs/managers, flatter orgs) specifically in AI-heavy functions, beyond what a normal cost cycle explains. Track manager-to-IC ratios as the signal.
