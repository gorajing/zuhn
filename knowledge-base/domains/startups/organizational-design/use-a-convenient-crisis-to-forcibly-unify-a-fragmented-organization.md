By 2010 Google was a collection of autonomous fiefdoms — Android, Chrome, YouTube, Search, Gmail all had their own leaders, OKRs, and in some cases separate login systems (YouTube had a different username/password system from the rest of Google). When Larry returned as CEO in 2011 he moved his office into the Plus building and made Google+ the mandatory integration target for every product. Company-wide bonuses were tied to Plus success. YouTube comments became Google+ posts. Ad units got plus-one buttons. Picasa got force-integrated. Every team was forced to cooperate with Vic Gundotra's team.

The product was a failure — nobody wanted a desktop-first Facebook competitor with 'circles' built by computer scientists. But the reorganization survived. What came out the other side: unified Google accounts, one identity across all products, consolidated design language, and a company that could be legitimately described as a single entity rather than a loose federation. The transcript frames this bluntly: 'Google+ is kind of just the thing [Larry] picked as the single thing to try to galvanize and unify the company around. And no matter what they picked and how they executed it, it was going to create a lot of carnage.'

The applicable principle: if your real problem is organizational fragmentation and political fiefdoms, you sometimes need a 'convenient crisis' to justify the recentralization — a project important enough that everyone has to cooperate but external enough that existing leaders can't claim territorial ownership. The project doesn't have to succeed. You're buying the reorganization, not the deliverable. The cost (Google+) was one failed product and years of distraction; the benefit (one unified Google) enabled everything that came after. Be honest with yourself about which one you're really buying.
