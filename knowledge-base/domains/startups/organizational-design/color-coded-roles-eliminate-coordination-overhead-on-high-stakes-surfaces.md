On a carrier flight deck — one of the most dangerous workplaces in the world — every person wears a color-coded shirt: yellow for aircraft navigation, red for ordnance, purple for fueling, green for catapults and maintenance, brown for plane captains, and so on. This eliminates the need to ask 'who handles X?' in an environment where noise, speed, and danger make verbal coordination impractical.

This pattern applies wherever teams operate under time pressure with high stakes. Startups scaling through hypergrowth often suffer from coordination collapse — people don't know who owns what. Visual or systematic role-encoding (whether literal uniforms, Slack channel conventions, or clear ownership labels in project tools) can substitute for the management overhead that traditional org charts require. The insight is that legibility of role, not hierarchy of authority, is the binding constraint in fast-moving operations.
