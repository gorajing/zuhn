The coffee temperature example illustrates a non-obvious design trap: features or metrics have an optimal range, not an infinite upside. Too cold is a pain, lukewarm is neutral, hot is a gain, scalding is a pain again. This U-shaped (or inverted-U) continuum appears across product, pricing, and UX: too many features overwhelm, too much personalization feels creepy, too fast a checkout flow feels untrustworthy.

For product managers, the implication is to map not just whether a dimension matters, but where the optimal range sits and where the reversal boundary is. Kano model analysis partially captures this through 'reverse quality' attributes, but the continuum framing adds granularity — it's not binary reverse/positive, it's a spectrum with identifiable thresholds.

This continuum thinking is especially critical in enterprise software where power users and casual users have different inflection points on the same feature. Understanding boundary conditions prevents over-engineering and helps calibrate when 'good enough' is actually the gain-maximizing answer.
