Coval's first idea (a general evals platform) had early users but never made money — Hopkins calls them tire-kickers. The pivot signal was qualitatively different: their first voice customer said, 'You don't have a single line of software written, but we're willing to just pay you to help us figure out this problem.' That willingness to pay for problem-solving before a product exists is the YC-style pull signal: it proves there is enough product and market pull and that no good solution exists yet.

The distinction matters because interest and even usage of an immature product are ambiguous, but pull is not. Real PMF later felt like customers chasing Coval down to book meetings, putting the company on their back to push it through their own procurement, and knocking down barriers on Coval's behalf. When buyers do the selling and clear the obstacles for you, the signal is unambiguous.

What made the pull so strong was an acute, expensive pain: manually testing a voice agent by calling it ten times costs an hour of a person's time, is inaccurate, and ten tests are nowhere near enough to justify launching to millions of conversations — yet the conversations are business-critical. The lesson for founders: weight 'will you pay me to solve this before I build anything' far above polite interest, and hunt for pains that are simultaneously expensive, frustrating, and high-stakes.
