Xin pushes back on the common worry that overfitting to one or two customers is dangerous: 'the downside of overfitting is much smaller than... if you try to be too ambitious and boil the ocean, it's a much bigger problem — because you might end up actually having no customer.' Databricks repeatedly validated this: Delta Lake, the transactional storage layer, was built to one large early customer's exact requirements (a specified rate of events per second and freshness target) by a single engineer; once it worked for them, 'it worked for everyone else.' Clean rooms were likewise built initially for just two customers. Even Omnigent was first an internal tool for their own AI team and mono-repo before generalizing.

The nuance is that overfitting is a starting point you pivot from, not a permanent commitment — and they note a corollary, that there is such a thing as a bad customer you should fire, and that optimizing entirely for tech-company customers can leave you unable to scale into enterprises with different governance, procurement, and legacy constraints. But the core asymmetry stands: concrete, narrow, real demand is recoverable and expandable, whereas broad, speculative scope most often yields nothing to expand from. For founders, the practical test Databricks applies is brutally simple — who is the named target customer, are you on a first-name basis with them, are you texting with them?
