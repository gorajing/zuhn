The TPU origin story is consequential not because it succeeded but because of its decision shape. In 2013, conventional wisdom held that custom silicon was a bad idea: CPUs were getting faster, the investment was risky, only specialized chip companies should attempt it. Google's bet was 30-40 engineers and low tens of millions of dollars to design TPU V1. This was significant for a tech company in 2013 but small relative to Google's overall budget. The asymmetry: cost of failure was bounded; cost of success was unbounded. The pattern generalizes: most breakthrough technical investments are contrarian-but-affordable bets that the consensus community would not approve of but that fit within the institution's discretionary capacity. Institutions that systematically reject small contrarian bets cannot produce breakthrough investments regardless of total resources, because the breakthroughs do not come from large committee-approved projects. The implication for organizational design: explicit budget for small contrarian bets, with clear permission to fail without career consequence. The implication for individual researchers and engineers: the contrarian bet you cannot defend at full justification is often the bet most worth making, and finding institutions with appetite for these bets is more career-defining than finding institutions with the most resources.
