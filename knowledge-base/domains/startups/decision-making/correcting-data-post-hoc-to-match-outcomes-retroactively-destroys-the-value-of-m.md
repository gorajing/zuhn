Graham notes parenthetically that exit poll numbers published online were revised by the next afternoon to match the actual returns — the only plausible explanation being that the numbers were corrected after the outcome was known. This destroys the epistemic value of the measurement: a poll that agrees with the final result because it was corrected to agree with it tells you nothing.

The principle generalizes widely in organizational and analytical contexts. Retroactive revision of forecasts, targets, or benchmarks to align with outcomes is a form of self-deception that feels like data management. It produces the illusion of calibration while preventing genuine calibration from developing. In any measurement system — A/B testing, financial modeling, ML evaluation, KPI tracking — the integrity of the prediction must be preserved independently of the outcome. If the outcome reveals the prediction was wrong, that wrongness is the information, not an embarrassment to be erased.
