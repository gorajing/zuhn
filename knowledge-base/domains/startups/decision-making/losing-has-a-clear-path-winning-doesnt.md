Zuckerberg, on Meta's 2012 HTML5-to-native rewrite during the IPO crash: 'It was actually pretty clear what we needed to do. Strategically, it's much harder to know what to do when you're winning. When stuff is going well, what is the next move to go from winning to winning more? When you're losing it's usually pretty clear what you have to do, and a lot of it is just: do you have the pain tolerance to go do it?'

This inverts the usual founder anxiety. Crisis mode is uncomfortable but legible — pause feature dev, rewrite the apps, eat eighteen months of stalled monetization, accept the analyst rage. Comfortable success is illegible — the team is happy, metrics are up, and the obvious thing to do is keep doing what's working, which is usually wrong because you're already on a local maximum that someone else will jump past.

The practical implication: the real strategic work happens during periods of strength, not crisis. When everything is going well, allocate disproportionate attention to 'what's the next hill we'd need to jump to' — not as paranoia, but because that's the only window when the org has the slack to make the jump intentionally rather than reactively. Crisis decisions are mostly about courage; peacetime decisions are mostly about taste.
