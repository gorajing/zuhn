Jessica Wu's reflection on her quant-research background: 'Finance was an interesting choice. I had an adviser who recommended that it's actually pretty good to spend some time in finance because it teaches you a way of thinking that's pretty special, especially if you're doing trading. It teaches you to be very objective, that you should always calculate odds for things... Doing a startup can be very emotional and always going back to first-principles thinking and trying to think in a more statistical way can be pretty helpful in making decisions even in a startup lens. Maybe you're looking at a super huge deployment that's something you have to really weigh, or someone is really looking at features — all of those things can be mapped out in a more rational way. You can say, what are the odds that this particular customer converts by this particular day? What are the things that I'm trading off?' The cognitive habits trained by finance: (a) Every decision is implicitly a bet; make the odds explicit. (b) Emotional pull toward 'I like this customer' is a cognitive bias to discount, not a reason to act. (c) Expected value combines probability and magnitude — focus on EV, not lottery-ticket outcomes. (d) Costs and benefits live in different timeframes and currencies — map them to a common unit before deciding. These transfer directly to startup decisions like: which customer to pursue (calculate conversion probability × deal size × strategic fit), which feature to build (probability of differentiation × adoption × retention impact), when to fundraise (cost of dilution now vs cost of running out of runway later). The poker comparison Wu invokes is right — startup decisions resemble poker more than chess, because incomplete information and variance dominate, and the right discipline is process not outcome. The implication for founders without finance background: deliberately train this cognition through reading expected-value-thinking literature (Annie Duke, Daniel Kahneman, Nassim Taleb) or playing poker with money on the line.
