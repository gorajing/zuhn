In the mid-2000s Steve Jobs approached Paul Otellini about manufacturing the iPhone SoC. Intel already made the Mac CPUs. The deal died because Otellini thought the offered price was too low to justify the factory capacity. Apple went to Samsung, then to TSMC, and the volumes from iPhone (plus Apple silicon, plus every hyperscaler custom chip that followed the same fabless model) rerouted the entire semiconductor industry away from Intel. Intel's market cap is now a fraction of TSMC's and smaller than ASML's.

The pattern repeats at every scale. Ford passed on hiring Morris Chang in 1955 over a $1/month salary difference — sending him to Sylvania, then TI, and eventually Taiwan. Early Uber investors passed at $300M because the valuation 'felt high.' The asymmetry these decisions have in common: the downside of saying yes is a known small loss (lower margin on this deal), and the upside is a category-defining partnership whose value you cannot back into from today's spreadsheet. When the upside is structurally asymmetric — the partner is going to define a new category — the price is almost never the variable that should drive the decision.

Apply this whenever a strategic counterparty is offering a deal that pencils out poorly on current economics: separate the 'is this the right partner for a category-defining move' question from the 'does this quarter's margin improve' question, and never let the second kill the first.
