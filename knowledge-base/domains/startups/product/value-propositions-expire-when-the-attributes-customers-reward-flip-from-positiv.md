McDonald's was built on fast and inexpensive meals — for decades this was its competitive advantage. But as customers began valuing healthy eating, fresh products, and customized experiences, fast-and-cheap became associated with low quality. The very attributes that built the brand now actively repelled the market segment it needed to attract.

This creates a particularly vicious trap: existing core customers chose the brand precisely for its cheap options, so they resist premium offerings. Meanwhile, quality-seeking customers have abundant alternatives. The company gets squeezed from both sides — unable to move upmarket without alienating its base, unable to stay put without continuing to decline. Regular reassessment of customer jobs-to-be-done, pains, and gains is the only way to detect these shifts before they become existential.
