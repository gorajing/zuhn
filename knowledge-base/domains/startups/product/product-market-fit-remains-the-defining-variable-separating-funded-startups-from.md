Product-market fit is not a single moment but a dynamic calibration between what a product does, who it serves, and whether those users generate the retention and growth signals that indicate genuine demand. Weak PMF is often masked by early traction — paid acquisition, novelty effects, or founder network — which creates a dangerous false positive that leads to premature scaling.

The structural test of PMF is whether users would be genuinely disappointed if the product disappeared. This Sean Ellis benchmark (>40% 'very disappointed' response) captures something revenue metrics miss: emotional lock-in that predicts organic retention and word-of-mouth. For investors evaluating early-stage companies, PMF signals matter more than team credentials or market size projections because they are empirical evidence of demand, not hypotheses about it. For founders, achieving PMF before raising growth capital is not conservative — it is the difference between building on rock versus sand.
