Graham identifies a specific failure mode he calls the Fluff Principle: content that requires zero effort to evaluate (amusing cartoons, rallying-cry rants) gets more upvotes than substantive content (novel theorems, detailed analyses) simply because the friction to vote is lower. A rant with a provocative title gets upvoted without being read; a proof requires actual engagement before a vote.

This has deep implications for platform design. The naive democratic model of 'let users vote on everything equally' systematically selects for shallow content. HN's countermeasures include banning common fluff categories, editor intervention to neutralize linkbait titles, and killing empty rants even when technically on-topic. The key insight is that curation isn't anti-democratic — it's necessary to preserve the signal that attracted quality users in the first place. Any platform designer who ignores this principle will watch their feed degrade toward lowest-common-denominator content.
