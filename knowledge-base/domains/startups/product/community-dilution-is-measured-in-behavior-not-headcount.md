Paul Graham observed that Hacker News community quality correlated more with behavioral expectations than user count. When people are expected to behave well, they tend to — and vice versa. This means the broken windows theory applies directly: minor bad behavior left unchecked encourages worse behavior, while enforcing norms gently repels bad actors without explicit barriers.

This has practical implications for any product with a community component. Rather than building elaborate gatekeeping mechanisms (invite-only, paywalls, approval queues), investing in visible behavioral norms and consistent enforcement yields better results. Reddit's deliberate zero-censorship policy combined with growth-maximization created a free-for-all, while HN's behavioral expectations maintained quality at 14x growth. The lesson is that community health is a moderation design problem, not a growth rate problem.
