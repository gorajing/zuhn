Horowitz wrote his 'Good Product Manager, Bad Product Manager' essay because he was furious at his seven PMs for 'doing everything but your job' — writing requirements, pitching customers, running around on random features nobody asked for. His entire point was brutally simple: the job is right product, right time, and if you don't deliver that, he doesn't care what else you did. In the AI era this sharpens further: Opus 4.6 can write PRDs, wrangle agents, and execute tactical steps — so the remaining human job narrows to the hardest part, which is deciding what problem to focus on and getting the timing right. The PM title doesn't matter; the accountability to right-product-right-time matters. If you're debating PRDs vs prompts or 'the landscape is different,' you're already dodging the job.
