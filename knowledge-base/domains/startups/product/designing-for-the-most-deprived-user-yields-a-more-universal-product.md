Canuma's MLX work began as a promise to restore information access to his father, who went blind in 2020 and lives in Africa where reliable internet and affordable subscriptions are scarce. Those two constraints — sensory deprivation and poor connectivity — directly dictated the architecture: it had to be multimodal (vision + voice, since typing wasn't viable) and fully on-device (since the cloud wasn't dependable). Designing for that hardest case is precisely what produced a framework that now serves a far broader audience.

The pattern is constraint-driven design: the most-deprived user surfaces requirements the median user never would (offline operation, voice-first control, low-power hardware), and solving for them yields capabilities everyone benefits from — the well-documented curb-cut effect. For founders, the actionable inversion is to pick a genuinely hard anchor user with a real unmet need rather than optimizing for an average user; the constraints become the product's differentiation, and a deeply personal motivation sustains the multi-year effort (three years, 1.5M+ downloads) that a market-sizing spreadsheet rarely does.
