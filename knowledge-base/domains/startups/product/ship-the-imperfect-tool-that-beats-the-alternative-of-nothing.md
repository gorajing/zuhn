Early single-celled organisms needed to fix carbon but RuBisCo was inefficient at it. The winning strategy wasn't to wait for a better enzyme — it was to produce massive quantities of the mediocre one. This brute-force approach let those organisms outcompete everything else and become the dominant life form on Earth.

For product builders, this illustrates why shipping a working-but-imperfect tool beats waiting for elegance. The organism that ships first captures the ecosystem. Once dominant, it can layer on adaptations (C4 photosynthesis, CAM pathways) to compensate for the original design's flaws. The same pattern plays out in markets: the product that exists and roughly works accumulates users, feedback, and network effects while competitors are still optimizing.
