The founder's $40K mistake included adding advanced features before solving basic needs — a pattern that stems from building toward a vision rather than toward current user pain. This inverts the correct priority order. Early-stage products should be optimized for solving one burning problem exceptionally well, not for feature completeness or architectural elegance.

The discipline required here is counterintuitive for technical founders who can see the full system they want to build. But premature feature expansion dilutes focus, extends time-to-feedback, and increases the cost of pivoting when (not if) the initial assumptions prove partially wrong. The correct sequence is: identify the most urgent pain, build the minimum that relieves it, ship immediately, then let real usage data guide what to build next.
