From his first store in Newport, Walton walked across the street into the Sterling store that was outselling him 2x and started taking notes. He never stopped. Family vacations became store tours. When two Ben Franklin stores in Minnesota were experimenting with self-service, he didn't read about it — he took an overnight bus there and stood in the aisles. The notebook habit became a tape recorder habit. He famously bragged he'd spent more hours inside Kmarts than most Kmart store employees.

This isn't about stealing ideas. It's about building the kind of tacit, physical sense of how operations actually work that can't be captured in a report. Reading about self-service can't tell you whether customers find it confusing. Reading about Kmart's margins can't tell you why their checkout lines are slow. The only way to know is to stand there.

The applied version for knowledge-work founders: your competitor's product is public. Use it weekly, not once during due diligence. Sign up. File support tickets. Read every changelog. Founders reflexively avoid this because it feels like it's not 'building' — but the strategic sense you develop from repeated first-hand exposure is the thing your analytics dashboard will never show.
