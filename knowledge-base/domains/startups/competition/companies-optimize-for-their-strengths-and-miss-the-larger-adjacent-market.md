Dara openly names the Uber Eats strategic mistake: Uber was an urban company, its rides business was optimized for fast and cheap, and this brand-identity filter made Uber build Eats around small delivery radii and speed. DoorDash made the opposite bet—suburbs and restaurant selection—and it turned out suburbs have bigger families, bigger orders, and a larger total market than cities. Dara notes he was still running the urban-fast-cheap playbook in 2018-2019, so this wasn't a founder-era mistake he inherited; it was a multi-year strategic blind spot driven by what Uber was 'good at.'

The generalizable pattern is that companies don't lose adjacent markets to better execution—they lose them to their own identity. Whatever your company is famous for becomes an implicit filter on which segments you even consider yours. Competitors who are willing to look stupid by attacking the 'wrong' segment (the less sexy suburbs, the less interesting customers) end up in the larger profit pool.

The counter-move for incumbents is to explicitly audit which segments you're dismissing because they don't match your self-image, and treat any segment you reflexively call 'not for us' as a candidate for attack. The competitor who wins the adjacent market eventually uses it as a profit pool to fund an attack on your core.
