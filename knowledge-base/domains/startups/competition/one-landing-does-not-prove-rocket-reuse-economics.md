Blue Origin's New Glenn booster recovery is genuinely significant — it makes Blue Origin only the second company to recover an orbital-class booster. But the milestone conflates engineering success with economic viability. The real test is refurbishment cycle time, cost per relaunch, and how many flights each booster can sustain before retirement.

SpaceX's advantage with Falcon 9 wasn't the first landing — it was the subsequent decade of iterating reuse into a reliable, low-cost operation. Blue Origin faces the same challenge: translate a single landing into a repeatable, cost-competitive cadence. Until then, the launch market remains SpaceX-dominant regardless of New Glenn's orbital capability.

This pattern applies broadly to any capital-intensive 'first': first prototype, first customer, first deployment. The milestone demonstrates feasibility but doesn't compress the long operational learning curve that converts proof-of-concept into durable competitive position.
