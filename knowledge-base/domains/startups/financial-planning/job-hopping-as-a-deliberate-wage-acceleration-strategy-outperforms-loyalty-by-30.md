Business Insider data shows Gen Z job-hoppers earn nearly 30% more than loyal stayers. This works because internal raises are typically capped at 3-5% while market-rate offers for the same role can be 15-30% higher. The compounding effect is strongest early in a career when base wages are low — a 20% jump on $50K adds $10K annually, which compounds through every subsequent negotiation.

This strategy is amplified by Gen Z's specific economic context: their wages are rising faster than any other age group partly because they started from the lowest base, making companies willing to pay competitive rates rather than retrain. The tactical insight is that early-career workers should optimize for learning and job-switching velocity rather than stability, banking the compounding wage gains for the first 5-7 years before potentially settling. This is especially important in inflationary environments where staying at the same salary means an effective pay cut every year.
