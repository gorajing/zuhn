Dara's biggest Expedia-era lesson from watching Booking.com crush them was that Booking obsessed over hotel supply while Expedia focused on consumer demand. Every hotel Booking added in a city wasn't just another SKU—it was another landing page Google could index, another conversion opportunity, and a better product in that market. Demand compounds on top of supply in fragmented marketplaces, not the other way around.

He imported this directly to Uber: supply-led recruitment of drivers and couriers is now Uber's core growth motion. The same logic holds—more drivers means better ETAs, lower surge, better pricing, and higher converting demand. The counterintuitive implication is that in fragmented marketplaces, the 'bring me more customers' instinct is exactly backwards. Whoever wires up the long tail of supply first wins, because demand is comparatively easy to buy once the supply side works.

This generalizes beyond travel and rideshare to any marketplace where supply is numerous and differentiated: creator platforms, freelance marketplaces, local commerce. If supply is concentrated (airlines), you can get away with demand-led. If supply is fragmented, supply-led is the only durable strategy.
