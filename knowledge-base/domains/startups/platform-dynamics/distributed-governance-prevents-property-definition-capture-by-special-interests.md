Graham argues that working democracies and multiple sovereign countries serve as a crucial defense against property definition capture. If a single autocratic government controlled the world, the RIAA and MPAA could simply buy the laws they wanted. But because there are countries that aren't 'copyright colonies' of the US, and because politicians still fear voters in sufficient numbers, there's no single point of attack for those trying to warp law.

For founders, this means regulatory moats built on lobbied-for property definitions are more fragile than they appear. Jurisdictional competition means overly restrictive regimes get routed around — businesses and users migrate to friendlier jurisdictions. This dynamic is especially relevant for startups in content, crypto, AI, and biotech, where property definitions are actively contested. The strategic implication: don't assume unfavorable regulation is permanent. Build for the regulatory environment that technology makes inevitable, not the one incumbents are currently defending.
