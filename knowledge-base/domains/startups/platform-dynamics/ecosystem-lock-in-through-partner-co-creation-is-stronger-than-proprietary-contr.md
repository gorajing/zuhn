ARM's decision to license rather than manufacture created something more powerful than a product — it created an ecosystem. With over 1,000 global partners (Samsung, Apple, Microsoft) building on ARM architectures, the switching costs are distributed across the entire value chain. Each partner has invested in ARM-specific tooling, software optimization, and manufacturing processes that would be enormously expensive to replicate for an alternative architecture.

This contrasts sharply with Intel's vertically integrated model, where the company both designed and manufactured chips. ARM's open approach meant that even as individual partners competed fiercely with each other, they all reinforced ARM's market position. The ecosystem became self-reinforcing: more partners meant more software compatibility, which attracted more partners. By 2012, this flywheel had captured 95% of smartphone and tablet chips — a market dominance achieved without ARM manufacturing a single chip.
