American Express ran a closed-loop system where it was both the issuing bank and the acquiring bank — it owned the full stack and could offer a polished, consistent experience on both sides. Visa, by contrast, was a bank-of-banks: each bank it signed up brought pre-existing consumer and merchant relationships into the network without Visa having to build them. This network-of-networks leverage meant Visa could add tens of millions of nodes with marketing and contracts instead of branch openings and underwriting.

The tradeoff is real: open-loop systems compromise on the lowest common denominator of data and UX because you cannot rule by fiat. But when the prize is ubiquity — where the value proposition literally depends on being 'everywhere you want to be' — scaling speed dominates experience quality. Closed-loop players in payments repeatedly lost ground because they had to solve international expansion as a banking problem, while Visa solved it as a licensing problem.

The lesson generalizes beyond payments: when a market rewards ubiquity and the marginal node provides access to a cluster of downstream users, federate; when it rewards polish and the node is the user, integrate. Most founders default to closed-loop because it feels more controllable, but that intuition fails when the real bottleneck is reach.
