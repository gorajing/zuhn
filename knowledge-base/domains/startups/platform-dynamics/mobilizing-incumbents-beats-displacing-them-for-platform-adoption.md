Apple Pay's go-to-market strategy was to mobilize the existing payments ecosystem rather than displace it. Card networks, banks, and merchants could continue their established business relationships — Apple simply added a mobile access layer on top. This contrasts sharply with disruptive approaches that require ecosystem participants to abandon existing workflows and adopt entirely new systems.

This 'mobilize don't displace' strategy dramatically reduces adoption friction. When every participant can say yes without saying no to something else, the activation energy for ecosystem adoption drops to near zero. Competitors like CurrentC (the merchant-backed alternative that CVS and Rite Aid initially backed) failed precisely because they required ecosystem rewiring — merchants had to reject existing card networks. The lesson for platform builders: design your entry strategy to be additive to existing relationships, not substitutive.
