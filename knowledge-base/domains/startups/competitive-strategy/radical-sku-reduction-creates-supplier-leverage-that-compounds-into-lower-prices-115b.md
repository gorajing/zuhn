While a typical supermarket carries 30,000 items and Walmart Supercenter stocks 140,000, Costco limits itself to roughly 4,000 SKUs. This constraint, which seems like a disadvantage, creates a flywheel: suppliers desperately want to be one of the one or two choices in each category, so they lower prices and even reengineer products to Costco's specifications (e.g., redesigning cashew containers to save 24,000 pallets/year).

This is a concrete example of how constraints create leverage. By refusing to offer variety, Costco concentrates its purchasing volume into fewer suppliers, giving each order massive scale. The insight generalizes: in any marketplace or platform, curating fewer options can paradoxically increase your bargaining power and quality, because suppliers compete for scarce slots rather than treating you as one channel among many.
