RuBisCo evolved when atmospheric CO2 was abundant and oxygen scarce, making its imprecision acceptable. Plants went 'all-in' on this enzyme strategy and conquered the planet. But as oxygen levels rose (ironically due to photosynthesis itself), RuBisCo began confusing O2 for CO2 roughly half the time, producing toxic byproducts that require additional enzymatic cleanup.

Despite this massive inefficiency, plants never evolved a replacement. Instead, they produce ~40 billion tons of RuBisCo at any given time to compensate through sheer volume. This maps directly to technology and business strategy: early architectural decisions that enable rapid scaling become nearly impossible to replace once the entire ecosystem depends on them. The rational response shifts from 'fix the core' to 'build workarounds and compensate with scale' — exactly what plants did, and exactly what companies do with legacy systems.
