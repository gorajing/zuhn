For decades China was dismissed as a low-cost assembler with no intellectual property. But that assembly work created something valuable that didn't appear on balance sheets: tacit process knowledge — the kind of manufacturing expertise that can only be learned by running factories at scale. This knowledge compound interest eventually enabled Chinese firms to move up the value chain from assembling foreign products to engineering their own.

This is a classic 'learning-by-doing' dynamic. The early low-margin work isn't a trap — it's tuition. Companies that accept inferior economics early in exchange for proximity to the production frontier can accumulate process knowledge that becomes the basis for competing at the frontier later.

For founders and operators, the implication is that taking 'boring' execution roles in complex domains often has asymmetric upside: the tacit knowledge gained is hard for others to replicate and hard to buy. Choosing to stay in a low-margin position to learn is often better strategy than exiting early to preserve margin.
