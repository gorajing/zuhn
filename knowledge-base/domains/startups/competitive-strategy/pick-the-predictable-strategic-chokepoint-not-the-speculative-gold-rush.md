While hundreds of prospectors rushed to western Pennsylvania to drill for oil in the 1860s, Rockefeller deliberately stayed out of production. He saw refining as the boring-but-strategic step: it required capital (which excluded most of the drillers), it was the only place in the value chain where you could control quality and cost, and it sat between an unpredictable supply side and a growing city demand. Drillers made and lost fortunes in weeks; refiners who survived the cycles compounded.

The generalizable lesson: in any commodity boom, the step of the value chain with the highest capital requirement, the most operating leverage, and the least glamor is usually the durable position. Drillers get written about; refiners get rich. The same dynamic has played out repeatedly — AWS vs. dotcoms, TSMC vs. chip designers, Nvidia vs. AI applications. Before joining a gold rush, ask which part of the chain is the choke point when the speculation ends.
