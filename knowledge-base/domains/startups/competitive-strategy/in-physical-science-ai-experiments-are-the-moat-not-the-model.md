Krause's central thesis is that in materials science 'models aren't the moat, experiments are.' He expects most foundation models to be open-source within five years, and Radical deliberately open-sources models (Matrix, TorchSim) while keeping the proprietary experimental dataset closed. The reasoning: anyone can download a better diffusion model or interatomic potential and drop it into their stack, but no one can replicate the physical loop of synthesizing, characterizing, and testing thousands of novel alloys. The asset that compounds is the data only your lab can generate.

This generalizes to any AI vertical where the ground truth lives in the physical world (robotics, biology, manufacturing, hardware) rather than in text. In those domains, betting on model superiority is a losing strategy because models commoditize; betting on a hard-to-replicate data-generation engine is durable. The corollary is that open-sourcing models is net-positive when your edge is data: it recruits community improvements, builds the ecosystem you depend on, and costs you nothing because the model was never the secret. Founders in physical-AI spaces should ask 'what proprietary data-generation capability am I building?' before 'how good is my model?'
