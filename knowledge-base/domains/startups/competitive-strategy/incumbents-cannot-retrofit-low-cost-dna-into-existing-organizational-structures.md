The pattern is remarkably consistent across decades and continents: Delta created Song (failed), US Airways created MetroJet (failed), United created Shuttle then Ted (both failed), Air France created Transavia and Lufthansa created Eurowings (both losing money). The failures aren't from lack of capital or market understanding—they stem from structural impossibility.

Legacy airlines cannot escape unionized labor agreements, hub-and-spoke infrastructure dependencies, and service-level expectations baked into their brand. This is a direct case of Clayton Christensen's innovator's dilemma: the very organizational capabilities that make incumbents successful at serving existing customers make them structurally incapable of competing on a fundamentally different cost basis. The lesson for startups is that incumbent attempts to copy your low-cost model through sub-brands are rarely a serious threat if your advantage is structural rather than cosmetic.
