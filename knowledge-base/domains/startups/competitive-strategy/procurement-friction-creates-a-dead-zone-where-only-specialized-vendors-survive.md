Joel Spolsky observed that corporate software under $1,000 could be bought by individual managers, but anything above required committee approval. The cost of navigating that approval process was so high for vendors that it didn't make sense to charge less than $50,000—meaning $5,000 software had to be priced at $50,000.

The deeper problem isn't overpaying—it's market breakdown. When you're hard enough to sell to, the best product-makers don't bother. The only companies willing to sell to you are those that specialize in navigating your procurement process, not in building great products. You've effectively opted out of market competition, the very mechanism that was supposed to protect you. This pattern applies beyond software: any sufficiently burdensome check on suppliers filters for compliance specialists rather than quality producers.
