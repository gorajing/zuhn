RuBisCo is objectively bad at its job — it's slow, confused by oxygen, and requires plants to produce enormous quantities to compensate. Yet no alternative carbon fixation pathway has displaced it across the dominant plant kingdom. The reason is pure lock-in: every downstream metabolic pathway, every cellular structure, every regulatory mechanism was built assuming RuBisCo's specific outputs and behavior.

This illustrates a competitive strategy principle stronger than typical business moats. When your product becomes infrastructure — when other systems are built assuming your existence — displacement requires replacing not just your product but the entire ecosystem built on it. This is why x86, QWERTY, and TCP/IP persist despite known limitations. The strategic implication: if you can become the substrate others build on, even mediocre performance creates an effectively permanent position.
