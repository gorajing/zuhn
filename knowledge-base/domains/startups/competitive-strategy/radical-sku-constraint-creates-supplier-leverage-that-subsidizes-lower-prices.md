By carrying only 4,000 unique items versus a supermarket's 30,000 or Walmart's 140,000, Costco transforms each product slot into intensely contested real estate. Suppliers will lower prices, customize packaging, and reengineer products (like the cashew container saving 24,000 pallets/year) just to be one of the one or two options available to Costco's 94 million members.

This is a counterintuitive competitive strategy: offering less creates more leverage. The constraint functions as a commitment device — suppliers know Costco won't hedge by carrying five alternatives, so winning the slot means capturing the entire category's volume. This same principle applies to any platform or marketplace: radical curation of supply creates leverage over suppliers that broad selection never can. The tradeoff is customer choice, but Costco converts this apparent weakness into a shopping experience advantage by eliminating decision paralysis.
