Manhattan's independent pizzerias show an astonishing 92% survival rate over a decade, and Brooklyn's is even higher. But pizza isn't a magical business that never fails. The apparent immortality is an illusion created by a specific market dynamic: in a city where real estate is scarce, a struggling operator is quickly replaced by someone drawn by low barriers to entry, attractive margins, and a fully built kitchen.

Critically, new owners generally keep the old name. To outsiders, nothing changes. The neighborhood fabric is undisturbed. The true failure rate is hidden behind private backroom deals. This creates a powerful survivorship bias in the data -- the 8% visible closure rate only captures locations that are too troubled to transfer (bad location, damaged reputation, unrealistic asking price). This dynamic likely applies to other location-dependent businesses: barbershops, laundromats, corner stores. What looks like business resilience may actually be high operator turnover masked by location continuity.
