NATO's Operation Ocean Shield reduced piracy significantly, but created a textbook free-rider problem: companies and countries that didn't contribute to patrols benefited equally from safer waters. Similarly, if all companies collectively refused ransoms, piracy would collapse — but each individual company's rational choice is always to pay, because the cost of losing crew and cargo exceeds the ransom. This is a pure Prisoner's Dilemma at scale.

This pattern recurs across startup ecosystems: open-source maintenance (everyone benefits, few fund it), industry standards bodies, shared infrastructure security, and market education in emerging categories. The lesson is that appeals to collective benefit never sustain voluntary contribution. Stable solutions require either enforcement mechanisms (regulation, binding agreements), internalized incentives (insurance pricing that rewards collective behavior), or structural redesign that makes free-riding impossible. When designing platforms or ecosystems, assume free-riding will occur and build the enforcement into the architecture from day one.
