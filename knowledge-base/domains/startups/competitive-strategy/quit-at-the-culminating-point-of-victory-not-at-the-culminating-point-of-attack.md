Sarah Paine distinguishes between the 'culminating point of attack' (an operational term — how far a single battle can go before overextension) and the 'culminating point of victory' (a strategic term — the moment when continuing the war will cost you more than stopping). Japan in the Russo-Japanese War is the canonical example: Field Marshal Yamagata argued at Mukden that Japan had to quit immediately despite a string of victories, because Russian forces in theater were three times larger, Japanese officer corps was bleeding out, and supply lines were one battle away from collapse. Japan had lined up Roosevelt as mediator before the war even started.

The opposite case is Japan itself in WWII: winning the Sino-Japanese War and Russo-Japanese War made military leaders believe war was easier and less risky than it actually was, so they kept pushing into an unlimited objective (regime change in China) and pulled in the US, UK, and Commonwealth — exactly the adversaries Japan had been careful to isolate in 1904. The same miscalculation kills startups, military campaigns, and political movements: you execute brilliantly until momentum becomes identity, and then you refuse to stop when stopping is the entire game.

Apply this whenever you're winning: explicitly write down the conditions under which you will stop, line up your exit mechanism before you start, and treat 'we're on a roll' as the most dangerous signal in strategy.
