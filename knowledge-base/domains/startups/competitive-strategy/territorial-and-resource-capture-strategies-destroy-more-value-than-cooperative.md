The US relationship with Greenland was positive-sum and cooperative — the US already had access to its natural resources and shipping routes. Conquering it would earn enmity from Europeans and Greenlanders while adding no real economic value. The Iraq War similarly failed to produce significant economic benefits from seized oil fields. Venezuela's oil remains uninvestable due to political chaos regardless of regime change.

This pattern maps directly to business strategy. Companies that shift from cooperative to extractive relationships with partners, suppliers, or adjacent markets often discover the same dynamic: the value they're trying to capture depended on the cooperative relationship they just destroyed. The insight applies to platform dynamics (extracting too much from developers), M&A (hostile acquisitions that destroy the target's culture), and market entry (competing aggressively with potential partners). The cooperative surplus is often larger than what any party could extract unilaterally.
