Graham draws a sharp distinction between platforms that are merely open and those that are open and good. Linux was open and powerful, attracting hackers away from Macs in the 1990s. But OS X combined the Unix foundation hackers needed with Apple's design excellence, pulling them back. The Thinkpad was 'not actively repellent' — damning with faint praise that reveals how low the design bar had fallen.

This principle applies beyond hardware. Developer tools, APIs, and platforms that optimize only for openness or only for developer power miss the compounding effect. When a platform is both open and genuinely delightful to use, hackers adopt it for personal use, write novel software for it, and that software becomes the killer app that drives mainstream adoption. The lesson: design quality is not a luxury — it is the mechanism that converts developer mindshare into market share.
