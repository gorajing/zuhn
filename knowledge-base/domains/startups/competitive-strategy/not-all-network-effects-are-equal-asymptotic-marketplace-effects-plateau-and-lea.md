Not all network effects create equal defensibility. In asymptotic marketplaces like ridesharing, the value curve flattens rapidly — the difference between a 4-minute and 2-minute wait is negligible to users, even though doubling driver supply is enormously expensive. This means a competitor with half the supply can deliver comparable perceived value.

This structural weakness is compounded by multi-tenanting: both riders and drivers freely use Uber and Lyft simultaneously with zero switching cost. Founders building marketplace businesses should evaluate where their value curve sits — delayed-value marketplaces (like OpenTable, which required massive supply before any demand-side value) paradoxically become stronger once they cross critical mass, while asymptotic ones remain permanently vulnerable. The practical implication is that asymptotic marketplace companies must find supplementary defensibility (brand, data, embedding) rather than relying on network effects alone.
