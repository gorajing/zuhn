Clay's signal taxonomy from working with hundreds of customers: 'There are two types of signals. One are branded signals — things you can buy from many places: people movement (job changers, new hires, promotions), website intent data (deanonymizing IP addresses), product reviews and news, people searching review sites (G2, Trust Radius, Bombora). Most companies should run some of these. But there's another type of signal which is more helpful — a CUSTOM signal that is based on something unique to the business.' Concrete examples from real customers: (1) Aviation companies pulling FAA violations on their accounts. (2) Companies monitoring specific types of social media posts (not all posts — specific patterns indicating buying intent). (3) Companies tracking website changes — for example, when a target account adds a new AI feature to their product, that's a signal a competing AI tool might be relevant. (4) Waste Management (the national waste disposal company known for green dumpsters): an agent in Clay uses Google Street View to look at the dumpster outside each potential prospect's business. If the dumpster is NOT green (not theirs), it's a prospect for outreach. Why custom > branded: branded signals are zero-sum across the buyer pool (everyone with the same provider gets the same data). Custom signals are uniquely yours, so the leads they surface are uniquely yours. The compounding effect: 'When you buy job changers data, you don't really iterate on it. It's like you didn't have it before, now you have it.' Branded data is static — you bought it, you have it. Custom signals can be ITERATED over time: tweak the prompt that decides which social posts to flag, refine the rule that defines what counts as a relevant FAA violation, tighten the AI agent that evaluates dumpster colors. Each iteration improves the signal's precision. Combined with field feedback (which leads converted, which messaging worked), the iteration becomes a compounding moat. The corollary for any operator: stop benchmarking yourself against competitors who buy the same commoditized data. Invest engineering effort in surfacing custom signals nobody else has. The best custom signals come from asking 'what specific situation would make me want to call this account RIGHT NOW?' and then building a detector for that situation. The pattern generalizes far beyond GTM: in product, custom-built feature instrumentation > standard analytics dashboards; in finance, custom-built alpha sources > standard factor exposure; in research, custom-built measurement methodology > standard benchmark datasets.
