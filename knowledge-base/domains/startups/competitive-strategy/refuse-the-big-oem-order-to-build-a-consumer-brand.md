In 1955 Sony was a tiny Japanese company trying to enter the US market when Bulova, an established American watch brand, offered to buy 100,000 transistor radios — an order many orders of magnitude larger than anything Sony had ever done. The only catch was that the radios had to ship under the Bulova name. Morita refused and told Bulova they could sell the Sony TR-63 or nothing. He later identified this as the most important decision of his career.

The logic is that once consumers associate a product with a distributor's brand, the underlying manufacturer becomes invisible and is stuck as a component supplier forever. Short-term revenue from OEM deals is real, but it trades away the option value of ever having a direct consumer relationship. Brand is the only asset that compounds across product generations; unit economics on a single product line can always be replaced.

Apply this when a small company gets a life-changing order that comes with a label or channel demand attached. The correct question is not 'can we afford to turn this down' but 'can we afford to foreclose the brand path permanently.' Nearly always, the answer is to refuse.
