Graham argues that the conventional explanation for union decline — that workers lost courage or that capital captured politics — misses the simpler structural truth. Unions flourished when manufacturing was a fast-growing sector where reliable labor supply was more valuable than cheap labor. As growth slowed and global competition intensified, the calculus flipped: labor cost mattered more than labor reliability.

This reframe has significant implications: it suggests that the timing and sector of your work matters more than the institutional structures you build around it. Institutions that look durable during a growth phase can be fragile once the underlying economic conditions shift.

For investors and founders, the lesson is to be skeptical of attributing durable competitive advantages to what may actually be temporary market conditions. A business or workforce segment that looks entrenched may simply be benefiting from a growth wave — and that advantage can evaporate quickly when the wave passes.
