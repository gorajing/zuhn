Thiel identifies two historical categories where innovators actually captured value: software businesses (due to zero marginal cost and fast adoption) and vertically integrated complex monopolies (Second Industrial Revolution: Ford, Standard Oil). The second category is systematically underexplored today because our culture resists anything complicated that takes a long time to build.

Tesla integrated car distribution to prevent dealers from capturing profits. SpaceX pulled in subcontractors that had been charging monopoly prices to large aerospace companies. Neither had a single 10x breakthrough in battery storage or rocketry — what was impressive was integrating all the pieces in a way competitors couldn't replicate. This contrasts with the disk drive industry of the 1980s, where continuous innovation produced no lasting wealth because each breakthrough was superseded within two years. The lesson: if your industry structure allows rapid supersession, even a 10x breakthrough won't create durable value. Vertical integration creates a different kind of moat — one based on coordination complexity rather than any single technical edge.
