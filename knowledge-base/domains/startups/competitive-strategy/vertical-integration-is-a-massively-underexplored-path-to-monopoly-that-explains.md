Thiel identifies two broad categories in 250 years of innovation history where creators actually captured value: vertically integrated complex monopolies (Second Industrial Revolution) and software businesses. The first category — exemplified by Ford and Standard Oil historically, and Tesla and SpaceX today — requires assembling many pieces in precisely the right way to create a tremendous integrated advantage.

Tesla integrated car distribution (preventing dealers from extracting profits as happened across the US auto industry). SpaceX pulled in subcontractors in-house (where most aerospace companies have single-source subcontractors that charge monopoly prices). Neither company had a single 10x breakthrough — they innovated across many dimensions simultaneously. Thiel argues this business form is 'very underexplored' today because our culture makes it hard to get people to buy into complex, long-timeline projects. For founders, this suggests an alternative to the software-default playbook: if you can coordinate complex integration that competitors can't replicate, you may build a more durable monopoly than a pure technology advantage provides.
