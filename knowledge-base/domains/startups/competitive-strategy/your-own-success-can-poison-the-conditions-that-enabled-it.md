RuBisCo evolved when Earth's atmosphere contained almost no oxygen. In that context it was a workable solution: fix CO2, produce organic carbon, reproduce. Over hundreds of millions of years, photosynthesis gradually flooded the atmosphere with oxygen as a byproduct. RuBisCo, never having 'encountered' oxygen before, couldn't reliably distinguish between O2 and CO2 — leading to photorespiration, a costly process that consumes energy without producing useful carbon.

The organism's own primary competitive advantage (photosynthesis) created the environmental condition (oxygen atmosphere) that degraded the effectiveness of its core mechanism (RuBisCo). The success was irreversible: having colonized the planet, there was no way to un-oxygenate the atmosphere.

For startups, this pattern appears when growth creates the conditions for disruption. A marketplace that achieves liquidity may attract competitors who use that same liquidity against them. A platform that drives developer adoption may educate the ecosystem enough that developers build alternatives. A company that creates a new job category may train the workforce that eventually replaces it with AI. Early advantage doesn't protect against environmental changes you caused — it often accelerates them.
