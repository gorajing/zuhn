Plants produce roughly 40 billion tons of RuBisCo at any given time — an enzyme that fails at its job up to half the time by grabbing oxygen instead of CO2. Yet every plant on Earth depends on it because early organisms committed to the RuBisCo strategy before atmospheric oxygen made it problematic. By the time the environment changed, the entire metabolic architecture was built around this enzyme, making replacement impossible.

This mirrors how startups and platforms accumulate technical and strategic debt. Early architectural decisions — a database choice, a pricing model, a core abstraction — get baked into every subsequent layer. The switching cost isn't just rewriting code; it's that every downstream system assumes the original choice. Like plants that evolved elaborate workarounds (photorespiration, C4/CAM pathways) rather than replacing RuBisCo, organizations build increasingly complex patches around foundational decisions rather than replacing them. The lesson: your earliest commitments have the highest lock-in potential, so invest disproportionate care there.
