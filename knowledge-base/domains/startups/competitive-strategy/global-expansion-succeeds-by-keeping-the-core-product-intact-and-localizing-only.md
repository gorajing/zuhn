McDonald's early international expansion into the Netherlands, Canada, and the Caribbean failed because they tried adapting the menu to local tastes. When Fred Turner took McDonald's to Japan, he reversed the approach: the American menu stayed identical, but local Japanese operators handled all marketing and positioning. The Japanese loved the authentic American experience and admired its efficiency.

This counterintuitive finding — that authenticity beats localization of the core product — has implications beyond restaurants. The product IS the brand promise; changing it undermines the reason people want it in the first place. What needs to be local is the communication and distribution, not the offering itself. This mirrors how global tech products (iPhone, Tesla, Netflix) succeed with a universal product and localized go-to-market. The key distinction is between localizing the message (necessary) and localizing the product (often destructive to what makes it special).
