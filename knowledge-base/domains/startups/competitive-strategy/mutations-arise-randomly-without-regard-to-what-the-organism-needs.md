Carroll emphasizes that mutations are blind to the organism's needs. A color change might make one creature invisible to predators and another fatally visible. The same mutation that helps in warm climates hurts in cold ones. Even the external conditions — tectonic activity, volcanism — are largely random.

This is the core insight of undirected variation: you do not need to predict what will work. You need to generate enough variety and let the environment select. This principle operates in startup ecosystems (many experiments, few survivors), in creative work (generate many ideas, edit ruthlessly), and in machine learning (random initialization, gradient-based selection). The quality of the selection mechanism matters more than the quality of the initial variation.
