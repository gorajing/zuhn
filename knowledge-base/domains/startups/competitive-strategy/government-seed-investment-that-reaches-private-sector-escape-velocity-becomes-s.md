China's initial state-directed infrastructure spending created the conditions—roads, rails, power—for private Chinese businesses to operate profitably in Africa. Of an estimated 10,000 Chinese firms on the continent, 90% are privately owned. This demonstrates a powerful pattern: government investment can create market infrastructure that private enterprise then exploits without further state subsidy. The critical threshold is when private returns exceed the friction of operating in the new market. Once crossed, the phenomenon becomes self-reinforcing as successful businesses attract more entrants. This pattern repeats in technology (government-funded internet → private tech economy), energy (subsidized renewables → commercial solar), and space (NASA contracts → SpaceX). The strategic implication is that the most leveraged government spending isn't ongoing subsidy but one-time infrastructure that permanently lowers the barrier to private profitability.
