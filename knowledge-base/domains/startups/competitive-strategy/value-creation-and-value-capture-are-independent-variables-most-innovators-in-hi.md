Thiel argues the most important business concept people don't understand is that value creation and value capture are completely independent. The US airline industry generated $195 billion in domestic revenue in 2012 but has cumulative profits of approximately zero over its entire 100-year history. Google, with just $50 billion in revenue, was worth 4x the entire airline industry combined.

This framework explains why scientists never make money (Y=0% across the board), why the First Industrial Revolution's textile innovators didn't get rich despite 60-70 years of 5-7% annual efficiency gains, and why most of the 250-year history of technological innovation features Y≈0%. The rare exceptions — vertically integrated monopolies of the Second Industrial Revolution and modern software companies — succeeded because of market structure, not just innovation quality. The implication for founders: obsessing over product innovation while ignoring market structure is the default path to building something valuable that you don't capture.
