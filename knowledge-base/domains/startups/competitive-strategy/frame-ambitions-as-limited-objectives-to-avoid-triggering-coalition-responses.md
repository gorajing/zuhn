Bismarck fought three wars — Danish, Austro-Prussian, Franco-Prussian — each framed as a limited territorial or succession dispute, never as regime change. Britain and other great powers watched each war individually and judged the stakes too small to intervene. Cumulatively, those 'limited' wars transformed the weakest of five great powers into a continental hegemon. The lesson for competitive strategy is that the same move framed as 'total war' triggers coordinated opposition, while the same move framed as 'limited adjustment' gets waved through.

For startups, this is the playbook behind disruption-from-below: each step looks like a narrow niche that incumbents don't bother to contest, but the sequence adds up to displacement. When Amazon launched books, it wasn't framed as 'we will own all retail.' When Tesla launched the Roadster, it wasn't framed as 'we will end the combustion engine.' The framing matters as much as the actual ambition, because coalitions form in response to perceived threat, not actual threat.

The corollary: be extremely careful about announcing unlimited objectives early. Stating 'we will dominate X' invites everyone in X to organize against you before you have the power to withstand it.
