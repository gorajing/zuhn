Hard salary caps like the NFL's are considered the gold standard for competitive parity, but they leak constantly: teams trade cap impact across years, restructure contracts, offer non-monetary inducements, and use ownership-side businesses to provide endorsement deals that shift value outside the cap. The MLB and European soccer prove that softer caps and luxury taxes degrade into permanent dynasty leagues for the wealthiest owners.

Lalit Modi's IPL player auction — borrowed from his family's Sotheby's partnership — eliminates these gaming vectors by collapsing all player acquisition into a single live event where every team has a fixed purse, contracts are standardized at three years, and the only way to gain advantage is superior analytics and player evaluation. Side deals can't conjure additional purse capacity, and collusion fails because the bidder at the next table will simply outbid you at fair market value. In year one the cheapest franchise (Rajasthan Royals, with the lowest salary spend) won the championship purely on the strength of better player evaluation.

The principle generalizes anywhere you need to enforce parity across actors with asymmetric outside resources: collapse the transaction into a single point-in-time event with a hard budget, standardize the contract terms so there's nothing to negotiate around the price, and let market forces do price discovery. This is why YC's standardized SAFE works — it eliminates legal arbitrage by making the only variable the cap. The deeper insight: gameable rules invite gaming, but gameable rules become ungameable when transactions are collapsed in time and standardized in form.
