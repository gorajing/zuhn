RuBisCo, the enzyme responsible for carbon fixation in photosynthesis, confuses oxygen for CO2 roughly half the time, producing toxic byproducts plants must waste energy cleaning up. Yet it remains the foundation of nearly all life on Earth because early organisms went all-in on the RuBisCo strategy when atmospheric conditions were different. By the time oxygen levels rose and exposed the flaw, the entire biosphere depended on it.

This mirrors startup dynamics where first movers with mediocre solutions can lock in an entire ecosystem. The lesson isn't that being first guarantees success — it's that once a strategy becomes load-bearing infrastructure for an entire network, the switching cost becomes astronomical regardless of quality. Startups should recognize that winning the 'adequate solution at the right moment' race matters more than perfecting a solution that arrives late.
