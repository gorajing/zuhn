RuBisCo was fit-for-purpose in its original environment (high CO2, low O2) but became deeply problematic when plants themselves changed the atmosphere by producing oxygen. Despite this, no alternative carbon fixation enzyme has displaced RuBisCo across the plant kingdom.

This is the strongest possible case study for first-mover advantage through ecosystem lock-in. The enzyme isn't protected by patents or network effects in the traditional sense — it's protected by the fact that every downstream process in plant metabolism was built assuming RuBisCo's outputs. Replacing it would require re-engineering the entire Calvin Cycle and associated detoxification pathways simultaneously.

For competitive strategy, this suggests that the deepest moats aren't built on product quality but on becoming the substrate that everything else depends on. Platforms, standards, and protocols that become embedded in other systems' assumptions are nearly impossible to displace even when clearly inferior.
