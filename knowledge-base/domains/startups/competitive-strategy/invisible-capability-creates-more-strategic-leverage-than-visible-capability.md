Aircraft carriers project power through visibility; submarines project power through invisibility. A submarine that could be anywhere forces an adversary to defend everywhere, which is far more expensive than the submarine itself. This is the asymmetry of deterrence — the cost of uncertainty falls on the opponent.

In competitive strategy, this maps directly: a startup with unknown capabilities forces incumbents into defensive postures across multiple fronts. Companies like Apple leverage this by maintaining extreme secrecy about product roadmaps, forcing competitors to hedge across many possible directions. The principle is that selectively concealing your capabilities can be more strategically valuable than demonstrating them, because your opponent must allocate resources against the full possibility space rather than your actual position.
