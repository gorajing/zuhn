The narrative that 'Amazon killed retail' is a misdiagnosis. Sears tried to do everything and did everything poorly. Toys-R-Us dismissed the internet as a fad. JC Penney forgot who its customers were. These failures were strategic, not Amazon-caused — Amazon provided the final pressure that exposed pre-existing rot.

Meanwhile, retailers with clear, well-executed strategies grew: Ross, Lush, Aldi, Dollar General, and Costco all expanded during the same period. Their survival and growth is evidence that the mechanism of failure was strategic drift or complacency, not the existence of a better-capitalized online competitor.

For founders, this reframes the disruption risk question. The threat from a well-funded competitor is highest for companies with fuzzy strategy, conflicted incentives, or stagnant product. Companies with genuine differentiation and aligned incentives are more durable than the 'Amazon will eat everyone' narrative suggests.
