Thiel's DCF analysis of PayPal in March 2001 (27 months after founding, 100% annual growth, 30% discount rate) revealed that approximately 75% of the business value came from cash flows in years 2011 and beyond. He argues this math holds for essentially every tech company: 75-85% of value comes from cash flows a decade or more in the future.

This inverts the Silicon Valley obsession with growth rates. Growth is measurable in the here and now, but durability — whether a company will still exist and dominate in 10-20 years — is what actually dominates the value equation. Thiel cites Capablanca's chess principle: 'you must begin by studying the end game.' Microsoft was valuable as the last operating system for decades, Google as the last search engine, Facebook potentially as the last social network. The strategic question isn't 'how do I move first?' but 'why will this still be the leading company in 15-20 years?' This reframes monopoly characteristics (network effects, proprietary tech, economies of scale) from static advantages to ones that must compound over time.
