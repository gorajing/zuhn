Brooks Running was a $60M everything-shoes company losing $5M/year in 2002. Jim Weber's turnaround was to cut product lines, blow up distribution, lay off half the revenue, and become only a performance running shoe company. Twenty years later, Brooks does $1.2B with 30-40% annual growth. The New York Times ran the opposite play: while every mid-sized newspaper went bankrupt, NYT scaled up to be one of a few global trusted news brands, justifying enormous fixed costs in reporting and technology.

The internet creates a barbell because it (1) lets the best global product capture massive returns to scale (winner-take-most platforms) and (2) lets a tiny operation aggregate weirdos worldwide who care about a hyper-specific niche. The middle has neither global scale economics nor differentiation — pure squeeze.

Applied beyond media: this is happening in venture capital (huge multi-stage funds vs niche micro-funds, mid-sized firms struggling), and will likely happen to universities (Harvard/Stanford brands strengthen, mid-tier suffers) and many other industries. When designing a business, identifying which end of the barbell you're aiming for is the first strategic question.
