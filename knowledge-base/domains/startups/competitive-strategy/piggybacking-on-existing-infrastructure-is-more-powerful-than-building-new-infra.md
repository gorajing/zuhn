M-PESA's architecture reveals a powerful competitive strategy: instead of building banking infrastructure from scratch (branches, ATMs, card networks), it layered financial services on top of infrastructure that already had universal penetration — mobile phones and SIM cards. The expensive part (towers, equipment, carrier networks) was already built and paid for by telecom companies.

This piggybacking strategy appears repeatedly in successful platforms. Just as M-PESA used phone networks, Airbnb used existing housing stock, Uber used existing cars, and YouTube used existing internet infrastructure. The pattern suggests that when entering a market, the highest-leverage move is identifying which existing infrastructure can serve as your distribution rail. Building proprietary infrastructure creates a moat but dramatically slows adoption. In markets where speed of adoption determines the winner (due to network effects), piggybacking beats building every time.
