Graham reveals a decisive moment: moving YC from Boston to Silicon Valley was partly self-indulgent (better weather, denser startup ecosystem) but also strategically defensive. He explicitly didn't want a competitor to 'copy us and describe it as the Y Combinator of Silicon Valley.' He wanted YC itself to hold that position.

This reflects a broader competitive principle: when you've created a new category or model, you must occupy the key strategic positions before imitators do. In startups, the 'X of Y' framing is powerful — being 'the Y Combinator of Silicon Valley' would have been a potent competitor pitch. By moving first, Graham made the copy impossible. The lesson applies to any category creator: identify where your model would be most powerful and plant your flag there, even if it means uncomfortable, fast moves.
