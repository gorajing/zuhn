Kent Yoshimura's empirical observations as one of the few brands operating profitably on TikTok Shop post-pivot: 'TikTok Shop is pretty interesting. Before middle of 2025 you could create content — TikTok Shop looks worked a lot like regular TikTok where you'll have infinite virality without having to spend a single dime. Now they basically STOP YOUR GROWTH unless you spend money to break through, and then they stop your growth AGAIN. So now it's tranche systems where you have to pay into virality if you're on TikTok Shop.' Concrete impact data: 'Force Factor — one of the fastest growing brands in January — they're moving off TikTok Shop completely because they can't reach profitability now. They're a few hundred million dollars a year supplement brand.' Why Neurogum survives: 'I think we're one of the only brands that literally operates profitably on TikTok because we are able to keep breaking certain things and interact with the TikTok team in a certain way where: no, we're not going to do mega discounts. No, we're not going to do XYZ. With certain products, we realize we did a whole price breakdown of all our SKUs — what are the products we want to push forward because we know we're going to get incremental profitability, versus the ones we know aren't going to ever make profitability on TikTok. We just completely killed them. All these brands are trying to push everything they can on TikTok, use TikTok as an Amazon-style marketplace. That's not the case anymore.' The broader pattern: when a platform shifts from organic-virality to pay-to-scale, this is the platform-rent-extraction moment that has happened with every major platform: (1) Facebook organic reach in 2013-2015 → pay-to-reach by 2018. (2) Instagram organic reach in 2015-2018 → pay-to-reach by 2021. (3) YouTube organic reach in 2018-2022 → still partial but trending. (4) Now: TikTok Shop in mid-2025. The platform's incentive structure: when growth is slowing, the lever to maintain revenue is to extract more from existing creators/brands rather than acquire new ones. This is structurally what 'enshittification' looks like for platform marketing channels. The corollary for brands: don't build core business model on any platform's organic reach. The reach IS going to get gated within 3-5 years of the platform's mass adoption. Build email lists, direct customer relationships, multi-channel presence. TikTok-only brands today are at risk that platform-rent-extraction goes further. The corollary for capital allocators: when evaluating a TikTok-Shop-dependent brand for investment in 2026, ask explicitly: 'How does your model adapt if TikTok Shop becomes pay-only?' If the answer is 'we'd need to fundraise to keep growing,' that's structural fragility. The corollary for product portfolio: even on platforms that haven't yet shifted to pay-to-scale, identify which of your products are lead-gen (drive ecosystem growth) vs which are pure-revenue-extraction (one-time purchases). Only push the lead-gen products via platform marketing channels. Keep pure-extraction products on lower-risk channels (Amazon, your own website).
