Rosalind Franklin's Photo 51 — the X-ray diffraction image that revealed DNA's helical structure — was shown to Watson by Maurice Wilkins without Franklin's consent. Watson and Crick used this critical data to build their double helix model and won the Nobel Prize. Franklin died in 1958 and was never credited in the award.

This is not just a historical injustice — it's a structural pattern. When you share proprietary data or preliminary findings without publication, patents, or contractual protection, you create an information asymmetry that benefits the recipient. In startup contexts, this maps to sharing product roadmaps with potential acquirers, giving detailed technical presentations to competitors disguised as partners, or open-sourcing core IP before establishing market position. The lesson isn't to hoard information, but to ensure structural protections (publication, IP filings, NDAs with teeth) exist before sharing anything that constitutes a competitive advantage.
