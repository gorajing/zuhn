Android's killer move wasn't being free — Microsoft Windows Mobile charged carriers $5-10 per device while Android was free, which was already unbeatable. The killer move was that Google also paid OEMs and carriers a revenue share on searches originating from Android phones. Bill Gurley called this the 'less than free' business model. Microsoft couldn't respond because their entire mobile P&L depended on the $5-10 license fee — matching Google would require blowing up their core business model.

The structural precondition is that your revenue comes from a layer downstream of the thing you're giving away. Google makes money on search ads, so the phone OS is purely a distribution channel whose job is to keep search queries flowing through infrastructure Google controls. Any dollar spent on OEM rev-share that protects search traffic is cheaper than losing that traffic to a competitor's default. Today Google pays ~$10B/year to Android OEMs and $20B/year to Apple — trivial against $200B+ of search revenue, and the alternative (losing the default slot) is existential.

The generalizable pattern: identify businesses where a free-or-paid-for distribution layer protects a massive downstream revenue layer. You can always outbid competitors whose business model IS the distribution layer. This is what enables Chromium being open-source, Gmail being free, Maps being free — all of it is an acceptable tax on a monopoly position two layers downstream.
