Graham observes that most applications — and most startups — grow out of personal projects. Apple itself exists because Wozniak wanted a computer he could afford. The iPhone's app ecosystem thrived primarily because programmers carried iPhones, not because of market share statistics. RIM's Blackberry market share was irrelevant because developers didn't use Blackberries personally.

This insight has profound strategic implications: to win a platform war, you must win the developer's pocket first. Consumer market share is a lagging indicator; programmer adoption is the leading one. A competitor to a dominant platform doesn't need to win on general appeal — it needs to offer something specifically compelling to developers. Graham's prediction that the competitor would need to get 'iPhones out of programmers' hands' proved prescient as Android eventually succeeded partly by offering developers more freedom.
