When Amazon creates its own batteries or backpacks, it's not equivalent to Target's Up&Up brand. Amazon has access to seller data (what's selling, at what price, with what margins), controls search rankings and Buy Box placement, and sets the rules competitors must follow. The information asymmetry is qualitatively different from a physical retailer placing their generic next to a name brand.

The same pattern appears with Apple's App Store: Apple Music doesn't pay the 30% fee, doesn't need to follow the same rules, and comes pre-installed. For Spotify, whose thin-margin business model breaks at 30% platform tax, this isn't just a competitive disadvantage — it's an existential threat. The key insight for platform builders is that once you own distribution, competing with your own sellers becomes almost irresistibly profitable, but this is also the behavior most likely to trigger regulatory action.
