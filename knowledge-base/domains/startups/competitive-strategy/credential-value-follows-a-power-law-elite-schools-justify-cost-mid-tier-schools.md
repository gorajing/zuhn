Hormozi distinguishes sharply between elite institutions (Harvard, Stanford, Yale, Princeton) and the broad middle tier. Elite schools provide two irreplaceable assets: a personal brand effect ('he went to Harvard' shifts perception in every room) and access to the ruling-class network where graduates have median incomes in the tens of millions. These schools also offer generous endowments that offset tuition for non-wealthy students, making the arbitrage even stronger.

Mid-tier schools, by contrast, charge near-elite prices without delivering elite network effects or brand recognition. The 75th percentile earner from Harvard may make $6-15 million lifetime vs. $3 million median for a generic bachelor's degree. This power-law distribution means the college decision isn't binary — it's about whether your specific school clears the threshold where brand + network justify cost. The strategic insight: if you can't get into a top-20 school, the risk-adjusted return of direct career entry often dominates.
