Most retailers compete by offering maximum selection — the assumption being that more choice equals more captured demand. Costco inverts this by stocking roughly 4,000 unique items versus a typical supermarket's 30,000 or Walmart's 140,000. For each product category, only one or two options exist on shelves.

This constraint creates fierce competition among suppliers to be the chosen brand, driving them to offer lower wholesale prices and even redesign products to Costco's specifications (e.g., reengineering cashew containers to fit more per pallet, saving 24,000 pallets annually). When suppliers can't meet Costco's standards, the company simply creates its own Kirkland Signature brand, which now accounts for 25% of annual sales. The principle is counterintuitive: by deliberately limiting what you offer, you concentrate purchasing volume and transform yourself from a buyer into a gatekeeper that suppliers must court.
