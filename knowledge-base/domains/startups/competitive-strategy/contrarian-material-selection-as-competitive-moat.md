When Nakamura surveyed the blue LED landscape, zinc selenide had 500+ researchers at conferences while gallium nitride talks had 5 attendees. Rather than competing in a crowded field, he chose the abandoned material partly because he needed to publish 5 papers solo for his PhD - impossible in a crowded space. This wasn't blind contrarianism; he had a unique advantage (his custom-built MOCVD reactor) that let him approach the material's problems from angles others couldn't. The lesson for competitive strategy is that contrarian bets work best when paired with a differentiated capability. Nichia went from a struggling semiconductor also-ran to a billion-dollar LED manufacturer because they were alone in a field everyone else had written off.
