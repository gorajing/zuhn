The default startup playbook emphasizes speed and public iteration — build in public, get feedback fast, iterate. But this article argues the failure mode is more often premature exposure than slow building. When a startup goes public too early, it locks in narratives, creates expectations around half-baked features, and subjects fragile prototypes to premature judgment.

This is counterintuitive because the lean startup orthodoxy treats customer feedback as universally good and early. But feedback from the wrong audience at the wrong time can be destructive — it shapes the product toward current expectations rather than the founder's vision. Stealth 'slows the clock in a useful way,' giving technical teams time to build something that survives first contact with the market rather than something that merely reflects early user requests.
