Taleb simulated a competition between two people: one with knowledge and one with convexity (asymmetric payoff where gains exceed losses). The convexity bias — the advantage of the option-holder over the knowledge-holder — is enormous. This explains a historical puzzle: trial and error has produced most of the world's technology, not top-down theoretical knowledge.

The key insight is that trial and error is itself an option. Like cooking — you add an ingredient, taste it; if it works you keep it, if it fails you've lost almost nothing. The Romans built magnificent structures for 16 centuries without knowing Euclidean geometry. Technology generally precedes science, not the other way around. For startups, this means structuring for maximum optionality (many cheap experiments with capped downside) rather than trying to predict the right answer upfront.
