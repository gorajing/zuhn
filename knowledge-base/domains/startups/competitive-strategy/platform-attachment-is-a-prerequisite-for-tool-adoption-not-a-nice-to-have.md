Graham argues that programming languages don't exist in isolation — they become popular by being 'the scripting language of a popular system.' C rode Unix, Perl rode system administration, Java rode web browsers. Lisp, despite being arguably superior, failed to maintain popularity because it wasn't attached to any dominant platform after the MIT Lisp Machines died.

This insight generalizes beyond languages: any developer tool, framework, or infrastructure product needs to be the natural companion to something people are already using. You either supply the complete system yourself (language + platform) or design your tool to be the obvious choice within an existing ecosystem. Building an excellent standalone tool without distribution through a popular platform is the most common mode of failure in developer tools.
