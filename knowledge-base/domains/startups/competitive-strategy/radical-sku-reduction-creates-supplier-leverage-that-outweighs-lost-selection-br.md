Costco stocks roughly 4,000 unique items versus Walmart's 140,000. This apparent limitation is actually a strategic weapon: suppliers compete intensely to be one of Costco's one or two choices per category, accepting lower margins and even redesigning packaging (like the cashew container that saved 24,000 pallets/year) to win placement.

This is a counterintuitive form of leverage — by offering less, Costco demands more from each supplier. The concentrated volume per SKU gives Costco bargaining power that a broad-selection retailer can never match on any individual product. When a company represents a massive share of a supplier's sales volume for a single product, the supplier has no choice but to negotiate aggressively. The lesson extends beyond retail: radical focus on fewer offerings can generate disproportionate leverage with partners and vendors.
