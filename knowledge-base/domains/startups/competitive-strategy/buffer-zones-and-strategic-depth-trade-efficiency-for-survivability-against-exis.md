Russia's territorial expansion into Siberia and Eastern Europe looks economically irrational—vast, sparsely populated, expensive to govern. But it made Russia unconquerable: no army could sustain supply lines across thousands of miles of inhospitable terrain. The USSR's Eastern European buffer states served the same function, keeping Western threats far from Moscow. This principle applies beyond geopolitics. Startups and companies face analogous choices: do you optimize for efficiency (lean operations, single points of failure) or build buffer layers (redundant systems, defensive IP portfolios, diversified supply chains)? The geography lesson is clear—entities that sacrifice some efficiency for strategic depth survive existential shocks that destroy leaner competitors. The key is distinguishing existential threats (where buffers are worth the cost) from ordinary competition (where lean wins).
