When Morgan entered the railroad industry, dozens of competing companies offered similar routes and slashed prices in destructive competition. Rather than picking a winner, Morgan systematically acquired and merged these competitors into unified conglomerates that could set prices and benefit from economies of scale. This 'morganization' approach was so successful it became a named strategy.

The pattern applies beyond railroads: in any market where competitors offer near-identical products and compete primarily on price, consolidation can transform a race-to-the-bottom into a profitable monopoly. Morgan later applied the same playbook to steel (creating U.S. Steel) and electricity (creating General Electric). The key insight is that sometimes the highest-leverage move isn't winning the competition but eliminating it entirely through strategic acquisition.
