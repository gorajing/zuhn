RuBisCo evolved when atmospheric CO2 was abundant and O2 was scarce. It was mediocre at carbon fixation but better than nothing, so early organisms went all-in — building their entire metabolic infrastructure around it. When oxygen levels rose (ironically, because of photosynthesis itself), RuBisCo started confusing O2 for CO2 roughly half the time, producing toxic byproducts that plants must actively neutralize.

Rather than evolving a better enzyme, plants compensated by producing enormous quantities of RuBisCo (estimated 40 billion tons on Earth at any time) and developing detoxification pathways. This is the biological equivalent of scaling horizontally to compensate for bad architecture. The lesson for startups and technology: once an ecosystem commits to a standard — however flawed — the switching costs compound until replacement becomes practically impossible. The question is not whether your solution is optimal, but whether you can achieve lock-in before alternatives emerge.
