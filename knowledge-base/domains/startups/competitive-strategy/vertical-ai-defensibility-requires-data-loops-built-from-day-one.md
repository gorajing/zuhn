Euclid Ventures argues that in vertical AI, being first to market is table stakes—it gets you started but doesn't keep you alive. Real defensibility comes from data feedback loops where each customer interaction improves the product in ways competitors can't replicate, combined with switching costs that make leaving painful.

This is a critical insight for AI founders who often prioritize shipping fast over building structural advantages. The implication is that your data architecture, customer integration depth, and compounding improvement mechanisms should be first-class design decisions from the start, not afterthoughts. Companies that treat AI as a feature bolted onto existing workflows will lose to those that embed themselves as infrastructure.
