Rolex's 30% market share of Swiss watch revenue (4x the #2 player Omega) is not the result of being the most expensive or most artisanal — it comes from how they manage the market around their product. During the 2008 crash, while Cartier and others slashed prices and cut marketing, Rolex held prices, doubled US ad spend, and kept sponsoring the US Open. During the 2020-2022 demand boom, they refused to ramp production meaningfully, leaving the secondary market to absorb the craze. The logic is 'business physics': a brand can't easily shrink from its largest-ever year, so the asymmetric risk is overproducing in good times and being forced to discount in bad times, which permanently damages brand equity. Contrast with Cartier, which overproduced in 2016 and destroyed hundreds of millions in inventory. The same pattern shows up in Hermès's Birkin strategy. Key mechanic: slow, modest, inflation-matching price increases every year set a floor that compounds into an implicit return guarantee, making customers treat the object as a quasi-financial instrument. This strategy requires either patient capital or the willingness to leave money on the table, which is why it's rare.
