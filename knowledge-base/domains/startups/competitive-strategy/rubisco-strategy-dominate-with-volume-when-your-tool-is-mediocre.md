RuBisCo, the enzyme that fixes carbon in photosynthesis, is notoriously inefficient — it confuses oxygen for CO2 roughly half the time, creating toxic byproducts plants must clean up. Yet it became the most abundant protein on Earth (~40 billion tons) because early organisms compensated for poor quality with sheer volume.

This mirrors a recurring startup pattern: the first solution that works 'well enough' at scale often becomes the standard, even when superior alternatives exist later. Plants are now so deeply committed to the RuBisCo pathway that switching to a better enzyme would require redesigning the entire Calvin Cycle — a biological equivalent of technical debt so deep that rewriting is harder than patching. The lesson: speed and volume of deployment can matter more than quality of the individual unit, but this creates path dependence that constrains all future evolution.
