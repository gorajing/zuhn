RuBisCo was optimized for an atmosphere rich in CO2 and nearly devoid of oxygen. When photosynthesis itself changed the atmosphere by flooding it with oxygen, RuBisCo started confusing O2 for CO2 up to half the time. Plants couldn't redesign the enzyme — they could only bolt on expensive compensatory mechanisms (C4 pathways, photorespiration recycling).

Startups see this constantly: a go-to-market strategy perfected for an uncrowded market fails when competitors arrive; a pricing model built for cheap compute breaks when costs rise; a growth loop optimized for one platform's algorithm collapses when the platform changes rules. The pattern suggests that the most dangerous optimization is one that assumes current conditions are permanent. Building sensing mechanisms that detect environmental shifts early — and maintaining architectural flexibility to adapt — is more valuable than squeezing maximum efficiency from today's conditions.
