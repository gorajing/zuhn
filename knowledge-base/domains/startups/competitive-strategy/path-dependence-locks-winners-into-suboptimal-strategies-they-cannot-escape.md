RuBisCo evolved when Earth had abundant CO2 and almost no oxygen. It was never good at its job—it confuses O2 for CO2 roughly half the time, producing toxic byproducts. But it was better than no enzyme at all, so plants went all-in on the RuBisCo strategy. By the time oxygen levels rose and exposed its flaw, plants had built their entire metabolic architecture around it. Rather than evolving a replacement, they evolved workarounds—additional enzymes to break down the toxic phosphoglycolate into usable compounds.

This is a powerful model for competitive strategy and technical debt. The first solution that works 'well enough' often becomes permanent infrastructure, not because it's optimal but because the cost of switching exceeds the cost of patching. Startups that achieve product-market fit with a flawed architecture face the same trap: the codebase, team knowledge, and customer expectations all calcify around the original design. The lesson isn't to avoid shipping imperfect solutions—RuBisCo literally conquered the planet—but to recognize that early architectural choices become irreversible faster than you expect.
