When Microsoft's $69B Activision-Blizzard acquisition was blocked by regulators concerned about monopolization of cloud gaming, Nware — a small European cloud gaming startup — proactively contacted Microsoft executives to offer themselves as evidence that the cloud gaming market remained competitive. Days after the UK CMA ruled against the acquisition, they jointly announced a 10-year global partnership. The deal brought massive media coverage (VentureBeat, Reuters, Engadget) and positioned Nware as a legitimate player.

This pattern is replicable: large M&A deals regularly face regulatory challenges where the acquirer needs to demonstrate market competitiveness. A startup in the adjacent space can become a strategic asset simply by existing and being willing to partner. The key tactical insight is proactive outreach — Nware didn't wait to be discovered, they researched decision-makers and initiated contact. The three-day response time from Microsoft's VP validates that incumbents under regulatory pressure are highly receptive to these approaches.
