A turbine blade operates in one of the harshest environments humans have ever engineered: 1,500C gas stream, 12,500 RPM rotation (creating 20-ton centripetal force per 300g blade), oxidative atmosphere, dust erosion, and tens of thousands of hours of required service life. You could use tungsten for its 3,400C melting point, but it's 2.5x denser than steel and brittle. You could use titanium for weight, but it fails at lower temperatures.

The winning solution — nickel superalloys with 10+ elements, single-crystal structure, thermal barrier coatings, and internal cooling channels — doesn't maximize any single parameter. Instead, it carefully balances all of them at once. Too much gamma prime makes the blade strong but brittle. Too little lets it creep. The alloy composition, crystal growth, coating, and cooling design must all be co-optimized.

This maps directly to competitive moats in complex products: when your product must simultaneously satisfy multiple constraints at their limits, no competitor can replicate it by excelling at just one dimension. The integration knowledge — knowing exactly how much gamma prime, exactly which 10 elements, exactly how to grow the crystal — IS the moat. It takes decades of accumulated manufacturing knowledge that can't be reverse-engineered from the finished product.
