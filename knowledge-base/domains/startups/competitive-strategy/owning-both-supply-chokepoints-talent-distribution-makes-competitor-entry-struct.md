When ZTV launched the rival Indian Cricket League in 2007 with deep pockets, real broadcaster backing, and the same T20 format Lalit Modi was planning, the BCCI killed it in months by issuing a single decree: any player who signed with ICL was banned for life from international cricket and every state-level competition. The economic threat was overwhelming because the BCCI had quietly accumulated two cornered resources under Jagmohan Dalmiya — centralized player contracts (instituted in 2001) and centralized media rights (established by Supreme Court ruling). Owning either alone is insufficient. Owning both creates a structural impossibility for entrants regardless of their capital, format innovation, or broadcaster relationships.

The lesson generalizes beyond sports: aggregation power comes from sitting astride two adjacent chokepoints that each have lock-in dynamics. Controlling supply (talent) means competitors can't field a credible product; controlling distribution (rights) means competitors can't reach the audience. A startup challenging an incumbent should map the chokepoints and ask whether the incumbent owns one or both — if both, a frontal assault is hopeless and the only viable strategy is to redefine the category so the existing chokepoints don't apply.

The converse insight for builders: if you can engineer a position where you own both sides of a two-sided market's critical inputs, defensibility becomes near-absolute. The BCCI didn't set out to be a monopoly stewardship body; centralized contracts and central rights were each justified on their own merits, but combined they became uncontestable.
