Sarah Tavel articulates the Benchmark model as 'strategy is all about making trade-offs and aligning all of your trade-offs so that they're a force multiplier rather than in conflict with each other.' The five partners walk through how their constraints interlock: no growth fund means no pricing conflict in future rounds, which means founders can raise at better valuations, which means less dilution, which means Benchmark's early-stage brand stays pristine, which means entrepreneurs refer other entrepreneurs, which is where 100% of their new investments come from. Break any one link and the chain degrades.

The diagnostic move is to stop asking 'is this individual decision good?' and start asking 'does this trade-off reinforce every other trade-off I've made?' Most firms and companies accumulate good-idea-by-good-idea — growth funds, new geographies, more partners, new products — each defensible in isolation and collectively incoherent. The Benchmark partners explicitly invoke Johnny Ive's definition of focus: saying no to ideas that every bone in your body thinks are good. The quality of a strategy is measured by how many obviously-good things it forces you to refuse, not by how many opportunities it lets you pursue.
