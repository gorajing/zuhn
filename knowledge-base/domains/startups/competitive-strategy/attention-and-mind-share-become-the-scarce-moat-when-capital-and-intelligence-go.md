Geoff frames the thesis as a chain of substitution: science and capital are the levers of civilization, but in a world flooded with capital and where 'intelligence is just going to be metered out through compute,' the genuinely scarce resource is attention. Whoever can reliably command mind-share at scale — and steer cultural taste — holds the lever that money and raw intelligence can no longer buy. This is why an investor would partner with a creator: the asymmetry that someone can 'just live his life and get a bunch of views' while a competitor 'has to light $100 million on fire to get a bunch of views' is the moat made visible.

The practical implication for builders and investors is to treat distribution and attention not as a downstream marketing afterthought but as the primary asset to accumulate, because it is the input that is becoming structurally harder to manufacture. a16z's own media strategy ('your own propaganda arm pushing out your view of the world') is offered as proof that even a capital-rich incumbent is competing on attention. The claim is contestable — attention can be fickle and capital can still buy a great deal of it — but the directional bet is that in an AI-abundance regime, owned mind-share compounds while undifferentiated capital and intelligence converge toward commodity returns.
