Patents are a deal: you get a temporary monopoly in exchange for publishing your invention so others can build on it after the patent expires. For Coca-Cola, that deal would have been catastrophic. The formula was simple enough that any competent chemist could recreate it from a published patent, and the value of the brand depends on the perception that it's irreplaceable. So Coke chose the opposite path: keep the recipe out of every legal record, store it only in two people's heads, and build elaborate lore around it.

Asa Candler had his son Howard memorize the contents of unlabeled containers in a locked room for days, learning to recognize the ingredients by sight and smell. The formula wasn't even written down until 1919, when Ernest Woodruff needed it as collateral for a bank loan. The secrecy itself became part of the brand's marketing — the idea that there's a vault and two people who can't fly on the same plane is mythology that reinforces uniqueness.

The choice between patent and trade secret comes down to two questions: how easy is it to reverse-engineer from the finished product, and how long do you expect the advantage to be valuable? If reverse engineering is hard and the advantage is durable (recipes, manufacturing processes, training data curation), trade secrets dominate. If reverse engineering is easy or the patent is enforceable against meaningful infringers, patents win. Coke is the canonical case where the trade secret was correct.
