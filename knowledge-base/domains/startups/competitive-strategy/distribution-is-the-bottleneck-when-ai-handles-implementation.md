Müller ran a structured experiment: every engineer at Browser Use had one week to take a new startup from zero to wherever they could get it. Constraints: must automate the entire process; goal is that AI makes money for everyone. Result: 'super simple to create something, create a prompt, create a website... but the distribution was for most the hardest part where it required the most creativity.' Code is no longer the bottleneck (the Lopopolo insight). Marketing, audience-building, and product-market-fit-discovery are. This validates the broader thesis that the constraint on entrepreneurship is shifting from technical execution to distribution/marketing.

Application: when planning a new product or startup, invest disproportionately in distribution strategy, audience-building, and marketing-as-engineering. The MVP doesn't need to be impressive technically — it needs to be discoverable, shareable, and triggering existing community conversations. For Memric specifically: the technical platform (engine, schemas, audit deliverables) is now relatively cheap to build via agents; the distribution channel (Memric Brief Substack, warm intros to FO/VC CIOs) is the actual hard problem and deserves disproportionate investment.
