The proposed California wealth tax includes unrealized gains and values company stakes by voting rights rather than ownership percentage. This means a typical founder who maintains board control despite dilution could be taxed on more wealth than they actually own. Since startup founders routinely hold billions in paper wealth for years before any liquidity event, this would force premature equity sales or restructuring.

The retroactive provision — applying to anyone who was a California resident in January, despite not being voted on until November — compounds the problem by preventing orderly planning. Even if the tax fails at the ballot box or in courts, the threat alone has driven 20 of 21 surveyed California tech billionaires to develop exit plans, potentially causing the very capital flight that reduces state revenue regardless of the measure's outcome.
