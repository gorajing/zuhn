In 1977, after winning the MITS arbitration, Bill Gates immediately licensed Microsoft BASIC to Apple, Commodore, Radio Shack and others — but at remarkably modest prices. The Apple deal was $31,000 for eight years of unlimited access. Gates explicitly was not value-maximizing. His logic: 'we want to make it a no-brainer for everybody who's selling a microcomputer to have Microsoft's BASIC on it.' Once Microsoft BASIC was the standard programming environment, software written by users would only run on Microsoft's interpreter, competitors couldn't catch up, and the position would compound through network effects.

Gates phrased this as 'success reinforces success in a growing market — one way of doing something gets a slight advantage over its competitors. This is most likely to happen with high technology products that can be made in great volume for very little increase in cost.' The slight advantage compounds. The strategic implication: in a brand new category with high fixed costs and near-zero marginal costs, the price you charge is mostly a friction tax against ubiquity. Charging more per deal slows adoption and invites competitors to find a price gap. Charging just enough to fund operations while flooding the market closes the door on competition.

A critical caveat: this only works when you can afford the fixed cost investment with low overhead. Microsoft could run this play because they were five people. Today's equivalent in any meaningful market requires billions in fixed cost — the playbook isn't dead, but the entry cost has changed.
