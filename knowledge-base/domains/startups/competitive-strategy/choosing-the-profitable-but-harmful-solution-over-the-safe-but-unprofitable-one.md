Thomas Midgley's anti-knock additive search illustrates a pattern in competitive strategy: when two solutions exist — one safe but low-margin (ethanol at 10% mixture) and one dangerous but highly profitable (tetraethyl lead at 0.1% concentration) — the profit-maximizing choice creates catastrophic externalities that dwarf the original gains. The Ethyl Corporation generated roughly $3 billion (inflation-adjusted), while the global death toll from lead-related cardiovascular disease alone may approach 100 million.

The key mechanism is delayed feedback. At decision time, the trade-off appears rational: proven efficacy, low cost, easy production, patentable (unlike ethanol). The harm is diffuse, delayed by decades, and distributed across populations rather than concentrated on the decision-maker. Midgley himself suffered lead poisoning but continued promoting the product.

For founders and strategists, this pattern repeats: choosing the expedient-but-harmful path looks rational when externalities are invisible. The antidote is not just ethical reasoning but structural — building feedback loops that make long-term harm visible at decision time. Modern examples include social media engagement optimization, addictive product design, and environmental shortcuts.
