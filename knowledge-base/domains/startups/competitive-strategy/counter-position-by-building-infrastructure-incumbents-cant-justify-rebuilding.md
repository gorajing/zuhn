Barnes & Noble had a vastly larger distribution system than Amazon in 1997—but it was tuned for shipping large batches of books to physical stores. Building Amazon-style fulfillment centers that ship unique one-off orders to individual customers would have required reorganizing their entire operation, diverting their best executives, and knowingly cannibalizing the higher-margin store business. The incentives made it impossible, so they didn't do it. Brad Stone captured this exactly: 'for Amazon that was just daily business.'

The same dynamic played out again with eBay. When Meg Whitman and Pierre Omidyar visited Amazon's fulfillment center in 1998, Pierre thought it was cool. Meg reportedly told him 'warehouses are not cool, we never want to operate warehouses'—because eBay's high-margin asset-light business model would have been destroyed by the capex and operating drag. Both incumbents made locally rational decisions that were strategically fatal.

The operating lesson is that counter-positioning works when your new approach is cheaper or better on some vector the incumbent structurally can't match without destroying their own economics. Don't compete where the incumbent can easily copy you—compete where copying you means they have to fire their best customers or tear up their income statement.
