Paine closes her lecture with Pericles' warning on the eve of Athens' fatal blunders: 'I am more afraid of our own blunders than of the enemy's devices.' Her concrete case is Imperial Japan, which tried the go-it-alone, my-way-or-the-highway strategy against China in World War II — when China was a failed state — and it still wrecked Japan. Today, China has a population multiple of the US and an industrial base multiple of the US, yet some policymakers are contemplating fighting trade wars against allies while simultaneously confronting Beijing. Her verdict: 'Why on earth would you ever want to take them on alone?'

The deeper principle is that alliance systems are force multipliers that compound over time — the US Marshall Plan bought decades of European growth that then bought American exports, a win-win that a purely transactional lens misses. The World War II generation understood this because they'd survived the Depression and two world wars; today's leaders haven't been chastened the same way and are tempted to trade durable alliances for tactical wins.

Applied beyond geopolitics, this is a cross-domain competitive principle. Startups facing incumbents, minority factions fighting majorities, small powers facing bullies — in every case, the math of coalition-building beats solo aggression. Humiliating potential partners to feel good today is 'pointless' because it makes them mad forever. When evaluating any competitive strategy, the first question should be: am I maximizing the growth of my friends and partners, or am I erecting barriers that force them to look for other friends?
