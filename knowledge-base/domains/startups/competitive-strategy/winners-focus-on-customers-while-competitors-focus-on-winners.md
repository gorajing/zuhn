Hormozi argues that competitive focus is fundamentally misallocated attention. When a competitor copies you, they've already lost because they need you as a reference point while you don't need them. Their attention flows toward mimicking your moves rather than listening to their own customers, which is the only data source that drives real differentiation.

The counterintuitive implication is that you should worry when people stop copying you — it means you've stopped producing signals worth following. Rather than viewing imitation as a threat requiring defensive action, treat it as market validation. Hormozi's $25 million mistake came specifically from letting a competitor dictate his behavior, reinforcing that reactive competitive moves are among the costliest strategic errors an entrepreneur can make.
