When OxyContin's patent was expiring, Purdue executed one of the most cynical competitive strategies in pharmaceutical history. After decades of insisting OxyContin was safe and non-addictive, they reversed position and lobbied that the drug was dangerous — specifically that their reformulated version was safer than any generic could be. The FDA agreed in 2013 to block generic OxyContin, while Purdue's version remained available.

This maneuver converted a product liability into a competitive moat. What appeared to be a 'rare noble act' was simply monopoly extension through regulatory capture. The pattern is generalizable: when a company faces both patent expiration and safety criticism, it can weaponize the safety narrative to argue that only the original manufacturer has the expertise to produce the product safely. This is a dark application of the 'regulatory moat' concept — using government power not to protect consumers but to eliminate competition.
