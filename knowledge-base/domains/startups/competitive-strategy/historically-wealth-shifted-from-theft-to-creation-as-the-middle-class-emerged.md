Before the middle class emerged in 10th-11th century European towns, the only two classes were warrior aristocracy and serfs. Serfs had no incentive to create wealth because masters took everything. The middle class changed this by gaining enough independence to keep what they created. But wealth creation didn't definitively replace corruption until the Industrial Revolution, when 'technology had made it possible to create wealth faster than you could steal it.'

This historical pattern matters for evaluating modern economies. Most countries in the world haven't completed this transition — corruption still dominates. When we see increasing income inequality in developed nations, the instinct is to worry about regression toward kleptocracy. But Graham argues the opposite: increasing income variation in a modern democracy signals that wealth creation is working, not that corruption is returning. For startup founders, this means choosing jurisdictions where the creation-to-corruption ratio favors builders.
