Graham observes a continuous transfer of power from dealmakers to technicians — from the charismatic to the competent — over the past century. Where fierceness was once optional in nerds' traditional territory of scholarship (Darwin wasn't particularly fierce), it's now essential for business leadership at scale. The result is that fierce nerds increasingly monopolize the biggest successes.

This has strategic implications for how founders position themselves. The trend suggests that technical depth is becoming more valuable than relationship-building or sales ability as a founder superpower. It also implies that the 'MBA CEO parachuted into a technical company' model will continue losing ground to technical founder-CEOs. For investors, this means pattern-matching on charisma and polish is increasingly a counter-signal — the most valuable founders may be the socially awkward ones with fierce competitive drive.
