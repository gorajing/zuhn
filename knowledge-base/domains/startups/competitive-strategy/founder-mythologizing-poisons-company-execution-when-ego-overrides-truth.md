Jack Dorsey's behavior at Twitter represents an extreme case of founder mythologizing gone toxic. He told Vanity Fair the idea came to him at age 8, told CBS it was inspired by trains and maps, told the LA Times he presented it to childhood classmates in 1984 -- each story more grandiose than the last. He monitored every newspaper article and demanded corrections to include his name as 'the founder.'

This wasn't just vanity -- it was strategic. Jack used his fabricated public persona to build leverage with the board, recruit allies among disgruntled employees, and orchestrate Ev's removal. The pattern repeated with Dick Costolo. The cost to Twitter was enormous: instead of stable leadership focused on monetization and product, the company cycled through four CEOs in a decade, each transition engineered partly by Jack's machinations. The broader lesson is that when a founder's identity becomes entangled with their company's mythology, they'll sacrifice company health to maintain the narrative.
