Paul Graham's Patent Pledge demonstrates a powerful competitive dynamics pattern: when you can't change the rules from the top (government regulation), you can attack the problem downstream by creating voluntary commitments. The key mechanism is that once enough good actors sign on, the pledge becomes a filter — not signing becomes a strong negative signal to employees, investors, and customers.

This works because the pledge costs good actors nothing (they weren't going to sue startups anyway) but creates a binary sorting mechanism. The strategy is generalizable: any industry norm can be shifted by making the desired behavior an explicit, public commitment rather than an implicit expectation. The asymmetry is crucial — the cost of signing is near zero for good actors, but the cost of not signing escalates as adoption grows.
