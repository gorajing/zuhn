Naval identifies an asymmetry in how building and selling skills age. Early in a career, building skills cut through noise because many hustlers lack substance—a track record of actually shipping products is a powerful signal. However, building is exhausting, requires staying current with rapidly changing tools, and faces constant competition from younger builders with more time and newer skills.

Sales skills, by contrast, compound over time. A reputation for being trustworthy and persuasive becomes self-fulfilling—people seek you out based on past interactions. This suggests an optimal career arc: lead with building to establish credibility, then deliberately transition toward selling and relationship-building as your primary value creation mode. The Scott Adams 'skill stack' principle applies—being top-20% at both building and selling beats being top-1% at either alone.
