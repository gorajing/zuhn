Coca-Cola's New Coke disaster is one of the clearest demonstrations that brand equity operates on emotional and identity layers independent of product quality. In blind taste tests, New Coke consistently beat both original Coke and Pepsi. Yet when launched, it triggered massive public outrage — thousands of angry calls daily, protests at stadiums, and letters comparing the change to 'making grass purple.'

The lesson for competitive strategy is that once a brand becomes part of a customer's identity and daily ritual, the product itself becomes secondary. Coca-Cola's century of marketing had made the drink an 'old friend' — a symbol of stability and comfort. Competitors who try to win on product superiority alone miss that deeply entrenched brands create switching costs that are emotional, not rational. This is why Coke spends billions on advertising despite 94% global logo recognition — the image requires constant reinforcement because the product alone cannot sustain the moat.
