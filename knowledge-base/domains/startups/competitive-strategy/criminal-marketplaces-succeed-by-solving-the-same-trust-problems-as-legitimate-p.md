Brett Johnson explains that successful cybercrime requires three things working together: gathering data, committing the crime, and cashing out. No single person can do all three — you need specialists, often across geographic boundaries (e.g., Ukrainians had the data but couldn't cash out in the US). ShadowCrew's breakthrough was creating the institutional trust infrastructure that let these specialists cooperate: review systems, vouching, escrow, communication forums with searchable history, and screen-name reputation.

This mirrors exactly what legitimate two-sided marketplaces must solve. eBay, Airbnb, and Uber all succeeded primarily by solving the trust problem between strangers, not through technical innovation. The lesson for platform builders: trust mechanisms (reviews, identity verification, escrow, dispute resolution) are the core product, not a feature. Johnson notes that before ShadowCrew, criminals could only use IRC chat rooms where 'everyone's a criminal and you can't trust anybody' — the same coordination failure that plagues any nascent marketplace without trust infrastructure.
