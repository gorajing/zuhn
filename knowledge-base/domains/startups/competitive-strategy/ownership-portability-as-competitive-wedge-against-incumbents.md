The essay identifies a classic innovator's dilemma in gaming: large developers benefit from virtual goods lock-in (users who invested hundreds or thousands in Counter-Strike skins can't take them elsewhere), so they have no incentive to enable cross-game portability. Web3's blockchain architecture makes portable ownership technically feasible, but adoption will start with new entrants, not incumbents.

This pattern — where user-friendly features threaten incumbent business models — is a recurring startup strategy template. The essay wisely notes that web3 is 'an evolution, not a revolution' and that the game must be great first. The ownership layer enhances engagement for users who care about it without being the core value proposition. This framing avoids the play-to-earn trap where financial incentives distorted gameplay. The strategic insight: look for features that are better for users but threatening to incumbents' economics.
