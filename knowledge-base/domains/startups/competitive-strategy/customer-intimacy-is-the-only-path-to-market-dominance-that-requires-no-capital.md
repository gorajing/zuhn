Michael Treacy and Fred Wiersema's Harvard Business Review framework identifies three paths to market dominance, each requiring a specific organizational structure: best price (logistics focus, e.g., Walmart/Amazon), best product (R&D focus, e.g., Apple), and best overall solution (customer intimacy, e.g., luxury brands and hospitality). Kevin Hale argues the third path is uniquely suited to startups because it requires almost no money—just humility and good manners.

This explains Wufoo's anomalous success: a 10-person remote team with $118K in total funding competing in a market with well-funded competitors. They couldn't win on price or R&D spend, but they could win on customer intimacy through handwritten thank-you cards, 7-minute support response times, and product delight. The framework also explains why the practice of weekly handwritten thank-you notes wasn't just a nice touch but a strategic moat—it's the kind of unscalable, high-touch behavior that well-funded competitors organized around logistics or R&D cannot replicate without restructuring their entire company.
