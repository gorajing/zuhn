Large organizations treat people as interchangeable parts — a strategy that works for parallelizable tasks but actively undermines programming, which is fundamentally about ideas held in individual minds. The bureaucratic structure (meetings, shared codebases, specs-first processes) prevents any single person from achieving the deep understanding needed for breakthrough work.

This creates a structural blind spot that startups can exploit. By choosing problems that require one person (or a tiny team) to hold the entire solution space in their head — problems that can't be effectively decomposed into interchangeable units — startups compete on a dimension where their smallness is an advantage, not a liability. The implication is that problem selection should factor in cognitive indivisibility as a moat.
