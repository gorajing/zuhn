In the 1980s-90s, the US government actively suppressed cryptography research. The NSA placed secrecy orders on researchers, making it a federal crime to reveal their work. The NSA director wrote to scientific associations demanding they stop hosting cryptography sessions. Researchers had to smuggle code through shirts and tattoos.

This suppression didn't kill the movement — it shaped its architecture. The cypherpunks, radicalized by government overreach, didn't just want to build digital cash; they wanted to build digital cash that governments couldn't stop. Friedrich Hayek's 1984 quote became their north star: 'introduce something that they can't stop.' Bitcoin's core design — decentralized, no servers to shut down, no payments to block — is a direct architectural response to the suppression its predecessors faced. The regulatory pressure didn't prevent the technology; it ensured the eventual version would be specifically hardened against regulatory attack.
