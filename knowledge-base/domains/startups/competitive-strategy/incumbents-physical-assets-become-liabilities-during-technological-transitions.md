Blockbuster had every advantage: $6B valuation, $800M in late fees alone, 60,000 employees, and massive brand recognition. They were even approached by Enron in 1999 to build a streaming service and by Netflix in 2000 to acquire them for just $50M — they rejected both. The core problem wasn't ignorance but structural: Blockbuster's entire business was built on physical real estate, and every store represented a sunk cost that made pivoting to digital feel like self-cannibalization. Their revenue model (late fees, rewind fees) depended on customer friction that Netflix was eliminating. This pattern repeats across industries — Nokia in phones, Kodak in photography. Companies get 'stuck doing things a certain way and are too bloated and bureaucratic to react quickly.' Even Reed Hastings acknowledged: 'If they had started two years earlier they probably would have won.' The lesson isn't that incumbents are stupid — it's that their existing assets create gravitational pull toward the status quo.
