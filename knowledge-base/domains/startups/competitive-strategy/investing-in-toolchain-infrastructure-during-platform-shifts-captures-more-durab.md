Gil's career demonstrates a consistent thesis: when new platforms create new generations of developers, the leverage sits in the toolchain. His first expression was Mixer Labs (geo APIs for early smartphone developers), and the same logic later led to investments in Figma and GitLab. In the AI era, he applies the same lens — while AI-native applications can succeed through distribution, the most resilient value accumulates in infrastructure, observability tools, and the 'middle layers' that sit in the critical path of deployment.

This pattern recurs across every major platform shift. Early mobile rewarded app builders briefly, but the durable winners were the tools and infrastructure layers. The same dynamic is playing out in AI: foundation model costs are dropping, application-layer competition is fierce, but the infrastructure connecting models to production — orchestration, observability, evaluation — becomes increasingly entrenched. For founders, this suggests evaluating where in the stack your product sits and whether it becomes harder or easier to replace as the ecosystem matures.
