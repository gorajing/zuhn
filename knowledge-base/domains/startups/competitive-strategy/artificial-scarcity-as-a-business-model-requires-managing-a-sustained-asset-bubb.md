Patek Philippe's artificial scarcity model reveals the hidden operational complexity behind luxury pricing. Buyers must spend years purchasing lower-tier models at a loss before qualifying for scarce top-tier pieces. Patek actively monitors secondary markets, traces serial numbers, rebuys hundreds of watches annually to identify sellers, and will cut off entire retailers whose customers leak too many pieces.

This is effectively bubble management. Secondary market prices for scarce models must stay above retail or the entire cascade fails — people buying lower-tier watches stop doing so because the aspiration evaporates. A price collapse in the secondary market would function exactly like a bursting asset bubble, with cascading effects across the product line. The CEO effectively controls a 'money supply' of watch production. This model only works when purchase decisions are driven by brand rather than performance — in a normal market, restricting supply just sends customers to competitors.
