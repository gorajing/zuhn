Vine had 200M monthly active users and cultural relevance in 2015, yet was dead by 2017. The proximate cause was a meeting where 18 top creators demanded $1.2 million each per year to continue posting three Vines per week. Twitter (Vine's parent) feared setting a precedent that would lead to people wanting payment for tweets, and Vine was already losing money, so they refused. The creators left, taking their audiences with them.

BytesDance studied this failure carefully and made creator economics a Day 1 priority. They built a creator marketplace connecting brands with creators directly, offered tipping via live streams, let users pay to boost posts, and eventually integrated e-commerce so creators could earn from product sales. The strategic insight is that in content platforms, creators are not just users — they are the supply side of a marketplace. If the supply side can't sustain itself economically, no amount of demand-side growth (viewers) will save the platform. This is why YouTube survived (AdSense) while Vine didn't.
