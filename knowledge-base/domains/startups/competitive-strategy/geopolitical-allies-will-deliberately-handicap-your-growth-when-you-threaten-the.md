The US initially helped Japan build its export economy by setting the yen artificially low, then reversed course with the Plaza Accord when Japanese manufacturers began dominating American markets. Japan agreed to strengthen its own currency — effectively a self-imposed handicap on its exporters — partly to preserve its alliance with the US, partly due to internal pressure, and partly hoping it would force beneficial structural reforms.

The yen appreciated far more than anyone predicted, triggering the panic that led to the cheap-credit policy that inflated the bubble. This sequence illustrates a pattern relevant to any entity (company or country) growing rapidly within an ecosystem controlled by a more powerful ally: the ally will tolerate your growth only until it threatens their position, then use structural leverage to slow you down. The lesson for competitive strategy is that dependence on a single dominant partner for market access creates existential vulnerability regardless of how strong the relationship appears.
