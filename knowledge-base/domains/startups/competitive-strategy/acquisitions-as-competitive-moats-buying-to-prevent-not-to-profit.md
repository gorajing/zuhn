WhatsApp generated just $1.2M in revenue in the nine months before Facebook's $19B acquisition — a price-to-revenue ratio that baffled analysts. But the acquisition logic wasn't about WhatsApp's revenue; it was about preventing a competitor (Google was in talks) from owning a billion-user messaging platform and about absorbing those users into Facebook's data graph.

This reveals how platform monopolies think about competition: the cost of losing a billion users to a rival far exceeds $19B when your business model monetizes attention and data at scale. The acquisition price is justified by the defensive value (what you'd lose if a competitor got it) rather than the offensive value (what you'll earn from it). This framework — pricing acquisitions by competitive threat rather than target revenue — explains many otherwise irrational-seeming tech acquisitions.
