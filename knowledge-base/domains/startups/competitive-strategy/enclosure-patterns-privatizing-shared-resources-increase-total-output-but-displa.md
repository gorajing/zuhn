The British enclosure movement of the 16th-18th centuries privatized agricultural land that had been held in common by multiple tenants for centuries. The result was dramatically increased agricultural productivity — but at the cost of displacing tenant farmers who lost their livelihoods. This is one of history's clearest examples of a pattern that recurs in technology: aggregate value increases while the distribution of that value concentrates.

Modern 'enclosure' patterns are visible across tech: Uber enclosed the taxi commons (anyone could hail a cab) into a platform-controlled marketplace. Amazon enclosed the retail commons (distributed storefronts) into a centralized platform. Social media enclosed the attention commons (distributed blogs and forums) into algorithmic feeds. In each case, total transaction volume and efficiency increased while the original participants — drivers, retailers, content creators — lost autonomy and often economic ground. For startup strategists, recognizing enclosure dynamics early reveals both the opportunity (aggregate productivity gains) and the political risk (displacement backlash that eventually triggers regulation).
