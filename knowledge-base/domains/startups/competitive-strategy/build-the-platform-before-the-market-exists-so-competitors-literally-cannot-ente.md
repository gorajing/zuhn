Nvidia committed to CUDA in 2006 with no identifiable market. Scientific computing, the nominal target, was maybe a couple billion dollars total — not nearly enough to justify the 1,100+ engineers eventually dedicated to the platform. For six years it generated essentially no revenue, and the stock got crushed 80% in 2008 partly because of it. The bet only paid off because AlexNet in 2012 accidentally proved deep learning worked on GPUs, and CUDA was the only mature stack available when researchers arrived.

The strategic insight is that platform bets have to precede their markets by years, because the platform itself is what enables the market. If Nvidia had waited for the deep learning gold rush before building CUDA, they would have arrived with nothing to sell. Jensen reframed 'build it and they will come' as 'if we don't build it, they literally cannot come' — a subtle but critical distinction that justifies patience through multiple drawdown cycles.

This is the opposite of lean startup logic. Lean startup says validate demand before building. Platform strategy says build the enabling infrastructure before demand is validateable, because the infrastructure is what makes the demand possible. The cost is enormous and failure is likely — but the only way to own a generational platform is to be the one willing to make the bet when no rational analyst can justify it.
