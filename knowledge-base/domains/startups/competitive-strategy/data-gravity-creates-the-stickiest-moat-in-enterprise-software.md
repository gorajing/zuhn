The database market is $100B growing at 10%, and databases are the stickiest software category ever built. But the real lock-in isn't the software — it's the data itself. The internet's bandwidth has not scaled anywhere close to the rate data storage has grown, which is why AWS ships physical 'Snowmobile' trucks to migrate exabytes of data. Even with a semi truck full of storage, large migrations take six months; over the network they'd take years or decades.

The implication for moats: once you're the system of record for a customer's data, you also become the default place they run their analytics, their ML, their applications, and their backups. Google may have built the best ML tools in the world, but it loses the ML inference business by default because ML runs where the data lives. AWS knew this, which is why early S3 pricing was so aggressive — every GB stored was a future decade of compute, database, and networking revenue.

When building any data-heavy product, the strategic question is: am I becoming the system of record? If yes, the adjacent markets will come to you for free. If no, you're a feature someone else's platform will eventually absorb.
