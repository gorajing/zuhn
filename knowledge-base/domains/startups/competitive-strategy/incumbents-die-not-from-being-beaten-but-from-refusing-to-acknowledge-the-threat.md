BlackBerry's decline is a case study in the incumbency trap: the belief that market leadership in one era confers competitive advantage in the next. When the iPhone launched, BlackBerry leadership interpreted it as a product for consumers, not enterprise — a segmentation error that bought them years of false comfort while their user base eroded beneath them.

The more generalizable pattern is that disruption rarely looks threatening from inside the incumbent's frame of reference. The new entrant is usually worse on the metrics the incumbent optimizes for, and better on metrics the incumbent doesn't yet track. BlackBerry optimized for email efficiency and keyboard ergonomics; Apple introduced a new metric — the richness of the software ecosystem — that BlackBerry wasn't measuring and couldn't quickly replicate.

For founders building in markets with large incumbents, this is useful: find the dimension where they're not measuring, and win there first. For leaders of established products, the discipline is regular competitive analysis that specifically looks for threats on dimensions you're not currently optimizing.
