Carroll explains that speciation — the creation of genuinely new species — requires geographic or reproductive isolation. Island biology showed Darwin and Wallace that separated populations accumulate unique mutations over time until they become genetically incompatible. For large animals, this takes roughly 2 million years.

The modern understanding adds nuance: species barriers are more porous than previously thought (Homo sapiens interbred with Neanderthals). This maps to innovation strategy: true differentiation requires some isolation from the mainstream to accumulate enough unique capabilities. But barriers are porous — you can still cross-pollinate. The insight is that evolution is a splitting process, not a linear replacement. New branches don't eliminate old ones.
