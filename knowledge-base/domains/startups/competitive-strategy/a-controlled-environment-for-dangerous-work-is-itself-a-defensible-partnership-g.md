Myshatyn describes a counterintuitive partnership dynamic: the most richly funded AI frontier labs — which 'really can't afford to hire anyone they want' — still seek out Los Alamos as a source of data and partnership. Why? Because the lab is 'a safe place to do dangerous things,' and 'we've been doing that for decades.' Concrete example: chem/bio safety work done with OpenAI, the kind of dangerous experimentation that a commercial lab cannot responsibly or legally run on its own.

The defensible asset isn't capital or talent in the conventional sense — frontier labs have more of both. It's the combination of (a) a controlled, secured environment licensed and equipped to handle hazardous or classified work, (b) petabytes of unique data that has never touched and never will touch the internet, and (c) deep subject-matter expertise in chem/bio, materials physics, and cybersecurity. These are slow-to-build, hard-to-replicate, and in some cases legally impossible to recreate commercially.

The strategic lesson generalizes beyond national labs: when you control an environment or asset that lets others do something they cannot do safely, legally, or at all on their own, you become an essential partner regardless of resource asymmetry. The value exchange — early model access in return for unique data and a safe testbed — is structurally durable because the other side can't buy their way around it.
