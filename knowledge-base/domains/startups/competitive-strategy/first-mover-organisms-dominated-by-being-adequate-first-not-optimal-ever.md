Photosynthesis is explicitly described as 'complicated, inefficient and confusing' — yet photosynthetic organisms conquered the planet. They didn't win by being optimal; they won by being adequate 450 million years ago, then literally changed the atmosphere (flooding it with oxygen) in ways that made their own enzyme (RuBisCo) work worse but simultaneously created barriers for competitors.

This maps directly to platform competition: early adequate platforms (TCP/IP, JavaScript, QWERTY) reshape their environments — building ecosystems, training workforces, establishing standards — in ways that make them harder to displace even as their flaws become more apparent. The strategic implication is that in winner-take-all markets, speed to adequate beats speed to optimal, and the most powerful moats aren't built deliberately but emerge as side effects of scale (like oxygen was a 'useless byproduct' that became essential).
