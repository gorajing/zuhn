Lane describes how for 2+ billion years, life existed in a 'gentle but limited world' of filter feeders and passive organisms. The Cambrian explosion — the most creative burst in evolutionary history — was driven by predation, not by new resources becoming available. When organisms started eating each other, the resulting arms race produced eyes (evolvable in ~500,000 years), shells, locomotion, and nervous systems.

The parallel to markets is direct: companies in non-competitive niches (like pre-Cambrian filter feeders) optimize for efficiency but rarely innovate dramatically. Markets with aggressive competitors who can kill your business (true predators, not just resource competitors) produce the fastest innovation. Lane notes that evolution driven by environmental scarcity alone produces bacterial-level optimization — impressive but limited. The 'death penalty' of predation is what forces genuinely novel solutions. This suggests that founders should sometimes prefer markets with dangerous competitors over comfortable niches, because the competitive pressure itself is the innovation engine.
