Costco's radical curation — roughly 4,000 unique items compared to 30,000 at a typical supermarket or 140,000 at Walmart — creates a counterintuitive competitive advantage. By offering only one or two choices per category, suppliers compete intensely for those limited slots, willingly lowering prices and even reengineering products (like the cashew container redesign that saved 24,000 pallets annually).

This is a constraint-as-leverage play: less selection means more volume per SKU, which means more negotiating power per supplier relationship. It also simplifies operations — stocking, logistics, and customer decision-making all become easier. The insight for product builders is that deliberate constraint on offerings can generate more value than exhaustive breadth, especially when the constraint concentrates purchasing power.
