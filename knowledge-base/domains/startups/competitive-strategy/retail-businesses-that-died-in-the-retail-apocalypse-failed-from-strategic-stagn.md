The 'retail apocalypse' narrative attributes mass store closures to Amazon, but the companies that actually collapsed — Sears, Toys-R-Us, J.C. Penney, Payless ShoeSource, RadioShack — shared a different common factor: they failed to evolve their value proposition. Sears tried to be everything and executed nothing well. Toys-R-Us treated e-commerce as a fad. J.C. Penney lost touch with its customer base.

Meanwhile, retailers like Ross, Aldi, Dollar General, Lush, and Costco thrived in the same environment. The differentiator wasn't resistance to Amazon — it was strategic clarity and willingness to adapt. Attributing failure to a powerful competitor is a self-exculpating narrative that obscures the internal decisions that actually caused the decline.

For founders and strategists: when incumbents in an industry collapse, resist the temptation to identify a single external cause. Disruption usually reveals pre-existing weakness. The more useful diagnostic is: what did the survivors do differently? Their divergence from the casualties explains far more than the disruptor's existence.
