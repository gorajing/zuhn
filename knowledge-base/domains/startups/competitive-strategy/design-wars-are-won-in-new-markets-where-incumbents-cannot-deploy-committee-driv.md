Large companies deliberately decrease the standard deviation of design outcomes by separating design (product managers) from implementation (programmers). This protects them from disasters but also eliminates the possibility of breakthroughs. When you damp oscillations, you lose the highs along with the lows. Big companies don't win by making great products — they win by sucking less than other big companies.

The strategic implication is that startups should seek new markets where no fortifications exist, because engaging an incumbent inside their established monopoly is like fighting someone inside a castle. In new markets, the startup's ability to have brilliant individuals both design and implement gives them a quality advantage that committee-driven competitors structurally cannot match. Microsoft, Apple, and HP all won this way initially.
