Consumer electronics companies face a brutal treadmill: each generation must be compelling enough to justify full-price purchase, but R&D costs are front-loaded and each release devalues the previous generation. GoPro tried to set the tempo with 9 generations in 10 years, but improvements became trivial and the use case never evolved beyond extreme sports.

The survivors all diversified. Nikon's cameras are now just a third of revenue -- semiconductors, healthcare, and 3D printing carry the rest. Canon's core business is actually printers (50%+ of revenue), not cameras. Even Apple used iPod profits to fund iPhone development. Kodak's cautionary tale is the inverse: they refused to diversify away from film, and when digital photos went mainstream, their entire business became obsolete. GoPro tried diversifying into media and drones but executed poorly. Insta360 succeeded by expanding from 360 cameras into action cameras and broader content creation tools -- following markets rather than forcing them.
