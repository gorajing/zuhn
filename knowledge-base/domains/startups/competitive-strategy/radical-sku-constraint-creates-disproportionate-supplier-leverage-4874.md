Conventional retail wisdom holds that more selection attracts more customers. Costco deliberately violates this by offering roughly 4,000 unique items compared to a typical supermarket's 30,000 or Walmart Supercenter's 140,000. The counterintuitive result is that constraining selection concentrates enormous purchasing volume into each chosen product, making Costco one of the largest buyers for any supplier it works with.

This concentrated demand transforms the power dynamic: suppliers compete fiercely for the one or two slots in each category, accepting lower prices and even reengineering packaging (e.g., redesigning cashew containers to save 24,000 pallets annually). The constraint also simplifies operations—fewer SKUs means simpler stocking, less decision fatigue for shoppers, and the credible threat of creating a Kirkland Signature alternative if suppliers don't cooperate. The lesson generalizes: deliberate scarcity of shelf space can be more powerful than abundance.
