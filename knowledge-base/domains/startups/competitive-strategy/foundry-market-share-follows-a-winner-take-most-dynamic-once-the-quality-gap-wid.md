The TSMC vs Samsung foundry dynamic illustrates a winner-take-most pattern in precision manufacturing. TSMC's superior yields and fewer defects secured contracts with Apple, Nvidia, and AMD — the highest-volume, highest-margin customers. That revenue funds further process improvements, which widens the quality gap, which attracts more clients. Samsung is caught in the inverse flywheel: lower yields mean fewer premium contracts, less revenue for R&D, and a growing technical deficit.

This pattern extends beyond semiconductors to any industry where quality differences compound through reinvestment cycles — ASML in lithography, Boeing vs Airbus in widebody aircraft, or even cloud providers in AI infrastructure. The lesson for competitive strategy is that in manufacturing-intensive markets, closing a quality gap requires disproportionate investment relative to the gap size, because the leader's flywheel accelerates while you're catching up.
