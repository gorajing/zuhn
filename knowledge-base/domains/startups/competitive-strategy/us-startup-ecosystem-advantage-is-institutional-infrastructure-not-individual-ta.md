When asked whether founders need to be in the US, Lubetzky identified four structural advantages: rule of law, the ability to fail and try again, freedom of expression, and a financial system that rewards ambition. Notably, he omitted commonly cited factors like talent density, university ecosystems, or network effects — focusing instead on institutional infrastructure that takes decades or centuries to build.

This framing explains why copying Silicon Valley consistently fails. Countries can replicate accelerators, venture funds, and co-working spaces within years. But establishing genuine rule of law, cultural acceptance of failure, and deep capital markets that reward risk-taking requires generational institutional development. These are the load-bearing walls of the ecosystem, not the furniture.

For founders making geographic decisions, this suggests evaluating ecosystems by their institutional maturity rather than their surface-level startup activity. A city with strong courts, bankruptcy protection, and deep financial markets but few startups may be a better long-term bet than a buzzy ecosystem built on government subsidies and artificial clustering without the institutional foundations.
