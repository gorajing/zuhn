In 2005 and 2015 war games, Swedish and French submarines respectively penetrated US carrier strike group defenses and reached positions where they could have launched lethal torpedo strikes. These exercises exposed a critical vulnerability: despite billions in defensive systems, a relatively inexpensive submarine could theoretically destroy the most powerful single military asset in existence.

This maps directly to business strategy. Incumbents who concentrate value in a single dominant asset (a flagship product, a key platform, a star employee) create a target for asymmetric disruption. The disruptor doesn't need to match the incumbent's total investment — they just need one vector the incumbent underestimates. Startups should both exploit this dynamic when attacking incumbents and guard against it when they themselves become the concentrated asset. The carrier paradox is that the more powerful and expensive the asset becomes, the more catastrophic and attractive its loss becomes to adversaries.
