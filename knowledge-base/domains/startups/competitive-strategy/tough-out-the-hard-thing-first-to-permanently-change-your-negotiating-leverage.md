Most retailers accept credit cards from day one because they're afraid customers won't shop without them. That assumption locks in 2-3% of every transaction as interchange forever. Costco started cash-and-check only, partly because their wholesale-club origin made it natural and partly because Sol Price's discipline forbid paying for things customers would do anyway. After years of demonstrated demand, Costco knew the counter-factual — customers come whether or not you take Visa — which let them hold an auction among Amex, then Citi/Visa, with the card network paying Costco for the privilege rather than the other way around.

The pattern generalizes far beyond payment processing. Doing the hard thing first — operating without a feature that 'everyone says you need' — gives you proof that your value proposition stands on its own. That proof is the only thing that flips a vendor relationship from 'we need them' to 'they need us.' If you accept the feature on day one, you never get to run the experiment, and you pay the toll forever.

The corollary: any time a vendor tells you 'every business in your category needs this,' the question to ask is 'what would I learn by going without it for 12 months?' If the answer is 'whether my business actually requires it,' the experiment is probably worth running.
