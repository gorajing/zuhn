For the first decade, IKEA was a pure aggregator — a catalog agent sourcing furniture from independent Swedish makers and drop-shipping it. The core business was thin-margin arbitrage. Then competitors, furious that IKEA was commoditizing the category, pressured suppliers into boycotting IKEA, lobbied the government to restrict its catalogs, and blocked it from trade fairs. In a weaker company, this kills the business. In IKEA, it forced Kamprad to go to manufacturers with his own designs — which unlocked flat pack (you can't flat pack someone else's furniture), in-house brand identity, and eventually Polish manufacturing (1961) to escape the Swedish supplier cartel entirely.

The pattern generalizes: a competitive squeeze against your current model is often the thing that pushes you into the model that turns out to be the real business. The response that looks like a desperate workaround becomes the structural advantage. Netflix was forced into streaming because studios jacked DVD licensing fees. Amazon built AWS because it needed to solve its own infra pain at internal margins competitors couldn't match.

To apply: when competitors or suppliers collude against you, resist the instinct to negotiate your way back into the old arrangement. Ask instead: what does this block force me to build? The forced-build is usually the answer that wasn't in your strategy deck but should have been.
