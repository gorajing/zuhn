Nvidia launched as the first dedicated 3D graphics card company and immediately made two first-mover bets that blew up: they picked quadrilaterals as their polygon primitive (Microsoft's DirectX standardized on triangles eighteen months later), and they designed for expensive memory (Moore's law dropped component memory costs from $200 to $50 by the time competitors shipped). Both decisions were defensible given 1993 information but catastrophic given 1996 reality.

The generalizable lesson is that in markets driven by exponential cost curves and emerging standards, being first means committing capital against assumptions that will be falsified by the time you ship. Competitors who fund eighteen months later get to design against the real cost structure and the real standards. Jensen later drew the explicit lesson: 'premeditate the exponential curve of Moore's law and design for things that are 2-3-4 generations beyond what we actually have available' — meaning first-movers must forecast forward, not design for now.

This reframes venture timing in commodity-trajectory markets: the optimal entry is late enough to read the standards and cost curves but with founders capable of shipping faster than incumbents. Racing to be first in such markets is rewarded only if the team has the resilience to survive their own initial assumptions being wrong.
