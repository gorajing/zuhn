Thiel identifies a critical pattern: monopolies and perfectly competitive businesses appear similar from the outside because both systematically misrepresent their market size, but in opposite directions. Google describes itself as a 'technology company' competing in a $1 trillion market (3.5% share) rather than a search engine with 66% dominance. Meanwhile, a restaurant in Palo Alto claims to be 'the only British food restaurant in Palo Alto' — an intersection so narrow it's fictitious.

The practical test is set-theoretic: if a company describes its market as the intersection of multiple categories ('mobile social local apps'), it's likely a non-monopolist trying to appear unique. If it describes its market as the union of many categories, it's likely a monopolist trying to appear competitive. Founders should use this framework to honestly assess their own position: are you defining your market as an intersection (dangerous self-deception) or do you genuinely dominate a real, if small, category?
