When Patrick Doyle became CEO after Domino's stock hit $3, he conducted focus groups that brutally criticized their pizza. Instead of quietly reformulating, Domino's ran a massive campaign showing actual footage of customers trashing their product. They publicly admitted the old recipe was bad, documented the chefs rebuilding the pizza from scratch, and had chefs personally deliver new pizzas to their harshest critics on camera.

This radical transparency was risky — they were a well-established chain admitting their core product had deteriorated. But people respected the honesty, tried the improved pizza, and became advocates. The campaign generated over a billion media impressions. Domino's stock went from under $3 to over $400, outpacing the S&P 500 and most tech companies. The lesson: when your product genuinely has problems, transparent admission plus visible action creates more trust than silent improvement ever could.
