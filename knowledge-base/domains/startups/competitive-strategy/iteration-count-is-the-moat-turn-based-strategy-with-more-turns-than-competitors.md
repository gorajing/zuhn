Asked why Meta has survived nine existential threats (MySpace, Twitter, Instagram pre-acquisition, Snapchat, WhatsApp, TikTok, ATT, ChatGPT, etc.), Zuckerberg's answer is structural: Meta is a technology company optimized for iteration speed, not a single product. His stated strategy is 'we can learn faster than every other company, so we're going to win.' The competitive advantage isn't insight — it's cycle count.

The operational implication is that you must ship before you're ready. Zuckerberg explicitly says many internal product conversations land at 'we could make this much better in another two months,' and his answer is to ship anyway, because waiting for praise costs you a learning cycle that the next ship would have produced. The downside risk — small brand damage from rough releases — is outweighed by the upside of compounded learning. This is the opposite of Apple's model, which Mark acknowledges also works, but for different reasons (closed ecosystem, premium positioning).

The key prerequisite is engineering depth across the management team. If only one head-of-engineering on your management team is technical, you can't iterate fast enough to make this strategy work. The 'turn-based game with more turns' approach requires that your organization can actually convert talk into shipped product faster than competitors — which means being culturally and structurally a technology company, not just one that uses technology.
