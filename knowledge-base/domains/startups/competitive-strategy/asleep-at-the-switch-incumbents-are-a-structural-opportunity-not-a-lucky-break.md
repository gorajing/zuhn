Paine points out that Britain and the other great powers 'could have weighed in on the scale at any point in these things to alter outcomes and they didn't.' This isn't a story about British failure — it's about how threat perception actually works in dominant players. Incumbents evaluate threats one at a time, against a threshold of 'is this worth the cost of response,' and each challenger move is calibrated to fall just below that threshold. The cumulative trajectory is invisible to an actor that only asks 'is this specific move a problem?'

This is the structural reason disruption works. It's not that incumbents are stupid or blind — they're applying a locally rational threshold test that systematically fails against a challenger who understands the test. Each of Bismarck's wars, each of Amazon's category expansions, each of Tesla's model launches passed the 'not worth intervening' check, until suddenly the balance was gone.

For a challenger, the implication is that the window of incumbent tolerance is wider than it feels, but it's also finite. You get to make many small moves before the incumbent's perception updates, but once it does, the response is sudden and severe. The art is staying under the threshold until you have enough accumulated position that intervention is no longer feasible.
