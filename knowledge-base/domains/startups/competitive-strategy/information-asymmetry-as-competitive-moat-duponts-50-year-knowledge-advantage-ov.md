DuPont's internal research program on C8/PFOA created a massive information asymmetry. By 1961, they knew C8 caused liver abnormalities in rats. By 1962, they confirmed lethal effects. By the mid-1970s, their own workers showed liver disease and contamination levels 1,000x the general population. By the early 1980s, they confirmed C8 caused testicular tumors in rats. Yet none of these studies were shared externally. When they discovered their landfill wastewater contained 1,600 parts per billion of C8 — against their own internal safety threshold of 1 ppb — they told no one. This information advantage persisted for roughly 50 years. The structural lesson: when companies are the primary funders of safety research on their own products, the incentive to suppress unfavorable findings creates a predictable pattern of concealment. External researchers didn't independently discover widespread blood contamination until 1975, and even then 3M denied responsibility. The $600 million eventual settlement was trivial relative to DuPont's $80 billion annual revenue.
