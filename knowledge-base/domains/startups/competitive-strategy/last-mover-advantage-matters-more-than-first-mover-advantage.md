Thiel calculated that in March 2001, with PayPal growing 100% annually and discounting at 30%, approximately 75% of the company's value came from cash flows in 2011 and beyond. The same math applies to any emerging tech company: the vast majority of value exists far in the future. This means the question 'will this company still be the leader in 10-20 years?' dominates the value equation.

The implication is that 'last mover advantage' trumps 'first mover advantage.' Microsoft was the last operating system for decades, Google the last search engine, and Facebook (potentially) the last social network. The first mover advantage in chess is about one-third of a pawn — a small edge. What matters is who wins the endgame. Capablanca's principle applies: 'you must begin by studying the end game.' This reframes due diligence from 'how fast is it growing?' to 'why will this monopoly be durable?' — examining whether network effects strengthen over time, whether proprietary technology can be superseded, and whether economies of scale compound.
