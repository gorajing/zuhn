Rockefeller's career featured a consistent pattern: every economic downturn accelerated his dominance. During the 1873 depression (which lasted 6 years), Standard Oil was one of the only profitable refiners thanks to modernized facilities, strong bank credit, and careful financial management. While competitors faced bankruptcy, Rockefeller launched a nationwide buyout campaign, acquiring refineries in Pittsburgh, Philadelphia, and New York at distressed prices.

He repeated this in the 1893 depression, again prospering while 600 banks closed. The prerequisite was maintaining a fortress balance sheet during good times — keeping costs low, maintaining bank relationships, and modernizing operations. The strategic insight is that market dominance often isn't won during booms (when everyone is flush) but during busts (when only the strongest survive). This requires counter-intuitive discipline: saving aggressively during prosperity specifically to deploy capital during downturns.
