Graham notes that 'real standards don't have to be promoted' — C, Unix, and HTML all became dominant without marketing campaigns, while Java required enormous corporate hype from Sun Microsystems. The need for aggressive promotion signals that a technology can't win on its own merits.

This pattern holds across technology and startups more broadly. Products that spread through word-of-mouth among practitioners have passed a genuine quality filter. Heavy marketing can generate initial adoption but creates a fragile user base of people who chose it for extrinsic reasons (funding pressure, management mandates, resume building) rather than intrinsic love. The 'nobody loves it' test — whether passionate practitioners voluntarily choose your technology — remains one of the most reliable predictors of long-term success.
