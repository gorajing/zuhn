The Sassanian and Byzantine empires had been fighting each other for roughly 300 years when the early Islamic forces emerged. The Sassanians were 'super tired' and had been struck by plague. This mutual exhaustion created a strategic window that a smaller, more unified force could exploit.

This pattern recurs in competitive markets: two dominant incumbents locked in prolonged rivalry deplete each other's resources, talent, and strategic flexibility. A new entrant that arrives with a unified value proposition and lower internal friction can capture territory disproportionate to its size. The key insight is timing — the window opens not when one incumbent is weak, but when both are simultaneously depleted from fighting each other.
