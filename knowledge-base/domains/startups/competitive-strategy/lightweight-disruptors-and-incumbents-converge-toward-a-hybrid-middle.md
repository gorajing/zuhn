Rather than one side winning outright, Graham predicted mutual convergence: super-angels would grow their fund sizes and deal sizes upward, while VCs would develop processes for making more, smaller, faster investments. This convergence pattern—where disruptors scale up and incumbents adopt disruptor tactics—appears across industries (fintech vs. banks, streaming vs. cable, DTC vs. retail). The 16 years since this essay have largely confirmed the prediction: the line between seed funds and early-stage VCs is now thoroughly blurred. For founders and strategists, this pattern suggests that disruption rarely produces permanent new categories. Instead, it forces structural adaptation from both sides, with survivors from each camp converging on the same hybrid model. The competitive advantage window for the disruptor is temporary—measured in years, not decades.
