In 1999, after losing Qualcomm's foundry business to TSMC, IBM approached TSMC to co-develop the 0.13-micron node, positioning itself as the senior partner. Chang refused without deliberation. IBM then went to UMC (TSMC's smaller Taiwanese rival), which accepted — and, in Chang's words, 'regretted seriously their acceptance.' UMC never recovered its independent process lead.

The reasoning is structural, not relational. Co-development means sending your engineers to the partner's fabs, absorbing their culture and tooling, and eventually depending on their process roadmap. You stop building the internal muscle required to develop the next node on your own. Cultural mismatch and coordination friction guarantee the joint effort will be slower and messier than a solo effort. Meanwhile the senior partner retains all the learning for itself. What looks like a way to access advanced technology is actually a mechanism for transferring your process-development capability to someone else.

The broader principle applies wherever process power is the moat — not just semiconductors. If your competitive advantage is that you got better than competitors by iterating your own process thousands of times, outsourcing or co-owning that process destroys the very loop that made you good. Chang's confidence here came from having seen IBM's foundry business collapse from the foundry side: he watched Qualcomm move away from IBM and knew IBM was in trouble, which told him the co-development ask was really a plea. Sitting in the foundry position gave TSMC a unique vantage point to see competitors weakening before anyone else could.
