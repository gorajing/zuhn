Fossil evidence shows foragers had healthier bones and teeth and worked fewer hours than early farmers. Yet agriculture was independently adopted across every inhabited continent. The reason: farming's 'worse' individual outcomes were offset by its ability to generate caloric surplus, which unlocked specialization of labor, which enabled toolmakers, which improved farming efficiency in a compounding loop.

This pattern recurs in technology adoption. The individually worse but systemically superior solution wins. Early internet was worse than libraries for research quality, but its surplus of accessible information enabled specialization cascades (e-commerce, social media, SaaS) that libraries never could. Founders should evaluate technologies not by whether they improve the current task, but by whether they generate surplus that enables new categories of work.
