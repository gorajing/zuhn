When the Franco-Prussian War destroyed Louis Vuitton's workshop in 1871, he could have rebuilt in the same location. Instead, he exploited the post-war real estate vacuum — many shops sat empty — to relocate to a wealthy aristocratic district next to the railway station and Grand Hotel. This put his brand directly in the path of affluent travelers, his ideal customers.

The principle extends beyond luxury: catastrophic disruption creates a temporary market dislocation where premium positioning becomes accessible at discount prices. Founders who rebuild 'in place' miss this window. The strategic move is to treat total loss not as a setback to recover from but as a forced hand to reposition. Vuitton's post-war location upgrade likely did more for his brand trajectory than a decade of organic growth in the original spot would have.
