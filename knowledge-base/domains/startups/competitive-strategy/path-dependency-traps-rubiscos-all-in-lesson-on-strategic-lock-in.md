RuBisCo is the enzyme that fixes carbon in photosynthesis, and it evolved when atmospheric CO2 was abundant and O2 was scarce. When plants themselves changed the atmosphere by producing oxygen, RuBisCo started confusing O2 for CO2 roughly half the time, creating a toxic byproduct (phosphoglycolate) that plants must spend additional energy breaking down.

Plants had already 'gone all-in' on the RuBisCo strategy — their entire metabolic architecture depended on it. Switching to a better enzyme would require redesigning the Calvin Cycle itself. So instead, plants evolved elaborate workaround pathways (C4, CAM photosynthesis) to manage RuBisCo's failures. This mirrors how companies locked into legacy architectures spend more on compatibility shims and workarounds than a rewrite would cost, but can never justify the rewrite because the existing system 'works.' The lesson: delaying commitment until the competitive landscape stabilizes can prevent permanent lock-in to suboptimal strategies.
