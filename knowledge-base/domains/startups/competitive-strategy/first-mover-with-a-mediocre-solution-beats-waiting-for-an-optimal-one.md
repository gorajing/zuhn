RuBisCo, the enzyme responsible for all carbon fixation in photosynthesis, is remarkably bad at its job — it confuses oxygen for CO2 roughly half the time, producing toxic byproducts plants must then clean up. Yet it became the universal standard because it was the first enzyme that could convert inorganic carbon to organic carbon, and early organisms simply made huge quantities of it to compensate for its inefficiency.

This maps directly to startup strategy: the first product to solve a real problem — even poorly — often becomes the entrenched standard. Users build workflows, integrations, and mental models around it. By the time a theoretically superior alternative appears, switching costs (biological or economic) make displacement nearly impossible. The lesson isn't that quality doesn't matter, but that solving a real problem first creates compounding advantages that elegance alone cannot overcome.
