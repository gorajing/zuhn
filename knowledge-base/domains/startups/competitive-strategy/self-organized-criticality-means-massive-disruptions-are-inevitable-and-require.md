Forest fires follow a power law: most burn a few trees, some burn acres, and occasionally one burns 1.4 million acres (the 1988 Yellowstone fire — 70x the previous record). The key insight from self-organized criticality is that there was nothing special about the 1988 fire's trigger — it was the same lightning strike as always. What made it a megafire was the state of the forest: decades of fire suppression had created dense, interconnected fuel loads.

The system naturally drives itself to criticality through a feedback loop: trees grow, density increases, connectivity rises, until a single spark can cascade everywhere. Then the fire resets the system and the cycle begins again. This exact pattern appears in financial markets (credit buildup leading to crashes), technology (gradual platform lock-in leading to sudden disruption), and organizations (accumulated technical debt leading to system failures). The counterintuitive lesson: suppressing small failures (the US Forest Service's 10 AM policy) doesn't prevent large failures — it guarantees them by letting the system reach maximum criticality. The correct strategy is to allow and even encourage small, controlled disruptions.
