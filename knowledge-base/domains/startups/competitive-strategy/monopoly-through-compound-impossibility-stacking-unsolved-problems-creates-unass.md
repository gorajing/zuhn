ASML's EUV machine is the most complicated commercial product ever built, costing $400M per unit. Its monopoly doesn't come from any single breakthrough but from the compounding of dozens of engineering challenges that each seemed impossible on their own. The mirrors must be atomically smooth (if scaled to Germany, the largest bump would be 1mm). The laser must hit 50,000 tin droplets per second without a single miss. The light source must generate 13.5nm wavelength photons from plasma at 220,000 Kelvin.

This illustrates a powerful competitive strategy: when your product requires solving N interdependent 'impossible' problems, the barrier to entry isn't N times harder — it's combinatorially harder. Each solved problem depends on the others. ASML spent 30+ years and billions assembling this capability across multiple organizations (Zeiss for mirrors, ASML for sources). Any competitor would need to replicate not just the technology but the institutional knowledge accumulated over decades. This is the ultimate moat — not patents or network effects, but compound impossibility.
