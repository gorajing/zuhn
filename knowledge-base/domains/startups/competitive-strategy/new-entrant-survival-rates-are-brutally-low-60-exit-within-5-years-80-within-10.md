The entry and exit research reveals several stylized facts: (1) 30-45 firms per 100 in an industry will enter over 5 years, and a similar number will exit, creating 30-45% turnover; (2) new entrants are about 30% the size of existing firms; (3) entry rates are higher in profitable industries, exit rates higher in mature/declining ones; (4) entry and exit rates are highly correlated across sectors (r=0.93). About 80% of U.S. establishments survive one year but only 50% survive five years.

The rate of entrance into value-creating industries has actually declined in the 2000s versus the late 1900s, yet scholars find that challengers remain overconfident, neglect high base rates of failure, and are drawn to the ambiguity of payoffs. This is directly relevant for founders evaluating market entry and for investors assessing competitive threat: the historical pattern is that most new entrants fail, but those that survive can materially reshape industry structure within a decade.
