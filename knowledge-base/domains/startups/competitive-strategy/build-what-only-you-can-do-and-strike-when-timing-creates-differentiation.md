Nvidia watched Intel dominate the CPU and the motherboard for two decades, had to live humiliatingly as a PCI Express peripheral in data centers, and only launched the Grace CPU in 2022 when they had a specific architectural reason: data center GPU clusters needed a CPU designed from the ground up to orchestrate GPU workloads. A less patient CEO would have launched a 'me-too' CPU a decade earlier out of competitive frustration and gotten crushed on margins.

Jensen's explicit framing: 'You build a great company by doing things that other people can't do. You don't build a company by fighting other people to do things that everyone can do.' This produces a counterintuitive discipline — skip entire obvious adjacencies until a novel capability creates a unique wedge. The same principle explains why Nvidia skipped commodity mobile SoCs, didn't build a commodity cloud, and only introduced DGX Cloud once they could position it as a differentiated turnkey AI solution running across hyperscaler infrastructure.

For founders: the defensive instinct to 'enter category X before competitors do' usually produces mediocre products with no pricing power. Better to stay patient, invest in the capabilities that only you are uniquely positioned to build, and enter adjacencies only when that capability can be applied in a way nobody else can copy.
