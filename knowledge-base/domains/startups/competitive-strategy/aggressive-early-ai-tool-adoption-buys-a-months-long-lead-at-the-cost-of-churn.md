Shopify got GitHub Copilot a year before public release (trading 3,000 engineers' honest feedback for access), ate substantial early churn when the tool 'wasn't that great,' and later gave unlimited budget across many tools. The rationale is a deliberate trade: spend more money and tolerate more instability to sit six months ahead of competitors. Crucially, this is explicitly framed as not worth it for everyone — a company whose business is physical and not innovation-paced should just wait for the tools to mature.

The second, often-underrated driver is recruitment: 'how would I hire the best if I said you cannot have these tools?' Forefront tooling is a talent-attraction signal as much as a productivity bet. The strategic frame is that this looks irrational up close (everyone churning on immature tools simultaneously) but is individually rational for innovation/talent-competitive firms — being first is worth the cost when your moat is speed and the people you can attract. The decision rule: weigh expected lead-time advantage and recruiting leverage against churn cost, and adopt aggressively only where those upsides actually compound for your business model.
