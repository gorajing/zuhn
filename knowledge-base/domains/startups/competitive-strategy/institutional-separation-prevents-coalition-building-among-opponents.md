British colonial administration in Palestine deliberately established separate institutions for each religious community, making it structurally difficult for Palestinian Christians and Muslims to cooperate. This classic divide-and-rule tactic worked precisely because institutional boundaries create different incentive structures, communication channels, and identity reinforcement for each group.

The pattern generalizes: any entity facing potential coalition opposition benefits from fragmenting that opposition through institutional design. In competitive strategy, incumbents sometimes achieve this by creating incompatible standards or ecosystems. Conversely, if you're trying to build a coalition (users, employees, community), shared institutions and communication channels are essential — separate Slack channels for each team, for instance, can inadvertently replicate divide-and-rule dynamics.
