ARM's willingness to incur R&D costs an average of eight years before revenue starts represents a profound competitive moat. At 42% of revenue ($773M in 2018), this sustained investment creates an ever-growing library of optimized designs that no new entrant can replicate quickly. The long time horizon functions as a natural barrier to entry because it requires both deep technical capability and patient capital.

This principle extends beyond semiconductors: any domain where accumulated R&D compounds over time rewards companies that can sustain long investment horizons. The key enabler is ARM's licensing model itself — because marginal costs are near zero, the company can afford to reinvest heavily in future designs while remaining profitable. This creates a virtuous cycle where today's licensing revenue funds tomorrow's competitive advantages.
