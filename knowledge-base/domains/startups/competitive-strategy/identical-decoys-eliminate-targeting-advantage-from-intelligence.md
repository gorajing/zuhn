The presidential motorcade always includes two identical limousines with matching license plates, and Marine One flies in formation with up to five identical helicopters that shuffle positions mid-flight. This isn't redundancy for reliability — it's deliberate deception that renders adversary intelligence useless. Even if an attacker has a spotter reporting which vehicle the president entered, the identical decoys neutralize that advantage.

In competitive strategy, this maps to making your real moves indistinguishable from feints. Companies that telegraph their strategic direction through a single visible initiative give competitors a clear target. Those that run multiple parallel efforts — some real, some exploratory — force competitors to either defend against all possibilities (spreading resources thin) or guess (accepting risk). The cost of maintaining decoys is almost always less than the cost of being precisely targeted.
