While Apple deliberated on India, Xiaomi and Samsung flooded the market with affordable smartphones, capturing hundreds of millions of users. These users then built digital lives — apps, payment methods, habits, accessory ecosystems — around Android and specific manufacturers' software layers. By the time Apple considered serious India investment, the switching costs weren't just financial (buying a $1,100 phone) but behavioral and ecosystem-based.

This mirrors a pattern across technology markets: distribution speed at accessible price points creates compounding advantages through network effects and switching costs. The lesson extends beyond hardware — any market where users invest in ecosystems rewards early mass-market entrants over delayed premium ones. For startups, this argues for prioritizing accessible distribution in high-growth markets even at lower margins, because the switching-cost moat you build early is worth more than the margin you sacrifice.
