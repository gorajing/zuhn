Destiny argues that conservatives' decision to demonize and disengage from universities — rather than critically engaging with them — created a self-reinforcing cycle: fewer conservatives participate, the remaining population skews further left, which validates the narrative that the institution is irredeemable, driving further disengagement. Shapiro partially agrees but traces the timeline back to the 1960s, citing Shelby Steele's account of liberal administrators who lacked the 'spiritual strength' to push back against radical demands.

The dynamic both describe is a general institutional pattern: when one faction exits rather than contests, the remaining faction faces no internal friction and accelerates toward extremes. This applies directly to companies (when pragmatists leave a startup, the remaining idealists make increasingly detached decisions), open-source projects (when moderate contributors leave, remaining maintainers can impose extreme governance), and markets (when rational investors exit a bubble, remaining participants become more euphoric). The key insight is that the decision to 'build alternatives' vs. 'reform from within' has dramatically different systemic consequences — alternatives create competition but also accelerate the decline of what they're replacing.
