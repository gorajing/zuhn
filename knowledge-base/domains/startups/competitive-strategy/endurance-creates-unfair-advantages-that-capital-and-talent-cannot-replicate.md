NVIDIA's CUDA platform required years of investment with little external affirmation — margins were sacrificed, direction was questioned, and the opportunity remained internally contested. What sustained the effort was not superior foresight but tolerance for extended ambiguity. This pattern repeated with AI, where accelerated computing was treated as foundational long before the market narrative hardened.

The deeper insight is that longevity at a single company compounds into an unfair advantage. Each cycle of reinvention builds on scars, misjudgments, and partial wins from the last. CUDA, AI, and platform expansion weren't resets — they were extensions shaped by institutional memory. This advantage looks unfair from the outside because it cannot be replicated quickly regardless of resources. In a tech culture that celebrates serial entrepreneurship and pivots, NVIDIA demonstrates that the deepest moats may come from simply refusing to leave.
