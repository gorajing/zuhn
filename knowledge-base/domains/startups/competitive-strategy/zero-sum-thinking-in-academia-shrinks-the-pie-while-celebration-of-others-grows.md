Keating contrasts the zero-sum culture of academic science — where celebrating a colleague's achievement feels like diminishing your own chances for funding and recognition — with Joe Rogan's approach in the comedy world, where genuinely promoting others' success grew the entire podcast ecosystem. Scientists are uniquely positioned to explain why a discovery matters, yet ego and credit anxiety prevent them from doing so publicly.

This pattern recurs in any industry with perceived scarcity. Startup founders who refuse to acknowledge competitors' innovations, investors who won't share deal flow, creators who won't cross-promote — all operate from the assumption that recognition is zero-sum. But as Rogan demonstrated, public generosity creates a flywheel: more excitement about the field attracts more resources, attention, and talent, expanding the pie for everyone. The actionable insight is that in any field where public perception drives resource allocation, celebrating others is not charity — it's ecosystem strategy.
