Greg Creed articulated one of the most powerful pricing frameworks in fast food: 'If we need a $1 price point, we create products at $1. We don't discount core menu items.' This seems simple but is the exact opposite of what McDonald's (discounted Big Macs), Burger King (discounted Whoppers), and KFC (discounted buckets) all did throughout the 2000s and 2010s.

The mechanics are clear: when you repeatedly discount a flagship product, customers anchor to the discounted price and refuse to pay full price. You've permanently destroyed your own pricing power. Taco Bell's approach used the same seven base ingredients (tortilla, beef, beans, cheese, lettuce, sour cream, sauce) recombined into dedicated value products that were profitable at the low price point. Customers got the deal they wanted; franchisees maintained positive margins even on sub-dollar items; and the core menu retained its pricing integrity. The result: Taco Bell's per-store earnings outperform McDonald's, Burger King, and KFC, and its store-level margins are among the highest in the entire restaurant industry.
