The Constitutional framers faced a sophisticated design problem: the Articles failed because government was too weak, but they also feared creating a new tyranny. Their insight was that tyranny can come from two directions — an autocratic government or an unchecked democratic majority redistributing property. The solution was structural: separation of powers (three branches checking each other), federalism (national vs state authority), and democratic dampening (Electoral College, appointed Senate, staggered terms).

This dual-threat model applies to any organization designing governance. Most startups only protect against one failure mode — either they fear founder autocracy (so they create consensus requirements that lead to paralysis) or they fear slow decision-making (so they concentrate power and risk unchecked bad judgment). The Constitutional model shows that durable systems need structural checks that operate automatically, not ones that depend on individuals behaving well. The framers explicitly did not trust anyone — not the president, not Congress, not the people — and designed accordingly.
