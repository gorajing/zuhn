Porter draws a sharp distinction between CSR (externally-driven, separate from profit, budget-constrained) and CSV (company-specific, integral to profit maximization, mobilizing the entire corporate budget). Traditional CSR responds to external pressure points and rarely moves the needle on social issues in measurable ways. CSV requires deep understanding of your specific value chain, products, and customers to find where business competitiveness and social conditions intersect.

Porter identifies four levels for creating shared value: (1) redefining operating practices within the value chain, (2) reconceiving products to meet social and economic needs simultaneously, (3) building supportive clusters in local communities, and (4) addressing broader economic and social issues collaboratively. The strategic implication for startups is clear: social impact designed into the business model from the start creates competitive advantage, while bolted-on CSR programs drain resources without generating returns. Companies that find their specific intersection of business strength and social need unlock value that pure profit-maximizers and pure philanthropists both miss.
