When atmospheric oxygen levels rose, RuBisCo began confusing O2 for CO2 roughly half the time, producing toxic phosphoglycolate. Plants couldn't replace RuBisCo, so they evolved entire additional metabolic pathways (photorespiration) solely to clean up after this bug. These cleanup systems now consume significant energy — a permanent tax on every plant's metabolism.

This is a precise biological model for technical debt in organizations. Legacy systems don't just underperform — they generate failure byproducts that require dedicated resources to manage. Banks maintaining COBOL systems, airlines running on decades-old reservation software, enterprises running compatibility layers — all are spending resources not on value creation but on neutralizing the toxic byproducts of systems they can't replace. The critical insight is that these costs compound: each workaround introduces its own edge cases and maintenance burden, creating a growing operational tax that never shrinks.
