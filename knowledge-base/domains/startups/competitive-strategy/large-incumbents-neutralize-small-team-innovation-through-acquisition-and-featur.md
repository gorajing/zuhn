The article highlights a critical vulnerability in the lean startup playbook: small teams excel at zero-to-one innovation but struggle to defend against incumbents who can replicate features and leverage existing distribution. Snapchat pioneered ephemeral stories, but Instagram's larger team and user base allowed it to copy and dominate the feature within a year. Similarly, Facebook acquired Instagram and WhatsApp partly to eliminate competitive threats.

For AI-native startups, this threat is amplified because AI capabilities are increasingly commoditized. If your advantage is 'we use AI to do X faster,' any well-resourced competitor can deploy the same AI tools. The defensible moat must come from proprietary data, unique workflow integration, network effects, or domain expertise that AI tools alone can't replicate.

Founders of lean teams should explicitly plan their moat strategy early: are you building for acquisition (optimize for attention), for defensible niche dominance (optimize for depth), or for network effects (optimize for user lock-in)? The choice shapes everything from product architecture to hiring priorities.
