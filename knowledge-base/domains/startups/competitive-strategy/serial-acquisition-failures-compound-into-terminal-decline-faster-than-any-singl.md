Yahoo's decline wasn't caused by any single missed deal — it was the compounding effect of repeatedly making the same category of error. Passing on Google in 2002 was costly but survivable. But then passing on Facebook in 2006 (over a $100M gap), rejecting Microsoft's $46B buyout in 2008, and choosing Tumblr over Netflix in 2013 created a pattern where each miss narrowed Yahoo's strategic options while competitors grew exponentially.

The compounding dynamic is what makes serial acquisition failures lethal: each missed deal doesn't just forfeit that specific opportunity, it strengthens a competitor who then makes the next acquisition even more expensive or impossible. Facebook at $1.1B in 2006 became Facebook at $900B+ — not just a missed financial return, but a competitor that now dominates the social advertising market Yahoo needed. Founders and boards should treat a second missed acquisition as a red-flag signal of systematic bias in their evaluation process, not just bad luck.
