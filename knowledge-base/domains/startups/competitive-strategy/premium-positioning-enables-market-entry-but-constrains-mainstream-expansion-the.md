Under Armour's first era was a masterclass in premium positioning: they charged $5-10 more than competitors, rarely discounted, and built retailer confidence by maintaining pricing integrity. Wholesalers appreciated the premium because it meant higher per-unit profits. Athletes appreciated the quality focus. The brand grew from zero to a billion in revenue through this disciplined approach.

But premium positioning is a double-edged sword. When Under Armour tried to go mainstream, the $70 hoodies that athletes would pay were unfathomable to average Europeans and Asians. The $100 running shoe market was tiny during a recession. Kevin Plank's frustration was palpable: 'A brand as powerful as Under Armour should be building a much higher price point.' But the market disagreed. They begrudgingly walked back prices to make footwear accessible, undermining the very DNA that had built the brand. The tension between premium identity and mass-market ambition is irreconcilable without a barbell strategy -- and Under Armour never figured that out.
