Uber's rides app is now the largest new-customer acquisition channel for Eats—larger than Google, Meta, and Instagram combined—at roughly a quarter of the cost of external channels. On the supply side, Eats is used as a lower-friction recruitment funnel for drivers: because food delivery requires less background-check overhead than rideshare, people can start earning within hours, and Uber then upsells them into rides later. Neither doordash nor Lyft can copy this because they don't own the other side.

The non-obvious part is the timing. Dara's framing is that this advantage 'looks great on paper' for years before it shows up in the financials, because new customers in a repeat-business category are less than 10% of GMV in any single year. One year of cheaper acquisition is invisible. Three to four years of compounding cheaper acquisition shows up as a margin advantage that widens faster than competitors can close.

This has implications for how investors should evaluate conglomerate-style platforms: the advantage is real but lags the strategy by 3-4 years, which means public markets consistently underprice cross-product platforms during the build phase and then overshoot once the flywheel becomes visible in quarterly numbers.
