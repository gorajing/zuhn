ByteDance's strategy reveals that TikTok was never meant to be a standalone product. It's a user-acquisition engine for a much broader empire. The Chinese version already demonstrated the playbook: in-app e-commerce generated $30M in sales in a single day, food delivery was being tested, and ByteDance launched enterprise software (Lark), gaming studios, education apps, cloud hosting, search, and even a streaming competitor to Netflix (Watermelon Video, with BBC/PBS licensing deals and Disney+'s former head).

This 'flagship as trojan horse' pattern echoes Amazon (retail → AWS → streaming → healthcare) and WeChat (messaging → payments → mini-programs → everything). The key enabler is data: TikTok's algorithm knows what users want before they do, making it trivially easy to surface relevant products, services, and content from ByteDance's other properties. With 110,000+ employees across 200+ cities, ByteDance is building a conglomerate that uses attention as the entry point for capturing economic activity across multiple industries. The lesson for startups: your first product doesn't need to be your business model — it can be your distribution moat for everything that follows.
