Naval highlights a critical nonlinearity: in domains with high leverage (technology, media, capital), small differences in judgment quality get amplified enormously. Someone who is right 90% of the time can literally earn hundreds of times more than someone right 80% of the time, because errors compound as much as successes do.

This has two practical implications. First, it explains why genuine curiosity matters so much — you need to be fully obsessed to reach the top percentiles of judgment in a domain, because someone who is 100% into it will outperform you nonlinearly. Second, it means the career strategy of following hot fields is doubly punished: you're less likely to develop deep judgment in something you're not genuinely curious about, and the leverage dynamics ensure that being merely good rather than exceptional produces dramatically less value.
