A big incumbent can always copy a single clever move from a smaller rival. What they can't copy is a coherent system of choices where every decision presupposes the others. Trader Joe's has 4,000 SKUs (vs 50,000 at a supermarket), which only works because they carry private label (no brand coupons), which only works because they built a 'merchant' brand (not a real estate one), which only works because employees rotate jobs and know every product, which only works because they pay 40–150% above industry rates, which only works because they have high gross margin per square foot, which only works because stores are 15k sq ft instead of 50k, which only works because they don't try to carry everything you need.

The right diagnostic question isn't 'what's our differentiator?' but 'which trade-offs are we willing to embrace that our competitors are structurally unable to embrace?' A would-be Trader Joe's copycat inside Kroger would need to simultaneously fire the slotting-fee team, rip out retail media, rebuild the merchandising org, halve the SKU count, double labor comp, and stop running coupons — and any one of those changes alone would destroy margins without earning the customer loyalty that comes from the whole bundle.
