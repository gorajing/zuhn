Jensen Huang clarified his viral 'I wouldn't start Nvidia again' clip by saying the comment was about the impossible difficulty of the journey, not regret. Zuckerberg agrees: he didn't even consider Facebook a real company until forced to, and openly says the early years were not the part of his life he'd want to relive. The convergence between the most successful chip founder and the most successful social founder is striking — both attribute their success in part to not knowing what they were getting into.

The practical implication is twofold. First, post-hoc 'lessons learned' from founders are systematically misleading because they're filtered through survivors who've internalized the cost. Pre-game advice from successful founders to aspiring ones tends to underweight the actual psychological toll. Second, the corollary is that being too informed before starting may be a competitive disadvantage — you'll talk yourself out of bets that look impossible from inside the experience but turn out to be worth making.

This isn't an argument for recklessness — it's an argument that the psychological setup for ambitious work involves a degree of constructive denial. The founders who succeed are not the ones who calculated the odds correctly; they're the ones who didn't calculate at all and stayed naive enough long enough to find out the answer empirically.
