Graham identifies 'playing house' — founders who raise money, rent cool offices, and hire people while neglecting to make something people want. This happens because the education system trains students to optimize for artificial metrics (grades, extracurriculars, exam tricks) rather than genuine mastery. When these students start companies, they instinctively seek the equivalent tricks.

Startups are described as 'impersonal as physics' — there is no boss to trick, only users who care whether your product works. While gaming can fool investors for one or two rounds, the company is ultimately doomed. This represents a fundamental environment shift: from systems where appearance and social maneuvering create advantage, to systems where only real value creation matters. The dangerous middle ground is that faking partially works on investors, giving founders enough rope to waste years.
