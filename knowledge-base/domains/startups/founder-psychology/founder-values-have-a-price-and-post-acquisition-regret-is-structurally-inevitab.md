WhatsApp's founders were privacy activists who left Yahoo over disagreements about advertising. They built WhatsApp explicitly as an anti-ad, pro-privacy product. Then they sold to Facebook — the world's largest advertising-driven data company — for $19B with promises of independence. Brian Acton quit two years later, forfeiting $850M in unvested stock, and funded Signal as a direct competitor.

This isn't a story about broken promises; it's a structural inevitability. When a company's entire business model (data monetization) fundamentally conflicts with the acquired product's founding values (privacy), no amount of autonomy promises can bridge the gap. The acquirer's incentive structure will always win. For founders considering exits: if the buyer's core business model contradicts your product's reason for existing, the only question is how long before the clash becomes intolerable — not whether it will happen.
