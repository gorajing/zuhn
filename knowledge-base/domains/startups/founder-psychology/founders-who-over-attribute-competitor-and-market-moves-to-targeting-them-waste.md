Graham's essay applies directly to startup psychology: the tendency to see competitor actions, press coverage, or market shifts as deliberately targeting your company is the same anthropocentric bias at a smaller scale. When a large incumbent releases a feature adjacent to yours, 'they noticed us and are trying to kill us' competes with 'they had this on the roadmap and were pursuing their own users.'

The self-centered interpretation, while emotionally satisfying, tends to drive reactive strategy — pivoting, over-investing in defense, or demoralizing the team — when the more likely explanation required no response at all.

Founders who default to 'see randomness' maintain cleaner strategic focus: they respond to what competitors actually ship, not to the narrative about why it was shipped. This is especially important in early stages where the startup is not yet large enough to be genuinely in a competitor's crosshairs.
