Jason Cohen's essay on startup identity highlights a structural trap: founders who pour years into a company often fuse their sense of self with the company's existence. When an exit occurs — even a wildly successful one — this triggers a form of identity grief. The company that defined them is now controlled by someone else, and the daily rituals, team, and mission that gave life structure are gone.

This dynamic is underappreciated in startup culture, which celebrates exits as unambiguous wins. The psychological reality is more complex. Founders who successfully decoupled their identity from their specific company — anchoring instead on 'I am a builder' rather than 'I am [Company X]' — tend to transition better post-exit and re-enter entrepreneurship faster. The practical implication is that working through identity questions before an exit, not after, is a form of founder preparation that the industry largely ignores.
