Graham notes that across many fields, the most successful people are slightly overconfident — which seems paradoxical since perfectly calibrated self-assessment should be optimal. The resolution is that overconfidence compensates for multiple opposing biases: your own tendency to judge early work too harshly, other people's skepticism, and the social pressure against ambitious attempts.

This isn't an argument for delusion. It's a claim about error correction — if the world systematically undervalues early-stage ambitious work, then slightly overvaluing your own contribution brings your effective estimate closer to the truth. The key word is 'slightly'; too much overconfidence leads to ignoring real feedback, but the right amount acts as psychological armor during the vulnerable early phase.
