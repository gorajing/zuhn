Target offered to acquire Casper for $900M. The founders refused because the price wasn't at least $1B — the magical unicorn threshold. Unable to buy, Target settled for just being an investor. One year later, Casper IPO'd at a slashed $500M valuation (50% cut from its $1.1B pre-IPO valuation), then dropped another $100M, shut down European operations, lost its COO/CFO, and laid off 21% of its workforce.

The $900M Target offer looks more regrettable by the day. This is the unicorn ego trap: the billion-dollar valuation becomes an identity, not a number. Founders who anchor to vanity valuations rather than realistic assessments of their business fundamentals end up destroying value for themselves, their employees, and their investors. The counterweight: a $900M exit on a business losing $90M/year is an extraordinary outcome. Ego turned it into a disaster.
