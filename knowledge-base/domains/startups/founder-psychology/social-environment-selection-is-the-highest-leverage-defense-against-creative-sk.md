Graham distinguishes sharply between generic encouragement (which you learn to discount) and informed encouragement from people working on similarly ambitious projects. The latter is valuable because these peers can actually evaluate whether your ugly early version has the structural qualities of something that will become great, versus something that's genuinely bad. This is why university departments and research labs work — not because they're institutions, but because they concentrate people with the judgment to assess early-stage work in their field. Silicon Valley's cultural advantage isn't moral superiority but structural: investors, founders, and advisors all have incentives aligned toward seeing promise in early work, and this practiced optimism has evolved into genuine cultural custom. The actionable insight is that environment selection — deliberately seeking out other people attempting ambitious new things — is a higher-leverage intervention than any individual psychological trick.
