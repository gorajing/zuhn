Cox describes the classic escalation pattern: his manager encouraged him to white-out a borrower's 30-day late payment, assuring him the worst that could happen was getting fired. When it worked, rather than feeling relieved and stopping, he felt emboldened. Within weeks he was altering W-2s, pay stubs, and appraisals. Within months he was creating synthetic identities.

The critical insight for anyone in business is that ethical boundaries don't erode at the point of the big decision — they erode at the first small compromise. Cox rationalized each escalation by telling himself he was 'helping people' who couldn't otherwise get homes. The self-deception narrative scaled perfectly with the fraud. Every founder, executive, and professional faces versions of this: small corner-cutting that 'doesn't really matter' but fundamentally recalibrates the moral compass. The defense isn't willpower at the moment of the big test — it's zero tolerance for the first small compromise.
