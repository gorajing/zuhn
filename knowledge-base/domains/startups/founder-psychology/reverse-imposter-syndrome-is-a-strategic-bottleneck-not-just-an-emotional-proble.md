Reverse imposter syndrome — where internal competence outpaces external recognition — is typically framed as an emotional challenge. But the article argues it's fundamentally a strategic bottleneck. When founders stay invisible, investors pattern-match to more visible competitors, strategic partners hesitate, and hiring slows because talent follows narratives, not cap tables.

The compounding effect is what makes this dangerous: teams take cues from founder energy, so when external credibility doesn't travel, internal doubt creeps in. The founder may still believe in the mission, but the environment around them starts warping — board trust wavers, press misses the thread, and every win demands double the explanation. This reframes visibility work from vanity to operational necessity.
