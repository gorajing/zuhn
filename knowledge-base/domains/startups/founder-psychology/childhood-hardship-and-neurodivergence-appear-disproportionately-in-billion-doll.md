A study of 25 founders who built multi-billion dollar companies before age 30 found recurring patterns that conventional screening would reject: childhood hardship, neurodivergence, and eclectic rather than focused interests. This challenges the pattern-matching that dominates both venture capital and corporate hiring.

The insight cuts two ways. For investors, it suggests that the founders most likely to build transformative companies may look nothing like the Stanford CS grad archetype. For founders themselves, traits that feel like disadvantages — ADHD, difficult upbringings, scattered curiosity — may actually be load-bearing features of their capacity to build at scale. The wide-ranging interests create unusual pattern recognition across domains, while adversity builds the resilience needed to survive the startup gauntlet.
