Paul Graham observes that even Y Combinator founders—who already have funding and a shot at wealth—get dramatically more productive when facing peer demos (Prototype Day). The financial outcome feels abstract most of the time; what actually drives daily effort is not wanting to look bad.

This has a practical implication: founders should deliberately construct social environments that create accountability pressure. Choosing peers who will judge you as a founder, publicly committing to milestones, and positioning yourself so graduation or life transitions remove the 'student' escape hatch all harness this force. The corollary is that staying in school while running a startup provides a built-in excuse for failure that weakens this motivational engine.
