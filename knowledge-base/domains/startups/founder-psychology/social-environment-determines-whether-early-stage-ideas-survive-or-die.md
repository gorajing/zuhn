Silicon Valley's advantage is not just capital or talent but a cultural norm of evaluating early-stage ideas differently from finished work. Most social environments judge embryonic projects by the standards of completed ones, which kills them. The key is not surrounding yourself with indiscriminate cheerleaders—you'd learn to discount that—but finding peers who can tell an ugly duckling from a genuinely bad idea. The best source of such peers is people working on similarly ambitious projects of their own, which is why university departments, research labs, and startup ecosystems are so productive. Graham argues this cultural optimism, currently concentrated in a few places, is learnable and will spread because it's extremely lucrative.
