Graham argues that Silicon Valley's support for wild-sounding ideas is not primarily about smart investors or better infrastructure — it is a evolved social custom. When people have learned (through repeated financial incentive) to treat dismissal of new ideas as dangerous, the baseline stance toward an unknown person with a strange idea shifts from skepticism to curiosity.

This cultural norm has compounding effects: would-be founders face less social friction, early-stage companies survive longer in their awkward phase, and the people around them actively search for ways the idea could work. None of this requires any individual to be unusually wise or generous — it's a Nash equilibrium that emerged from aligned incentives.

The practical takeaway for builders: the environment you put yourself in shapes your project's survival odds more than most people account for. Seeking out colleagues, communities, or institutions that treat early ideas charitably isn't just emotional support — it's a structural advantage.
