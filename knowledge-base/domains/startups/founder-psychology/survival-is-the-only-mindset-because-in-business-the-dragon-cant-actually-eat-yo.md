Nvidia's early years illustrate this perfectly. Funded into a market with 70-80 competitors, then attacked by Intel integrating graphics onto the motherboard, AND having bet on a wrong technical approach (quadrilateral polygons), Jensen Huang laid off 70% of the company and made two desperate decisions: ship chips designed only in software emulation (skipping prototype runs to save 6 months) and ship them broken, then convince developers that the disabled features were 'a feature, not a bug.' These were not strategic choices — they were survival moves no rational CEO would make if they had options.

Eric Yuan of Zoom told Acquired that even today he wakes up thinking about 'how to survive, how to survive, how to survive.' When asked whether he set out to build a multi-billion-dollar company, he didn't even understand the question — survival was the entire frame.

Applied: when facing an existential threat, the question isn't 'what's the optimal move' but 'what move keeps us alive long enough to make the next move.' Founders who internalize this outlast competitors with better strategies but weaker stomachs.
