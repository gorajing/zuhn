Graham identifies 'fraud' as the telltale word that distinguishes a hater from an ordinary critic. The logic is structural: a hater can't deny the target's fame (it's what triggers them), and they're convinced the target has no talent, so the only reconciliation is deception — the target must have tricked everyone. This makes 'fraud' accusations a reliable signal for filtering criticism.

For founders, this is immediately practical. As a company grows, fraud accusations will emerge. The instinct is to take them seriously and defend against them. But Graham's framework provides a quick diagnostic: check neutral, thoughtful observers. If knowledgeable people respect the work, the fraud accusation is hater-pattern, not genuine critique. This saves enormous emotional and strategic energy that would otherwise go toward addressing bad-faith criticism.
