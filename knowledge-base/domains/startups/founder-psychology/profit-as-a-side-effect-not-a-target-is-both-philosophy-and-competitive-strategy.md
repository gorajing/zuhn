Musk's formulation — 'aim to make more than you take' — is a compact version of a principle that shows up across enduring founders: money arrives as a byproduct of value, not the other way around. This isn't naive idealism; it's a competitive strategy. When you optimize directly for valuation, you get companies built around narrative and optics. When you optimize for usefulness, you get compounding.

The practical implication is about prioritization under pressure. When founders face the choice between a feature that makes the product genuinely better versus one that makes the deck look better for the next round, this principle provides a clear tie-breaker. Usefulness over hype, outcomes over optics.

This matters especially in the current AI wave, where a lot of capital is chasing narrative momentum. The founders who come out of this cycle with durable companies will likely be those who treated fundraising as a means, not a scorecard.
