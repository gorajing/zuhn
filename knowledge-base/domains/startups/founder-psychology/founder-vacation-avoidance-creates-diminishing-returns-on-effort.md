The startup ecosystem glorifies relentless hustle, but the evidence increasingly shows that founders who never disengage suffer compounding cognitive impairment — worse judgment on hiring, strategy, and product decisions. The cost of a bad decision made while burned out far exceeds the cost of a week offline.

This creates a paradox: the founders who feel they can least afford to step away are often the ones who most need to. Strategic disengagement — planned breaks with clear delegation — is not a luxury but a risk management practice. The best-performing founders treat rest as a competitive advantage rather than a concession.
