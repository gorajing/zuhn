Graham observes that persistent people are usually quite rational — except at the very top of their decision tree. When choosing between problems of roughly equal expected value, they follow personal preference, even deliberately widening their bands of acceptable EV to ensure the problem they love qualifies. Empirically, this works.

Two mechanisms explain why. First, humans simply work harder on problems they love, so the EV calculation changes once you factor in effort. Second, and more subtly, our preferences aren't random — when we love a problem others don't care about, it's often because we've unconsciously noticed it's more important than others realize. This validates the common startup advice to 'work on something you care about' with a deeper mechanism than mere motivation: your taste in problems is itself a signal about where value is hiding.
