Graham argues that Silicon Valley's distinctive optimism toward early-stage ideas is not primarily a moral achievement but a financial one. Investors and ecosystem participants have learned — through repeated painful experience of missing winners — that dismissing strange-sounding ideas from unknown people is expensive. This created an incentive to develop genuine skill at seeing potential in ugly ducklings.

Over time, this financially-motivated behavior calcified into cultural custom. Now it 'just seems like what one does with startups.' The origin in self-interest doesn't undermine the cultural norm — it makes it stable and self-reinforcing, because the incentive to maintain it remains.

The broader implication is that the right incentive structure can genuinely change how a community evaluates early-stage work. This is a design insight: if you want a team or organization to evaluate ideas more generously, you need to make that generosity financially or reputationally rewarded, not just morally praised.
