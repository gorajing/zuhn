Graham points out that safe jobs with strong employment protections are essentially annuities — they deliver a stream of future income with minimal variance. Socialist economies with powerful tenured bureaucrats have real wealth inequality even if measured income looks flat, because the value of that tenure doesn't show up in statistics.

This is a measurement critique worth taking seriously: we tend to study what's easy to count. Gini coefficients and income distributions capture flows of money but not the variance or security of those flows. A $60k/year job you can't be fired from is worth more than a $60k/year contract gig with no security, but they look identical in most economic data.

For anyone reasoning about labor market trends or startup compensation, this framing matters. The shift from stable corporate employment to startup equity + contract work may involve less of an inequality increase than statistics suggest, because the statistics never properly valued the old system's stability premium. What's changed is who bears the variance risk, not necessarily the total economic value being distributed.
