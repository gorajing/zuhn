Abdul-Karim Mohamed's piece frames Africa's $1B entrepreneurship initiatives as a failed employment solution — a framing that reveals a fundamental category error in how development capital approaches startup ecosystems. When programs optimize for employment headcount rather than business model viability, they fund the wrong companies and measure the wrong things. Businesses built to create jobs rather than to solve scalable problems tend to neither create jobs sustainably nor build defensible companies. This critique applies broadly to government-backed startup programs globally, where metric-driven funding creates incentive misalignment between funders and founders.
