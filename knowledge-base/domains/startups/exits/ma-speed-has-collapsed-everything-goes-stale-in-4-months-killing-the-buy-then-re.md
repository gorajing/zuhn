Calacanis's diagnostic via the HubSpot AEO acquisition: HubSpot bought an AEO startup for $30M months ago. The product is already non-competitive vs. peak (the dedicated AEO startups Harry Stebbings funds). HubSpot couldn't have predicted this — they bought the best they could afford at the time, and the field moved past them in months. The classic 'tuck-in' acquisition strategy (small for product-market-fit, rebuild natively) and the 'go big' strategy (full platform acquisition) both struggle in this environment because the underlying technology depreciates faster than the integration cycle.

The implications: (1) Public-company corp-dev teams are stuck in a no-win — overpriced startups they can't afford or low-priced startups that are stale, (2) Founders should sell early-and-often (Eli Gil's advice: 'have an exit discussion every year') because the window of premium pricing is narrow, (3) The only acquisitions that work are those where the integration adds value beyond the technology (distribution, customer relationships, talent that builds future things). For Zuhn editorially: this is the fast-decay-rate complement to the model-is-not-the-moat thesis. Models depreciate; products built on them depreciate faster; acquisitions of those products depreciate fastest of all.
