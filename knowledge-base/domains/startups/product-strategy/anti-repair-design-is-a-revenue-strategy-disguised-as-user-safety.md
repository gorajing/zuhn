Apple disables features when screens or batteries are replaced with genuine parts that lack matching serial numbers, connects MacBook display flex cables so the entire display must be replaced instead of a 50-cent cable, and has raided repair shops for stocking genuine parts. The safety argument doesn't hold when qualified external technicians are equally blocked. This pattern — coupling components to force expensive assembly-level replacements — is a deliberate revenue strategy. The 50-cent flex cable forcing a $700+ display replacement illustrates the magnitude. For product strategists, this reveals a tension: anti-repair design generates short-term revenue but accumulates regulatory and brand risk, as the EU response demonstrates.
