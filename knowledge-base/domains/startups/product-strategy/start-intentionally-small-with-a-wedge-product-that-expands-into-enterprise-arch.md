Levie argues that enterprise startups must start intentionally small — finding a specific slice of a problem where they can deliver a dramatically better user experience or business model, then expand over time to larger customers or broader use cases. The key word is 'intentionally': smallness isn't a limitation but a deliberate strategy. Incumbents will always go for the full solution, so competing head-on is suicidal.

Zen Payroll (now Gusto) exemplifies this: they took the narrow, painful slice of payroll management for small businesses, plugged into existing infrastructure, and made it dead simple. Incumbents dismissed them as too small and too niche. But the wedge gave them a foothold from which they expanded both up-market (larger companies) and horizontally (more HR services). The pattern is: find the gap in the full solution significant enough that customers want it solved, but small enough that incumbents won't bother defending it.
