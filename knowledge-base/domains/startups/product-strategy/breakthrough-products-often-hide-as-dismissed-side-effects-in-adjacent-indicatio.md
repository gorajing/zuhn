Lotte Bjerre Knudsen noticed during animal trials that rats given large doses of liraglutide would stop eating. The effect persisted mildly in human phase 3 diabetes trials. Pushing Novo to pursue a parallel FDA indication for weight management was considered 'out there' — weight-loss drugs had a toxic history (fen-phen heart damage, amphetamines in the 70s) and obesity was culturally framed as a personal responsibility problem rather than a disease. Researchers widely believed a safe, effective weight-loss drug was impossible.

The bigger market by orders of magnitude turned out to be the 'side effect.' Today, 40% of Americans are obese versus 10% diabetic. The pattern generalizes: when something interesting happens off-axis from your intended use case, the question is not 'is this worth investigating scientifically' but 'is there social or organizational stigma making the team underweight this signal?' Stigma-adjacent markets are often huge precisely because incumbents have refused to serve them. For founders, the playbook is to track dismissed side effects and unusual usage patterns, and to ask whether the reason no one pursues them is technical or cultural — the cultural ones are the real opportunities.
