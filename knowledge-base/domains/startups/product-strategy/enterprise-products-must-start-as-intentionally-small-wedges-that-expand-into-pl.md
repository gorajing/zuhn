Levie argues that enterprise startups must be 'intentionally small' — not accidentally small due to limited resources, but strategically small because that's where incumbents leave gaps. The wedge should be a discrete problem painful enough that customers adopt a standalone solution, yet positioned to expand into larger enterprise architecture over time.

Zen Payroll (now Gusto) exemplifies this: they started with just payroll for small businesses — a slice incumbents dismissed as too small — then expanded into benefits, HR, and compliance as they moved upmarket. The critical distinction is that incumbents always pursue the 'full solution' approach, which means they optimize for breadth over depth in any single area. This creates natural gaps where a focused startup can deliver a dramatically better experience on one specific problem, establish trust, and then expand. The wedge must be chosen so that expansion into adjacent use cases and larger customers is a natural trajectory, not a pivot.
