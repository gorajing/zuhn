Axie Infinity demonstrated this pattern clearly: at peak, players earned up to $1,000/month and the game became a job in the Philippines, with 40%+ of users there. But as new user growth inevitably slowed, demand for in-game NFTs (averaging $787 each) couldn't be sustained. The decline started before the $620M hack, not because of it.

The structural problem is that earn-first games attract users whose continued participation depends on financial returns. When the ratio of new users to existing users falls, asset prices drop, earnings decline, and users churn — which further reduces demand. This is identical to Ponzi dynamics where early participants are paid by later entrants.

The lesson generalizes beyond gaming: any product where the primary value proposition is financial return rather than intrinsic utility will face this reflexivity problem. Sustainable products must deliver standalone value that persists independent of growth rates.
