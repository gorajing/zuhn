Levie draws a critical distinction in enterprise product development: listen deeply to customer problems but never build exactly what they tell you to build. Customers describe solutions in terms of their current workflows and mental models — they'll ask for features that patch existing processes rather than reimagine them. Your job is to distill many complex requests down into the simplest, most elegant solution that addresses the underlying need.

He cites Palantir as an exemplar: they work with customers facing extraordinarily complex data problems and translate those into simple solutions the customer would never have known how to ask for. The complementary principle is to modularize rather than customize — build a platform with APIs and extension points rather than baking every customer-specific requirement directly into the product. Customization creates technical debt and slows iteration; modularization lets you serve diverse enterprise needs while maintaining a clean, evolvable core.
