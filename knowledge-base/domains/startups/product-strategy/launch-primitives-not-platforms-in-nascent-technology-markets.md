Microsoft and Google both initially pursued platform-as-a-service: they tried to define a new programming paradigm for the cloud (Azure Cloud Services, Google App Engine with custom runtimes). They lost the first five years of the cloud market almost uncontested. Amazon launched the most basic possible compute, storage, database, and CDN — explicitly unopinionated infrastructure primitives — and said 'surprise us with what you build.'

Primitives win early in a new category for two reasons. First, they serve both greenfield startups (who want flexibility) and lift-and-shift enterprises (who want to move existing workloads unchanged). A platform only serves the former, and typically can't even serve them well because the market for that paradigm doesn't yet exist. Second, primitives let the market teach you what to build next, while a platform locks you into assumptions made before anyone knew what the category actually was.

When building developer tools or infrastructure in an emerging category, resist the temptation to be clever and opinionated. Ship the lego bricks, not the finished castle.
