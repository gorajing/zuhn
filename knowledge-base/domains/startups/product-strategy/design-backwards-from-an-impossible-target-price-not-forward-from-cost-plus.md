IKEA's 'hot dog product policy' formalizes a rule most companies get backwards: instead of costing out a product and adding margin, Ingvar Kamprad insisted every category have one item priced at least 50% below any competitor — and then forced the organization to redesign everything upstream to meet that number. The LACK table ($9.99 today) exists because someone decided a $10 coffee table was the goal, which meant abandoning solid wood, inventing board-on-frame construction, using sawdust and pulp scrap as the primary material, and engineering for flat pack. The design target collapses dozens of decisions simultaneously.

The policy works because the price becomes a forcing function across the whole company. Kamprad wrote that 'expensive solutions to any kind of problem are usually the work of mediocrity' — designing a 5,000-kroner desk is easy, but designing a good functional 100-kroner desk takes real skill. Cost discipline isn't a cost-center activity; it's the highest form of design. Most founders treat pricing as the last question. IKEA treats it as the first.

To apply: in any product category you serve, pick one SKU and set an 'impossible' target price — 50-70% below the competitive set. Then audit every step of the value chain and ask which ones need to be reinvented (not optimized) to hit it. If the answer is 'none,' your target isn't aggressive enough.
