Microsoft Multiplan tried to compete with Lotus 1-2-3 on the existing 8-bit/character-mode platform and got crushed. Lotus dominated by counter-positioning: building specifically for the IBM PC's capabilities. Microsoft's lesson, internalized hard, was that in a fast-moving technology landscape you should never fight the last war. The applications team got an explicit mandate to look around at the most cutting-edge platform — regardless of what the rest of the company was doing — and build the killer app for it. That platform turned out to be the Macintosh GUI, and the killer app became Excel, the world's first graphical spreadsheet.

The general rule: when a competitor has established dominance on a current technology generation, you cannot win by doing the same thing slightly better. The competitive vector that's open is the next leap forward, where the dominant player is locked into compatibility with their existing customer base. Being first and best on the new platform — even if it's a competitor's platform — recreates the standard from scratch with your product as the default.

This is why Microsoft was willing to make Excel for the Mac before making it for Windows: the strategic value of being the killer app on the most exciting new platform outweighed any concerns about advantaging Apple. The killer app and the platform make each other.
