Jacob Effron's Redpoint framework distills AI startup evaluation into three filters: Is there an effective wedge (a narrow, high-urgency use case that end-users love)? How much can the company expand from that wedge into large adjacencies? And will quality matter in this market — because if 'good-enough for cheap' wins, race-to-the-bottom risk kills margins.

The framework identifies four repeatable adoption patterns: conversational interfaces, document search/summarization, speech (especially healthcare), and code generation. The case studies reinforce this — Abridge wedged into doctor-patient conversation notes (killing 'pajama time' charting) and expanded into broader clinical workflows, reaching a $5.3B valuation. Legora won legal AI through speed and vertical depth in the Nordics before expanding. The key insight is that velocity (shipping features as models evolve) combined with wedge scalability (natural expansion into larger product surfaces) is how durable AI companies are built.
