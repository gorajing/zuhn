Proactivity is one of Viktor's most powerful features: it can join a conversation unprompted, check PostHog during a growth discussion, and point out that a claimed A/B result isn't statistically significant. When this lands, the agent gets activated more broadly across the workspace — a virtuous loop for the product.

But the same behavior on day one backfires. When someone adds Viktor and it immediately starts DMing everyone and jumping into threads, security teams 'start raging.' The capability that drives adoption when trusted creates alarm when unearned.

The resolution is sequencing: earn proactivity with the first few users, demonstrate value in a contained way, then roll it out slowly. The general principle for autonomous, high-agency software: the more proactive and powerful the agent, the more its rollout must be staged to build trust before scope — autonomy granted too fast reads as a threat, not a benefit.
