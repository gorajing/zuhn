The most concrete lesson in the talk is a self-indictment: every time Kitze felt the urge to market his all-in-one productivity app, he instead told himself 'maybe one more feature' and kept building. Years later it still has no landing page and isn't 'properly finished.' Meanwhile someone extracted a single Benji-equivalent feature — snap a photo of food, get calorie counts — and built a multi-million-dollar business on it alone.

The directional claim is that feature accumulation often functions as productive-feeling avoidance of the genuinely hard work (distribution, marketing, finishing). A sprawling unshipped product has zero users; a focused single-feature product that ships has a market. The wedge is not 'fewer features because minimalism is virtuous' — it's that a sharp, marketed wedge reaches people while the super-app never leaves the workshop.

Application: when you catch yourself adding scope to a tool you haven't marketed, treat the urge as a signal to ship and promote what exists. The asymmetry — one shipped feature beating sixty unshipped ones — should bias every roadmap decision toward narrowing and releasing.
