Between Mach 0.8 and 1.2, airflow around an aircraft is neither fully subsonic nor supersonic, causing drag to spike exponentially and destabilizing the plane. Flying in this transonic range actually requires more fuel than flying above Mach 1.2. This creates a 'dead zone' where incremental speed gains are prohibitively expensive, forcing a binary choice: fly well below (commercial aviation at ~Mach 0.72) or well above (supersonic at Mach 1.5+).

Many industries have analogous dead zones — performance ranges where the cost curve goes nonlinear. In software, the gap between a good-enough MVP and enterprise-grade reliability often has this shape. In content, the zone between amateur and professional production quality burns resources without proportional audience gain. These physics-like barriers create natural market segments that persist even as technology improves, because the underlying cost dynamics are structural, not technological.
