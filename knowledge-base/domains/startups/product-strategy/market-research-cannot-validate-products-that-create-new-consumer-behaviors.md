In 1979 Sony's marketers, engineers, and board all opposed the Walkman. The product had no speakers, no recording capability, and required people to put things in their ears while walking around in public — a behavior that simply did not exist at scale. Marketers said nobody wanted this. Engineers said the power budget was impossible. Morita overrode all of them and personally committed to resign if the first 30,000 units didn't sell in six months. The product tripled monthly for many consecutive months and ultimately sold a quarter billion units.

Morita's own words: 'I do not believe any amount of market research could have told us that the Sony Walkman would be successful.' This is the same principle Steve Jobs later articulated ('customers don't know what they want until you show them'), but it originated with Morita more than a decade earlier. The deeper point is that research methods can only measure existing behaviors and stated preferences — they are structurally incapable of predicting products that create new behaviors.

Apply this when research is telling you a category-creating product has no demand. Research is answering a different question than the one you are asking. The only valid test is to ship the product at enough scale that consumer behavior can actually emerge. But this only works if someone in the organization has enough conviction to bet their job on it — without that forcing function, the internal skeptics always win.
