Graham illustrates this with VisiCalc: Bricklin and Frankston wrote it for the Apple II because they personally liked that machine. They could have chosen any platform — the Apple II became a star because its creators chose it as users first and developers second. This is not a one-off anecdote but a structural pattern in technology markets.

The implication inverts conventional platform strategy. Instead of pursuing developers with incentives, evangelism programs, or economic arguments, the highest-leverage move is to build something developers genuinely prefer for their own daily work. When the platform is the developer's personal choice, novel software creation becomes automatic and self-sustaining. No developer relations budget can replicate the quality and volume of software that emerges from authentic developer enthusiasm.
