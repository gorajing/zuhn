Real Graphene's key commercialization insight wasn't the graphene itself but its integration method: adding a composite graphene sheet 1-5 atomic layers thick inside existing lithium cells. This preserves identical cell shape and size, meaning phone manufacturers can swap in graphene-enhanced batteries without retooling production lines or redesigning devices. The CEO explicitly called it 'easy plug-and-play.'

This pattern — making the new technology conform to existing form factors and supply chains — dramatically reduces adoption friction. It transforms the decision from 'redesign our product around a new battery architecture' to 'buy a different battery from a different supplier at 30% higher component cost.' The lesson generalizes: when commercializing a breakthrough material or component, optimizing for drop-in compatibility with existing systems often matters more than maximizing the raw performance improvement.
