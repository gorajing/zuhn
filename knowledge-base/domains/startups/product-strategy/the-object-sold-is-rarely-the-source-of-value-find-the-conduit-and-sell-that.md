Schultz's Milan epiphany in 1983 wasn't about espresso quality or brewing technique — Italian coffee bars revealed that the actual product was the sense of community that coffee happened to conduct. When he pitched this to investors, he wasn't selling beverages; he was selling 'the third place' — the space between home and work where human connection happens. Coffee was the mechanism, not the offering.

This frame shaped every subsequent decision in a way pure product-focus wouldn't have. The cup with your name on it, the in-store design, the baristas' intimacy with regulars, even the refusal to segment Starbucks as luxury (so a CEO and a blue-collar truck driver stood in the same line) — all of these are nonsensical if you think you're in the coffee business, and obvious if you know you're in the third-place business. 'We became the first experiential brand at scale.' Zero marketing spend for decades, because the conduit insight meant the stores themselves were the marketing.

The diagnostic question: if your customers got the literal product elsewhere for the same price, would they still come to you? If yes, you have a conduit. Find what it's actually conducting. Most founders can't articulate this for their own business because they're too close to the object — and competitors who figure it out first will reshape the category around their framing, not yours. Schultz competed with Folgers and Maxwell House at the product layer; he crushed them at the conduit layer and they never recovered.
