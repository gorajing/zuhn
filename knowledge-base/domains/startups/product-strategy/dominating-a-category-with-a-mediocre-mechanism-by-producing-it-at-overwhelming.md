RuBisCo's error rate would disqualify it as an engineered solution: it confuses oxygen for CO2 roughly 50% of the time under current atmospheric conditions, requiring an expensive repair pathway. Yet plants never evolved a better replacement—instead, they evolved to overproduce RuBisCo massively, ensuring that even with a ~50% misfire rate, enough correct reactions occur to sustain life.

This reflects a strategic trade-off that recurs in competitive systems: when the cost of quality improvement is too high (or impossible given evolutionary constraints), volume can substitute. A sales team that closes 10% of calls at high volume may outperform a team that closes 30% at low volume. A recommendation algorithm with modest precision that runs on every user touchpoint may outperform a higher-quality algorithm deployed rarely.

The practical implication is to resist the assumption that quality must be fixed before scale is pursued. In some environments, deploying an imperfect mechanism at sufficient scale and accepting the overhead of handling failures is more effective than waiting for a better mechanism. The decision depends on whether the 'error cleanup' cost scales linearly or sublinearly with volume.
