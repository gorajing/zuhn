Levie argues that enterprise startups must resist the temptation to build full solutions from day one. Instead, they should find a narrow wedge—a specific pain point that falls between the gaps of existing solutions—and make the user experience on that sliver exceptional. The incumbents will dismiss the wedge as 'too small' or 'only for small businesses,' which is exactly the desired response.

The wedge strategy works because it creates an entry point with low friction (small businesses or single use cases), then the startup expands along two axes: up-market to larger customers, and horizontally to adjacent use cases. Zen Payroll (now Gusto) exemplified this by targeting just payroll management for startups—a painful but narrow problem—then expanding into full HR, benefits, and compliance. PlanGrid did the same in construction by starting with blueprint management on iPads. The incumbents' dismissal of the wedge is actually the startup's protection during its vulnerable early phase.
