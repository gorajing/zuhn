The Testament of a Furniture Dealer contains one of the most underrated product-design principles ever written: 'Quality must never be an end in and of itself. It must be adjusted to the consumer's needs.' Kamprad's example is deliberately mundane — a tabletop needs a durable finish because people set hot mugs and plates on it; a shelf inside a bookcase does not, because books don't wear surfaces. Spending equally on both finishes looks like craftsmanship but is actually a tax the customer pays for no benefit.

IKEA institutionalizes this. The backs of furniture that face walls aren't finished. Bottoms that face floors are rough. Particle board replaces solid wood wherever structural load doesn't require it. This is why IKEA products feel 'cheap' when you examine parts you're not supposed to examine — and why they're actually well-engineered in every place that matters for daily use.

The software analog is obvious once named: engineering effort poured into perfect abstractions, internal APIs, or edge cases no user will hit is the same mistake as a finished shelf back. Real quality is knowing what to cut. The rule to internalize: for every component of your product, ask 'does the customer experience this, and how?' If the answer is 'never' or 'barely,' that's where your quality budget should drop to the floor so it can be redeployed where the customer actually touches the product.
