Most retailer house brands (Great Value, Good & Gather, Amazon Basics) exist to offer shoppers a cheaper version of a branded product on the same shelf — the signal is 'same thing, less money.' This keeps the retailer positioned as a landlord for real brands; the house brand is a hedge, not the identity. Trader Joe's inverts this: the rule from the start was that a private label product must be differentiated on some dimension — a different spice blend, a unique format (Wolfgang Puck's frozen pizzas made smaller to fit a toaster oven), an unusual ingredient, a packaging voice — before it's worth making at all.

The signal to the customer flips from 'price arbitrage' to 'this is an N-of-1 you can only get here.' And the fact that the house brand shares the store name (no other major retailer does this) is a tell: Trader Joe's has no interest in also carrying branded products. The store experience and the product experience are a single brand promise. Any private label launched as a me-too generic is working against this — it trains customers that the store is just a cheaper version of everyone else, which is exactly the commodity positioning Joe Coulombe spent his career escaping.
