In a 'supermarket as real estate company' model, the retailer promises consistent availability of branded products and the brand does the merchandising. In a wine merchant model, the retailer promises curation and delight: 'come in, be surprised, and when the wine is gone it's gone.' The customer explicitly accepts inconsistency as part of the value proposition — scarcity becomes a feature, not a bug.

This reframing cascades through the business. You don't need every SKU a big chain carries; you need each SKU to have a story the crew member can tell. You don't need coupons; you need a Fearless Flyer that reads like a wine insider's report. You don't fear stockouts; they're proof of rotation and discovery. The model works especially well with unbranded or weakly branded categories (wine, nuts, dried fruit, almond butter, health foods in the 70s) — and is essentially impossible to run in a category where brand loyalty has already been captured by a big CPG company. Any founder building in a category dominated by faceless commodities should ask whether there's a 'wine merchant' posture available to them.
