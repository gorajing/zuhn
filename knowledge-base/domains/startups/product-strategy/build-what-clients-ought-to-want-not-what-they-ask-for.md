Karp describes Palantir's product philosophy as fundamentally different from traditional enterprise software. Where most companies figure out what the client wants, give them a version of it, and make switching costly, Palantir builds what the client ought to want — even if they don't ask for it. This means clients might not like you initially, but when the product delivers real outcomes (like bringing soldiers home safely), they learn to value it.

This approach requires genuine domain insight and conviction. It only works if you're actually right about what the client needs. AIP was launched secretly in the middle of the night because Karp knew normal internal processes would have killed it — every product head would have quit, and the consensus was that LLMs alone would solve everything. Karp bet that orchestration mattered more than raw LLMs, and he was right. The lesson: stated customer demand and expert consensus can both be wrong simultaneously.
