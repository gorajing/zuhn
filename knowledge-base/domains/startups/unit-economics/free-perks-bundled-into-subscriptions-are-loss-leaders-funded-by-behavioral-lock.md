The framing of Amazon's streaming as 'free' is a consumer-facing fiction that obscures a sophisticated unit economics calculation. Amazon's hypothesis — later validated — was that Prime subscribers who engage with streaming content shop on Amazon.com more frequently and with less price comparison, effectively subsidizing the cost of the streaming content through incremental retail margin.

This is the classic loss-leader logic applied to digital bundles: the 'free' element is not free to Amazon, but the behavioral change it induces (increased purchase frequency, reduced churn, reduced price sensitivity) more than covers the content licensing and production costs over the subscriber's lifetime.

For founders designing subscription tiers, this suggests that the ROI of 'free' bundled features should be measured in behavioral change metrics (session frequency, purchase frequency, NPS, churn rate) rather than direct attribution revenue. A feature that appears to cost money on a P&L may be the single biggest driver of LTV when you account for the behavior it creates.
