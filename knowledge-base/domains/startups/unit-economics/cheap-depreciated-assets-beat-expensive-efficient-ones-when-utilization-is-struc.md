FedEx's fleet is overwhelmingly composed of old aircraft like Airbus A300s that passenger airlines have retired. This seems irrational until you understand the utilization math: FedEx planes fly roughly 2 hours per day versus 12+ hours for commercial airlines (6x lower utilization). At such low utilization, the dominant cost factor shifts from operating efficiency to acquisition cost.

A new fuel-efficient plane costing hundreds of millions can't amortize its purchase price over just 2 flight-hours daily. But a cheap retired plane doesn't need to — its acquisition cost is already near zero. This principle applies broadly: when a business model structurally limits how much you use an asset (seasonal businesses, peak-only operations, overnight-only services), optimizing for purchase price beats optimizing for per-unit operating cost. UPS validates the exception — they do buy new 747-800s but specifically route them on their longest flights where high utilization justifies the premium.
