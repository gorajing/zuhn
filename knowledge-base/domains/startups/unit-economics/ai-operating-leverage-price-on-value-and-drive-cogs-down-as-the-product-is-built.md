The whole thesis lives or dies in the P&L. COGS has three components — model costs, hosting costs, and humans in the loop — and each needs a number, a trend line, and an owner from day one; be deeply suspicious of zero- or negative-margin pilots, which are fun to learn from but dangerous to get hooked on. The central bet, 'AI operating leverage,' is that the more the product is built, the lower the COGS and the better the gross margin over time. Traditional services firms top out around 30% margins and pure software/agent companies have higher margins but smaller TAMs; the AI-services wager is reaching software-like margins (50%+) on a market two-to-three times bigger than software — you don't need to be there immediately, but the trajectory must be believable. On pricing, you're competing with the cost of labor, not other software, so sell outcomes rather than seats or tokens. Prefer clean per-unit pricing (per return, per claim, per loan) or incentive-aligned outcome-based pricing, and avoid the two traps: cost-plus pricing permanently caps your upside, and straight-line undercutting signals cheap, low-quality work. Price on value.
