Graham frames this precisely: confidence must be shown, never stated. Saying 'we're passionate about this' is the fastest way to signal the opposite — investors discard the claim and update negatively. Confidence that reads as authentic comes through pacing, directness, and the implicit stance that you're doing investors a favor by telling them about this.

The framing inversion is useful: if you've built something genuinely good, declining to pitch confidently is a disservice to investors who might otherwise miss a good opportunity. This reframe eliminates the apologetic, hedging presentation style that technical founders default to.

Apologetic delivery is especially damaging because it compounds: nervous presentation makes investors doubt both the product and the team's ability to execute under pressure. Conversely, calm confidence makes audiences work to find the merit — they lean in rather than push back.
