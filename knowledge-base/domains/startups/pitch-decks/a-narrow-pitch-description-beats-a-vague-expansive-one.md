Founders resist narrow descriptions because their product feels full of potential — but the attempt to capture all possibilities produces meaningless boilerplate that investors filter out immediately. Graham's calculus analogy is precise: as you describe something that could be anything, the description conveys nothing.

The fix is iterative narrowing: start with a gripping, specific description (even if it undersells scope), then expand from there. This mirrors good software development — ship working code first, add features later. A 'working description' that fits in an investor's head is infinitely more valuable than a technically accurate but unprocessable one.

Practical implication: if you can't describe your startup in one concrete sentence without flinching, that's a signal your plans lack focus — not just your pitch.
