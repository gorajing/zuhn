Most companies claim to be 'easy to use,' 'faster,' or 'more reliable' without evidence. Dunford argues these generic claims actively hurt pitches—they signal that the seller doesn't understand what actually matters to the buyer, and they invite the obvious counter ('everyone says that'). Differentiated value must be specific enough that it can be falsified, and proven enough that it can be verified.

The proof phase of Dunford's framework pairs each differentiation claim with evidence: case studies, metrics, demonstrations, or references from comparable customers. This specificity serves two functions: it filters for buyers who actually need that specific capability (qualifying), and it builds credibility with the buyers who do. A pitch that makes three specific, provable claims outperforms one that makes ten vague ones.
