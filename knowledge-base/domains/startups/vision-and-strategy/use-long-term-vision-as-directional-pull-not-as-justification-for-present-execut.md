---
SpaceX manages the tension between ambitious vision and near-term credibility through strict separation: near-term milestones are concrete and testable, medium-term goals expand the market, and long-term vision (Mars) provides direction without being used to justify today's execution. This sequencing is what makes talking about Mars feel like a direction rather than a deflection.

The failure mode for most ambitious startups is conflating these layers. When founders invoke 10-year vision to explain why last quarter's metrics missed, they contaminate the vision with accountability debt. Investors start discounting the vision because they've learned it's being used as cover.

The fix is structural: in any investor communication, present near-term results and near-term milestones as a self-contained story that stands on its own merits. Then, separately, present the long-term direction as context for why the market matters. Never let the two layers interact causally in a way that uses the big picture to excuse the small picture.
