Google's mission statement, written in 1999 for their first press release after the Series A, has survived unchanged for a quarter century while the company expanded from search to ads, mail, maps, Android, YouTube, cloud, and AI. It satisfies three criteria that most mission statements miss.

First, it's broad enough to cover future pivots without forcing refoundation — any product that organizes or surfaces information fits the thesis. Second, it reads as a public good, which gave Larry and Sergey a recruiting advantage: Jeff Dean, Urs Hölzle, and others joined Google over higher-paying or more stable roles because the mission felt like academic research that would benefit humanity. Third, it maps cleanly onto a monetization mechanism — organizing information creates intent signals, and intent is the most valuable thing to sell to advertisers.

Their S-1 stated it directly: 'We believe that the most effective and ultimately the most profitable way to accomplish our mission is to put the needs of our users first.' The trifecta is: altruistic framing, broad decades-long scope, and a direct causal link from serving users to earning money. When writing a founding mission, check whether all three are present. Missions that sound noble but don't imply a revenue theory recruit well and die broke; missions that describe a revenue model but not a public good fail to attract the talent needed to execute.
