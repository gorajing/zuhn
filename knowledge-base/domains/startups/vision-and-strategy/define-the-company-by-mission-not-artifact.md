Zuckerberg's answer for why Meta keeps winning across Myspace, Twitter, Snapchat, TikTok, ATT, and ChatGPT waves: 'We're a technology company focused on human connection — not a specific type of app.' Building AR glasses is, in his framing, the literal continuation of Facebook's 2004 mission, not a pivot.

The mechanism: a too-narrow self-definition makes any platform shift feel like an identity crisis the org can't authorize itself to navigate. A broader (but still concrete) mission gives the team permission to abandon the current artifact when the substrate moves. Note the trap on the other side — missions stated too abstractly ('we make the world better') give no direction. 'Human connection' is the right grain: specific enough to filter ideas, abstract enough to outlive any particular UI.

For founders, this means writing the mission statement *thinking forward 20 years through likely platform shifts*, not backward from your current product. Ask: 'If phones disappeared tomorrow, would our mission still tell us what to build?'
