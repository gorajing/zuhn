Clay Christensen's 1997 definition of disruptive innovation states they 'involve no new technologies; rather they consist of components built around proven technologies put together in novel architectures.' The M1 chip validates this perfectly — ARM architecture existed since 1983, but nobody combined it with custom silicon design, massive R&D budgets from iPhone revenue, and laptop form factors until Apple did. The disruption wasn't a breakthrough invention; it was patient architectural recombination. This pattern repeats across industries: the disruptor's advantage is usually architectural (how components are assembled) rather than component-level (inventing something new). Founders should look for proven technologies underutilized in adjacent markets rather than chasing novel inventions.
