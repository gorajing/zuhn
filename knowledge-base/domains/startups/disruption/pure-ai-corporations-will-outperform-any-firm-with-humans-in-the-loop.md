Musk uses a sharp analogy: 'computer' used to be a job, and entire buildings held humans doing calculations. A single laptop with Excel replaced all of them. Now imagine a spreadsheet where some cells are still computed by humans — it would be dramatically worse than one fully computed by software. That, he argues, is the position any human-in-the-loop corporation will find itself in within a few years.

This cuts against the dominant narrative that AI will 'augment' human workers. Augmentation is a transitional state; the equilibrium is that pure-AI corporations operate faster, cheaper, and without coordination overhead, so competitive pressure forces full replacement. The implication for startup strategy is stark: build AI-native companies from the ground up rather than retrofitting AI onto human workflows, because the retrofits are structurally handicapped.

The test case is customer service — roughly a trillion-dollar industry with low integration barriers. Once digital human emulation works, a pure-AI customer service firm can underprice incumbents by an order of magnitude with zero integration work, because it just uses the same apps a human would. Apply the same logic to any industry whose output is fundamentally digital (Nvidia FTPing files to Taiwan, Apple sending files to China, Meta, Google, Microsoft).
