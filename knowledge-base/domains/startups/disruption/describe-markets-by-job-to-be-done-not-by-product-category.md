By 1980 quartz watches were cheaper and more accurate than mechanical watches for the functional job of telling time. Every Swiss watch industry reporter knew mechanical was dead. Most Swiss firms panicked into quartz, restructured around fixed-cost semiconductor-style manufacturing, and got rolled up or went bankrupt. Rolex explored quartz (the Oyster Quartz) but refused to commit — they recognized that their cost structure (variable-cost artisan labor) and brand assets (old-world craftsmanship) made them structurally unfit to compete in commodity timekeeping. Meanwhile, Jean-Claude Biver's 1983 Blancpan relaunch demonstrated the counter-move at small scale: 'since 1735 there has never been a Blancpan quartz watch, and there never will be.' The weakness of mechanical watches — inaccurate, labor-intensive, expensive to produce — got reframed into strengths: heritage, craftsmanship, rarity. Joe Thompson called this 'a feat of jiu-jitsu.' The deeper lesson: the same physical object (a wristwatch) filled two completely different jobs — functional timekeeping and status signaling — and the technology disruption killed one while amplifying the other. Firms that defined their market by the object lost. Firms that defined it by the job they filled survived.
