Xin argues that infrastructure became heavyweight mostly because it was originally designed to serve a small number of high-value services — not because of any fundamental technical limitation. The word 'infrastructure' itself evokes PG&E and oil pipelines: durable, expensive, mission-critical. Cloud and agent architectures now make it possible to rethink that assumption.

Agentic coding flips the value distribution: each individual service or experiment may be very low-value, but in aggregate the long tail is very large. That long tail is precisely what most incumbents have never targeted and have a very difficult time transitioning to, because their cost structures and product motions are built for heavyweight, high-value workloads. For founders, this is a classic disruption opening — go after the cheap, numerous, individually-unprofitable-looking workloads that incumbents can't economically serve, and ride them up as they scale.
