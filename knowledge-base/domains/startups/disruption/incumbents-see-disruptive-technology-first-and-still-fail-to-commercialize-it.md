The standard framing of the innovator's dilemma is 'incumbents don't see disruption coming.' TSMC's story shows a more uncomfortable version: incumbents see it, invest in it, and still fail to commercialize it because each quarter the rational decision is to squeeze one more generation out of the proven process. Intel invested heavily in EUV R&D years before TSMC committed production capacity to it. They had the talent, the capital, and a decade's head start in the underlying physics. Yet by 2025 there is still no shipping Intel chip manufactured with Intel's own EUV — every time the decision came up, the counterfactual of 'improve existing lithography one more node' looked safer, cheaper, and higher-yield.

This is identical to Kodak inventing the digital camera in its own lab and then shelving it, and to Blockbuster's early streaming pilots. The pattern is not 'failure to see,' it's 'failure to counterposition against your own profit center.' Once an existing process is generating billions in operating profit, switching to an unproven process requires a CEO to voluntarily cannibalize the business that is paying everyone's bonuses — and no incumbent CEO survives that decision. The disruptive technology always ships first from a company that has no existing profit pool to protect.

Apply this when evaluating whether an incumbent will adopt a technology shift: don't ask 'do they see it' (they almost always do), ask 'does productizing it require cannibalizing their current cash cow.' If yes, they almost certainly won't, regardless of how much they invest in R&D. This is the strongest argument for why startups — not incumbent R&D budgets — are the mechanism by which paradigm shifts actually ship.
