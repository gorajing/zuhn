In one of the video's most counterintuitive examples, Flybe's Bombardier Dash 8 turboprop (top speed 370 mph) flies Edinburgh to London 6 minutes faster than British Airways' A320 jet (top speed 541 mph). On such short routes, aircraft never reach cruising speed — the time is dominated by taxi, takeoff, climb, and descent. The 46% speed advantage of the jet literally doesn't matter.

This principle generalizes to any domain where the 'route' is short enough that the performance ceiling is never reached. In software, a faster algorithm on tiny datasets loses to a simpler one with less overhead. In delivery logistics, a faster vehicle on last-mile routes loses to a cheaper one that can make more runs. Electric aircraft could exploit this by dominating the 200-400 mile market on pure cost, despite being slower. For startups, this means identifying segments where the incumbent's premium capability is structurally wasted, then competing on the axis that actually matters in that segment.
