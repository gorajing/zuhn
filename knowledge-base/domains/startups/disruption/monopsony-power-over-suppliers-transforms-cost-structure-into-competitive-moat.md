Ryanair's airport strategy is a masterclass in supplier power inversion. By choosing small regional airports (Beauvais instead of CDG, Stansted instead of Heathrow), they become the airport's most important or only significant customer. This creates an asymmetric dependency: Ryanair can credibly threaten to leave, which would devastate the airport's economics, while the airport has no equivalent leverage.

This is a deliberate monopsony strategy—becoming a buyer so dominant that you dictate terms. The airports offer lower landing fees, favorable scheduling, and operational flexibility because the alternative is economic irrelevance. For startups, this principle suggests actively seeking underserved suppliers or channels where you can become the dominant customer, rather than competing for attention from suppliers who serve many large buyers. The cost advantage this creates compounds over time as the supplier relationship deepens and switching costs rise on both sides.
