Graham's observation is subtle: the iPhone is not really a phone — it's a small tablet that can also make calls. We call it a phone only because the iPhone preceded the iPad. If the iPad had come first, we would have called the iPhone a 'pocket tablet' or similar. The category we assign to a product is a function of what we saw first, not the product's intrinsic nature.

This anchoring bias is consequential for founders and investors. The frame you put around a product shapes what you compare it to, what features seem important, what competitors you track, and what market size you estimate. A product framed as 'a better phone' competes differently than one framed as 'a general-purpose pocket computer.' Deliberately questioning the inherited category — asking 'what is this actually replacing?' rather than 'what does it resemble?' — often reveals larger addressable markets and less obvious competitive dynamics.
