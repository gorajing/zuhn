The dinosaur extinction is the ultimate case study in incumbent disruption. 165 million years of dominance provided zero protection against an external shock that changed the rules of the game entirely. The survivors — small mammals and birds — were not the dominant species but the ones whose characteristics happened to be adaptive in the new environment. This maps precisely to market disruption: Kodak's 100-year dominance didn't protect against digital photography. Nokia's market share didn't predict smartphone survival. The lesson for startup strategy is that incumbent longevity is not a moat — it's often the opposite, creating rigidity and over-optimization for the current paradigm. The question isn't 'how strong is the incumbent?' but 'what exogenous shock could change which traits are adaptive?'
