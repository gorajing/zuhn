In spring 1997, Larry and Sergey tried to sell back rub to Excite for ~$1M. In the final demo, Excite's CEO saw that Google's algorithm delivered the right answer immediately while Excite's forced users to click around. His verdict: 'Why on earth would we move to your algorithm? I want people to stay on my site. I make money when people stay on my site.' The deal died on the spot, and similar rejections came from Yahoo, Infoseek, and Lycos.

The lesson: incumbent rejection of superior technology is rarely about taste, NIH syndrome, or missing the signal. It's about precise incentive misalignment. Every portal in 1997 monetized through CPM banner ads — they were paid per impression, so anything that reduced impressions destroyed revenue. Better search was strictly negative within their business model.

For operators: when pitching a better technology to an incumbent, check whether adoption would reduce the metric their revenue depends on. If yes, the pitch is dead regardless of how good the tech is — and you should instead build a direct competitor, because their incentive structure guarantees they cannot respond. This is the structural origin of most successful disruption stories, not just Google's.
