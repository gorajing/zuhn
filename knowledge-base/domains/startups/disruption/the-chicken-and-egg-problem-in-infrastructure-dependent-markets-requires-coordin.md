To match the average American's 4-minute proximity to gas stations, Tesla would need 31,251 additional Supercharging stations at $250,000 each ($7.8 billion total), serving only 750,000 cars—one $250,000 station per 23 vehicles. The stations could never break even at that utilization rate. This is the classic chicken-and-egg: you need infrastructure to sell cars but can't justify infrastructure without cars.

For startup strategists, this illustrates why infrastructure-dependent disruptions often require either government coordination, industry consortia, or creative business models that decouple infrastructure economics from direct usage revenue. Companies entering such markets should evaluate whether they can bootstrap density in constrained geographies (as Tesla did with corridor-based Supercharger placement) rather than attempting uniform national coverage, or whether regulatory mandates can create shared infrastructure that benefits all entrants.
