Samuel Gong highlighted that many companies claiming graphene batteries are actually using graphite — graphene's three-dimensional cousin that lacks the conductivity and strength benefits. This 'graphene-washing' creates market confusion but also creates an unexpected advantage for authentic producers: being genuinely first-to-market with real graphene means you can define the category and establish trust before the market learns to distinguish real from fake.

This pattern generalizes beyond batteries. Whenever a breakthrough technology generates hype, incumbents rush to associate their products with the buzzword through misleading claims. The first authentic entrant who can prove their technology is real — through third-party testing, transparent manufacturing processes, or measurable performance metrics — builds a verification moat. The confusion itself becomes a barrier that protects authentic first movers from fast followers who might otherwise quickly replicate the approach.
