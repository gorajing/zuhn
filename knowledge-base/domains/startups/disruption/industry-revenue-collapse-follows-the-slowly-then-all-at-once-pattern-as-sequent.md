The Hemingway bankruptcy quote — 'gradually, then suddenly' — describes exactly what happened to print media. Classifieds moved to the internet first (Craigslist, early 2000s). Then display advertising migrated as digital CPMs fell. Then the newsstand habit broke when smartphones arrived. Each pillar fell on a slightly different timeline, giving incumbents the illusion of manageable decline. But each lost revenue stream reduced the margin to absorb the next hit.

This pattern is useful for analyzing any incumbent under technological pressure. The question isn't 'is this company dying?' but 'how many revenue pillars are still standing, and what's the sequence in which they'll fall?' For newspapers, editors and publishers saw each transition as a distinct problem to solve individually (new classifieds product, digital advertising strategy, app strategy) rather than recognizing them as sequential stages of a unified collapse. Founders disrupting incumbents should deliberately target the highest-margin revenue pillar first, not the easiest — because removing the structural subsidy triggers cascading failure in the remaining components.
