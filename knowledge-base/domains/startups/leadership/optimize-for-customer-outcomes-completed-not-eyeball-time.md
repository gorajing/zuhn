On the cannibalization fear — that agents doing tasks (answering email, searching) reduce human eyeball-hours on your products — Kilpatrick argues the goal of the technology is 'so it can go and do stuff for you.' Success for Google 'probably doesn't look like maximizing eyeball time in front of our products; it's maximizing outcome for customers to do the thing they want so they can go and live their life.' He expects Google to deliberately go 'down the route of maximizing outcomes for customers and not maximizing eyeballs.'

This is a counterintuitive stance from inside an engagement-and-ads-driven business, and it carries an empirical backstop: everyone assumed AI answering questions would be negative-sum for search, but it turned out 'incredibly positive sum' — people search more, agents search too, and a whole new market spawned. The leadership lesson is to choose the north-star metric that matches an agentic future: completed customer outcomes rather than attention captured. Picking the wrong metric (eyeballs) makes you defend a shrinking surface and miss the positive-sum expansion. The note of caution: human-behavior effects are 'somewhat clear' over 1-2 years but 'much less clear' over 3-5, so treat outcome-orientation as the durable bet while staying humble about second-order dynamics.
