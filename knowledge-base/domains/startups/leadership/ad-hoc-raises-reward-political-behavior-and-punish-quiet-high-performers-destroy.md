Horowitz identifies a powerful second-order effect: when one employee gets a raise by asking, every other employee in the company learns that not asking means potentially missing compensation they deserve. This creates what he calls a 'fiduciary responsibility to their family to ask for a raise all the time.' The cultural conclusion is devastating — you've replaced performance evaluation with a lobbying system. The solution is formal periodic reviews where all employees are evaluated simultaneously. This feels bureaucratic to startup founders who want to stay organic, but the process actually protects culture by ensuring compensation tracks performance rather than assertiveness. Paradoxically, employees become more comfortable with formal processes because they don't have to worry about whether their personality type disadvantages them.
