Skunk Works Rule 14 is a direct counter to the default corporate pattern: 'Ways must be provided to reward good performance by pay not based on number of personnel supervised.' Kelly explained it bluntly: 'In the main plant they give raises on the basis of the more people supervised. I give raises to the guy who supervises the least. But most executives don't think like that at all — they're empire builders.'

The incentive is self-reinforcing. If promotion and pay track headcount, every manager has a personal reason to hire even when they don't need to, and to oppose any restructuring that would shrink their team. Over time this produces the bloated middle layers that every large company eventually has to fire. If instead you reward impact divided by team size, the best managers actively try to get smaller while delivering more — the same principle that makes capital-efficient startups valuable.

The practical implication: audit your promotion criteria. If 'grew their team from 5 to 15' is a promo bullet, you are building a pyramid scheme. Replace it with impact-per-head or revenue-per-head metrics that reward the opposite behavior.
