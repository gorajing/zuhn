Nejatian's second core principle: defaults in life are as powerful as defaults in software. People accept nearly every default they're given, and if you start rejecting too many, you 'fire' your current life. He lasted 11 days at a private equity firm (the default career path from business school) before recognizing it wasn't what he wanted — and quit with full force.

The critical insight is that you can't half-override a default. The wall of convention will stop you unless you go at it with everything. You can realistically change one or two defaults if you're deliberate about them. This has product-building implications too: your responsibility in setting defaults is enormous, because users will accept whatever you give them. Set bad defaults and they'll just fire your software. The constraint — pick your non-default choices carefully and commit totally — is what makes them work.
