McDermott's framing as a young Xerox rep was explicit: 'Performance is the price of freedom. I'll be number one in the country or number one in the world, just let me run.' He treated time in internal meetings as theft from his customers, and his managers accepted the bargain because they got outsized numbers in return. The generalizable lesson: high performers will pay for autonomy with results, and bureaucratic companies systematically trade that deal away.

The deeper point is about unleashing 'animal instinct' — the willingness to make decisions in the field without seeking permission. McDermott's operational rule is 'it's easier to beg for forgiveness than ask for permission. I'd rather have roughly right executed right now than perfect two weeks from now.' In fast-moving markets, the cost of delay dominates the cost of suboptimal decisions, so the manager's job is to compress the decision loop, not to gate it.

For founders: resist the urge to pull your best salespeople (or best engineers, or best operators) into coordination meetings and review processes. If you need to verify they're on track, verify the outcome, not the activity. The performers who are worth keeping will opt in to this deal eagerly; the ones who prefer the meetings were never going to post the numbers anyway.
