When a great employee asks for a raise, the CEO faces enormous pressure to say yes — the employee is valuable, they clearly feel strongly about it, and granting the raise creates immediate goodwill. But the employee who didn't ask and may be performing equally well or better learns that the company rewards lobbying, not performance. This creates a cultural mandate: every employee now has a fiduciary duty to their family to regularly ask for raises, since not asking means potentially missing out. Horowitz's solution is a formal evaluation process — periodic, comprehensive, input-gathering — that explicitly decouples compensation decisions from individual requests. The process evaluates everyone simultaneously using consistent criteria. This feels bureaucratic to startup founders who prize organic culture, but it's actually the process that protects the culture by ensuring performance, not political skill, determines compensation.
