Many novice leaders, especially in Korean cultural contexts, believe they need objective evidence before delivering difficult feedback. This creates a destructive cycle: they delay the conversation while searching for unchallengeable proof, meanwhile the discomfort compounds internally. By the time they finally speak up, they've accumulated so much resentment that the feedback explodes rather than lands constructively.

Han Gi-yong's hard-won insight: there is no such thing as objective evidence that everyone will accept. Judgment about people and their work is inherently subjective. The worst thing a leader can do is stall for time — they keep the discomfort at bay while hating the person more inside. The answer is trusting your own judgment and expressing concerns early, before emotions become too deep. Excessive humility leads to self-censorship, which erodes confidence in your own judgment, which deepens the emotional rift and wastes the leader's most important asset: time.
