When an excellent employee asks for a raise, the natural impulse is to say yes — they deserve it, you want to retain them, and the immediate outcome is positive. But the employee who didn't ask and may be performing better now learns that advocacy, not performance, drives compensation. This creates a fiduciary obligation for every employee to constantly lobby for raises, poisoning the culture. The solution is a formal evaluation process — periodic, comprehensive, input-gathering — that makes asking irrelevant. Counterintuitively, this process makes employees more comfortable because they know evaluation is systematic rather than political. As Horowitz notes, even startup founders who resist process need this one because the cultural cost of ad-hoc compensation is a forest fire that grows silently.
