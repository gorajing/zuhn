When a recession hit TSMC in 2008, the acting CEO laid off 600-700 people under the cover of 'worst performance ratings.' Chang had explicitly told him in advance not to do this and to bring any layoff to the board. The CEO circumvented the guardrail by reframing it as performance-based firing. Chang returned as CEO in 2009, rehired anyone who wanted to come back, and made this one of the reasons he took the job back.

Chang's argument has two prongs. First, the math: severance is typically about half a year of salary, and it takes at least another half year to train a replacement. So if you will need that headcount back within a year — and in a cyclical industry with Moore's Law driving constant expansion, you will — laying off destroys value instead of saving it. Second, credibility: 700 ratings come from 700 different supervisors, each applying subjective judgment. People can accept being laid off for economic reasons, but they cannot respect being singled out as 'worst performers' by a system everyone knows is subjective. The company's standing with its own workforce collapses.

The generalizable rule: when a downturn hits a cyclical business, run the replacement-cost math before any layoff. If you will rehire within a year, the only honest alternatives are probation, transfers, and absorbing the margin hit.
