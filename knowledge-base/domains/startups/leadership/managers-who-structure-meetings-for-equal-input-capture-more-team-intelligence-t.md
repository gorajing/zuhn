Standard meeting formats have a structural bias: they reward speed of response and comfort with verbal assertion, which systematically underweights input from introverted team members who may have the deepest analysis. Cain recommends managers use round-robin formats or collect written ideas before meetings to equalize participation.

This matters because roughly 30-50% of people are introverts, meaning open-floor meetings may be systematically ignoring half the team's intelligence. The Wharton research Cain references (Grant's ambivert advantage paper) suggests the best outcomes come from balanced teams—but only if the meeting structure actually captures input from both temperaments. A manager who defaults to 'whoever speaks first' is unknowingly degrading their team's collective decision quality.
