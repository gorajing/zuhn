Atul Gawande, a surgeon and author of 'The Checklist Manifesto,' describes hitting a mid-career plateau around 10-12 years into practice. Despite assiduously tracking his surgical complications, improvements had flatlined. He invited a former professor to observe him during a thyroidectomy he considered excellent.

The coach found 20 minutes of specific feedback: the surgical light had drifted off course, the anesthesiologist struggled with blood pressure for 10 minutes without communicating it, and Gawande's elbow was rising due to foot positioning. Monthly coaching sessions produced lower complication rates and better outcomes. Itzhak Perlman, the greatest violinist of his generation, had his wife — a Juilliard-trained violinist — observe every performance and provide feedback throughout his career. The pattern is universal: self-assessment has a ceiling; external observation breaks through it.
