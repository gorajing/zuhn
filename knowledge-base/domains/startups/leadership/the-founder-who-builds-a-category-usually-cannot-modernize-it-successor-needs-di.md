When Liberty Media acquired F1, they initially kept Bernie Ecclestone as CEO with Chase Carey as chairman. Within months it became obvious there was no room for two bosses: as long as Bernie held the reins, the strategic moves Liberty needed — fixing team relationships, partnering with race promoters, letting drivers post on Instagram, greenlighting Drive to Survive — were structurally blocked. His entire identity and network depended on controlling access, charging maximum race fees, and keeping the sport behind a paywall. Liberty fired him. The modernization the sport needed could not happen with him in the room.

This is the cleanest real-world illustration of 'what got you here won't get you there.' The traits that make a founder successful in the build phase — total control, ruthless negotiation, personal relationships with every stakeholder, willingness to extract value — become the exact constraints blocking the scale or platform phase. The founder isn't wrong; their strategy was correct for its era. But their mental model, relationships, and reflexes are calibrated to a world that no longer exists.

Application for operators and boards: don't mistake loyalty to a founder for strategic clarity about the next era. The question isn't 'is this person smart and valuable?' — they almost always are. The question is: 'Can the moves required in the next five years be made by someone whose identity depends on the moves of the last fifteen?' When the answer is no, the transition is not a betrayal; it's a structural necessity.
