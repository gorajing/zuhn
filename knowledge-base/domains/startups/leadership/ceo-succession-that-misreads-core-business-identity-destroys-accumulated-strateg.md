By 2007, Blockbuster had built an internet movie component that was actually outcompeting Netflix. Netflix's own management wanted to sell to Blockbuster. Then a boardroom dispute installed James Keyes, formerly of 7-Eleven, who saw Blockbuster as a retail business rather than an entertainment company. He deprioritized the online component, and within 18 months lost 85% of company value. Within three years, Blockbuster filed for bankruptcy.

This is a distinct failure mode from missing a trend — Blockbuster had already caught the trend and was winning. The destruction came from a leadership change that misread the company's core identity. The new CEO's mental model (retail) was incompatible with the strategic direction (digital entertainment) that was actually working.

For founders and boards, this highlights that CEO succession is not just about competence but about whether the incoming leader's mental model of the business matches its actual competitive position. A competent retail executive was the worst possible choice for a company whose survival depended on digital entertainment transition.
