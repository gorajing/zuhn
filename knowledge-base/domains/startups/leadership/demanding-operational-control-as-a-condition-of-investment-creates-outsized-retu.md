Morgan distinguished himself from other financiers by refusing to be a passive investor. When struggling railroads came to him for financing, he would only help if given full operational control, including the power to reshuffle leadership and direct corporate strategy. This wasn't ego for its own sake; it was a mechanism to ensure his capital was deployed according to his vision.

This created a virtuous cycle: Morgan's active involvement stabilized companies, which attracted more European investment capital, which made him wealthier and more influential, which gave him leverage to demand even more control in future deals. The lesson for modern investors and founders is that capital alone is often insufficient. The combination of capital plus operational authority plus strategic vision is what produces transformational outcomes rather than incremental ones.
