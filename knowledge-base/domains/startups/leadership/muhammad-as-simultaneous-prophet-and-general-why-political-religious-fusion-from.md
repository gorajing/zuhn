Jesus never ran a country; Muhammad did almost from the beginning. This founder-era difference created a permanent structural divergence: Christianity developed separate traditions of civic and religious law, while Islam's sharia encompasses both domains. The pattern wasn't an accident of theology — it was a consequence of the founder's lived role.

This illustrates a powerful principle in institutional design: whatever the founder does becomes the template. If the founder combines two functions, the organization will treat those functions as inseparable. If the founder delegates early, the organization will expect delegation. Path dependence is strongest at the founding moment, and the cost of restructuring increases with every generation that normalizes the original pattern.
