When Gawande expanded his coaching goals beyond personal technique to teaching, his coach identified a critical pattern: when trainees encountered bleeding, Gawande would immediately reach in and handle it himself. The fix was deceptively simple — count to 30 in your head before intervening.

Gawande admits he never quite reached 30, but the delay was enough to let trainees struggle and learn. This is a universal management pattern: the instinct to jump in and solve problems for your team feels productive but prevents their development. The count-to-30 rule works because it creates a structured pause between the trigger (seeing someone struggle) and the habitual response (doing it yourself). The discomfort of watching someone struggle is the cost of their growth.
