Centivax's founder identified the core challenge of managing genius teams: ideas in science are closely tethered to ego, so debate easily becomes personal. His solution was modeling the desired behavior from the top — publicly saying 'that's much nicer, let's do that instead' when someone corrected his strategy or offered a better approach.

The mechanism is specific: it's not just saying 'we value disagreement' — it's the leader visibly and repeatedly demonstrating that being corrected is rewarded, not merely tolerated. This creates a template others follow, turning the organization into a collective intelligence system where ideas are 'pulled out and shared by all, gathered around and chipped at from all sides.' The insight applies to any knowledge-intensive team where the quality of decisions depends on surfacing the best ideas regardless of hierarchy.
