Only about a third of family businesses survive the second generation, and another 50% don't last to the third. In-N-Out defied these odds through six leadership changes — including three deaths, an interim outside president, and a generational handoff to a 30-year-old heir. The company emerged from each transition with its core values intact.

The key mechanism was structural: the values weren't just cultural, they were encoded into operational constraints. The no-franchising rule, the distribution radius limit, the minimal menu — these aren't just preferences, they're structural guardrails that prevent any new leader from easily abandoning the founding philosophy. When Lynsi Snyder inherited control, the business model itself resisted deviation. This suggests that founders who want their values to outlast them should encode those values into operational structure, not just mission statements.
