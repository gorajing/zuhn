Intelligence professionals follow two complementary razors: Occam's (simplest answer is usually correct) and what might be called the 'competency razor'—never prescribe to conspiracy what can be explained through incompetence. Bustamante calls this a principle backed by 'ages of experience' in intelligence work. The reasoning is that carrying out a complex coordinated lie requires a level of organizational competency that most groups simply don't possess.

Applied to the 9/11 commission findings, the intelligence community's failures spoke 'brashly and openly to incompetence'—FBI and CIA weren't coordinating, there was infighting, poor intel sharing. The same pattern repeated with Afghanistan, Hong Kong, and Ukraine misjudgments. For startup founders and organizational leaders, this razor is invaluable: when things go wrong, the instinct is to assume malicious coordination (a competitor sabotaged us, employees conspired against us). The far more likely and productive diagnosis is incompetence—poor communication, misaligned incentives, lack of capability. Diagnosing incompetence leads to fixable solutions; diagnosing conspiracy leads to paranoia and witch hunts.
