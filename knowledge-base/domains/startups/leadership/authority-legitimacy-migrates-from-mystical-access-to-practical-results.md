In early Mesopotamian city-states, priests held power because they claimed direct communication with capricious gods — an unfalsifiable credential. Over roughly a thousand years, power migrated to kings who could point to concrete results: military victories, economic growth, and territorial expansion.

This mirrors how organizational authority evolves. Early-stage companies often defer to whoever 'gets' the vision (the visionary founder, the domain mystic). As organizations mature, authority shifts toward those who deliver measurable results. The Mesopotamian kings even co-opted the priestly role through 'sacred marriage,' just as modern leaders adopt the language of their predecessors while redirecting its purpose — a pattern worth recognizing when navigating power transitions.
