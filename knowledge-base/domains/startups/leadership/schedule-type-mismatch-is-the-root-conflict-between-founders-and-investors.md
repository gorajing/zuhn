Graham identifies that most powerful people — executives, investors, board members — operate on manager's schedules, and because they hold positional authority, their scheduling preferences become the organizational default. This creates a structural disadvantage for makers who need long uninterrupted blocks.

The insight extends beyond individual productivity into organizational design. Companies that recognize this mismatch and protect maker time gain a structural advantage in output quality. Y Combinator's deliberate choice to run on a maker's schedule, despite being an investor, is presented as a competitive differentiation — and Graham predicts more companies will follow this pattern as founders resist the transition to managerial rhythms.
