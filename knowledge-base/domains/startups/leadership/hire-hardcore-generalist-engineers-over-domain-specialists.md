Seo states flatly that Neuralink requires no neuroscience background and that some of its best people are simply hardcore engineers. The reasoning is that the genuinely hard, unsolved problems are engineering challenges — miniaturized low-power electronics, the surgical robot, manufacturing at scale, firmware and software — and that neuroscience can be picked up by a strong engineer in context. The most acute current need he names is software and firmware.

This inverts the intuitive hiring filter for a brain-computer-interface company. Rather than gating on the most specialized credential, the company gates on general engineering horsepower and treats domain knowledge as something the team 'learns as we go.' For a deep-tech startup, the binding constraint is rarely the supply of domain experts; it is the supply of people who can actually build hard physical and software systems, and those people are both rarer and harder to upskill.

A further wrinkle Seo raises: as neural data scales, a new hard problem emerges — labeling it, since you cannot directly know a human's true intent — which is fundamentally a data and ML engineering problem, not a neuroscience one. This reinforces the thesis that the talent the company most needs is general technical depth, recruited to compete against frontier AI labs for the same engineers.
