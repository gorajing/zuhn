Zuckerberg articulates a profound leadership principle through his jiu-jitsu experience: the thing that stops most successful adults from growing is refusing to be a beginner again. After building an identity and expertise, people resist looking stupid. But every meaningful new capability — whether in martial arts, business, or technology — requires starting from zero.

He applies this directly to running Meta, describing how they take on 10-plus-year missions where early work looks silly and people doubt them. Their ethos of shipping imperfect work quickly for feedback is the organizational equivalent of being willing to get your ass kicked on the mat. The cycle time is what matters — jiu-jitsu gives you feedback in seconds, while business gives it in months or years. Physical disciplines provide rapid, unambiguous humbling that keeps leaders calibrated.
