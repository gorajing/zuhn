Despite being perhaps the greatest business operator of his era, Rockefeller was a poor investor. His personal investments outside Standard Oil were largely failing. His solution was counter-intuitive: he hired Frederick Gates, a Baptist minister with no formal business training, to manage his fortune. Gates turned out to be brilliant — he orchestrated the Mesabi iron range acquisition, restructured the portfolio, and trained Rockefeller Jr. to continue the work.

The insight is twofold. First, operational excellence and investment acumen are different skills — being great at building a business doesn't automatically make you great at allocating capital across businesses. Second, the best person for a role often comes from unexpected backgrounds. Rockefeller's willingness to delegate to Gates (and later his 27-year-old son) rather than insisting on personal control over every dollar was what enabled his wealth to compound across industries and generations. Modern founders often make the inverse mistake: assuming their startup success qualifies them as investment geniuses.
