Stauch contrasts the old mental model — 'I am building software that's going to last for 20 years' — with the AI-native one: 'I'm going to build software that hopefully will last 6 months, and then I might have to rebuild it once the paradigm shifts.' Being AI-native, he argues, isn't only about the product being AI-native; it's about the organization being willing to reinvent and disrupt itself far faster than companies ever have. They actively uproot things they were convinced were true months ago, rename parts of the product, and shift how things work 'over and over and over again.'

The structural reason is the buffer role Serval plays: foundation-model capabilities change very fast, customers change slowly, and the company in the middle is the translation layer that keeps customers from drowning in the downpour of new capabilities. To keep absorbing rapid upstream change without overwhelming downstream customers, the org itself has to be able to re-architect on short cycles.

For leadership this reframes the job: the scarce capability isn't a durable plan but the cultural and structural capacity to abandon yesterday's plan gracefully. Stauch ties this to keeping the team small and dense (easier to turn the ship) and to hiring people who thrive in ambiguity — Serval explicitly isn't a place for clear career paths, heavy mentorship, or a fixed org chart, because the functions themselves keep changing. The leadership norm is to make self-disruption expected and low-drama rather than a periodic crisis.
