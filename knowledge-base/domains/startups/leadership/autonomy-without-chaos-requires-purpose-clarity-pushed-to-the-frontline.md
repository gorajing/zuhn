Joly empowered all Best Buy employees — especially customer-facing staff — to make decisions autonomously by ensuring they deeply understood the company's purpose, values, and strategy. Managers' approval wasn't required; employees only needed to consider their manager's input.

This reframes the autonomy problem: most leaders think the bottleneck is trust or competence, but Joly's model shows it's shared context. When everyone holds the same strategic frame, coordination emerges without centralized control. The prerequisite is a high tolerance for failure — you can't grant autonomy and then punish the inevitable mistakes that come with distributed decision-making.
