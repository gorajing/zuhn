Lubetzky observes across industries — startups, restaurants, global corporations — that founder departure triggers an irreversible energy loss. His framing ('when the founder leaves, the soul leaves too') points to something deeper than management competence: founders carry the original problem-obsession, taste, and willingness to make irrational bets that created the company's differentiation.

Professional managers optimize what exists. They reduce variance, improve margins, and professionalize operations. But they rarely generate the creative tension that produced the company's original value. This is why Lubetzky only invests in companies still led by entrepreneurs who remain close to the product — he's filtering for ongoing creative energy, not just operational excellence.

This has practical implications for both investors and founders. For investors, founder departure should be treated as a material risk event that fundamentally changes the investment thesis. For founders, it suggests that succession planning should focus on finding builder-type leaders rather than professional managers, and that the transition period requires deliberate cultural preservation mechanisms.
