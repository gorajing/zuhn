When Apple asked TSMC to develop a custom 20nm node in 2011, TSMC needed to raise billions beyond its already-doubled capex. Chang immediately turned to Goldman Sachs for advice on whether to cut dividends, issue stock, or borrow. But Goldman was not a cold call — Chang had built the relationship years earlier by routing TSMC's ADR listing through Goldman and even serving on Goldman's board. He called these deliberate early relationships 'planted seeds.'

The Jensen Huang relationship follows the same pattern. Chang took an unknown, near-bankrupt NVIDIA seriously in 1997 on the basis of a single letter, gave Jensen his direct attention, and built a personal rapport deep enough that a decade later he could resolve a hundred-million-dollar dispute over pizza and salad at Jensen's house. The relationship infrastructure already existed; the conflict conversation slotted into it.

The principle is that crisis-time asks require trust-time deposits. You cannot call a world-class advisor, customer, or partner for the first time when you need them to move fast on a high-stakes decision. They will either not take the meeting or not have the context to help. The only way is to deliberately maintain a portfolio of senior relationships during calm periods — accept the board seat, take the meeting, keep the dinner tradition alive — so that when something goes wrong, the channel is already open. This is an operating discipline, not a personality trait. It requires scheduling time for relationships that have no immediate business justification.
