Horowitz relays Bill Campbell's principle: you can take someone's job, but you don't have to take their dignity. When communicating a firing to the company, the instinct is often either to trash the person (to justify the decision) or to be so vague it creates confusion. The right approach is to thank them for their work, let people know they're moving on, and not share personal details. The multi-perspective reasoning is powerful: whatever you say at that meeting becomes the terminated person's reputation, because every employee in your company will eventually get a reference call. If you publicly humiliate them, it won't be interpreted as 'we made a hiring mistake' — it will be heard as 'they screwed up,' poisoning their career. Meanwhile, every remaining employee watches how you treat departing colleagues and calibrates their own loyalty accordingly. The honest conversation happens privately with the individual; the public message preserves dignity.
