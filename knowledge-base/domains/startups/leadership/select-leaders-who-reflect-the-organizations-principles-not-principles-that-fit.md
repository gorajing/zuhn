This is a distinctive formulation because it inverts the default pattern in most organizations, where the principles implicitly bend around whoever the current CEO is. In a principle-first organization, candidates are filtered for fit to the principles before any other consideration; in a leader-first organization, the principles get rewritten each time a new leader arrives with different priorities. Berkshire's approach is only possible because the principles are explicit (see the six-values framework in the same letter), stable across decades, and validated by a founder who wrote them into the operating model long before the succession. The lesson generalizes: if you want cultural continuity across leadership changes, make the values explicit and non-negotiable BEFORE the transition, not during or after. Organizations that let the values drift to match the new leader lose their cultural moat within one or two CEO cycles.
