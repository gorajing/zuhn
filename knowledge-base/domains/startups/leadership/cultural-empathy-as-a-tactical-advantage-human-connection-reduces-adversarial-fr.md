Gabbard describes how soldiers from Hawaii, Samoa, and Guam naturally adopted a relationship-first approach with Iraqi locals: pointing weapons skyward while driving through towns (rather than at pedestrians), throwing shakas, breaking bread, and sharing tea. The previous unit had operated from an assumption of suspicion. The result was measurable: a downward shift in casualties, because locals started proactively warning American soldiers about IEDs and weapons caches.

The principle scales far beyond military contexts. In any environment where you're entering someone else's territory -- a new market, a new team, an acquisition -- the instinct to lead with suspicion and control (pointing the gun at people) creates adversarial dynamics. Leading with genuine relationship-building (pointing the gun at the sky) reduces friction and creates information advantages. The locals had the intelligence about threats all along; the question was whether they'd share it, and that depended entirely on trust.
