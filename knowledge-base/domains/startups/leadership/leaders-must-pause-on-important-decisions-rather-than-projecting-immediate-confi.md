Leaders feel compelled to have immediate answers—it signals competence and decisiveness. But Horowitz argues this instinct is actively destructive for complex management decisions. When a great employee asks for a raise, the emotional pressure to respond immediately is enormous: you don't want to lose them, they clearly thought hard about it, and saying yes creates instant goodwill. But the right answer requires modeling how the entire company will interpret your decision, which you cannot do in real-time under emotional pressure.

The practical discipline is to say: 'I'm taking this seriously, but I need to pause and think it through from all perspectives.' Most CEOs learn this the hard way—they make 3-4 snap decisions that seem fine in the moment, then explode weeks or months later as the cultural consequences ripple through the organization. Horowitz calls it 'the kimchi problem: the deeper you bury it, the hotter it gets.' Bill Campbell, whom Horowitz considers the best practitioner of this skill, built his legendary coaching reputation primarily on this one ability: seeing the company through the eyes of all employees before acting.
