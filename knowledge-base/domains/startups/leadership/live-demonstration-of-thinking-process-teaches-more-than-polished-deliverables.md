Malan's signature move is coding live in front of 800 students, narrating his thought process as he goes — including moments where he pauses, reconsiders, or makes mistakes. This 'thinking out loud' approach lets students see the cognitive process behind programming, not just the product. They learn that experienced programmers also encounter confusion, try approaches that don't work, and iterate toward solutions.

This has direct implications for startup leadership and mentorship. Leaders who only share polished strategy documents or finished code teach their teams what good output looks like, but not how to produce it. Leaders who think out loud during problem-solving — showing their heuristics, their uncertainty, their course corrections — give their teams a template for expert reasoning. The vulnerability of showing imperfect thinking is what makes it powerful: it demystifies expertise and makes it learnable. In practice, consider pair-programming sessions, live architecture discussions, or narrated decision-making as leadership tools.
