Many new leaders mistake busyness for the reason for their existence. They find their value in solving team members' problems directly — every time someone asks for help, they jump in. This feels productive but has compounding costs: the leader burns out, team members stop growing, and the team's talent density drops because everyone depends on one person.

The fix is to redefine what 'busy' means as a leader. Han Gi-yong shifted 40% of his time to meeting potential hires — lunches with candidates, relationship-building before roles opened. The leader's job is not to solve problems but to set clear goals, drive the team toward them, and build a team strong enough that the leader is not needed for daily execution. If everyone comes to you with every problem, that's proof the team's talent density is too low.
