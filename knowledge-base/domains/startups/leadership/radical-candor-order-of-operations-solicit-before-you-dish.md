Scott prescribes a specific order of operations for radical candor: (1) solicit feedback — ask what you could do or stop doing, (2) give praise that is specific and genuine, not a head-fake, (3) deliver criticism directly, (4) gauge how your feedback landed by watching the listener's reaction.

The key insight is that radical candor is measured 'not in the speaker's mouth but at the listener's ear.' If someone seems sad after feedback, move up on caring personally. If they seem mad, get curious not furious — ask why. If they brush you off, push further on the challenge-directly axis. This makes feedback a dynamic, responsive conversation rather than a one-directional dump.
