Harris traces Trump's 'never apologize, never admit error' approach to Roy Cohn's explicit advice. The SignalGate incident illustrates this: a simple acknowledgment ('we made a mistake, we'll fix it') would have been costless, yet the administration chose denial. Harris notes even right-wing commentators recognized this as an easy win that was refused.

This pattern has direct relevance to leadership and crisis management in any organization. The never-apologize strategy works in contexts with weak accountability — when there are no competitors, no board oversight, no regulatory scrutiny. It fails catastrophically when accountability mechanisms engage because the strategy prevents the small course corrections that prevent large failures. Every unacknowledged error compounds, creating a growing gap between stated and actual reality. In startups and business, leaders who refuse to acknowledge mistakes create information vacuums where problems metastasize unchecked. The cost of early admission is almost always lower than the cost of eventual forced disclosure.
