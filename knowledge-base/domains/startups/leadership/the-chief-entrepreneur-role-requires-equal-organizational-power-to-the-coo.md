Osterwalder and Pigneur insist that the Chief Entrepreneur must have 'prestige and power'—not just a title, but genuine organizational authority equal to the execution side of the house. This is a specific structural prescription, not just a platitude about valuing innovation.

The reasoning is that execution engines naturally produce organizational antibodies against innovation. Established business units optimize for efficiency, predictability, and zero failure—values that are directly hostile to experimentation, failure, and learning. Without a powerful champion at the top, innovation initiatives get starved of resources, talent, and attention. The execution engine doesn't even need to be hostile—it just needs to be successful and demanding, and it will absorb all available oxygen. This is why the letter frames it as needing someone 'schizophrenic' to do both jobs—the mindsets are genuinely incompatible, and the solution is structural separation with equal authority.
