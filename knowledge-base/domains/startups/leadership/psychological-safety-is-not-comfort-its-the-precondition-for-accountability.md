Edmondson's 2x2 maps psychological safety against accountability for excellence. Low safety + low standards = apathy zone. High safety + low standards = comfort zone (leaving money on the table). Low safety + high standards = anxiety zone (where the nurse, pilot, and executive all silenced themselves). Only high safety + high standards = the learning zone, which is also the high-performance zone.

This reframing is critical because managers hear 'psychological safety' and fear they'll have to lower the bar. Edmondson's response: 'I'm talking about letting up on the brakes, not the gas.' Safety removes the friction of impression management so people can channel energy into actual work instead of self-protection. The key condition is uncertainty and interdependence — if work is predictable and solo, safety is nice but not necessary. In complex, collaborative environments, it's essential.
