Graham makes a subtle but powerful distinction: Lisp didn't succeed because McCarthy analyzed Fortran's weaknesses and addressed them. It succeeded because McCarthy was doing something entirely different — trying to create a mathematical notation for computation. The innovations emerged as natural consequences of that theoretical framework rather than as patches to existing problems.

This maps directly to product strategy. Companies that study competitors and build 'better' versions inherit the predecessor's framing and constraints. Companies that return to the underlying problem domain and model it from scratch often produce paradigmatically different solutions. The risk is that first-principles approaches look impractical or alien initially (like Lisp's parentheses), but the ideas they produce have far longer shelf lives than incremental improvements.
