In established companies, status tracks execution: delivering quarterly results, managing large teams, running reliable operations. Exploratory work — testing hypotheses, talking to customers, iterating on unproven ideas — looks like wandering. It produces no short-term deliverables and is difficult to measure, so it accrues little social capital even when it is strategically critical.

This prestige asymmetry means that talented people avoid being assigned to innovation initiatives, and those who are assigned to them are motivated to produce outputs that look like execution (reports, frameworks, roadmaps) rather than genuine learning. The organizational immune system mistakes exploration for incompetence. Osterwalder's proposed solution — creating a dedicated 'invention space' with its own leadership structure — is an attempt to carve out a protected zone where exploratory work can accumulate its own prestige metrics.
