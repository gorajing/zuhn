WD-40's approach treats innovation not as a specialized function but as a universal expectation woven into every role. This contrasts with companies that create Chief Innovation Officers or standalone innovation labs, which can inadvertently signal that innovation is someone else's job.

The distributed model works because the people closest to problems — on factory floors, in customer conversations, in back-office processes — have the richest context for spotting improvement opportunities. Centralizing innovation creates an information bottleneck where the innovation team must first learn what frontline workers already know. The tradeoff is that distributed innovation requires stronger cultural infrastructure (psychological safety, learning-from-failure norms) since there's no dedicated team to protect experimental work.
