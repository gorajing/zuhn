Steinberger argues OpenClaw 'would have never been able to come out of an American company, just because it would have been killed in legal long before it would have been released' — it has unsolved industry-wide problems that no big lab would ship over the internal pushback and the lack of 'market proof that this is what people want.' The category had to be created by 'someone outside, sitting there.' The enabling factor was personal risk calculus, not corporate process: when he built it, he asked 'what's the worst that can happen?' — it could exfiltrate his token, emails, photos — and concluded 'I can live with that risk. It will be uncomfortable, but it's manageable.' A company cannot make that bet on a user's behalf. 

The transferable principle: in domains where the risk/reward is genuinely uncertain and the downside is legible to a legal department, large incumbents are structurally disqualified from being first — their risk-aversion is rational but disabling. This is why permissionless individuals and small teams open new categories, and why incumbents adopt only after an outsider provides market proof (note OpenAI's later involvement *after* OpenClaw demonstrated demand). For founders, the actionable read is that legally-ambiguous, high-variance frontiers are precisely where the incumbent moat is weakest and the outsider advantage is largest — provided the founder can personally absorb the downside the incumbent cannot.
