Bacteriophages were discovered in the early 1900s, roughly the same era as antibiotics. Soviet medicine actually used phage therapy for decades. Yet Western medicine ignored phages entirely because antibiotics worked well enough — there was no incentive to invest billions in an alternative approach.

Only now, as antibiotic resistance creates genuine desperation ('we're sort of getting desperate; we screwed up'), are pharma companies and researchers seriously pursuing phage therapy. This pattern — viable solutions sitting dormant until crisis forces adoption — repeats across industries. The lesson for innovators is that timing often depends less on when a solution becomes possible and more on when the incumbent approach becomes untenable. Building for the moment of desperation, not the moment of invention, is the real timing challenge.
