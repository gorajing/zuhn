Wallets work fine. Cards swipe. The physical payment system, while imperfect, is deeply habitual and requires zero learning curve. This means that 'making payments digital' is not itself a compelling value proposition — the friction of habit change exceeds the convenience gain.

Apple Pay's insight was that the value proposition had to be qualitatively superior, not incrementally better. Security (tokenization, no card number transmitted), privacy (Apple doesn't track purchases), and speed (tap and done) together created a case that wasn't 'slightly easier' but 'actually safer.' That's a different emotional register — fear of financial exposure is a stronger motivator than mild inconvenience.

The general pattern: when you're trying to replace a physical artifact, your digital solution must be provably better on the dimension the physical version is weakest, not just better on convenience. Keys are hard to lose digitally; wallets expose card data; boarding passes get crumpled. Lead with the weakness of the thing you're replacing.
