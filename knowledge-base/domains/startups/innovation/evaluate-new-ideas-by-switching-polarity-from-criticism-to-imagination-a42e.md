Graham describes the YC evaluation mindset: when encountering a strange-sounding idea from an unknown person, the productive response is not skepticism but creative engagement. Instead of asking 'why will this fail?', ask 'under what conditions could this succeed?' This is not naive optimism—it's rational behavior in a domain with power-law outcomes, where missing one outlier success costs far more than wasting time on many failures.

This polarity switch is a trainable skill, not a personality trait. Graham notes he's become good at it through years of practice at YC. The broader principle applies beyond startups: in any domain where upside is unbounded and downside is capped (early-stage research, creative projects, career experiments), defaulting to imagination over criticism produces better expected outcomes. The cultural version of this—Silicon Valley's learned optimism—has already proven economically superior to the default dismissiveness found elsewhere.
