Graham distinguishes between having lower standards and switching polarity entirely. Lowering standards still operates within a failure-detection framework — you're just being less sensitive. Polarity inversion means actively searching for paths to success, which generates genuinely new information about the idea's potential.

This is particularly valuable in power-law outcome environments like startups, where the cost of missing one breakthrough vastly exceeds the cost of entertaining many duds. Y Combinator's practice of reviewing thousands of strange-sounding ideas every six months has made this polarity inversion an institutional muscle. The technique transfers to any domain where novel ideas are proposed — research, creative work, product development.
