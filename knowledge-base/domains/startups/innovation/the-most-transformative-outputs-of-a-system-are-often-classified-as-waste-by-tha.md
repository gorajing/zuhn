Crash Course describes oxygen as 'the most useful useless byproduct in history.' From a plant's metabolic perspective, oxygen is genuinely waste — an electron disposal mechanism. The plant gains nothing from it. Yet that discarded output terraformed the planet and made complex animal life possible. The creators of the byproduct had no mechanism to capture that value.

This pattern recurs constantly in technology: ARPANET's packet-switching protocol was a byproduct of military redundancy research; SMS was a maintenance channel for telecom engineers; Twitter started as a side feature of a podcast company. In each case, the actors closest to the value-creating process treated the byproduct as overhead, and external actors who recognized the opportunity built entire industries on top of it.

The practical implication is to audit your 'waste.' What are you producing at scale that you currently discard or ignore? Internal tools, proprietary datasets, customer interaction logs, and intermediate computational artifacts are frequent candidates. The byproduct-to-product pivot is one of the highest-leverage moves in product strategy precisely because the production cost has already been paid.
