Most people evaluate new ideas by defaulting to criticism—listing reasons the idea will fail. This feels intellectually rigorous but is systematically biased against novel work, because early-stage ambitious projects almost always look unimpressive. The correct move is to switch polarity entirely: treat a new idea as a challenge to your imagination and try to think of ways it could succeed. This is what Silicon Valley's best investors have learned to do, and it's extremely lucrative because outcomes follow power-law distributions where missing the needle in the haystack is catastrophically expensive. The skill isn't lower standards—it's a fundamentally different evaluation mode.
