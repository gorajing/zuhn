Graham describes how Y Combinator's evaluation method requires switching polarity entirely when encountering new ideas: instead of the default mode of finding flaws, you actively try to imagine how the idea could work. This isn't naive optimism but a disciplined practice born from understanding power-law distributions — missing one outlier in a batch of thousands is catastrophically expensive.

This polarity inversion is a learnable skill that improves with practice. The key insight is that it's not about lowering standards but about changing the direction of your analytical energy. Criticism is easy and feels productive; generative imagination about how something weird could work is harder but far more valuable in environments where upside is unbounded.
