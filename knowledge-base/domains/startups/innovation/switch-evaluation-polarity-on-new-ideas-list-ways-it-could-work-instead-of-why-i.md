Graham distinguishes between merely lowering your standards for new ideas and completely switching polarity—from finding flaws to finding possibilities. This is the evaluation mode practiced at Y Combinator, where power-law outcomes make it painfully obvious when you miss winners. The cost of false negatives (dismissing the next Google) dwarfs the cost of false positives.

This polarity switch is a learnable skill, not a personality trait. Silicon Valley developed it as a cultural custom because it proved extremely lucrative, and lucrative tricks spread. The implication is that any community or individual can train this evaluation mode, and the returns compound: you spot more opportunities, attract more ambitious people, and create a reinforcing cycle of optimism and results.
