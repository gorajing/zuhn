Apple wasn't first with touchscreens, dual cameras, mobile payments, voice assistants, or facial recognition. Yet across 12 generations of iPhones, it consistently delivered the most polished implementation of each feature. This contradicts the startup orthodoxy that first-mover advantage is paramount.

The pattern suggests that in consumer markets, execution quality and integration matter more than invention timing. Users don't care who invented a feature — they care who made it work seamlessly. This has strategic implications: rather than racing to be first, companies can wait for technology to mature and then invest in delivering a superior, integrated experience.
