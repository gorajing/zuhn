Evans names a pattern we keep forgetting: when a new technology arrives, the first wave is doing the old thing but more — more spreadsheets, more PowerPoints, faster email — and the genuinely transformative wave is doing something that was previously impossible. Steam engines didn't make faster horses; they made trains, which no quantity of horses could approximate. The first half of Spotify's story was 'you don't have to buy a $15 CD for one track'; the second, more important half was '$15 a month gets you all the music that exists,' which was simply impossible before. The benign-but-forgotten observation is that the value is in the second half, not the first.

For builders, this connects to his argument about why models won't 'do the whole thing': the most valuable software solves problems people — including the people who have them — didn't know existed. Reviewing a16z's pitch history, he notes how often the founder spent two years just persuading an industry that a problem existed at all before selling the solution. That's why a middle manager prompting a chatbot won't surface a global industry-restructuring opportunity: nobody knew the problem was there, let alone how to build the tool for it. The actionable orientation is to resist building 'the old thing, rebuilt' (the Google Docs trap — technically capable, ~20% share, because re-platforming an existing workflow misses the point) and instead hunt for the capability that fills a hole in the universe, the thing that becomes obvious only once someone explains it.
