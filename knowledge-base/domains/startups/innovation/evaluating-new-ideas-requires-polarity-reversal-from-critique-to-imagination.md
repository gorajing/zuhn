Most people evaluate new ideas by default-critiquing: listing reasons the idea won't work. This feels intellectually rigorous but is actually lazy pattern-matching against familiar failure modes. Graham argues the correct approach is a complete polarity reversal — actively exercising imagination to find paths where the idea could succeed.

This is not naive optimism or lowering standards. It's a different cognitive operation entirely: generative rather than reductive. Y Combinator's entire model depends on this skill because startup outcome distributions follow power laws. Missing the one outlier in a batch is catastrophically more expensive than funding a few failures. The same logic applies to any domain with fat-tailed outcomes — research, art, venture investing. The skill is learnable through practice, and Silicon Valley's culture proves it can scale from individual habit to social norm.
