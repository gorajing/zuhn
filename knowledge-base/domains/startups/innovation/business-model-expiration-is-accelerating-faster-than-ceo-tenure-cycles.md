Osterwalder and Pigneur argue that the era of a CEO managing one business model through their entire tenure is over. Business models now 'expire like yoghurt in the fridge,' and the expiration rate is accelerating. This means execution excellence alone—the skill that typically earns someone the CEO role—is necessary but insufficient.

The implication is structural: organizations need parallel capabilities for exploitation (optimizing current models) and exploration (inventing new ones). CEOs who only optimize the present are essentially managing a depreciating asset without building its replacement. The McKinsey data they cite—80% of CEOs believe their model is at risk, yet only 6% are satisfied with their innovation process—reveals a massive knowing-doing gap.
