Under Steve Ballmer, Microsoft's culture was singularly focused on improving and defending Windows — every initiative was filtered through a Windows-first lens. This produced some successes (Xbox, SharePoint) but none that matched Windows's scale, because the gravitational pull of the core business constrained what was possible.

Nadella's shift was structural, not just strategic: repositioning Windows as one component rather than the center of gravity, and opening the ecosystem to outside contributors. The principle is that improvement and invention require fundamentally different organizational cultures, incentive structures, and tolerance for uncertainty. A single structure optimized for execution will systematically starve exploratory efforts. Companies need parallel structures — one that exploits current advantages and another that is explicitly chartered to discover what comes next.
