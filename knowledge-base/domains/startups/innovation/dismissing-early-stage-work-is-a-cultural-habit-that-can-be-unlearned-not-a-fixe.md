Most people dismiss ambitious early-stage work because humanity has very little cultural experience evaluating incomplete novel projects. We default to judging them as finished work or comparing them to less ambitious projects. Silicon Valley demonstrates that this default can be overwritten: through repeated exposure to power-law outcomes, the community developed customs of constructive optimism toward strange-sounding ideas from unknown people.

This matters because it means the problem is solvable at the cultural level. Rather than fighting an innate cognitive bias, builders can seek or create environments where early work is evaluated on trajectory rather than current state. The trick isn't lowering standards — it's switching polarity from 'list reasons it won't work' to 'imagine ways it could work.' And because this optimistic posture is extremely lucrative (missing the needle in a power-law haystack is expensive), it tends to spread wherever the economic incentives align.
