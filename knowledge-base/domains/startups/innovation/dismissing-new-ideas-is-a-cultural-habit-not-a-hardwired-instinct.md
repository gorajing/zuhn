Making new things is itself new for humans as a species. Until the last few centuries, innovation happened too slowly for any individual to notice, so we never developed social customs for responding to novel ideas. The default response—dismissing early work as lame—isn't deeply rooted in human nature; it's simply a gap in our cultural toolkit. Silicon Valley demonstrates how quickly this gap can be filled: in just decades, it evolved customs that treat strange-sounding ideas from unknown people as worth investigating rather than dismissing. The key mental shift is switching polarity from listing reasons an idea won't work to imagining ways it could. This isn't naive optimism—Silicon Valley's track record, measured by revenue, shows this approach is better calibrated than the default skepticism found elsewhere.
