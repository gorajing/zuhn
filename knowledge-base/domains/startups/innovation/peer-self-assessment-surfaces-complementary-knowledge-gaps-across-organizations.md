The summit used a structured self-assessment framework with nine questions across three categories (leadership, culture/processes, organizational design). The aggregated results showed that no organization excelled across all dimensions — those scoring high on leadership support scored low on testing practices, and vice versa.

This pattern has a powerful design implication: peer learning summits become most valuable when participants assess themselves on a shared framework before meeting. The self-assessment both reveals what each organization can teach and what it needs to learn, enabling targeted knowledge exchange rather than generic best-practice sharing. The Chatham House Rules further amplified honesty in self-reporting, suggesting that psychological safety mechanisms are essential infrastructure for peer learning.
