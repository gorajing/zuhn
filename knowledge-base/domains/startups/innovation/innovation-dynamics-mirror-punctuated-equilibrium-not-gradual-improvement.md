Douglas's 2025 model found saltative branching in systems as different as enzymes and Indo-European language trees, suggesting a universal pattern: stability for long stretches, then rapid reorganization when a system hits a branch point. Technology and market evolution arguably show the same shape — smartphones, cloud computing, transformers, each a multi-year quiet period followed by an explosive transition window.

If this analogy holds, strategic implications are concrete: (1) incremental improvement is often illusory progress during equilibria; the real gains come at transitions. (2) The time to make a large bet is when a branch point is forming, not during the long stable interval before or the mature phase after. (3) Most 'continuous improvement' initiatives are essentially stasis with extra work — actual phenotype change comes from reorganization at a node.

Caveat: the analogy is suggestive but not empirically confirmed for technology/markets. Treat it as a model to test, not a law. But be skeptical of strategic plans that assume smooth linear progress through a phase transition — biology, language, and morphology all say that's not how complex systems change.
