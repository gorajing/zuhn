Henrich addresses the puzzle of why the internet didn't cause a second urbanization — why hasn't productivity growth accelerated as hard as the '90s optimists predicted? His in-progress answer: face-to-face interaction is doing something the internet isn't. Research he cites shows that whether two cities have direct flights between them predicts the flow of ideas between those places, even when both are fully internet-connected. And the effect is larger when the cities are more culturally different — precisely the cases where cross-pollination should be most valuable.

His hypothesis: trust is the bottleneck. People need to build trust before sharing valuable ideas, and the more culturally different someone is from you, the more trust you need. That trust seems to require physical co-presence. The implication is that remote-first organizations trying to collaborate with culturally-distant partners or contractors are fighting a headwind the internet doesn't eliminate.

For practical application: if you're trying to extract ideas from a culturally distant team or transfer tacit knowledge across a boundary, budget for actual in-person time. Don't assume Slack plus Zoom replaces it. The same principle explains why Silicon Valley's coffee-shop density is load-bearing for its innovation output — serendipitous physical encounters do work the internet cannot replicate.
