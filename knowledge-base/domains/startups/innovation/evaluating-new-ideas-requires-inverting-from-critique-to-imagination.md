Graham argues that most people evaluate new ideas using the same standards they'd apply to finished work or less ambitious projects, which systematically kills good ideas at their most vulnerable stage. The correct approach is to switch polarity entirely — from listing failure modes to actively imagining success paths. This is what experienced investors and YC partners learn to do.

This skill becomes urgent in power-law environments like startups, where the cost of missing a winner vastly exceeds the cost of backing a loser. Skeptics are more often right on any individual prediction, but catastrophically wrong when predictions are weighted by outcome. The encouraging finding is that this optimistic evaluation style is learnable and spreadable — it's already become a social custom in Silicon Valley, proving it can be culturally adopted rather than requiring individual genius.
