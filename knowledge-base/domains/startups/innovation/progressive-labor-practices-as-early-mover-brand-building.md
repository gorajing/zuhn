Tata implemented the 8-hour workday in 1912 — years before the ILO convention of 1919 and decades before most Western companies adopted it. They introduced employee medical benefits in 1917, again far ahead of standard practice. These weren't legally required; they reflected founder values about worker welfare.

The compounding effect is significant: by the time these practices became regulation, Tata had already built decades of employee loyalty, institutional knowledge retention, and brand reputation as a progressive employer. This created a talent moat — the best workers in India gravitated toward Tata because the company had a proven track record of treating people well. For modern founders, the analog is that adopting progressive practices (remote work, four-day weeks, mental health support) before they become standard creates a similar compounding advantage in talent acquisition and retention.
