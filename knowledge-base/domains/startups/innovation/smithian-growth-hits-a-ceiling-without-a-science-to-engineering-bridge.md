Harper argues Rome pushed Smithian growth — gains from trade, specialization, comparative advantage, and sophisticated finance — as far as a pre-industrial economy plausibly could. They had impersonal banking, contract enforcement, urbanization, and even artisanal-to-industrial scaling in some sectors. Yet productivity never compounded into a takeoff.

The missing ingredient was a tight coupling of three things: genuine basic science pursued for its own sake, empiricism that overrode argument-from-authority, and 'useful knowledge' — the deliberate application of high-abstract math and physics to practical engineering problems. Seventeenth-century Britain and France got this through institutions like the Royal Society, where Newton, Leibniz, and engineers like Denis Papin were in direct social contact. Rome had tinkerers and had mathematicians, but they weren't talking to each other.

The practical implication: institutional quality and market depth are necessary but not sufficient for sustained productivity growth. When evaluating innovation ecosystems — countries, companies, or research labs — look specifically for whether abstract theory and applied engineering are socially and physically proximate. Without that bridge, even very advanced economies can plateau for centuries.
