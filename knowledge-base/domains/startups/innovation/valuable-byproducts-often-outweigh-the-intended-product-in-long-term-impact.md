Photosystem II splits water molecules to steal their electrons, producing hydrogen ions and oxygen as byproducts. From the plant's perspective, oxygen is waste — it's the glucose and energy storage that matters. Yet this 'useless' byproduct is the single most important chemical output in Earth's biological history, enabling the entire aerobic branch of life.

Startups routinely discover that their most valuable output isn't what they set out to build. Slack was a byproduct of a failed game. YouTube started as a dating site. AWS emerged from Amazon's internal infrastructure needs. The pattern suggests that the act of building complex systems reliably generates valuable byproducts, and the founders who capture those byproducts often create more value than those who stubbornly optimize only the intended product. This argues for staying alert to what your process produces incidentally, not just intentionally.
