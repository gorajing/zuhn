When Photosystem II splits water to replace a lost electron, it produces hydrogen ions (useful) and oxygen (waste). The video calls oxygen 'the most useful useless byproduct in the history of useless byproducts.' This accident of chemistry enabled the entire animal kingdom.

In startups, some of the most valuable products began as byproducts. Slack emerged from internal tooling for a failed game. AWS grew from Amazon's excess infrastructure capacity. The pattern suggests that teams should pay attention to what their core process produces incidentally — internal tools, data exhaust, operational insights — because those byproducts may be more transformative than the primary output.

The structural reason this works: byproducts solve real problems (the team needed internal chat; Amazon needed scalable infra) without the distortion of trying to guess what a market wants. They're validated by necessity before they're ever productized.
