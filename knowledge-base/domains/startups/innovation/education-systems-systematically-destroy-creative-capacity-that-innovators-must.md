Ken Robinson's thesis, cited by Osterwalder as particularly valuable, identifies a structural problem in the innovation pipeline: the educational system that produces future innovators actively suppresses their creative capacity. Schools reward conformity, standardized answers, and risk avoidance — the opposite of what innovation requires.

This has practical implications for innovation leaders. First, hiring for creativity means looking beyond traditional academic credentials that may actually signal creativity suppression. Second, organizations must actively rebuild creative capacity in their teams through deliberate practice and safe-to-fail environments. Third, it explains why many breakthrough innovators are education system dropouts or rebels — they preserved creative capacity by opting out of the suppression system.
