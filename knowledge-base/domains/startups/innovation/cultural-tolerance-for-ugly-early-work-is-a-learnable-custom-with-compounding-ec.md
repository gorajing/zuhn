Graham argues that dismissing early-stage work is not deeply rooted in human nature but is a cultural default that emerged because making new things is historically novel for our species. We simply never developed customs for evaluating work-in-progress. Silicon Valley developed such customs — partly from self-interest (investors want founders to succeed), partly from practice (repeated exposure to ugly ducklings that became swans).

The key insight is that this tolerance became a self-reinforcing custom. It started as a rational economic calculation but evolved into 'just what one does.' This matters because customs spread and compound: once a community develops the habit of charitable evaluation of early work, it attracts more ambitious people, which produces more successes, which reinforces the custom. The implication for any organization or community: you can deliberately cultivate this tolerance, and the returns follow a power law — most encouraged projects will fail, but the rare successes will massively outweigh all the false positives combined.
