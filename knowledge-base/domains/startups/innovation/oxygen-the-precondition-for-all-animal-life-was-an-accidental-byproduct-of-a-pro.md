Plants split water molecules during the light-dependent reactions to replenish electrons — releasing oxygen as a byproduct. Photosynthesis did not evolve to produce oxygen; oxygen is what's left over after the process takes what it actually wants. Yet this 'useless byproduct' transformed Earth's atmosphere and enabled the entire evolutionary lineage leading to every breathing organism.

This pattern recurs throughout innovation history. SMS was a maintenance channel for telecom engineers before it became a global communication medium. Amazon Web Services was excess infrastructure capacity before it became the dominant cloud platform. GPS was a military positioning system before it became the substrate for ride-sharing, delivery logistics, and location-based services. The transformative application was not the goal — it was the exhaust.

For founders, this suggests a specific scouting strategy: look for infrastructure, processes, or data generated as byproducts of large-scale operations, and ask what becomes possible if you route those byproducts to people who value them. Platform businesses in particular often emerge from surfacing a company's internal capabilities as external products.
