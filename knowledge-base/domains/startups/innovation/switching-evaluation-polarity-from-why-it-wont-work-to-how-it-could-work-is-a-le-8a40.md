Graham argues that the right response to a new idea is to treat it as a challenge to your imagination—actively trying to think of ways it could work rather than defaulting to listing reasons it won't. This is not naive optimism but a disciplined inversion of the default evaluation mode. In a world with power-law distributions of outcomes, the cost of dismissing the one idea that works is catastrophically higher than the cost of being wrong about many that don't. Silicon Valley has evolved this as a cultural custom because the economic incentives are so strong—investors who dismiss strange-sounding ideas from unknown people systematically miss the biggest returns. This polarity-switching skill is learnable through practice, as Graham's own experience at YC demonstrates, and it tends to spread because it's extremely lucrative.
