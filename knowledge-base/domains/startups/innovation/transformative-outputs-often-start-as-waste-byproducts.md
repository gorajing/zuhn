When early photosynthetic organisms evolved water-splitting to replenish electrons, oxygen was literally waste — a toxic byproduct that poisoned most existing life in the Great Oxygenation Event. Yet this 'useless' output became the foundation for aerobic respiration and all complex animal life, fundamentally reshaping Earth's biosphere.

This pattern recurs in technology and business. SMS was an afterthought in the GSM cellular standard that became a dominant communication medium. Slack began as an internal tool for a failed gaming company. AWS started as Amazon's solution to its own infrastructure problems. The pattern suggests that optimizing narrowly for a primary goal often creates secondary outputs whose value dwarfs the original objective.

For founders and builders, this implies two things: first, pay attention to what your system produces as 'exhaust' — data, workflows, tools, or communities that exist as side effects of your core process. Second, don't prematurely optimize away byproducts before understanding their potential value to others.
