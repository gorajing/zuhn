The article draws a direct parallel between Stoke Space's hydrogen-cooled reentry approach and the Wright Brothers' three-axis control system. In both cases, the breakthrough came from applying a different mental model to an existing problem — mimicking birds for flight, using fuel as coolant for reentry. The ideas were 'obvious' enough that Stoke's founders assumed someone else had already tried their approach and were shocked to find no one had.

This pattern reveals something important about innovation: the scarcity isn't in ideas but in the combination of seeing the problem differently AND having the execution capability to prove the concept works. Most people either can't see the simple reframe or can't build the complex system required to validate it. The Wright Brothers weren't aviation experts — they were bicycle mechanics with a novel mental model. Andy and Tom were trained engineers who happened to see a gap everyone else had normalized. The lesson for founders is that 'someone must have tried this already' is often a false assumption worth testing.
