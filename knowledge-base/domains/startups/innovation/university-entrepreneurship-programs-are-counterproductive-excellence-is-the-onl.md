Graham argues that innovation programs at universities pursue the wrong targets — you get innovation by aiming at specific problems like better batteries, not by aiming at 'innovation' as an abstract goal. The empirical evidence supports this: both Microsoft and Facebook started as side projects during Harvard's Reading Period, when students had unstructured time on campus.

The practical implication is that universities should focus on three things: being the best research institution possible (to attract ambitious talent globally), adopting liberal IP policies that don't claim ownership of student/faculty inventions, and providing flexible leave-of-absence and deferred admission policies. The most valuable thing is creating an environment where smart people collide and have unstructured time — the startups emerge as a byproduct of excellence, not as the product of a program designed to create them.
