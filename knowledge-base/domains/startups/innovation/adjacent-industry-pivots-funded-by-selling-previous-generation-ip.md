Sakichi Toyoda built Japan's first power loom, then sold the patent rights for 1 million yen — a massive sum — and gave the money to his son Kiichiro with one condition: use it to build Japan's own automobile. This created an asymmetric advantage: Kiichiro entered the auto industry with both capital and inherited manufacturing expertise from textile machinery, while competitors had to raise funds independently. The pattern recurs in tech history — companies that monetize mature IP to fund adjacent bets start with a structural edge. The key insight is that the parent technology doesn't need to be related to the new venture; the capital and operational discipline transfer regardless.
