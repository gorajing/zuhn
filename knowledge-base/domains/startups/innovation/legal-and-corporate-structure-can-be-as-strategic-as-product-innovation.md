In 1870, U.S. corporate law did not contemplate the idea of a national business. A company incorporated in Ohio could not own property or operate in Pennsylvania. Every attempt at national consolidation bumped into this wall. Standard Oil's competitive breakthrough wasn't a refining improvement — it was Henry Flagler (not a lawyer, scribbling on legal pad paper) reviving the joint stock company and then layering on the trust: trustees holding shares in state-by-state companies, with dividends flowing directly to the Standard Oil of Ohio shareholders without touching the company itself. It was a legal invention that gave Standard a 20-year head start that none of its competitors even thought to attempt.

The same pattern recurs constantly. Airbnb's regulatory structure, Uber's contractor model, the LLC, SPACs, DAOs, Cayman holding companies for crypto — whenever a regulatory regime lags a business opportunity, the company that invents the workaround captures outsized value before the law catches up. Founders underweight this because it feels non-technical, but legal and corporate engineering can create moats as wide as any technical moat, especially in industries where competitors have all gone to the same law firms and reflexively accepted constraints as fixed.
