Graham distinguishes between two fundamentally different modes of evaluating new ideas. The default mode — listing reasons an idea won't work — is both easy and statistically likely to be correct for any given idea. But in a power-law distribution of outcomes, being right about most ideas while missing the exceptional ones is catastrophic. The alternative is polarity inversion: actively trying to imagine how a strange-sounding idea could work rather than why it won't.

This is not naive optimism or lowered standards. It's a different cognitive operation entirely — closer to creative problem-solving than judgment. Silicon Valley's cultural advantage isn't that people there are less critical, it's that they've learned to run the imagination protocol first and the criticism protocol second. Graham notes this became urgent at YC because the power-law distribution of startup outcomes makes false negatives far more costly than false positives.
