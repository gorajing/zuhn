Graham traces an interesting evolutionary path: Silicon Valley's supportive attitude toward strange ideas didn't start as moral virtue. It started as self-interest — investors want you to succeed because they profit, and everyone else wants to say they knew you early. But practiced long enough, this self-interested optimism hardened into a genuine cultural norm where encouraging startups is simply what one does.

This matters because it shows cultural attitudes toward new ideas are not fixed features of human nature but learned customs that can evolve quickly when incentives align. The rest of the world's default skepticism toward ambitious projects isn't some deep evolutionary truth — it's just a custom that hasn't yet been updated. The mechanism is: financial reward → repeated practice → cultural norm → self-reinforcing identity. This suggests that any ecosystem can develop similar optimism if the incentive structures make dismissiveness costly enough.
