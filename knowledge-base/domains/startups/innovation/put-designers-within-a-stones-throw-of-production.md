Kelly Johnson's design for Skunk Works put the engineering desks within steps of the production floor. His stated reason: designers need to glance up and see how things are actually being built so they can catch 'it looked good in the diagram, but in practice you have to bring this big thing around over here.' The cost of walking back to a shop is the cost of a design error compounding through production.

This principle generalizes beyond aerospace. The iPhone and iPod teams lived next to the prototyping labs. Modern software teams that sit engineers next to customer-facing operators ship dramatically better products than those separated by org charts and ticket queues. The inverse — 'throw it over the wall' handoffs between design, engineering, and manufacturing — is the default failure mode of every large organization.

The generalizable rule: measure the distance (physical, organizational, temporal) between the person making decisions and the person discovering the consequences. Shrink that distance aggressively. Every meter, hop, or delay is a tax on learning.
