Most regulatory debate focuses on protecting unwilling people from risk. But Vitalik points to a different category: preventing willing people from taking on known risks in exchange for early access. Medical trials, autonomous vehicle testing, and drone deployment all have populations of people who understand the risks and would eagerly participate — but are legally excluded from doing so.

The cost of this exclusion is not just individual autonomy but collective learning rate. Technologies iterate by encountering real-world edge cases. Restricting the population of willing early adopters creates an artificial bottleneck on feedback loops. The comparison class is consumer software, where anyone can use an unstable beta — and where rapid iteration has produced enormous value. Hardware and biology follow slower curves partly because regulation prevents equivalent opt-in experimentation at scale.

This doesn't argue for eliminating safety requirements — it argues for a tiered framework where informed consent unlocks access to earlier development stages. The question for startups in regulated industries is: what's the minimum viable consent and disclosure structure that would legally enable this?
