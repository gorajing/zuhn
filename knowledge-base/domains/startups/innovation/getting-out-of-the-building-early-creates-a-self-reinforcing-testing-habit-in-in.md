Osterwalder observes that teams who get out of the building early to test tend to 'get hooked' — they become enthusiastic about the process because they quickly see how much they learn from even rough experiments. This is a virtuous cycle: testing produces surprising insights, surprising insights reduce fear of being wrong, reduced fear makes the next test easier to initiate. The habit compounds.

The opposite pattern is also self-reinforcing. Teams that spend their early time refining and workshopping never experience the payoff of fast external feedback. They associate 'innovation work' with internal deliberation. When they finally do test, they've invested so much in the current version of the idea that disconfirming evidence feels like a failure rather than information. The implication for team leads and facilitators: the fastest path to a testing culture is getting the first test done as quickly as possible, even at low quality. The experience of learning from a rough test is more persuasive than any argument for why testing matters.
