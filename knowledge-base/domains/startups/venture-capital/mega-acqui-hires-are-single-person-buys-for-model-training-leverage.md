The keynote reframes the era's eye-watering acqui-hires as single-person buys driven by training-cost leverage. When a single model-training run costs hundreds of millions (sometimes billions) of dollars, the marginal value of the one person who can prevent a failed iteration — or get the architecture right the first time — is enormous. If the right person saves even one iteration, a $100M+ package pays for itself many times over.

The examples: Meta's ~$14B around scale.ai is read as 'practically buying one person' (and underrated as such); character.ai's ~$2.5B is read as effectively hiring its CEO back into Google, because his prior work materially advanced Gemini. The economics: rather than spend billions on a team whose model 'isn't going anywhere' (he points to Meta disbanding the original Llama-4-era team), it's rational to disband the team and pay one person a $100M+ (heading toward $1B) package to make the billions you ARE spending on training actually land.

The spreading pattern: the speaker notes this single-person-leverage logic, while most extreme in model training today, is starting to appear in other fields — not yet at the billion-per-person level elsewhere, but 'clearly getting there.' Where one person's judgment gates an enormous capital expenditure, that person's market value approaches a fraction of the capex they de-risk.

The implications: (1) for talent — in domains where individual judgment gates huge capital outlays, key-person compensation decouples from normal salary bands; (2) for investors — some 'acquisitions' and 'investments' are best analyzed as talent acquisition, so value the person, not the cap table; (3) the broader signal — as capital intensity rises (training, data centers), the leverage of the few people who can wield it efficiently rises with it. Time-sensitive because it's tied to the current ultra-high-capex training regime.
