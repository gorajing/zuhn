The Apollo 13 CO2 filter problem—fitting square command module canisters into round lunar module receptacles using only plastic bags, cardboard, and duct tape—is a canonical example of constraint-driven innovation. Mission control couldn't send parts; they could only prototype on the ground and radio instructions. The constraint of working only with materials already aboard forced a solution that no pre-mission engineering review would have designed.

This pattern recurs in startups: teams with limited runway, small headcount, or missing infrastructure often ship more creative solutions than well-resourced competitors. The key mechanism is that constraints eliminate the paradox of choice and force teams to work with what exists rather than waiting for ideal conditions. The lesson isn't to artificially constrain resources, but to recognize that when constraints appear, they're often the catalyst for the team's best work.
