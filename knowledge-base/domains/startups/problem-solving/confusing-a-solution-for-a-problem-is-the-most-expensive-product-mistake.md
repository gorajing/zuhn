The Airbnb social travel team spent six months building a product based on the assumption that travelers wanted to meet other travelers. The actual unmet need was 'travelers want to find high-quality non-touristy things to do' — hanging out with travelers was merely one possible solution. When another team correctly identified the real problem, they built Airbnb Experiences, which became a major business line.

This pattern — embedding a solution inside what appears to be a problem statement — is deceptively common because solutions feel concrete and actionable while true problems feel abstract. The antidote is disciplined problem statement hygiene: keep it to a single sentence describing an unmet need, make it solution-agnostic, include both the what (what's going wrong) and the why (why it matters), and explicitly test whether you've described a need or prescribed an approach. 'Build a loyalty program' is a solution; the problem it might solve is still unstated.
