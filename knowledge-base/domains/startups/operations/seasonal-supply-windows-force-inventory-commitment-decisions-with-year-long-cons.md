Barrow's logistics create a stark version of a common business problem: when resupply is infrequent, every order becomes a high-stakes forecast. Need a truck? You get one chance per year when the summer barge arrives. Underestimate and you wait 12 months. Overcommit and you've tied up capital in assets you can't return.

This pattern appears in many less extreme forms: seasonal product launches, annual budget cycles, hardware procurement with long lead times, or any business where replenishment windows are fixed and infrequent. The key insight is that constrained supply windows transform routine operational decisions into strategic ones. Each order requires forecasting a full cycle ahead, and errors compound because there's no mid-cycle correction mechanism. Businesses operating under such constraints need to over-invest in demand forecasting and build buffer inventory — the cost of holding excess stock is almost always less than the cost of stockout when the next window is months away.
