In the crowd crush archetype, three groups operate with completely disconnected information: the back of the crowd pushes forward (they want in and can't see the front), security holds the line (they see a disorderly crowd and restrict entry), and the core suffocates (they need to escape but have nowhere to go). Each group's rational individual behavior compounds the problem because none can see the full picture. This exact three-group disconnect pattern appeared in the Who concert (crowd vs. security vs. venue), Astroworld (arriving crowd vs. barricade-blocked crowd vs. stage operations), and the Hajj incidents.

This pattern maps directly to organizational and product failures: when engineering, product, and customers each operate with partial information and misaligned incentives, the system degrades predictably. The fix is never 'better individual behavior' — it's designing information flows that give each group visibility into the others' state. Real-time dashboards, cross-functional standups, and customer feedback loops serve the same function as crowd monitoring systems.
