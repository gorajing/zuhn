Barrow, Alaska demonstrates the extreme case of a supply chain entirely dependent on air freight. With no roads connecting to the outside world and only a brief summer barge window, everyday items like hand sanitizer ($15), frozen pizza ($17), and diapers ($22) carry enormous markups. The cost driver isn't the product's value but its physical characteristics — weight, bulk, and refrigeration needs.

This pattern applies broadly to any business operating in logistics-constrained environments. When your supply chain has a single expensive bottleneck (air freight, last-mile delivery in remote areas, or even digital bandwidth constraints), the entire cost structure reorganizes around that bottleneck's pricing dimensions rather than traditional value-based pricing. The 737-combi aircraft — half cargo, half passenger — represents an infrastructure adaptation to this constraint, optimizing for the dual needs of communities too small for dedicated cargo planes.
