Costco pays its 245,000 workers an average of $21/hour — double the U.S. retail average — plus better health insurance and retirement benefits. The business case: employees are three times more productive than the retail average. This is not charity; it's a leverage calculation. If a $21 worker produces output equivalent to three $10 workers, the math favors the higher wage even before accounting for reduced turnover, training costs, and theft reduction.

The deeper insight is that labor cost optimization should target cost-per-unit-of-output, not cost-per-hour. Most retail operations optimize for the latter, driving down wages and accepting high turnover as a fixed cost. Costco implicitly rejects this framing. This pattern appears in other high-performing operations (e.g., Trader Joe's, In-N-Out) and suggests that wage floors are often a false economy — the real variable is productivity per dollar spent, which high wages can actually improve by attracting better workers and reducing churn.
