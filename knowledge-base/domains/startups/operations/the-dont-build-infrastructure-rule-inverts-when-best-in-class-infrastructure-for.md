Jeff Bezos's famous 'focus on what makes your beer taste better' rule — don't build infrastructure that isn't your core differentiation — is usually right. Walmart is the edge case that proves when it's wrong. Their entire competitive advantage over Kmart was a distribution backend optimized for low-margin, high-turn discount retail. No such backend existed to buy in the 1960s — every common carrier and warehouse provider was built around 33% gross margin retail economics.

They built distribution centers that unboxed bulk shipments from suppliers and re-boxed them into store-specific daily orders, built their own trucking lines, and eventually built a $24 million private satellite network because no commercial telecom had the bandwidth to sync sales data fast enough. Each of these looked like 'unnecessary infrastructure' from a traditional lens. In reality, they were the business model.

The rule to extract: outsource infrastructure when best-in-class providers exist and use your model. Build infrastructure when (a) the operating economics your model requires are outside the envelope of what off-the-shelf providers can support, or (b) the infrastructure itself is a compounding advantage because efficiency improvements will be impossible for competitors to match. If neither is true, buy. If either is true, Bezos's own rule doesn't apply — and Walmart, ironically, is the proof.
