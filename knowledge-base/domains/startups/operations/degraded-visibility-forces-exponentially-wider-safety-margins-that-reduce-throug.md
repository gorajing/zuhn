Over the North Atlantic, the absence of radar coverage forces aircraft separation from 5 nautical miles to 15 minutes (~140 miles) — a 28x increase in required buffer space. This dramatically reduces the number of planes that can occupy a given corridor.

The pattern generalizes to any operational system: when you lose real-time observability (whether radar, monitoring dashboards, or customer feedback loops), you must compensate with massive safety margins that directly reduce throughput. This is why investing in monitoring infrastructure often pays for itself — not because it prevents failures, but because it lets you operate with tighter margins and higher throughput. The cost of flying blind isn't the crash; it's the 28x capacity penalty you pay to prevent it.
