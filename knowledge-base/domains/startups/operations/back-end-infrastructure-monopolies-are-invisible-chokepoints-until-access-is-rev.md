The Global Distribution System — controlled entirely by three companies (Amadeus, Sabre, Travelport) — is invisible to most travelers but essential for airline operations. It enables third-party booking, inter-airline ticketing, baggage transfers, and misconnection handling. When all three suspended Aeroflot, the airline vanished from every online travel agency and lost the ability to sell connecting itineraries with other carriers. Passengers could only book directly on Aeroflot's website.

This reveals a pattern: back-end infrastructure that 'just works' tends toward consolidation because there's no user-facing reason to maintain alternatives. But that consolidation creates a single point of failure that can be weaponized. The same vulnerability exists in payment rails (SWIFT), cloud infrastructure (AWS/Azure/GCP), app distribution (Apple/Google), and domain registration. For any business, mapping your dependency on oligopoly infrastructure — and understanding under what conditions access could be revoked — is an underrated form of risk assessment. The Russian aviation case shows that even state-owned enterprises can be cut off overnight with no workaround.
