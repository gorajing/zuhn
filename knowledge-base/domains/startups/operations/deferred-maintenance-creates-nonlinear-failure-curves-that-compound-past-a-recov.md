South Africa's power grid illustrates how deferred maintenance becomes nonlinear. A 1998 white paper predicted the exact year capacity would be exhausted, yet no action was taken. By the time new plants were built, the old ones were failing faster than new capacity could compensate — fires, strikes, coal shortages, and copper theft each compounded the underlying deficit. The key insight for any organization running critical infrastructure is that there exists a threshold beyond which maintenance debt compounds faster than investment can repay it. Eskom's experience shows this isn't theoretical: capacity rose through the 2010s but actual production stagnated because keeping aging plants running consumed all available resources. The lesson applies directly to technical systems, physical infrastructure, and organizational processes — once you cross the threshold where fixing breakdowns consumes your capacity to address root causes, you enter a death spiral that requires orders-of-magnitude more investment to escape than prevention would have cost.
