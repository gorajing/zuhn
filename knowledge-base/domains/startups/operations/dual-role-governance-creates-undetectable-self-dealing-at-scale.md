Isabel dos Santos exploited her dual positions to execute transactions that would be impossible under proper governance. She lent $460 million from Unitel to her own Unitel International Holdings, signing off as both lender and borrower. She had urban redevelopment projects greenlit by presidential decree using documents her own lawyers drafted. Her shell company with no oil expertise won Sonangol restructuring contracts.

This pattern applies far beyond kleptocracies. In any organization—startup, fund, non-profit—when the same person sits on both sides of a decision (board member who is also a vendor, founder who is also the compensation committee), the structural incentive to self-deal is enormous and the detection mechanism is absent. The Luanda Leaks required 715,000 documents analyzed by international journalists to confirm what was suspected for years. The startup lesson: governance separations that feel like bureaucratic overhead are actually fraud-prevention infrastructure. They're cheapest to install before they're needed.
