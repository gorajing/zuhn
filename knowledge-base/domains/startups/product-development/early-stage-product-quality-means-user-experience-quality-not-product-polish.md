Steve Jobs' 'insanely great' meant obsessive product design and manufacturing at Apple's scale. But for early-stage startups, the same principle morphs into something different: the total experience of being a user matters more than the product itself. A buggy, incomplete product paired with extraordinary personal attentiveness can deliver a better experience than a polished product with no human touch.

This isn't just a permissible hack — it's a necessary part of the feedback loop that makes the product good. Direct engagement with early users produces the highest-quality feedback you'll ever get. By the time you're big enough to need focus groups, you'll wish you could still go to users' homes and watch them use your product. The attentiveness phase is both a growth mechanism and a product development methodology, which is why companies that do it well tend to maintain customer-centric cultures even at scale.
