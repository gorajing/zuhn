Stripe's origin story illustrates this principle clearly: they didn't set out to build the best payment infrastructure. They identified a specific frustration — developers struggling with online payments — and built the simplest possible solution first. The anti-patterns are over-engineering complex tech nobody asked for, adding unnecessary features, making adoption difficult, and spending months refining a product nobody wants.

The practical framework is: early prototyping to capture core value, user testing with a small group for honest feedback, then iterative adjustments based on real-world responses. This approach minimizes risk and directs resources toward features that genuinely matter. Only after validation and iteration should founders shift focus to scalability — robust infrastructure, long-term vision, and sustainable business models. Premature scaling of an unvalidated product compounds the original mistake.
