Insta360's research revealed a critical insight: over 70% of action camera users who recorded family memories, vacations, and everyday moments had all their content sitting on a hard drive collecting dust. The bottleneck wasn't capture quality -- it was the editing workflow. Legacy manufacturers like Nikon and Canon had always focused exclusively on capture, leaving post-production to users to figure out on their own.

Insta360 flipped this by making editing part of the product itself. Mobile-first reframing, invisible selfie stick, real-time stabilization, slow motion -- all shipped as built-in features, not aftermarket add-ons. Seven years later, GoPro and Ricoh still cannot match Insta360's software feature set. This is a durable moat because software compounds (each generation adds features on top of previous ones) while hardware specs commoditize. The lesson extends beyond cameras: any hardware product where the user workflow extends beyond the physical device should treat that workflow software as the primary product, not an accessory.
