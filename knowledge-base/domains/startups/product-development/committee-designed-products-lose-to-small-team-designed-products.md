Graham points out that the best programming languages have been developed by small groups, and that if Java (run by a committee) turned out to be good, 'it will be the first time in history that a committee has designed a good language.' Committee design produces bureaucratic protocols and excessive process rather than elegant simplicity.

This connects to the broader principle that great products require a coherent, opinionated vision that committees structurally cannot produce. Committees optimize for minimizing objections rather than maximizing quality, leading to feature bloat, unnecessary protocols, and design-by-compromise. Small teams can make bold, taste-driven decisions and maintain internal consistency. The 'too many cooks' signal — when a product is governed by a large group of stakeholders — should raise immediate concerns about its long-term quality trajectory.
