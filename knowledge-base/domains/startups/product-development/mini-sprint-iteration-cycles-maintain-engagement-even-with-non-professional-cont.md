Osterwalder's comic book project kept children working 4-5 hours daily for a week without complaints by structuring the work as mini-sprints with frequent feedback. Each cycle — brainstorm, refine, prototype, critique — produced tangible artifacts that maintained momentum.

This pattern applies beyond children's projects: any creative endeavor involving non-professional contributors benefits from short cycles where people see their input materialize quickly. The key mechanism is that each feedback round validates contributors' ideas, making the next sprint feel purposeful rather than abstract. Long production phases without visible output erode motivation, while rapid prototyping sustains it.
