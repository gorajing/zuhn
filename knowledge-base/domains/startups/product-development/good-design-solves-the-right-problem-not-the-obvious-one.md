Graham illustrates this with the stove dial example: arranging four dials in a row is the simplest answer to 'how do I arrange these dials?' but it's answering the wrong question. The right question is 'how does a human match dials to burners?' — which demands a square layout matching the burner arrangement. Similarly, sans-serif fonts solve for pure letterforms but miss that legibility requires letters to be easily distinguishable.

This insight is critical for product builders. The most common failure mode isn't poor execution but misidentified problems. Software teams often build elegant solutions to problems users don't have, or optimize metrics that don't matter. The discipline of problem-reframing — asking 'is this actually what we need to solve?' before diving into solutions — prevents industrious misdirection. In software, replacing an intractable problem with an equivalent easy one is often the breakthrough.
