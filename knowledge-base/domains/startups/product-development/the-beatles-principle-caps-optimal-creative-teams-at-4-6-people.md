Bob Baxley draws on Brian Eno's concept of 'scenius' and the Beatles as a model: genius emerges from tiny, tightly-knit groups, not large organizations. The original Macintosh team was just 20 people; the iPhone team was 24. When teams grow beyond this threshold, coordination overhead replaces creative coherence, and the product loses its singular vision.

This has direct implications for how startups and product orgs should structure work. Rather than staffing up a feature with dozens of engineers and designers, the Beatles principle suggests breaking ambitious projects into small autonomous pods of 4-6, each with full creative ownership. The constraint isn't headcount — it's that beyond a small threshold, the cost of alignment exceeds the benefit of additional perspectives.
