The framework insists success criteria should be a specific metric with a defined goal that's easily measurable — '10% increase in X, 50% decrease in Y, 20% adoption within 3 months.' The goal should be believable but ambitious: something that would genuinely excite the team and leadership if achieved. This concreteness transforms the success criteria from a reporting artifact into an active decision-making tool throughout the project.

When teams resist quantifying success (and Rachitsky acknowledges this sometimes feels difficult), the fallback is to describe concretely what the world looks like if the project succeeds wildly. Even this narrative form is more useful than directional platitudes. The key insight is that success criteria serve double duty: they validate whether you solved the problem at the end, but more importantly they help you make scope and prioritization decisions during execution. Every feature proposal can be tested against 'does this move the metric?'
