David Malan explicitly tells CS50 students that their performance should be measured by personal delta, not by comparison to classmates. This is particularly powerful in a course where two-thirds of students have never programmed before — without this reframing, beginners would constantly feel behind the minority who arrived with prior experience.

The delta framing has direct product implications. Fitness apps that show improvement over your own baseline (Strava's personal records), language apps that visualize streak growth (Duolingo), and coding platforms that celebrate problems solved versus problems available all use this principle. The anti-pattern is leaderboards and peer-relative metrics in contexts where users have wildly different starting points — these create discouragement for exactly the users who would benefit most from continued engagement. The takeaway: in any product serving heterogeneous users, instrument and celebrate relative progress, not absolute position.
