Early photosynthetic organisms evolved RuBisCo to fix carbon in an atmosphere with almost no oxygen. The light-dependent reactions produced oxygen as a 'useless byproduct.' But as photosynthetic organisms dominated Earth, they fundamentally changed the atmosphere — filling it with oxygen. This environmental shift caused RuBisCo to malfunction, grabbing oxygen instead of CO2 roughly half the time and producing a toxic byproduct (phosphoglycolate) that plants then had to evolve elaborate detoxification pathways to handle.

The organism's own success created the conditions for its core mechanism's dysfunction. This is a precise biological analogy for platform self-disruption: social media companies whose engagement algorithms produce the toxicity that drives users away, marketplaces whose growth attracts the fraud that destroys trust, or open-source projects whose popularity creates the maintenance burden that kills contributor motivation.

The pattern is: (1) build mechanism that produces valuable output + 'harmless' byproduct, (2) scale until byproduct accumulates and changes the environment, (3) discover that your core mechanism now malfunctions in the new environment you created. The defense is monitoring your own byproducts as carefully as your primary output.
