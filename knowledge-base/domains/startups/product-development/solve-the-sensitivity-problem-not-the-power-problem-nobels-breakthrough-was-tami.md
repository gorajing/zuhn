Nitroglycerin existed for years before Nobel. It was 100x more powerful than gunpowder with blast pressure over 100x greater. The problem was never capability -- it was controllability. It would explode when bumped, dropped, or exposed to moisture, but wouldn't reliably detonate when you wanted it to.

Nobel's three inventions each solved a different aspect of this control problem: the blasting cap (reliable detonation on command), dynamite (safe handling via kieselguhr absorption), and gelignite (moldable form that eliminated sweating). None of these increased the raw power of nitroglycerin -- they all made it usable.

The parallel to product development is direct: most breakthrough products don't add new capability. They make existing capability reliable and controllable. The iPhone didn't invent the smartphone -- it made it usable. Claude Code didn't invent AI coding -- it made it controllable. The product opportunity is almost always in the sensitivity problem, not the power problem.
