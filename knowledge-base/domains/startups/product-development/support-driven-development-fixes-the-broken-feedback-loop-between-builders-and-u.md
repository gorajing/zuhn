Kevin Hale describes support-driven development (SDD) as the single most impactful practice at Wufoo. By requiring every team member—including engineers and designers—to do customer support shifts, the company created a direct feedback loop where builders experienced the consequences of their decisions firsthand. Paul English at Kayak demonstrated the same principle: after the second or third call about the same bug, engineers would stop and fix it permanently rather than endure more calls.

Jared Spool's research backs this up: teams need direct exposure to users for at least two hours every six weeks minimum, or software quality degrades. Wufoo's team got 4-8 hours weekly. The result was a 10-person team supporting 500,000 users with 7-12 minute response times, acquired by SurveyMonkey at a 29,000% return to investors. The practice also eliminated the need for elaborate project management—the social pressure of knowing your teammates would handle your bugs in their next support shift was sufficient motivation.
