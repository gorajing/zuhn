The Carnot efficiency equation reveals that jet engine efficiency equals 1 minus (cold temperature / hot temperature). Since you can't control the outside air temperature much (you already fly at 35,000 feet where it's -55C), the only lever is raising combustion chamber temperature. But the maximum temperature is set by what the turbine blades can survive. This makes turbine blade metallurgy the binding constraint for the entire $800B+ aviation industry.

Every other component — fuel injectors, compressors, fan design — is optimized around this single bottleneck. Rolls-Royce's entire precision casting facility, the development of nickel superalloys with 10+ elements, single-crystal blade growth, thermal barrier coatings, and intricate cooling channel design all exist to push this one number higher by a few hundred degrees.

The startup lesson: in any complex system, there's typically one binding constraint that limits the whole operation. Most teams optimize the easy variables while ignoring the hard constraint. The outsized returns come from identifying the true bottleneck and throwing disproportionate resources at it — even when the solution seems absurdly difficult (like growing a single crystal of metal in a vacuum furnace).
