Graham observes that a surprising number of large, serious programs began as throwaway programs — quick hacks for a limited task that grew. If this is true, then a tool's ability to support rapid, low-ceremony prototyping is critical because that is where most real software actually begins its life.

This has direct implications for product strategy: platforms and tools that impose heavy upfront structure (boilerplate, configuration, type systems) may lose to simpler alternatives that let people start fast. The projects that survive the throwaway phase will eventually need structure, but they need to exist first. Optimizing for the larval stage captures more eventual winners.
