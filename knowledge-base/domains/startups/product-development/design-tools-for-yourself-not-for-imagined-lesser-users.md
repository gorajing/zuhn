Paul Graham observes that the best programming languages (C, Lisp) were designed by their authors for their own use, while the worst (Cobol) were designed for 'other people' — always meaning people less capable than the designer. This patronizing stance produces tools that talk down to users and constrain power users.

This generalizes beyond languages: products built by people who are themselves power users of the domain tend to have better taste and tighter feedback loops. Designing for yourself and your peers sets a high bar, while designing for an abstract 'average user' often produces mediocre, lowest-common-denominator tools. The few power users write disproportionately more of the output that matters.
