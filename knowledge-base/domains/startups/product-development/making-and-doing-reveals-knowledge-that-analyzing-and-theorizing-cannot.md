Kean draws a sharp line: traditional archaeology is excellent at determining what the past looked like — walls, buildings, materials. But it cannot access what the past sounded, tasted, or smelled like. Experimental archaeology fills this gap by physically recreating ancient practices: cooking ancient recipes, building ships, making stone tools, constructing mummies.

The human mummy project in Baltimore solved mysteries that centuries of analysis could not: how much natron was needed (several hundred pounds), how long the process took (70 days), and whether the weathered appearance of mummies was from the process or millennia of dry storage (it was the process — visible in weeks). Similarly, ramp theories for pyramid construction looked plausible on paper but failed completely in physical experiments — log rollers do not work, and ramps would require more material than the pyramid itself. The insight is universal: prototyping, building, and physically doing things reveals constraints that analysis alone systematically misses.
