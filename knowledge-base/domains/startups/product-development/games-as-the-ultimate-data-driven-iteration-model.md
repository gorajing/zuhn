The essay argues that games companies have developed the most rigorous data-driven iteration culture of any tech sector. Unlike other tech companies where products can linger as 'zombies,' games must constantly evolve or die — there's no middle ground. This creates founders who are relentlessly measurement-focused, combining data science with creative instincts to optimize every aspect of their product.

This operational discipline — launching quickly, obsessing over metrics, running live ops that personalize experiences hourly — represents a transferable model for any product company. The key insight is that games generate enormous behavioral data across multiple modalities, giving teams an information advantage that compounds over time. Mobile gaming pioneers like King, Playtika, and MoonActive proved this approach, and it has since spread to PC and console gaming. The takeaway for non-gaming founders: if your iteration cycle is slower than a games company's, you're leaving value on the table.
