The conventional enterprise approach — write a detailed spec, then implement — implicitly assumes that understanding comes before coding. Graham inverts this: your code IS your understanding of the problem. You can't fully understand the problem without the code in your head, and you can't get the code in your head if the program is too large from the start.

By starting with a working prototype that solves a subset, you maintain the ability to hold the entire thing in your head, which in turn allows you to see redesigns that change the problem itself, not just the solution. This is why side projects and skunkworks efforts so often produce breakthrough software — they start small enough to be fully internalized. The planning-vs-doing tradeoff isn't about discipline; it's about cognitive capacity.
