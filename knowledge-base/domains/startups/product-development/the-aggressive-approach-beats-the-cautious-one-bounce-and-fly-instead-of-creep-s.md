Raibert describes seeing a slow, cautious six-legged robot that always kept three feet on the ground and immediately thinking 'that's wrong — that's not how animals work.' Animals bounce, fly, use springiness, and predict outcomes. This insight drove 40 years of legged robotics: the pogo stick robot, Big Dog, Atlas, and Spot all embody the principle that dynamic, aggressive approaches yield better results than conservative, static ones.

This directly applies to product development and startup strategy. The cautious approach — maintaining stability, avoiding risk, moving slowly — feels safe but produces mediocre outcomes. The aggressive approach — accepting temporary instability, predicting outcomes, using momentum — produces breakthrough results but requires tolerance for failure during development. Raibert notes the same principle needs to apply to robot manipulation: current grasping is 'all too safe' and needs the equivalent of the pogo stick moment.
