In the 1880s and 1890s, Coca-Cola advertising was intrinsic: 'ideal brain tonic and sovereign remedy for headache and nervousness.' Frank Robinson moved the brand toward 'delicious and refreshing,' and then in the 1920s Robert Woodruff and Archie Lee at the Darcy agency made the leap that defined modern consumer marketing — they stopped talking about the product at all and started selling the feelings around it. Coke became a stand-in for happiness, the good life, family, your sports heroes, beautiful people, and Christmas. Rolex would do the same thing in the 1950s; nearly every major consumer brand since has copied this template.

The mechanism is Pavlovian: enough repetition pairing the product with a positive emotion creates an unconscious association that survives even when the product itself is unremarkable. This is how a commodity (syrup, sugar, water) becomes a $300B company. The drink itself is replaceable; the feeling-association isn't.

If you're building a consumer brand, the practical implication is that you cannot win on features alone past a certain scale. Once the product is good enough, every dollar of marginal advertising is better spent associating the product with a feeling your customer wants to have than describing what the product does. Intrinsic advertising recruits the people who already need your category; extrinsic advertising recruits everyone else.
