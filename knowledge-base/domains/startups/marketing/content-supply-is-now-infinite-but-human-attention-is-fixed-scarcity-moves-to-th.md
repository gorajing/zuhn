The economic reframe: AI drives the marginal cost of producing content, code, and media toward zero, so supply becomes effectively infinite. But the demand side — human attention and consumption capacity — is fixed. A person still has ~2 hours a day to read, not 20. So the classic supply/demand picture inverts: supply explodes, demand holds constant, and the scarce, valuable resource becomes attention (the 'eyeball'), not production.

The competitive trap this creates: producing faster feels like an advantage, but it isn't a durable one, because everyone gets the same speedup simultaneously. You build in 2 days what used to take 2 months — but so does your competitor. The production-speed gain is competed away; what's left scarce is the limited attention you're all fighting for. So winning shifts from 'can you produce it' to 'can you capture attention for it.'

The implications for founders/creators: (1) don't compete on production volume or speed — it's table stakes that confers no edge; (2) invest in whatever captures and holds the fixed attention — distribution, trust, brand, taste, curation; (3) there's an ironic opening the speaker notes — if AI both produces AND summarizes, the winning move may be compression (give the one-sentence gist), not more content. This connects directly to the meta-microsoft 'trust layer' and 'premium brands resist GenAI / curation as moat' insights, and to Zuhn's own thesis that the compression/selection layer is the moat while production is commodity. The durable scarcity in an abundance economy is attention, and the durable skill is earning it.
