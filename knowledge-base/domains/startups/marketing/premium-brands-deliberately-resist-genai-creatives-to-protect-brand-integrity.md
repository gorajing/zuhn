There's a naive assumption that any cost-collapsing technology gets adopted fastest by the players with the most to gain. The luxury-brand case inverts this: premium brands are the slowest GenAI-creative adopters, and that slowness is deliberate, not laggard. Their brand image depends on tight control and curation; mass-generated creative dilutes exactly the scarcity/craft signal that justifies the premium.

The sharp framing the panelist offers: 'having the taste' is what differentiates a premium brand from one that 'tries anything all the time.' Restraint is a feature. GenAI lowers the cost of producing creative, but for premium brands the constraint was never cost — it was maintaining a consistent, controlled, high-craft identity. A tool that makes it easy to produce more, faster, lower-craft content is actively misaligned with that goal.

This predicts a bifurcation: performance/value brands race to GenAI volume (see the agency-disintermediation insight); premium brands hold the line on curated, human-crafted creative as a differentiator. Adoption rate becomes a downstream signal of where a brand sits on the premium spectrum.

The transferable principle beyond marketing: when a technology cheapens production, scarcity and curation become the premium signal. This is the same dynamic as 'AI slop' in writing/design — the floor rises, so the ceiling (taste, restraint, craft) is where differentiation moves.
