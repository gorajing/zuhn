Apple Pay's value proposition was not just about convenience — it was about a security architecture that required tight integration across layers that Apple uniquely controlled: the Secure Enclave in its custom silicon, iOS's biometric authentication, and its relationships with payment networks. No competitor owning only one or two of these layers could deliver the same experience.

This is the structural advantage of vertical integration in platform businesses. When you control the full stack, you can make tradeoffs across layers — sacrificing openness at one layer for security at another — in ways that horizontally-integrated competitors cannot. Google Wallet existed before Apple Pay but couldn't match the security guarantees because it ran on Android hardware made by third parties.

For startups, this implies a strategic question: if your value proposition depends on a seamless experience, which layers do you need to own? Partnering across layers works until a vertically-integrated player enters your space.
