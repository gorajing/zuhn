When TSMC launched in 1987, every credible buyer told Morris 'real men have Fabs' — Intel, TI, Motorola all passed. The short-term market was the dregs: excess capacity and unprofitable product lines the IDMs wanted to unload. What Morris saw, because he'd been inside TI and General Instrument, was that the bottleneck keeping great chip designers from leaving was capex, not talent. A pure-play foundry removed the $100M+ hurdle to start a chip company, and within a decade Qualcomm, Broadcom, Marvell, Nvidia (which only ever raised $20M) all existed because TSMC existed.

The generalizable pattern: platforms rarely win by serving the customers who are shouting loudest today. They win by structurally enabling a class of companies that the existing market can't yet support. The demand signal is invisible — you have to read it from talent that is frustrated inside incumbents because of a capital, distribution, or regulatory barrier. Y Combinator, AWS, Shopify, and Stripe all fit this pattern. Build the thing that turns a would-be founder's 'I can't' into 'I can.'

Apply this when evaluating platform plays: don't ask 'who's asking for this today,' ask 'who would start a company tomorrow if this existed.' If the answer is a cohort trapped inside incumbents by a structural bottleneck, that's the signal.
