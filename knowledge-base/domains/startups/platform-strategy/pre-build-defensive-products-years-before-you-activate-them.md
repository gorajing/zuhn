Google knew by 2001 they had a Microsoft-dependency problem: 90% of searches ran through Internet Explorer on Windows. Eric Schmidt explicitly refused to build a browser in 2001 ('I don't want to moon the giant'), but Google didn't just wait. They became Mozilla's primary funder, then hired Firefox's best engineers directly into Google under a newly-formed 'client products' group led by a McKinsey import named Sundar Pichai. This team sat inside Google for years as what the transcript calls a 'latent sleeper cell.' When Microsoft finally moved to buy Yahoo in 2008 and launch Bing, Chrome shipped within months — because the team, the V8 engine, the process-per-tab architecture, and the WebKit relationship were all already in place.

The parallel pattern played out with Android. Google bought Andy Rubin's 8-person startup in 2005 for $50M, 18 months before the iPhone reveal. Had they waited until January 2007 to start, the mobile OS war was already over — Microsoft would have owned the OEM-licensed side and Apple would have owned the integrated side. Chrome and Android are the two products that let Google survive the mobile platform shift. Both were started years before they were needed, on Eric's judgment that existential threats take years to neutralize and you can't compress that timeline.

The meta-lesson: if you can see a platform threat coming in 3-5 years, the question isn't 'should we act now?' but 'what's the minimum viable team we can build now that gives us optionality when the threat activates?' Hiring generational engineers takes years. Architectural breakthroughs take years. By the time you need the defense, the only variable you can control is whether you started early.
