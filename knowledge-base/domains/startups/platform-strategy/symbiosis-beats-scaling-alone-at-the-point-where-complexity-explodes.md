Lane describes giant bacteria that achieved large cell sizes not through innovation but through extreme polyploidy: tens or even hundreds of thousands of copies of their complete genome, all being maintained and expressed simultaneously. The energy cost is colossal, and it caps out. None of these giant bacteria ever developed sophisticated internal trafficking, complex multicellularity, or anything resembling a eukaryotic cell. They just got bigger through replication of the same machinery.

Eukaryotes took a completely different path. They absorbed a bacterium that became the mitochondrion, and critically, that endosymbiont shed nearly all of its genes—from around 3,000-4,000 down to 37 in humans—keeping only the ones essential to its specialized function. The host cell offloaded energy generation entirely to this specialist, which freed the host to grow larger, develop internal membranes, and eventually build multicellular bodies with differentiated tissues. Neither partner could have reached that endpoint alone.

The pattern generalizes to any complex system facing a scaling ceiling. When a company tries to solve every problem by hiring more generalists or duplicating its existing playbook, it behaves like a giant bacterium: the energy and coordination cost grows faster than the output. The eukaryotic move is to find a specialized partner, let them strip down to what they're uniquely good at, and build interfaces that let each side scale independently. This is why platform strategies so often beat vertical-integration strategies past a certain size: the symbiotic structure is the only way to maintain complexity without burning everything on internal overhead.
