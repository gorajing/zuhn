Sony launched Betamax in 1975 with the best video recording technology in its class. They marketed it around time-shifting TV broadcasts, which alarmed MCA/Universal's Lew Wasserman, who sued Sony for enabling copyright infringement. Matsushita and JVC then approached Universal with VHS, offering a cooperative content strategy. Universal's existing deep relationship with Matsushita meant VHS got the content deals and the pre-recorded movie market. Within a few years, VHS was the dominant format and Betamax became shorthand for losing a format war.

The lesson is not that Sony's technology was bad — it was better. The lesson is that in any format war, the side that controls the content controls the outcome, because consumers buy hardware to access content. Sony treated the battle as a hardware competition and tried to sue away the content problem. VHS treated it as a coalition-building exercise. Years later Morita concluded that the Betamax loss was the driving reason Sony bought Columbia Pictures — to never again be cut off from content leverage.

Apply this to any platform or standard where the hardware is separable from the content/apps running on it. Before launch, have the content deals locked up, or expect to lose even with technically superior hardware. This principle extends to streaming platforms, game consoles, AR/VR devices, and any two-sided market where the complementor side has concentrated power.
