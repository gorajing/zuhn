At YC Startup School 2008, Jeff Bezos used the European brewery analogy to convince startups (including the just-founding Airbnb founders in the audience) to use AWS instead of rolling their own infrastructure. Early breweries built their own electric generators; later ones outsourced to utility companies and ran roughshod over the first generation. The argument: focus only on what makes your beer taste better.

But the deeper lesson is what Bezos did, not what he said. He positioned Amazon to become an unregulated utility company for technology — a structurally exceptional business. That's why AWS made Amazon profitable, and it's the same model behind Square, Shopify, Stripe, Vanta, Modern Treasury, and most B2B infrastructure businesses. They provide a piece of mission-critical infrastructure that customers need but that doesn't differentiate the customers' products.

This is the economic theory of specialization of labor applied to business: GDP rises when entities specialize and trade rather than vertically integrate. Identifying the layer of the stack where you can be the utility — and where customers will gladly outsource because it doesn't help their beer taste better — is one of the most leveraged strategic decisions a founder can make.
