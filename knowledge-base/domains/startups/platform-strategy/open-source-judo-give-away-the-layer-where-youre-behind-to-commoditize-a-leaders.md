Zuckerberg articulates a deliberate strategic logic for open source that has nothing to do with idealism. Google built its distributed computing infrastructure first and kept it proprietary as a competitive moat. Meta built theirs second, and since keeping it proprietary wouldn't catch them up to Google, they open-sourced it instead. The result was Open Compute, which became the industry standard, standardized the data-center supply chain around Meta's designs, and saved Meta billions while improving component quality.

The same logic now drives Llama. Meta is behind OpenAI/Google in proprietary frontier models, but by open-sourcing competitive weights, they commoditize the layer where competitors hold an advantage. Open-source models reduce Meta's dependency on platform owners (the 'Apple tax' on the App Store burned them deeply), give them strategic optionality, and make it harder for any single AI lab to build a Google-scale moat.

The principle: open-source aggressively in domains where you're second-mover, and keep proprietary the domains where you're the leader. Mark explicitly says he's 'not a zealot' about open source — it's situational. The strategic question for any second-mover is which layer of the stack, if commoditized, would most damage the leader's advantage while accruing standardization benefits to you.
