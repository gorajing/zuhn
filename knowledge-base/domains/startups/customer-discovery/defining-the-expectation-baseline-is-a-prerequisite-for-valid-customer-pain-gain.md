Customer interviews frequently produce false signals because interviewers and subjects share an implicit but unverified assumption about what 'normal' is. When a user says they love that a feature 'just works,' they may be expressing relief from previously poor expectations rather than genuine enthusiasm — which won't drive acquisition or retention. The Strategyzer framework makes the expectation baseline explicit as a first step, preventing this conflation.

Practically, this means starting customer discovery interviews with questions that surface the baseline: 'What do you take for granted in this workflow?' and 'What would have to go wrong for this to be a bad experience?' before asking what would make it great. The baseline anchors all subsequent gain/pain coding.

This technique also has implications for competitive positioning: a company that sets a new expectation baseline for an industry category (one-day shipping, instant messaging read receipts) immediately re-codes all incumbents' standard performance as a pain. Baseline shifts are one of the most powerful forms of competitive disruption.
