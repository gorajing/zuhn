Burazin had shown the same ~20-30 prospects an earlier 'agent sandbox' a quarter before and heard 'this is the same as EC2, why is it different?' After his CTO rebuilt it around the right primitive, those identical prospects each ran their 15-minute demo calls 25-30 minutes long and all said 'we want access.' There was no login, just an API key. The decisive moment wasn't the demo going well — it was that the next day every single one called back asking 'where is my API key?' Across multiple companies in his career, Burazin says he had never experienced customers literally calling to demand access if it wasn't delivered.

The operational lesson: distinguish stated interest from revealed urgency. Prospects who like a product give feedback; prospects with acute unmet need behave as if you are withholding something they already depend on. The fact that the same audience that dismissed v0 as 'EC2 with extra steps' became desperate for v1 also shows PMF is often a primitive problem, not a market problem — the demand existed all along and was gated on finding the exact right abstraction.

For founders, this reframes the discovery loop: don't optimize for demo applause or NPS, watch for whether prospects pursue you between meetings. If you stop sending and nobody chases, you have interest, not fit.
