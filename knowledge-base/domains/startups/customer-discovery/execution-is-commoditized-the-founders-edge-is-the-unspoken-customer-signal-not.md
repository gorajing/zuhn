Franceschi's test for any problem is 'why can't AI solve it?' — and the reason a founder still matters is that the deciding signal was never in the model's training data. When you talk to a customer about their problem, 'they're not going to give you the answer shaped into a prompt that an LLM outputs into a billion-dollar product. They give you a very local-optimum answer based on their worldview and constraints.' Before AI, the job was both to choose what to build and to know how to execute it; now 'the execution is gone, the model does it better,' so the missing bottleneck is the wisdom to choose, which traces back to which signals are absent from the models.

The winning skill is reading what the customer is not verbalizing — making the implicit explicit. He ties this to 'theory of mind': the founders who navigate pivots well can simulate what the other person is thinking and catch the subtle, murmured signals. Crucially, when you don't yet know much about a customer, synthetic customers and role-model simulations don't help — those only pay off once you already understand the customer deeply (e.g. Brex's shift from selling to founders, whom they understood intimately, to selling to finance teams with a different value system).

The operating heuristic: 'a good proxy for how to spend your time is what are the things that only you can do that the models cannot?' Even in a company of one, allocate your scarce attention to exactly those — choosing which problems are worth solving and injecting the tacit signal no model has — and let AI absorb the rest.
