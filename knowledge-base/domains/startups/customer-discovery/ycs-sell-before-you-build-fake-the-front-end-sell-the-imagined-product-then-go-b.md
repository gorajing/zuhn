Jessica Wu's experience with YC: 'YC tells you to try and they really push you to try and sell something. This means that even if you don't have a working product, you can put up like a Lovable or something to put up a fake front-end and then you should try and go sell it.' Why this matters: the standard ordering (build first, then sell) optimizes for completeness, but the failure mode is spending 6 months building something nobody wants. The fake-front-end inversion creates a much earlier feedback loop. The most reliable signal that you're building the right thing isn't 'people nodded when I described it' or 'they signed up to be notified' — it's 'they wrote a check' or 'they said they would write a check if it existed.' Sola's actual experience: they met their first customer during YC, told them honestly 'this product doesn't exist but it will in a couple months,' and the customer said 'okay, come back when it does.' That's a confirmed signal even before any code. The cautions: this advice is bounded — once you're past initial validation, 'fake features to sell' becomes counterproductive and burns trust. The pattern is 'sell-then-build' for the initial direction-finding phase only. It's also 'not for the faint of heart' — you might succeed at the sell and now have a 6-week sprint in the basement to actually deliver. The deeper principle: replace cheap signals (interest, sign-ups, positive feedback) with expensive signals (paid commitment, contract terms, signed POs) as early as possible, because cheap signals are systematically misleading.
