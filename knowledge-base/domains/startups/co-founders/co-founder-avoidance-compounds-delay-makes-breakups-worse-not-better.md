Dalton describes a pattern he sees repeatedly: co-founders spending 8-12 hours a day together but admitting they haven't had a real conversation in weeks or months. At this point, the CEO's job shifts from repairing the relationship to executing the cleanest possible separation. But founders can't bring themselves to act because it 'feels bad.'

The compounding cost of delay is concrete: the longer a broken co-founder relationship persists, the more likely there is litigation, the more vested stock complicates the cap table, and the more organizational damage accumulates. Founders who break up and think they'll just start over discover it's much harder to get back to where they were — many regret not investing in the co-founder relationship upfront rather than blowing everything up and expecting a do-over.
