The B-Team members describe the DDS split with a mix of fondness and clear boundaries. Craig Jones misses specific aspects — John Danaher's behind-the-scenes humor, the depth of training partners — but acknowledges that 'the negative things outweighed all of those things.' The split happened because personal problems became intolerable, not because the team lacked talent or results.

The parallel to co-founder splits and team breakups in startups is direct: the highest-performing teams can still fracture when interpersonal dynamics become toxic. Jones also values the freedom gained from leaving a 'very strict regime,' suggesting that elite performance environments can become constraining once the costs of control exceed its benefits. The key lesson: 'nothing's all bad, nothing's all good' — and sometimes the right move is to leave even a legendary team.
