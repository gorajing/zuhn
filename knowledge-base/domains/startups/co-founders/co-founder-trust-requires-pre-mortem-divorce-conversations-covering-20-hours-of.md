Hoffman's co-founder vetting framework mirrors his advice for hiring CEOs: spend 20+ hours in deep conversation exploring every potential difference in opinion, belief, and work style before committing. Crucially, this includes discussing the 'divorce' — under what conditions would either party want to end the partnership? This pre-mortem conversation isn't about creating a contract but establishing conversational precedent. When stress hits (and it will — 'valley of the shadows' moments are inevitable), you've already discussed analogous scenarios, making it natural to raise problems rather than letting resentment build.

The best co-founding teams are those who can reason toward truth together, not those who sing from the same sheet. Hoffman looks for co-founders who challenge each other — 'did you think about this?' rather than unanimous agreement. The PayPal example of Max Levchin regularly walking into Peter Thiel's office to highlight existential threats illustrates this: productive co-founder relationships require the psychological safety to surface problems, not the comfort of avoiding them. Co-founder breakups are 'frequently fatal' to startups, making this upfront investment in alignment one of the highest-leverage activities a founder can undertake.
