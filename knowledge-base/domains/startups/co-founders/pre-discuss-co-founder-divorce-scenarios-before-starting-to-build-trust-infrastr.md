Hoffman recommends spending 20+ hours in deep conversation with potential co-founders, specifically covering areas of possible disagreement, belief differences, and work style conflicts. The most counterintuitive recommendation is to explicitly discuss the 'divorce' — what conditions would lead each person to say 'this isn't working.' This isn't pessimism; it's building conversational infrastructure for the inevitable high-stress moments.

The logic is that when you hit the 'valley of the shadows' — those months where you question why you ever thought the idea was good — you need pre-established patterns for raising difficult topics. If you've already discussed a wide variety of scenarios, bringing up a real problem later becomes 'we talked about a version of this' rather than an ambush. Hoffman also looks for co-founding teams where members actively challenge each other's reasoning (like Max Levchin bringing Peter Thiel lists of 'things that are going to kill us') rather than singing kumbaya. The signal of a strong co-founding team is constructive disagreement, not agreement.
