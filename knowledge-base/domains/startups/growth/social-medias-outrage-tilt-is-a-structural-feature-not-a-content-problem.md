Drawing on his experience running Hacker News from 2007-2014, Graham provides a rare first-person data point: users were approximately three times more likely to upvote something if it outraged them. This wasn't caused by any particular ideology — it's an inherent feature of social media, or at least the current generation of it.

This has direct implications for platform builders: any system that sorts content by engagement will systematically amplify outrage. The fix isn't content moderation or better algorithms on top of engagement sorting — it requires fundamentally different ranking mechanisms. Graham suggests it would be possible to create social media apps less driven by outrage, and that such apps could steal users because the smartest people would migrate first. This frames a viable startup thesis: platforms that optimize for signal quality over engagement could capture a high-value audience segment.
