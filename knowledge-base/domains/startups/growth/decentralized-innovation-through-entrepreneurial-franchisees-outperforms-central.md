Ray Kroc deliberately selected 'young, hungry entrepreneur types' as franchisees and gave them latitude to experiment within quality/service/cleanliness guardrails. This created a distributed innovation engine: individual operators invented the Big Mac, the drive-thru, playground areas, and the Ronald McDonald character through local experiments. Corporate's role was to identify the best innovations and propagate them system-wide.

This inverts the typical scaling problem where growth kills innovation. Most companies centralize decisions as they grow, creating bureaucratic bottlenecks. McDonald's did the opposite — each new franchisee was another experiment running in parallel. The three non-negotiable standards (quality, service, cleanliness) acted as constraints that channeled creativity rather than suppressing it. This is essentially an evolutionary algorithm: generate variation at the edges, select the fittest innovations centrally, then replicate globally. The pattern applies to any organization struggling to stay innovative at scale — push experimentation to the edges while maintaining hard constraints on what matters most.
