When WhatsApp updated its privacy policy to clarify years of data-sharing with Facebook, the backlash was enormous — yet most users stayed. As one Reddit user summarized: 'They don't trust WhatsApp. They just don't care enough about privacy.' But the real mechanism is network-effect lock-in: even users who deeply care about privacy can't switch if their entire social and professional circle remains on WhatsApp.

This reveals a dark property of network effects: they don't just accelerate growth, they create inertia that persists even when trust collapses. The switching cost isn't technical (Signal works fine) but social (you'd lose contact with everyone who won't switch). This means network-effect moats are far more durable than quality-based moats — a product can degrade significantly on dimensions users claim to care about (privacy, trust) without losing them, as long as the network remains intact. For competitors, this means superior features alone won't dislodge an incumbent; you need a mechanism to migrate entire social graphs simultaneously.
