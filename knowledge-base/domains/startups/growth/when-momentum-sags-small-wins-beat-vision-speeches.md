Altman identifies momentum as the prime directive of startup management — if he could tell founders only one thing, it would be to always keep momentum. A winning team feels good and keeps winning; a team that hasn't won in a while gets demotivated and keeps losing. The critical mistake is how founders try to recover lost momentum: they give long vision speeches and try to rally the troops. This doesn't work because demoralized employees don't want to hear vision — they want to win again.

The correct response is to find small, achievable wins and execute on them immediately. Facebook's growth group in 2008 is the canonical example: they worked on individually small things that collectively bent the growth curve back up. A board member's advice crystallizes this: 'sales fix everything in a startup.' When there's internal disagreement during a momentum sag, the tiebreaker is asking users what they want and doing that — it redirects energy from internal conflict to external value creation.
