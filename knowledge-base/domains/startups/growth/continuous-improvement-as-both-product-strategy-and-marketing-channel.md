Graham frames continuous feature shipping not just as good engineering practice but as a compounding loop: implementing ideas generates more ideas (like daily running making tomorrow's run easier), while pausing creates inertia that's hard to overcome. Users who see a site that hasn't changed in two months start to perceive it as dead.

The deeper insight is that most companies ignore user feedback, so actually listening creates outsized loyalty. Users become evangelists, eliminating the need for paid marketing. The psychological trap is that once a product passes the 'glaring flaws' stage, founders start to identify with its current state rather than pushing for what it could be. Graham's test: if your product seems finished, it's a thousand times more likely you lack imagination than that you've actually reached perfection.
