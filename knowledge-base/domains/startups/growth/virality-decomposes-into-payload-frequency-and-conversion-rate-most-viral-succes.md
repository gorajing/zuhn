Shawn Parker taught Facebook's growth team to decompose virality into three factors: payload (how many people you reach per viral event), frequency (how often you can trigger the event), and conversion rate (what percentage act on it). This framework explains why seemingly different viral products succeed through different mechanisms.

Hotmail had low payload (one recipient per email) but high frequency (you email the same people daily) and high conversion (people wanted free email not tied to their ISP). PayPal had low payload and low frequency but extremely high conversion -- 'I'm going to send you money' is near-100% conversion for sellers, and '$10 free' converted buyers. Facebook, counterintuitively, was NOT viral through any product mechanism -- it grew through word-of-mouth because there was no native way to contact non-users. The framework also connects to the K-factor calculation: if 100 people receive invites, 10% click, 50% sign up, and 10-20% subsequently invite others, your K-factor is 0.5-1.0 -- not truly viral.
