Kate noticed that Silicon Valley feels like 'everybody is in the industry,' and Graham confirms there's a qualitative difference — you keep your voice down because the person at the next table likely knows the people you're discussing, but the upside is they could also help you. This dual nature of dense industry networks creates both accountability (you can't trash-talk freely) and serendipitous opportunity.

This observation speaks to a structural advantage of geographic clustering that goes beyond intentional networking. The ambient density of relevant knowledge and connections creates a compounding effect — chance encounters, overheard conversations, and casual introductions generate deal flow and problem-solving that's difficult to engineer through deliberate remote networking. Whether this advantage persists as remote work normalizes is an open question, making this insight time-sensitive.
