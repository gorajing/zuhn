The quasi-star hypothesis proposes that early universe stars grew so massive that their cores collapsed into black holes while still forming. Rather than destroying the star (as happens today), a balance emerged: gravity fed the black hole while radiation pressure kept the star stable. The black hole consumed its host over millions of years, growing far beyond what normal stellar processes allow.

This is a powerful metaphor for startup growth: the most effective accelerators are often temporary structures designed to be consumed. An early enterprise contract that funds product development but won't scale. A founding team configuration that's perfect for 0-to-1 but must dissolve for 1-to-N. A market positioning that bootstraps growth but gets shed as the company outgrows it. The scaffold's value isn't in persisting — it's in creating conditions for something larger than itself.
