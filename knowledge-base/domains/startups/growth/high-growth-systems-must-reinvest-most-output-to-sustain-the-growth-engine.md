In the Calvin Cycle's carbon fixation, three turns of the cycle convert 3 CO2 molecules into 6 G3P molecules, but 5 of those 6 must be recycled to regenerate the 3 RuBP molecules that keep the cycle running. Only one G3P — roughly 17% of total output — leaves the cycle as usable product. The cycle literally cannot function if you extract more.

This maps directly to startup growth economics. Companies in hypergrowth phases typically reinvest 80-90% of revenue back into customer acquisition, product development, and infrastructure. Attempting to extract profit too early — taking more than your 'one G3P' — starves the regeneration loop and collapses growth. The biological ratio suggests a useful heuristic: if your growth engine requires more than ~85% reinvestment to sustain itself, you're in a legitimate growth phase; if it requires more than ~95%, the engine may be fundamentally inefficient and worth redesigning rather than feeding.
