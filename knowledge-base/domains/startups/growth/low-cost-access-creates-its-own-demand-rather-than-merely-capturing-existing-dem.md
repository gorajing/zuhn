Traditional airlines need routes with pre-existing high demand because their cost structure requires high-yield passengers (typically business travelers). Budget airlines invert this: by making a 2-3 hour flight available for under 10 euros, they make destinations viable for weekend trips, spontaneous travel, and tourism that would never have occurred at legacy price points. The demand is induced by the price, not pre-existing.

This is a critical distinction for startup strategy. Competing on price within an existing market is a race to the bottom, but reducing price enough to unlock entirely new use cases creates market expansion where you're the monopoly provider of the new demand. The competitive moat isn't the low price itself—it's that you've created a customer base that wouldn't exist without your cost structure, making you structurally immune to legacy competitors who can only serve the pre-existing demand pool.
