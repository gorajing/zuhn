Gmail launched April 1, 2004 with a gigabyte of free storage — an insane number when Hotmail offered 2MB and Yahoo Mail offered 4MB. Even assuming Google's commodity infrastructure gave them a 90% cost advantage, they still faced an effective per-user cost roughly 25x their competitors. They also had no public cloud to rent — every gigabyte meant physical servers in a datacenter they had to build and operate. There was no way to scale 'unlimited immediately.'

The invite system solved this: 1,000 seed invites to journalists and influencers, then each user got ~5 invites that topped back up at Google's discretion based on what infrastructure they could actually provision that week. The Acquired hosts note the team doesn't actually know whether it was designed primarily as operational rationing or as a prestige play — but the operational constraint was real, and the prestige benefits were emergent. Invites traded on eBay for $150. Getting one felt like joining a secret club.

The reusable pattern: when a constraint is forcing you to gate access anyway, lean into the gating as the marketing story rather than apologizing for it. Framed as artificial scarcity, it looks manipulative; framed as 'we care enough about quality/performance to grow this deliberately,' it becomes a feature. Every AI product launch since ChatGPT has copied this — but the original came from engineers trying not to melt their datacenter. The takeaway: don't design growth mechanics from a playbook, find the real constraint you're managing and turn it into the story.
