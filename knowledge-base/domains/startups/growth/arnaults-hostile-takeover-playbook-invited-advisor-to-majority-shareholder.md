After the Louis Vuitton-Moet Hennessy merger in 1987, Henry Racamier (LV's leader) clashed with the Moet Hennessy president. Racamier invited Bernard Arnault — a property developer, not a fashion executive — to help mediate. Arnault used the access to secretly acquire 43% of LVMH shares and allied with the Moet Hennessy families to gain majority control. The courts sided with Arnault, and for the first time in nearly 100 years, no Vuitton family member was involved with the brand.

This is a classic corporate governance failure pattern: when two internal factions invite a third party to break a deadlock, the third party has every incentive to capture the position for themselves rather than resolve the dispute. The lesson for founders navigating mergers or board disputes is that any 'neutral' party with capital is a potential acquirer. Mediation and investment are incompatible roles — the moment you give someone financial access to resolve a governance problem, you've given them the tools to become the problem's beneficiary.
