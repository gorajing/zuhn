Churn timing is diagnostic. When users drop off in the first 30-90 days, the failure is almost always in onboarding—the 'aha' moment comes too late, activation steps are buried, or sales overpromised what the product delivers. This is a value delivery speed problem that can be addressed with focused product work on the initial experience.

Late-stage churn is more insidious and more damaging. These customers stayed long enough to validate initial fit, then watched the product fail to evolve with their needs. Feature requests go unanswered, ROI becomes vague, login frequency drops. By the time cancellation appears on the dashboard, the customer mentally left months ago. This distinction matters because the interventions are completely different—early churn needs onboarding optimization while late churn needs product roadmap and customer success investment.
