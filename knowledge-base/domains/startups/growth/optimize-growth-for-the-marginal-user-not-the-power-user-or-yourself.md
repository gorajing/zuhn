Schultz identifies a systematic bias in Silicon Valley: founders and growth teams optimize for their own experience as power users. His canonical example is notifications -- every company he advises says 'we get too many notifications, let's reduce them.' But power users aren't leaving because of notifications. The marginal user -- the one who doesn't get a notification in a given day -- is the one who churns.

Facebook's growth accounting framework (new users + resurrected users - churned users) revealed that resurrected and churned user flows dominate new user acquisition once a product reaches maturity. These marginal users consistently had low friend counts and hadn't experienced the magic moment. Turning on first-like notifications for low-engaged users increased click-through rates across email, SMS, and push without spamming power users. The principle: build the product for power users, but drive growth by serving the marginal user.
