Founders typically frame churn as a monthly percentage—3% here, a few logos there. But the real cost is exponential, not linear. A $150K ARR customer in a high-margin SaaS business could generate over $1M in profit across a 7-8 year lifetime. When that customer leaves, you don't lose $150K—you lose the entire compounding trajectory.

This reframing matters because it changes how you allocate resources. If each churned customer represents a million-dollar leak, retention investments that seem expensive against monthly revenue suddenly look like bargains against lifetime value. The compounding nature of retention means small improvements in churn rate produce outsized gains over time, while small increases in churn silently erode the entire growth engine.
