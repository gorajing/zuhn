Rosolie describes Jungle Keepers' strategy of converting loggers and gold miners — people actively destroying the Amazon — into conservation rangers and ecotourism operators. The approach is purely economic: loggers making $15/day destroying ancient trees were offered $35/day plus health insurance and job security to protect those same trees. A gold miner whose equipment was destroyed by the Peruvian Navy was shown photos of ecotourism and built his own lodge within a year.

This 'adversary conversion' model works because it respects the adversary's core competence (jungle knowledge, physical toughness) while redirecting it. Rather than vilifying loggers — the standard conservationist approach — Rosolie's team socialized with them, built kinship through shared jungle experiences, and then presented the economic alternative. The principle generalizes beyond conservation: when fighting an entrenched behavior, offering a superior alternative that leverages existing skills is more effective than enforcement or moral arguments. This is why Jungle Keepers now protects 50,000+ acres.
