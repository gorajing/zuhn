When Nike featured Colin Kaepernick in their 2018 'Dream Crazy' campaign, stock dropped 2.5%, people burned Nike shoes on social media, and boycotts were declared. Yet the campaign generated $6 billion in brand value, $163 million in earned media, and a 31% sales increase. Nike also won its first Emmy for a commercial since 2002.

The key mechanism is asymmetric activation: controversy creates intense opposition from a smaller group while mobilizing a larger sympathetic audience who now purchase as an act of identity expression. Nike's marketing team calculated that their core demographic skewed younger and more diverse — the exact audience that would rally behind the message. This isn't reckless provocation; it's a calculated demographic bet. The lesson for marketers is that controversy only works when you've accurately mapped which side of the divide your actual customers fall on.
