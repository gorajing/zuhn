By 1953, mail-order furniture in Sweden had a trust crisis: competitors used attractive catalog photos that bore little resemblance to what arrived, with no recourse (no credit cards, no modern returns). Price wars were degenerating into quality fraud. Kamprad's answer wasn't to pull back from mail order or to become a chain of stores. He opened one showroom in a town of a thousand people — explicitly not a store, just a place to 'convince' customers. People would take the train from across the country to touch the furniture once, fill out an order form, and receive it by mail weeks later. The catalog did the selling; the showroom did the trusting.

The insight generalizes: every distribution channel has one structural weakness. For mail order in 1953, it was verification. For e-commerce in 2010, it was 'how does this actually feel' (solved by free returns, not stores). For DTC brands now, it's discovery (solved by wholesale partnerships, not paid ads). The trap is thinking you need to replace the channel or build a full second channel; you only need to bolt on the specific format that addresses the weakness. IKEA's 'exhibition' is the minimum viable fusion: one location, one experience, serving the mail-order catalog rather than competing with it.

In modern terms: if your primary channel has a trust, fit, or discovery gap, don't dilute it — ask what specific physical, social, or experiential component would close the gap and build only that. Bonobos Guideshops, Warby Parker's first showroom, Tesla mall stores all echo this pattern.
