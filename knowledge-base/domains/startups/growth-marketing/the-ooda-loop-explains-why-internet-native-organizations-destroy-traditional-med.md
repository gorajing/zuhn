Andreessen applies John Boyd's OODA loop (Observe-Orient-Decide-Act) to media competition. The framework says: whoever gets through their decision-making cycle faster wins. But there's a devastating second-order effect — if you're sustainably faster, the slower side never completes their cycle. They're perpetually interrupted, forced to restart, and eventually suffer psychological breakdown.

The NYT's OODA loop is 24 hours (their editorial process). The internet's viral post cycle is 12 hours up, 24 hours down, then gone — replaced by the next one. Traditional media is permanently inside the internet's OODA loop. They can never get ahead. They're always covering yesterday's viral post. The same dynamic applies to companies: Elon does this to aerospace competitors, Anduril does this to defense incumbents, a16z tries to do it to competing VCs. The practical implication for any organization: commit to speed. You cannot have long bureaucratic processes, risk-averse postures, or weeks-long deliberation cycles. Political campaigns adapted 30 years ago with 'rapid response' war rooms. Companies must do the same.
