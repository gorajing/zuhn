Cannon-Brookes reveals that when you talk to enterprise customers about AI pricing, they actively prefer seats — not because seats are economically optimal, but because seats are understandable and controllable. AI credits fail the 'casino chip' test: customers can't compare credits across vendors (unlike gigabytes), they can't control consumption (vendors add features that consume credits without customer action), and there's no portability.

The outcome-based pricing alternative has its own trap: if you save a customer $10 in year one (down from $20), in year two they say 'I only spent $10, now save me more.' The baseline constantly resets, making it difficult to capture value over time. Consumption-based pricing works only when customers control what they put in (like Splunk logging or S3 storage) and the unit is transferable across vendors.

For AI startups: the winning pricing model needs to feel fair (Dan Ariely's principle), be predictable for both buyer and seller, and be controllable by the customer. Per-seat pricing with AI-powered productivity gains may actually be the optimal model for many categories — the seat represents the human who now does 10x more work, and the price feels fair because it scales with company size.
