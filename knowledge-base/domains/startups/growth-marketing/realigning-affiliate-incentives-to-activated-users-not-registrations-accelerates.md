In 2004, eBay changed its affiliate payment model from paying per confirmed registered user (CRU) to paying per activated confirmed registered user (ACRU) — meaning the user had to actually bid, buy, or list an item. Overnight, confirmed registrations driven by affiliates dropped ~20%, but activated users only dropped ~5%. The ratio of CRU to ACRU improved, and growth of active users massively accelerated.

The mechanism is straightforward: when affiliates were paid for registrations, they optimized landing pages to force registration (e.g., landing trampoline searchers on the registration page). When paid for activation, they landed users on search results showing the product they wanted, creating a magic moment that led to genuine engagement. This is a textbook case of Goodhart's Law in reverse — fixing the metric to measure what actually matters realigned an entire ecosystem of external partners toward genuine value creation.
