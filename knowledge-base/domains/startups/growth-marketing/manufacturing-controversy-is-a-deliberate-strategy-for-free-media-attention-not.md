Lalit Modi was explicit about this in interviews: 'Our number one pillar of the IPL was that we needed to have a department called controversy. If you're controversial, good or bad doesn't matter. As long as people talk about us, they hate us, they like us, great, no problem.' The player auction was designed not just for price discovery but as a deliberately controversial public spectacle — opening sealed envelopes for sponsorship bids in front of cameras, 'cattle and sheep' headlines about auctioning humans, Porsche bribes for cricket performances. Each controversy generated front-page coverage that would have cost tens of millions in advertising to match.

The principle: in an attention economy, the marginal value of controversy-driven media is often higher than the marginal value of paid media, and the optimal strategy is to actively engineer controversies that the press cannot resist covering. This requires comfort with a portion of the audience hating you — but as Modi understood, hate-watching is still watching, and the fence-sitters become curious converts. Most founders and brand managers default to risk-minimization (avoid offense, blandly positive PR) and miss the much larger opportunity in deliberately polarizing moves.

The constraint: the underlying product must be real and high-quality. Controversy as a marketing tactic on top of a great product (NFL draft drama, IPL auctions, Tesla launches) compounds. Controversy on top of a weak product accelerates collapse. Modi could afford to be controversial because he had architected a structurally great league underneath; the spectacle was the marketing layer, not a substitute for substance.
