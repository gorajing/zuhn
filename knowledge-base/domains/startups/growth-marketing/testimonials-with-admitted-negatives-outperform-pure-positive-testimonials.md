Applying the damaging admission principle to social proof reveals why overly polished testimonials underperform. When a customer says 'The first month was honestly rough and the learning curve was steep, but by month three my revenue doubled,' this converts better than 'Amazing product, my revenue doubled!' The negative portion inoculates the reader against the skepticism they'd otherwise apply to the positive claim. It also makes the testimonial feel authentic rather than curated.

This has direct implications for how companies collect and display customer stories. Rather than editing out complaints or selecting only uniformly positive reviews, the optimal strategy is to feature testimonials that follow the negative-but-positive arc. The admitted negative should be genuine and specific (not 'I was a little skeptical at first' — which is a non-admission) to trigger the credibility mechanism. Companies that systematically coach customers to share their honest struggles before outcomes build a testimonial library that compounds trust over time.
