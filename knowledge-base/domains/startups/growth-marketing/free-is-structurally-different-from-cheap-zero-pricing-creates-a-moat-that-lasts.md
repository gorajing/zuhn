When Robinhood decided to offer zero-commission stock trading, they didn't debate pricing internally — it was intuitively clear that free would be much stronger to market than even 99 cents or $1. The insight proved correct: they had zero commissions to themselves for 3-4 years before incumbents caught up. Even then, incumbents only moved to zero because they were visibly losing customers at an accelerating rate.

The structural advantage of zero over cheap is that zero changes the category conversation. 'We charge less' is a feature comparison; 'it's free' is a category disruption that forces people to ask why it was ever paid in the first place. Zero also creates an operational moat: to sustain zero pricing, Robinhood was forced to minimize cost of servicing per account, which became a lasting efficiency advantage. They also set account minimums to $0, reasoning that solving scale problems would be better than avoiding them. The lesson: when entering an overpriced market, don't negotiate down — go to zero and force the industry to follow.
