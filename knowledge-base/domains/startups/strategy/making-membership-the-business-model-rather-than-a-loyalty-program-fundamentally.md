Most businesses treat loyalty programs as a retention tool layered on top of a transaction-based model — points, discounts, perks that encourage repeat purchases. Costco inverts this: the membership IS the business model. Because 75% of profit comes from the annual fee, every pricing decision is filtered through one question: will this make members renew next year? This alignment is self-enforcing. Raising prices nets a few cents today but costs $60 in lost membership next year — so the company self-imposes a 15% maximum markup rule. The result is a business where customer interests and company interests point in the same direction, which is exceedingly rare in retail. This pattern has broader relevance for any product or service: when the revenue mechanism directly depends on delivering ongoing value (subscriptions, retainers, annual licenses), the incentive structure naturally suppresses extraction behavior.
