RuBisCo's story is a parable about environmental disruption. The enzyme was well-adapted to its original environment (high CO2, low O2). But photosynthesis was so successful that it transformed the atmosphere — raising oxygen levels dramatically. RuBisCo, unable to distinguish O2 from CO2, began fixing oxygen instead of carbon roughly half the time, producing phosphoglycolate — a toxic compound plants must actively detoxify.

The cruel irony: photosynthesis created the very conditions that undermined its own core enzyme. This pattern recurs in business and technology. A platform's success changes the competitive landscape in ways that undermine its original advantages. Social networks that grow through openness face spam and abuse at scale. Marketplaces that grow through low barriers face quality problems. The strategic implication is that success itself changes the environment, and founders must anticipate how their own growth will create new failure modes in their core architecture.
