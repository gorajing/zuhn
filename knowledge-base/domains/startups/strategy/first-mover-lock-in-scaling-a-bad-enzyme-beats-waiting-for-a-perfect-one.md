RuBisCo, the enzyme responsible for carbon fixation in all plants, was evolved in a low-oxygen atmosphere where it worked adequately. When plants themselves raised atmospheric oxygen levels, RuBisCo started grabbing O2 instead of CO2 roughly half the time, producing toxic byproducts. Yet plants had already built their entire metabolic architecture around it.

Rather than evolving a replacement, plants simply produce staggering quantities — an estimated 40 billion tons exist on Earth at any time — and developed secondary pathways to detoxify the waste products. This mirrors how startups often scale a mediocre v1 with brute force (more servers, more engineers, more workarounds) because the switching cost of a rewrite exceeds the cost of compensating. The lesson is that path dependence is real: the first adequate solution that achieves scale becomes nearly impossible to displace, even when conditions change dramatically.
