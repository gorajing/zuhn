A critical principle of riot control is the distinction between containing violence and containing people. Police deliberately leave escape routes for demonstrators who decide they've had enough. Full containment would trap people who want to leave alongside those who want to fight, converting potential defectors into cornered combatants. The intimidating appearance and formation of riot police serves this exact function — not to crush opposition, but to make leaving the attractive option.

This maps directly to competitive strategy and conflict management. When facing opposition — whether competitors, hostile stakeholders, or internal resistance — the instinct to 'crush' or fully contain the threat often backfires by eliminating face-saving exit paths. The Charlottesville police also learned that an idealistic plan to physically pre-sort opposing groups was unrealistic; passionate actors don't self-select into orderly zones. Effective strategy accounts for adversary irrationality and always provides a dignified way out, because the goal is behavior change, not total victory.
