Costco's mandatory membership fee appears to be a fatal barrier to customer acquisition — charging people just to enter the store. In practice, the fee functions as a demographic filter that selects for affluent households (average income ~$100K) and business owners (one-third of members generating two-thirds of sales). The upfront cost triggers sunk-cost psychology that deepens loyalty while simultaneously ensuring the customer base has high purchasing power.

This principle extends beyond retail. Many successful businesses deliberately introduce friction that repels casual users while attracting committed, high-value ones. The confusing warehouse layout similarly converts from annoyance to engagement — shoppers wander through aisles discovering deals, feeling like treasure hunters rather than frustrated customers. Each apparent obstacle is engineered to serve a dual purpose: reduce costs while simultaneously increasing customer value and engagement.
