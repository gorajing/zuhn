stance: Dirac couldn't solve his simultaneous equations with 2x2 matrices
---
Dirac needed coefficients where the order of multiplication mattered (AB is not BA). He naturally started with 2x2 matrices — the smallest and simplest option. Some worked individually but he couldn't find solutions for all four coefficients simultaneously. He was stuck.

Then he had what he later described as a sudden realization: 'there was no need to stick to quantities which can be represented by matrices with just two rows and columns. Why not go to four rows and columns?' The moment he expanded the dimension, all four solutions fell out naturally. The 4x4 matrices also turned out to encode spin and antimatter — physics that 2x2 matrices literally couldn't contain.

This is a transferable problem-solving strategy: when you're stuck trying to solve a problem within a given frame, the solution often lives in a higher-dimensional version of the problem. In startups, this maps to platform expansion (a marketplace that can't find liquidity in one vertical finds it across three), organizational design (a flat org that can't scale adds one layer and everything clicks), or product architecture (a feature set that doesn't cohere in one product becomes coherent when split into two complementary products).
