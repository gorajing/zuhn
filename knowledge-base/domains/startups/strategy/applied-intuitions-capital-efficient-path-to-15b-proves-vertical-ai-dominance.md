Qasar Younis built Applied Intuition into a $15B company by focusing deeply on physical AI for automotive, serving 18 of the top 20 automakers. Two strategic moves stand out: going multi-product within the first year (expanding surface area before competitors could catch up) and preserving capital while rewarding long-term equity over inflated salaries.

This blueprint contrasts sharply with horizontal AI companies burning cash on broad market plays. By dominating a specific vertical first, Applied Intuition created deep switching costs—automakers who integrate simulation and AI tooling into their development pipeline cannot easily rip it out. The lesson for founders: vertical depth creates defensibility that horizontal breadth cannot, especially when the vertical has high regulatory and safety requirements that raise barriers to entry.
