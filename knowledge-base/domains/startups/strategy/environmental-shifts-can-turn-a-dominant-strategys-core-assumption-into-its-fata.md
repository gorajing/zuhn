RuBisCo evolved when atmospheric oxygen was negligible, so distinguishing between CO2 and O2 was unnecessary. But photosynthesis itself filled the atmosphere with oxygen, turning RuBisCo's design assumption into a critical flaw. The enzyme now grabs the wrong molecule roughly half the time — and because every plant on Earth depends on it, the problem is essentially unfixable at scale.

This is a precise analogy for how market dominance creates self-undermining dynamics. A startup's winning strategy often reshapes its environment in ways that erode the strategy's own foundations. The dominant social network changes user expectations about privacy. The marketplace that achieved liquidity changes seller economics. The key lesson is that success doesn't just attract competition — it literally changes the conditions that made the strategy work, and the deeper the commitment to that strategy, the harder it is to adapt.
