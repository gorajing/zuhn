Lucy Guo's framing on city choice: 'San Francisco by far number one, and then New York. Those are where the smartest entrepreneurs go. If you look at every Ivy League, Stanford, Berkeley — everyone ends up in SF and New York. You want somewhere with a lot of talent density and a lot of people that want to be entrepreneurs because those are the people that also have that relentless belief they're going to be billionaires. You're the average of the people you hang out with. In SF, every single person is trying to build a unicorn — you're going to feel incredibly lazy if you're an engineer at Google, and you're going to feel embarrassed every time someone asks what you do. Public embarrassment is actually a really great way to push you to be more ambitious.' The mechanism isn't capital (capital flows everywhere now) or talent supply (remote work disperses talent) — it's the AMBIENT NORM of what's considered 'ambitious enough.' In Austin, building a $10M company is impressive. In SF, building a $100M company is considered the baseline expectation among ambitious peers. The constant comparative pressure functions like a forcing field on what founders consider acceptable. The trade-offs are real: cost of living is brutal, the social culture is intensity-coded, and burnout rates are high. But for the specific outcome of 'building a $1B+ company,' the ambient pressure to keep ratcheting up is mechanically helpful in ways no other location replicates. The corollary for non-SF/NY operators: explicitly construct an artificial 'ambient norm' through curated peer groups (founder Discords, YC batches, in-person founder dinners) to substitute for what you'd absorb passively in SF. The trap: most founders who say 'I can build this from anywhere' end up with what's locally considered 'big' which is usually 10x smaller than what would have been their SF target.
