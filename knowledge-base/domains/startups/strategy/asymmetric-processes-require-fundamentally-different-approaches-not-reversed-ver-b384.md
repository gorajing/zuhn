DNA polymerase can only add nucleotides in one direction (5' to 3'). The leading strand replicates smoothly in this direction, but the lagging strand runs antiparallel — it can't simply reverse the process. Instead, biology invented Okazaki fragments: short segments synthesized in the allowed direction, then stitched together by ligase. It's an entirely novel mechanism, not a mirror of the forward process.

This principle applies broadly to business strategy. Scaling up and scaling down are not symmetric operations — you can't just reverse your growth playbook to downsize effectively. Building a marketplace is not the reverse of building a product. Entering a market and exiting it require fundamentally different strategies. When founders encounter a process that must work in both directions, the instinct to 'just reverse it' almost always fails. The lagging strand teaches that asymmetric constraints demand asymmetric solutions — sometimes radically different mechanisms that achieve the same end result through an entirely different path.
