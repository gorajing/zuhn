Graham identifies a recurring pattern across technology waves: investors take big trends too literally, investing in the most 'Internettish' companies (like Pets.com) when the real money flows to adjacent players. During the railroad boom, Carnegie's steelworks (which made the rails) and Standard Oil (which used railroads for distribution) captured far more value than most railroad companies.

This pattern has immediate strategic implications: when a new technology wave emerges, the highest-probability investment and startup strategy is to build something that the trend needs or that uses the trend as infrastructure, rather than competing to be the trend itself. For every Google, there will be ten JetBlues — companies that use the Internet to transform an existing industry rather than being 'Internet companies' per se.
