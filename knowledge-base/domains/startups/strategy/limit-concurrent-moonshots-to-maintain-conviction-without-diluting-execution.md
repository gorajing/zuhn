New Story's framework for managing radical innovation is instructive: be highly selective about which 'crazy until it's not' ideas to pursue, ensure none would sink the organization if it fails, and commit fully to the ones you choose. They applied this to 3D-printed homes — allocating significant capital despite widespread skepticism, but not betting the entire organization on it.

The underlying principle is that conviction and focus are scarce resources. Pursuing too many moonshots dilutes both, turning bold bets into half-hearted experiments. But the bets also need to be sized correctly — big enough to be meaningful if they work (100x impact potential), small enough to survive if they don't. This is essentially portfolio theory applied to innovation strategy: concentrate your bets for conviction, but don't put the whole portfolio in one position. When a bet pays off (as 3D printing did for New Story), the proof of concept transforms 'crazy' into 'obvious' and unlocks partnership, media attention, and talent that were inaccessible before.
