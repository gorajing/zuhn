Kent Yoshimura's explicit portfolio strategy: 'Let's say you have three products. Wintergreen, peppermint, and spearmint are three flavors of gum. We realize that people who buy the peppermint flavor are more likely to come back and buy the spearmint flavor. But someone that buys the wintergreen flavor is realistically just going to keep buying the wintergreen flavor or isn't willing to experiment. We will COMPLETELY CUT OUT wintergreen if that's the case, because we know that person will go on Amazon and buy wintergreen. We know they won't be on TikTok anymore. But that person buying peppermint that's going to come back to TikTok and buy spearmint — that's a really valuable customer and a really valuable product.' The Costco analog: 'The rotisserie chicken at Costco literally loses them money. The hot dog at Costco literally loses them money — it's been $1.50 forever. The CEO of Costco said, you change the price on that, I will fire you. There are customers who come to Costco, walk all the way to the back of the store to buy the rotisserie chicken, then buy everything else on the way back.' The strategic insight: ROI per product in isolation is misleading. The right metric is INCREMENTAL CUSTOMER LIFETIME VALUE per product. Some products have high margin but zero incremental LTV (the customer was going to spend the same total anyway). Others have low or negative margin but drive massive incremental LTV (the customer wouldn't be in your ecosystem without them). The portfolio audit framework: (1) For each product, measure: do customers who buy this product come back for other products? Do they bring new customers via word-of-mouth? Do they generate content/reviews? (2) Identify the products where the answer is YES — these are your hero products. Price aggressively, ship reliably, advertise heavily even at zero margin. (3) Identify products where the answer is NO — customers buy once and disappear. Kill these regardless of margin, because they consume operational complexity (inventory, returns, support) for no funnel value. The corollary for product expansion: when adding a new SKU, the question isn't 'is this profitable on its own' — it's 'does this product expand or stabilize the customer cohort that buys our hero products?' If yes, even unprofitable-in-isolation SKUs can earn their place. If no, profitable SKUs can be drag on the overall business. The pattern generalizes far beyond DTC: software companies use free tiers as hero products, content platforms use viral 'gateway content' as hero pieces, restaurants use loss-leader signature dishes. Any time the customer relationship is multi-product or multi-purchase, the Costco rotisserie chicken model applies.
