Chinese local governments hold a monopoly on converting agricultural land to residential use. Since housing is socially mandatory (marriage prerequisite, primary savings vehicle), this monopoly position functions as a taxation mechanism with no rate ceiling. Unlike explicit taxes, which face political resistance and legal constraints, land sale revenue is extracted through market transactions that obscure the government's role as monopoly supplier.

This pattern maps directly to platform economics in startups: any entity that controls the sole gateway to a necessity can extract rents far exceeding what competitive markets or democratic accountability would permit. App stores, payment processors, and licensing bodies all exhibit similar dynamics. The strategic insight is that the most defensible business positions aren't just about owning supply — they're about owning supply of something the buyer cannot choose to forgo. The China housing case is an extreme example, but the principle scales down to any market where a single gatekeeper sits between demand and a socially non-optional outcome.
