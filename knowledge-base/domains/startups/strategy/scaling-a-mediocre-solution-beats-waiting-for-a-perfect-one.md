RuBisCo, the enzyme responsible for carbon fixation in photosynthesis, is remarkably bad at its job — it confuses oxygen for CO2 roughly half the time, creating toxic byproducts that plants must expend energy to neutralize. Yet plants never evolved a better alternative. Instead, they doubled down: producing staggering quantities of RuBisCo to compensate for its error rate.

This mirrors a pattern in startups and technology: the first adequate solution that achieves scale often becomes permanent infrastructure, regardless of its elegance. Plants went 'all-in on the RuBisCo strategy' and the switching cost became infinite. The lesson isn't that quality doesn't matter — it's that deployment velocity and scale can make a mediocre solution unassailable. TCP/IP, QWERTY, JavaScript — all are arguably suboptimal designs that won through proliferation, not perfection.
