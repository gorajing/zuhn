Given that organizational change is the real bottleneck (companion insight), the strategic response the panel converged on is: don't fight it — fit into it. Two concrete examples:

1. Bedrock Robotics retrofits autonomy onto excavators designed to slot into existing construction sites. Their excavator works with a human-driven dump truck OR an autonomous one — it adapts to the customer's current setup. The panelist's verdict: 'a lot of companies have failed trying to redesign the entire process, thinking they know how to do the whole thing from scratch.'

2. Knightscope, a Silicon Valley robotics company, bought a human-guarding firm — to Wall Street's confusion. The logic: package the offering in a form a chief security officer already knows how to buy (human guarding contracts), land the large normal contract, THEN backfill with technology. Meet the buyer in the category they already purchase, rather than asking them to adopt a novel category.

The transferable principle: when selling into change-resistant organizations, minimize the change you require of the customer. Adapt your product to their existing infrastructure, processes, and procurement categories; earn the right to introduce novelty after you're inside. The failure mode is technologist hubris — assuming you can redesign the customer's world from scratch because your technology is better.

This is the GTM counterpart to the autonomous-driving lesson the same panel cited: the early 'just put magnet strips in every road and change all the infrastructure' approach failed because people won't accept wholesale infrastructure change; you have to meet them where they are.
