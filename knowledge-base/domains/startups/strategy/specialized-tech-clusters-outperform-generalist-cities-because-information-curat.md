The common assumption is that the internet has equalized information access, making geographic clusters obsolete. The essay argues this fundamentally misunderstands the problem: in a world of superabundant information, access isn't the bottleneck—knowing which information to pay attention to is. Human networks serve as the best knowledge curation filters, and the most valuable insider information is never published publicly.

This explains why specialized clusters (Utah for enterprise SaaS, NYC/London for fintech, Israel for security) outperform generalist tech scenes. When most nodes in your network work in the same domain, each interaction transmits more curated, relevant information. Founders visiting SF report learning more through osmosis in a few days of conversations than in the last year at home—a firsthand experience of the mathematical law that learning speed scales geometrically with network density and specialization.
