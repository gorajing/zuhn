Matt Ridley identifies four prerequisites for innovation: (1) a body of innovators around you to build on, (2) the ability to take many tries (requiring venture capital, startup-friendly environments), (3) tolerance for error and failure, and (4) feedback loops from a large customer base. Modern regulatory environments increasingly eliminate #3 — we 'don't like people making error anymore' — but by covering downside risk, we also cut off the upside.

This explains why Silicon Valley thrives in 'the last unregulated domains' and why physical-domain innovation (energy, transport, biotech) has stagnated compared to digital. The innovation speed in regulated physical domains is dramatically lower than in digital domains. Ridley notes that historically, politically fragmented regions (Renaissance Italy, Song Dynasty China, pre-unification Europe) innovated most because innovators could move between jurisdictions to find friendlier regimes.
