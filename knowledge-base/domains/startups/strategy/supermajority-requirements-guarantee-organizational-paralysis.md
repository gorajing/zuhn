The Articles of Confederation required a 9-of-13 supermajority for any congressional decision and unanimous consent for amendments. The result was predictable: zero amendments were ever passed, and the government could barely function. This is a textbook example of how consensus requirements scale inversely with group size.

This maps directly to organizational decision-making. Boards, partnerships, and co-founder agreements that require unanimity or near-unanimity for major decisions create veto power for every stakeholder. The stronger the consensus requirement, the more the status quo is preserved — even when the status quo is failing. The Constitutional Convention's solution was layered: simple majorities for routine legislation, 2/3 for overrides, 3/4 of states for amendments. Each threshold matches the gravity of the decision. Organizations should similarly calibrate their consensus requirements to decision stakes rather than applying one threshold universally.
