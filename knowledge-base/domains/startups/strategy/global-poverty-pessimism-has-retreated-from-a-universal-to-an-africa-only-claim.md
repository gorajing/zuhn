David Oks wrote a widely-discussed 2023 article arguing that economic development since 1990 had been broadly disappointing across the developing world. By 2026, he retreated to arguing that global poverty is set to rise — but only because poverty reduction outside Africa was so successful it essentially completed, leaving Africa as the sole remaining challenge. This implicit concession undermines his original thesis: the same data he now cites as evidence of future pessimism actually demonstrates that development was spectacularly real for billions of people in South Asia, East Asia, and Latin America.

This pattern of 'narrative retreat' — where a pessimistic thesis quietly narrows its scope when evidence accumulates against it — is a common failure mode in forecasting and punditry. The goalposts shift from 'development doesn't work anywhere' to 'development doesn't work in Africa' without acknowledging the original claim was wrong. For anyone evaluating macro forecasts, tracking how a forecaster's claims evolve over time is more informative than evaluating any single prediction in isolation.
