RuBisCo, the enzyme responsible for carbon fixation in photosynthesis, is remarkably inefficient — it confuses oxygen for CO2 roughly half the time, producing toxic byproducts. Yet plants never evolved a replacement. Instead, they compensate by producing staggering quantities (an estimated 40 billion tons on Earth at any time) and developing secondary pathways to handle the toxic waste.

This maps directly to startup strategy: when a core process is mediocre but entrenched, organizations often scale it rather than replace it. This works in stable environments but creates fragility. When conditions change (like atmospheric oxygen rising for plants), the accumulated investment in the suboptimal path makes switching nearly impossible. The lesson is that going all-in on a 'good enough' strategy creates path dependency that can outlast the conditions that made it viable.
