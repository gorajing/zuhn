Rwanda's development strategy is explicitly organized around climbing global indices: Ease of Doing Business (29th), corruption rankings (48th), safety statistics (2.5 murders per 100,000). This is a rational strategy given the signaling value of rankings to foreign investors, but it introduces a subtle failure mode. Accusations that GDP growth figures are inflated and that reforms are cosmetic rather than structural suggest the country may be optimizing the proxy rather than the underlying reality.

This maps directly to startup strategy: companies that optimize for metrics investors track (MRR growth, NPS scores, user counts) rather than the fundamentals those metrics proxy (genuine product-market fit, customer satisfaction, real engagement) can show impressive dashboards while building on unstable foundations. The tell is when a country's average income ($750/year) dramatically contradicts its business environment ranking (above Switzerland). The gap between the optimized signal and the unoptimized fundamentals is where the real risk lives — and where diligent investors and competitors should look.
