The common narrative attributes retail store closures to Amazon's dominance, but the evidence shows a more nuanced picture. Sears failed by trying to do everything poorly, J.C. Penney alienated its core customers, and Toys-R-Us dismissed the internet as a fad. Meanwhile, Ross, Lush, Aldi, Dollar General, and Costco all thrived during the same period by executing distinct strategies well.

This distinction matters for founders and strategists: disruption is often blamed on external forces when the real cause is internal failure to adapt. Companies that died weren't killed by Amazon—they were already weakened by strategic drift, customer neglect, or organizational inertia. The presence of thriving retailers proves the channel itself remains viable; what changed is the tolerance for mediocrity.
