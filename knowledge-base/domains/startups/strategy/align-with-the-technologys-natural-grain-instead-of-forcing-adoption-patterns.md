Graham's central thesis is that Web 2.0 wasn't a set of new trends but the web's inherent nature reasserting itself after the Bubble imposed broken models on it. Ajax was always possible in principle; democracy was inherent in the web's architecture; treating users well was the natural default before VC money created perverse incentives.

Google succeeded because they aligned with this grain—building on what hackers were already doing, treating users well not as a strategy but as a default, and positioning themselves where organic development was heading. The Bubble-era companies failed because they tried to impose television and print models onto a fundamentally different medium. The lesson generalizes beyond the web: in any technological shift, the winning strategy is to understand the medium's native properties and build with them, not against them.
