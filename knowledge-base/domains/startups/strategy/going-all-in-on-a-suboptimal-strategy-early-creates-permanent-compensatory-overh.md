RuBisCo evolved when atmospheric CO2 was abundant and oxygen scarce, making its poor selectivity irrelevant. As plants themselves changed the atmosphere by producing oxygen, RuBisCo began confusing O2 for CO2 roughly half the time, creating toxic byproducts. But by then, every plant on Earth had built its entire carbon fixation pathway around this one enzyme.

Rather than evolving a better enzyme (which would require restructuring the entire Calvin Cycle), plants went all-in on volume: producing enormous quantities of RuBisCo and evolving separate pathways (like C4 and CAM) to work around its limitations. This mirrors how startups that commit deeply to a mediocre technical architecture often find it cheaper to build compensatory layers (caching, queues, workarounds) than to rewrite from scratch. The lesson is that the window for choosing your core mechanism is narrow — once the ecosystem builds around it, you're locked in and paying the overhead forever.
