Costco turns three apparent obstacles into advantages. The $60 membership fee selects for affluent households (average income ~$100K) and triggers sunk-cost commitment. The confusing warehouse layout forces customers to wander through aisles, increasing exposure and impulse purchases. Bulk-only quantities attract business owners who represent one-third of members but two-thirds of sales.

This pattern — friction as filtration — appears across successful businesses. IKEA's assembly requirement creates pride of ownership. Luxury brands use high prices to signal exclusivity. The key insight is that not all friction is bad; strategically placed barriers can simultaneously reduce costs, select for ideal customers, and create psychological investment that competitors with frictionless experiences cannot replicate.
