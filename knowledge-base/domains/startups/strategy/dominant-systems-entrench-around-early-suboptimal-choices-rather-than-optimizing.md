RuBisCo evolved when atmospheric CO2 was abundant and oxygen was scarce, making its poor specificity irrelevant. When oxygen levels rose, RuBisCo began binding oxygen half the time—a catastrophic inefficiency. Yet plants doubled down: producing 40 billion tons of the enzyme and evolving photorespiration pathways to handle the toxic byproduct, rather than evolving a better enzyme.

This mirrors startup strategy directly. Early architectural decisions that achieve product-market fit become load-bearing walls. Companies like Twitter built on Ruby monoliths that couldn't scale, but the cost of rewriting exceeded the cost of workarounds for years. The lesson is that 'good enough first' beats 'optimal later' because switching costs compound with scale. Founders should expect to live with early technical debt rather than planning for a clean rewrite that rarely comes.
