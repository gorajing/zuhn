RuBisCo evolved when atmospheric CO2 was abundant and oxygen scarce, making its poor selectivity irrelevant. When oxygen levels rose, it began grabbing O2 instead of CO2 roughly half the time, creating toxic byproducts. Yet plants doubled down — producing massive quantities and evolving cleanup pathways rather than replacing the enzyme.

This mirrors startup technical debt perfectly: early architectural decisions made under different conditions become load-bearing walls. The cost of replacement grows faster than the cost of workarounds, so organizations keep patching. The lesson is that initial conditions matter enormously — what you build when constraints are loose becomes permanent when constraints tighten. Founders should treat early technical choices as near-irreversible, not as things to fix later.
