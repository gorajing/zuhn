RuBisCo evolved when atmospheric CO2 was abundant and oxygen scarce, making its lack of specificity irrelevant. When oxygen levels rose and RuBisCo started grabbing O2 instead of CO2 half the time, plants had already built their entire metabolic infrastructure around it. Rather than re-engineer, they simply produced massive quantities and evolved workarounds for the toxic byproduct.

This mirrors startup strategy: early architectural decisions that are 'good enough' become load-bearing. Companies that achieve dominance with a mediocre technical approach often find it cheaper to scale brute-force and bolt on fixes than to rebuild from scratch. The lesson isn't that quality doesn't matter — it's that path dependency is real, and the cost of switching strategies grows exponentially once an ecosystem depends on the current one.
