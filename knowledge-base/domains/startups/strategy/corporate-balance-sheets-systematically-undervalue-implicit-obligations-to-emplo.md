Graham identifies a structural accounting blind spot: when companies use high-paying senior positions as rewards for past work, they've created a real liability that never appears on their balance sheet. Corporate raiders in the 1980s could buy these companies and strip out the 'excess' VPs — but those executive positions weren't corruption or waste, they were payment for work already done.

This is a specific instance of a broader problem: institutions that rely on implicit promises instead of explicit contracts create hidden obligations that only become visible during stress or transition. The informal compensation system worked as long as the company persisted, but M&A activity broke the social contract without paying the debt.

For founders thinking about company design, this suggests that reward systems should be explicit and contractual where possible. Stock options, deferred compensation plans, and written promotion criteria are more durable than cultural norms about how the ladder works — because those norms evaporate when ownership changes.
