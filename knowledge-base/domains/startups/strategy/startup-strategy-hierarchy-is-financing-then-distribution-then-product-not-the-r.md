Most founders think product is the most fundamental strategic layer — get the product right and everything follows. Hoffman inverts this hierarchy: financing is the foundation because if you run out of money, nothing else matters. Distribution is the next layer because even great products die if they can't reach customers. Product sits on top, dependent on the other two. This means when raising money, you should already be thinking about the next fundraising round. When building product, you should be thinking about how distribution enables financing.

The practical implication is that founders should architect strategy from the bottom up: first ensure the business can survive financially, then solve the distribution problem, then optimize the product. This doesn't mean product doesn't matter — it means product decisions should be made within the constraints of what your distribution and financing strategies can support. PayPal's pivot from Palm Pilot payments to email payments exemplifies this: the product changed because the original distribution channel (requiring both parties to own Palm Pilots) was fundamentally broken.
