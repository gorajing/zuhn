Paine draws a sharp line between operational success (winning the engagement in front of you) and strategic success (achieving the reason you're fighting in the first place). Bushido-infused Japanese planning systematically collapsed the two: the samurai who fails his current plan is a failure, so every battle becomes existential, every hill must be taken, and there is no branches-and-sequels planning for 'this is too costly, let's try somewhere else.' The result was a series of impressive operational wins that never added up: Japan took Manchuria, coastal China, Southeast Asia, and huge Pacific territory — and still could not pacify China or secure its resources back home.

The deeper diagnosis is an absence of grand strategy: integrating all instruments of national power (military, diplomatic, economic, finance ministry) in pursuit of the real objective. Japan invaded French Indochina without either service checking 'how will the US react?' and triggered the 100% oil embargo — operational success, strategic catastrophe. 'Britain coined the term grand strategy. It is their gift to us.'

The pattern repeats in startups, product teams, and careers. 'We shipped the feature' is operational; 'did it move the metric we care about?' is strategic. Cultures that reward operational heroism while never auditing whether the wins compound tend to produce impressive activity and stagnant results.
