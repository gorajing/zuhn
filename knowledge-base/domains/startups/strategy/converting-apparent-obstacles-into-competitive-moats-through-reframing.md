Costco's strategy is a masterclass in obstacle-to-advantage conversion. Its membership fee should make customer acquisition fatally difficult — instead, it creates loyal, affluent patrons. Its warehouse layout should confuse and annoy shoppers — instead, it forces wandering through aisles (increasing impulse purchases) and makes customers feel like deal-hunters. Its limited selection should frustrate choice-seeking consumers — instead, it eliminates decision paralysis and concentrates buying power.

This pattern mirrors IKEA's do-it-yourself model, where requiring customers to assemble furniture transforms a cost-saving measure into a source of customer pride (the IKEA effect). The strategic principle is that friction points competitors refuse to touch become the hardest moats to copy — because copying them requires a competitor to deliberately make their own experience 'worse' by conventional metrics. Costco's entire model is nearly impossible for Walmart or Amazon to replicate without cannibalizing their existing open-access business models.
