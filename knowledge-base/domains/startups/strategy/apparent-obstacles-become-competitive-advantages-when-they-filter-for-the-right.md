Costco's membership fee, warehouse-style layout, and bulk quantities all look like obstacles on paper. In practice, each one filters for a specific customer: the $60 fee selects households averaging nearly $100K income, bulk sizes attract business owners (one-third of members generating two-thirds of sales), and the maze-like layout forces wandering through aisles, increasing impulse purchases.

This is the same principle behind IKEA's assembly-required model creating customer pride, or how nightclub cover charges create perceived exclusivity. The key insight is that removing friction isn't always optimal — strategic friction can select for higher-value customers, create psychological investment (sunk-cost loyalty), and turn constraints into brand identity. The Shanghai opening was so overwhelmed with demand they had to close for safety — proof that perceived exclusivity amplifies rather than suppresses demand.
