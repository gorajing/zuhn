RuBisCo evolved when CO2 was abundant and oxygen was scarce, making it adequate for carbon fixation. When plants' own light reactions flooded the atmosphere with oxygen, RuBisCo started confusing O2 for CO2 roughly half the time — creating toxic byproducts. But plants had already built their entire metabolic architecture around RuBisCo, so instead of evolving a better enzyme, they evolved workarounds: producing massive quantities (40 billion tons on Earth) and building detox pathways.

This mirrors startup path dependency perfectly. Early architectural decisions — a database choice, a pricing model, a distribution channel — get baked into everything downstream. When conditions change, the switching cost exceeds the improvement cost, so organizations layer workarounds on top of suboptimal foundations. The lesson isn't to avoid commitment — RuBisCo's strategy literally conquered the planet — but to recognize that early 'good enough' decisions often become permanent load-bearing constraints.
