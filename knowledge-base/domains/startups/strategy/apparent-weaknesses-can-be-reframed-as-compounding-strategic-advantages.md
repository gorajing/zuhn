Costco's strategy inverts conventional retail wisdom at nearly every point: charge customers to enter (creates loyalty), limit selection (creates leverage), use warehouse aesthetics (cuts costs), and make navigation confusing (increases browsing and basket size). Each 'weakness' reinforces the others—the warehouse format enables the low prices that justify the membership that funds the whole system.

This mirrors IKEA's model where do-it-yourself assembly creates customer pride rather than frustration. The key insight is that these advantages are difficult to copy precisely because they require embracing trade-offs that feel counterintuitive. A competitor would have to simultaneously adopt all of Costco's constraints—any partial copy (like adding a membership but keeping high margins) breaks the reinforcing logic. This is why Costco succeeds as a 'refreshingly boring' business in an era of growth-at-all-costs startups: its moat is systemic, not based on any single innovation.
