RuBisCo evolved when atmospheric oxygen was negligible, optimized solely for grabbing CO2. When photosynthesis itself flooded the atmosphere with oxygen, RuBisCo started confusing O2 for CO2 roughly half the time, producing a toxic byproduct. But plants had already built their entire carbon fixation strategy around it — 40 billion tons of it exist on Earth at any moment.

Rather than evolve a replacement enzyme, plants developed secondary pathways to detoxify the byproduct (photorespiration, C4 and CAM pathways). This is biological technical debt: the original architecture was so deeply embedded that patching was cheaper than replacing. The same dynamic plays out in software systems and startup strategy — early infrastructure choices that work fine at small scale become load-bearing walls that are cheaper to work around than to rebuild, even when the environment has fundamentally shifted.
