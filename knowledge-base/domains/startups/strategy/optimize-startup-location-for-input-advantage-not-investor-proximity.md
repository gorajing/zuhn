The traditional logic of locating near Sand Hill Road or major VC hubs is outdated because capital now flows globally via Zoom, async tools, and remote-first deal-making. However, physical resources remain geographically bound — university labs for deeptech, manufacturing clusters for hardware, regulatory ecosystems for fintech. Silicon Valley itself rose because of Stanford and federal research money, not VCs — capital followed invention.

The practical implication is a two-layer location strategy: optimize your physical presence for the inputs that accelerate your specific business (talent density, research infrastructure, supply chains), while treating investor access as a remote-first activity. This decoupling lets founders capture cost advantages of non-hub cities while maintaining the input advantages that actually drive product velocity.
