Vitalik distinguishes between SF remaining a productive city and SF remaining a necessary city. Pre-2015, the network effects of being in SF were so strong that choosing not to be there was a real competitive disadvantage — the investors, engineers, and customers were concentrated there. That lock-in is now broken. Remote work, crypto-native communities, and alternative hubs (Denver, Wyoming, Miami, Singapore) have all eroded the necessity premium.

The prediction is that SF will come to be seen as an 'establishment boomer town' — populated by the legacy companies (Apple, Facebook) that can't easily relocate, but not where the next generation of interesting companies is being built. This mirrors how Detroit or Pittsburgh maintained relevance in their industries after losing their status as the necessary hub.

For founders, the implication is that location choice is now genuinely strategic rather than defaulted. The relevant question is not 'how do I get to SF?' but 'which node in the distributed innovation graph has the highest density of the specific people and regulatory environment relevant to what I'm building?' Crypto-friendly regulation (Wyoming), Latin America proximity (Miami), and policy-receptive governance (Colorado) are each advantages that SF can't replicate.
