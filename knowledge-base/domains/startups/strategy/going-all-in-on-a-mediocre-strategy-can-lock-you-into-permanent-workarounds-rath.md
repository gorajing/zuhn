Plants evolved RuBisCo in a high-CO2, low-O2 atmosphere where its limitations didn't matter. When the atmosphere changed (ironically due to plants' own oxygen production), RuBisCo started confusing O2 for CO2 roughly half the time, creating toxic byproducts. Rather than evolving a better enzyme, plants doubled down — producing massive quantities and developing secondary pathways to detoxify errors.

This maps directly to startup strategy: early technical or strategic choices made under one set of conditions can become deeply embedded. When the market shifts, the cost of replacing the core system exceeds the cost of building workarounds, so organizations keep patching. The lesson isn't 'never commit' — RuBisCo enabled plants to dominate Earth. The lesson is that the cost of workarounds compounds silently, and the window for pivoting closes faster than it appears.

The 40 billion tons of RuBisCo on Earth at any moment is the biological equivalent of technical debt — massive resource expenditure to compensate for a known architectural flaw that's too deeply embedded to fix.
