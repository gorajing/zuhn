The age of sail created modern finance (stock exchanges, insurance, bond markets) as second-order effects of needing to fund risky ocean voyages. Railroads imposed standardized time zones and created national consumer brands like Sears. The automobile spawned suburbanization, consumer credit, and the entire modern consumer economy — none of which Henry Ford anticipated when designing the Model T.

This pattern has a direct strategic implication: the people who built infrastructure (ports, rail depots, highways, container cranes) generally captured enormous value, while those who waited for second-order effects to become obvious arrived too late. For founders and investors, this means betting on enabling infrastructure layers during cost collapses rather than waiting to see which specific applications emerge.
