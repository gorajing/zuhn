When people ask 'how can you buy the Amazon?', Rosolie's answer reframes the problem: someone already owns it. Third-generation jungle families own 10,000-acre parcels they can't use. They will inevitably sell to logging companies, narcos, or gold miners for quick cash. JungleKeepers' approach is to get there first: buy the land concessions, convert loggers and miners into rangers with better jobs, and protect the ecosystem. They've secured 130,000 acres and need 200,000 more -- most of which is uncontacted tribe territory. The strategic insight applies broadly: when an asset is going to be transacted regardless, the question isn't whether to participate in the market but whether you get there before the destructive buyer does. This is the same logic as defensive patent acquisition or preemptive talent hiring. The conservation framing obscures what is fundamentally a competitive market-timing play.
