The standard 'pick a vertical' advice gets sharper in the foundation-model era: a vertical large enough to attract OpenAI/Anthropic/Google direct entry is a trap. Anthropic's recent financial-industry play shows the pattern — when a major lab decides a vertical is big enough, they enter directly with their own product, and the existing vertical-AI startups serving that space get squeezed.

The defensive position: pick verticals where the value-add is legacy-system integration depth (custom APIs to mainframe systems, regulatory-specific workflows, domain-specific data schemas that nobody outside the industry would know). Big labs optimize for breadth; they cannot afford to do per-vertical integration work for every industry. Founders who do that work first compound the moat.

The size diagnostic: if your TAM is small enough that big labs ignore it but large enough that 3-5 enterprise customers produce a real business, you're in the safe zone. If your TAM is large enough to attract big labs, your moat must come from something they cannot replicate — proprietary data partnerships, regulatory certification, or operator-level domain knowledge.

This converges with the 'enterprise build-vs-buy tilts to build when integration is the dominant cost' insight from the PayPal source (INS-260530-XXXX) — both point at integration depth as the durable moat in enterprise AI.
