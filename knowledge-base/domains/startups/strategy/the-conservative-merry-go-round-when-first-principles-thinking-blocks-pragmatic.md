The Shapiro-Destiny education debate reveals a recurring pattern applicable far beyond politics. Destiny proposes minimum school funding for air conditioning and lunch programs. Shapiro redirects to the root cause: two-parent households are the primary predictor of educational outcomes. Destiny counters: you can't legislate marriage, so shouldn't we at least do the achievable thing? Shapiro responds: doing the achievable thing without addressing the root cause is 'hitting a gnat with a hammer.'

Destiny calls this the 'conservative merry-go-round' — every concrete proposal is dismissed in favor of a deeper structural problem that no policy can directly fix. This pattern appears everywhere in strategy: the perfect-as-enemy-of-good loop where identifying root causes becomes an excuse for inaction on tractable problems. The lesson for any decision-maker: root cause analysis is valuable, but if the root cause is beyond your control, executing on second-order improvements is not 'deck chairs on the Titanic' — it's the only rational action available.
