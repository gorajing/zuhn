The article's meta-framework is broadly applicable beyond policy: when you can't predict which jobs will exist, what the macroeconomy will look like, or how income distribution will shift, the correct response is not to pick the most likely scenario and optimize for it. Instead, you design interventions that deliver value regardless of which scenario materializes.

This is the strategic equivalent of robust optimization in engineering — minimizing worst-case loss rather than maximizing expected value. The 2010s progressive program failed partly because it was optimized for a specific macroeconomic state (demand shortfall) that had already changed by the time the policies were implemented. The lesson for any strategist operating in AI-disrupted environments: build for the envelope of plausible futures, not the point estimate. Abundance, broad equity ownership, and human-AI complementarity research all share this property of being valuable across wildly different AI timelines.
