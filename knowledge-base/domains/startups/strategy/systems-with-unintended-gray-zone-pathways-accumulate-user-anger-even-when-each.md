Smith describes how U.S. asylum law — which grants hearings even to those who entered illegally — creates a gray zone where people are 'illegal when they cross, legal while awaiting hearing, then illegal again if denied.' Most Americans don't understand the mechanism but sense that their democratic will is being violated. This diffuse, poorly-understood anger builds until it produces an overcorrection (Trump's ICE).

This pattern recurs in product and platform design. When a system has loopholes that technically comply with rules but clearly violate their spirit — think of Amazon sellers gaming review systems, or social media accounts that technically don't violate terms of service — users develop a growing sense that the system is 'rigged' even if they can't articulate exactly how. This accumulated frustration doesn't resolve gradually; it builds until a disruptive correction occurs (platform crackdown, regulatory intervention, user exodus). The lesson for system designers is that gray-zone pathways must be closed proactively, because the eventual correction is always more severe than early reform would have been.
