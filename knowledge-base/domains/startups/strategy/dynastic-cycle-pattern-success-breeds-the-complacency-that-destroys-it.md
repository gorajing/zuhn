Chinese dynastic history follows a remarkably consistent pattern: a vigorous founder establishes order, successors enjoy prosperity, later generations become complacent and corrupt, and eventually the system collapses and is replaced. This cycle repeated across the Shang, Zhou, Qin, Han, and subsequent dynasties over two millennia.

This maps directly to the corporate lifecycle and Christensen's innovator's dilemma. Early-stage companies have founding energy and existential urgency. Success brings resources but also bureaucracy, risk aversion, and entitlement. The very stability that made the organization successful insulates leadership from feedback signals. The strategic implication is that organizations must build deliberate mechanisms for self-disruption and renewal — treating complacency as the default state that must be actively countered, not as an aberration.
