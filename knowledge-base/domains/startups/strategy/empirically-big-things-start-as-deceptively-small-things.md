Graham argues that the popular image of a visionary — someone with a clear view of the future who builds toward it — is empirically wrong. Microsoft started with a BASIC interpreter for a tiny machine. Facebook started as a Harvard-only social directory. Neither founder knew how big the company would become; they just knew they were onto something.

The tactical advice is to 'head in a general westerly direction' like Columbus rather than constructing a precise blueprint. This works for two reasons: first, your initial blueprint is almost certainly wrong, so detailed planning is wasted effort. Second, starting small reduces the psychological burden — you avoid the repulsion effect of announcing massive ambitions, and you avoid creating an army of skeptics measuring you against premature expectations. The framing matters: say you're building todo-list software, not that you're replacing email.
