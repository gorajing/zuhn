RuBisCo, the enzyme that fixes carbon in photosynthesis, was evolved in a low-oxygen atmosphere and now fails up to 50% of the time by grabbing oxygen instead of CO2. Plants compensate by producing ~40 billion tons of it and evolving separate pathways to neutralize its toxic byproducts. They cannot replace it because every downstream process depends on its specific outputs.

This maps directly to technology platforms and business strategy. Once a core component becomes load-bearing across an entire ecosystem — a database schema, a programming language, a market standard — the cost of replacement exceeds the cost of compensation. The strategic implication: when choosing foundational components early, optimize for adaptability over raw performance, because you will be stuck with your choice long after the environment changes.

The RuBisCo pattern also explains why 'good enough first' often beats 'perfect later.' RuBisCo won not by being good, but by being first and sufficient. Organisms that waited for a better enzyme never evolved at all.
