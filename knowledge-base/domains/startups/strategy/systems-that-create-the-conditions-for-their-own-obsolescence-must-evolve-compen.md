Photosynthesis was so successful at producing oxygen that it fundamentally changed Earth's atmosphere. But this very success poisoned RuBisCo — the enzyme central to carbon fixation — which evolved in a low-oxygen world and now binds oxygen instead of CO2 roughly half the time, producing toxic phosphoglycolate. Plants survived only by evolving additional enzyme pathways to break down the toxic byproduct into usable compounds like glycine.

This self-undermining pattern is common in business. Successful platforms attract so many participants that they become noisy and less valuable (social media feed degradation). Successful hiring strategies attract so many applicants that signal-to-noise collapses. Successful pricing strategies attract competitors that commoditize the market. The companies that survive are not those that avoid this trap — it's unavoidable if you're successful — but those that develop compensatory mechanisms fastest. Instagram added algorithmic feeds to compensate for content overload. Google added quality scores to compensate for ad spam. The question isn't 'will your success undermine itself?' but 'can you build the detoxification pathway before the poison kills you?'
