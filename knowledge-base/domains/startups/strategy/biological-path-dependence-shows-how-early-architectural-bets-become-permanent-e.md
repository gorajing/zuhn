RuBisCo evolved when Earth's atmosphere had almost no oxygen, so its inability to distinguish CO2 from O2 didn't matter. When plants themselves filled the atmosphere with oxygen through the light-dependent reactions, RuBisCo started grabbing oxygen about half the time — creating a toxic byproduct (phosphoglycolate) that plants must spend additional energy to neutralize.

Rather than evolving a better enzyme, plants simply produce enormous quantities of RuBisCo (an estimated 40 billion tons exist at any moment, making it the most abundant protein on Earth) and evolved secondary pathways to deal with the toxic waste. This mirrors how startups and software systems double down on compensating workarounds rather than replacing core architecture once they've scaled — the switching cost exceeds the ongoing waste cost. The lesson: your earliest architectural decisions are the hardest to reverse, because everything else gets built assuming they're permanent.
