Conventional retail wisdom says more selection means more customers served. Costco inverts this: by offering only one or two options per category, it becomes the gatekeeper for enormous purchase volume. Suppliers compete aggressively to be the chosen product — lowering prices, redesigning packaging (Costco once had a cashew container redesigned to fit 24,000 more pallets per year), and adapting to Costhn requirements. The scarcity of shelf slots becomes leverage.

This compounds with operations: fewer SKUs mean simpler logistics, no backroom inventory, direct pallet-to-shelf stocking, and reduced cognitive overhead for shoppers. The 'paradox of choice' research suggests fewer options can actually increase satisfaction — shoppers feel like they're getting a curated recommendation rather than drowning in alternatives. For product builders, this is a reminder that constraint can be a moat. Saying no to most options concentrates the power of the ones you keep.
