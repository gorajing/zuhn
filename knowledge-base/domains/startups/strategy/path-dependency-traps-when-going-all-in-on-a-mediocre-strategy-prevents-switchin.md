Plants evolved RuBisCo when atmospheric oxygen was negligible, making it adequate for carbon fixation. When oxygen levels rose—ironically due to plants' own success—RuBisCo began binding oxygen ~50% of the time, producing toxic byproducts. Rather than evolving a replacement, plants doubled down: producing 40 billion tons of RuBisCo globally and building detoxification pathways.

This mirrors startup technical debt and platform lock-in. Early architectural choices that work fine at small scale become liabilities at growth, but by then the entire system depends on them. The cost of workarounds compounds, yet the switching cost feels higher than continuing to patch. The lesson: recognize when you're building detoxification pathways around a core design flaw, because that's the signal you've entered a RuBisCo trap.
