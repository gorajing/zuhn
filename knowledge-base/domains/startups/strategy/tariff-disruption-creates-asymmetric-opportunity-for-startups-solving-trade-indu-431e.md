Tariff disruption is converting previously optional software categories into must-haves. Warehouse management systems, AI-powered supply chain optimization platforms, shipment tracking tools, supplier diversification analytics, and trade compliance automation are all seeing demand spikes. Enterprise buyers who never budgeted for these tools are now allocating spend because the cost of not having visibility into tariff exposure exceeds the software cost.

This represents a classic 'picks and shovels' opportunity: while hardware startups absorb tariff costs directly, the startups that help others navigate supply chain chaos capture value from the disruption itself. The pattern extends to sectors gaining strategic relevance—defense tech ($3B deployed in 2024, a record), semiconductors, clean energy, and industrial automation—where government partnerships and public funding create additional tailwinds. Founders in adjacent spaces should actively explore enterprise pilots and government-supported programs that weren't available before the policy shift.
