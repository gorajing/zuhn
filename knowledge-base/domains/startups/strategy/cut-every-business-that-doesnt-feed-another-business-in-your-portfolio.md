Dimon contrasts JP Morgan with the old Citigroup: Citi had consumer finance, life insurance, property & casualty, and truck leasing — 'none of it fit, and it's hard for anyone to understand the risk in each one.' JP Morgan's consumer, middle-market, investment banking, credit card, and wealth management units all cross-refer clients: middle-market clients use investment banking; consumer clients need FX; branches become distribution for wealth management. Each division lowers the customer acquisition cost of the next.

The test Dimon applies is not 'is this profitable?' but 'does this fit?' He calls unrelated units 'hobbies' and says he doesn't like hobbies. The fortress balance sheet is only defensible when paired with a coherent strategy that doesn't flip and flop — otherwise diversification becomes a grab bag of uncorrelated bets, each with hidden tail risk management doesn't fully understand.

For founders and operators: before adding a new product line or acquisition, ask which existing line it will feed and which existing line will feed it. If the answer is 'none, but it's attractive on its own,' the right move is usually to pass or to spin it out — not to bolt it on.
