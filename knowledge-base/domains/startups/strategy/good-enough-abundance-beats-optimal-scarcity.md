RuBisCo, the enzyme responsible for carbon fixation in photosynthesis, is notoriously inefficient — it confuses oxygen for CO2 roughly half the time. Yet it became the most abundant protein on Earth (~40 billion tons) because the first organisms using it simply made enormous quantities to overcome its deficiencies. Once the strategy achieved planetary scale, no alternative could displace it.

This pattern appears in markets too: dominant-but-imperfect incumbents often win not through excellence but through volume, distribution, or switching costs that make replacement implausible. The key insight is that 'good enough + ubiquitous' can defeat 'optimal + scarce' across evolutionary, technological, and business contexts.

The strategic implication: early movers who flood the market before quality alternatives emerge can cement positions even when technically inferior. Challengers need not just a better product but a viable path past the switching-cost barrier.
