RuBisCo evolved when CO2 was abundant and oxygen was scarce, making it adequate for carbon fixation. As plants themselves increased atmospheric oxygen through photosynthesis, RuBisCo began confusing O2 for CO2 roughly half the time, producing toxic byproducts that require additional enzymatic cleanup. Despite this catastrophic failure rate, plants never evolved a replacement — they simply produce enormous quantities (estimated 40 billion tons globally) to brute-force the problem.

This is the biological equivalent of technical debt at planetary scale. The lesson for strategy is that switching costs compound over time. Once an ecosystem builds around a suboptimal solution, the cost of replacement exceeds the cost of compensation. Startups face this constantly: a mediocre architecture chosen at founding becomes load-bearing infrastructure that's cheaper to scale horizontally than to redesign. The key insight is that 'going all-in' on a good-enough early strategy can be rational even when better alternatives exist, because the switching cost grows faster than the performance gap.
