In the deep sea, organisms like the vampire squid abandoned active hunting entirely in favor of passive food collection — extending sticky arms to brush particles from the water rather than chasing prey. This isn't an optimization of surface-level hunting; it's a fundamentally different approach born from extreme scarcity.

This mirrors how startups in resource-constrained environments shouldn't just do what well-funded competitors do more cheaply. The winning move is often to invent an entirely different operating model — one that would look bizarre in abundance but becomes the only viable strategy under constraint. The deep sea teaches that true scarcity doesn't reward incrementalism; it rewards category-breaking reinvention of how value is captured.
