Graham frames technology as a lever whose length grows at an accelerating rate. In a world where one person can build software used by billions while another performs manual labor, the variation in value created per person is enormous. The mid-20th century masked this by forcing everyone into large organizations that couldn't measure or reward individual contributions — salaries were flat because there was no market mechanism to differentiate.

Once career mobility returned and companies could estimate individual contributions to revenue, pay diverged toward market price. Since human productivity follows a power law distribution, market-rate pay necessarily produces inequality. The Baumol Effect compounds this: Google pays millions to retain people who might leave for startups, dragging up wages for everyone in their reference class. This means any society that allows wealth creation through technology will see increasing inequality, and the only way to prevent it is to discourage the wealth creation itself — a cure worse than the disease.
