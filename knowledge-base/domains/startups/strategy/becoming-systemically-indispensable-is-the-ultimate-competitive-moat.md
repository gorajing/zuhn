Morgan twice rescued the U.S. economy from collapse: in 1895 when gold reserves dropped to $9M (he organized a private syndicate to purchase $65M in gold bonds), and in 1907 when he corralled bankers into his private library and literally locked them in until they agreed to inject capital into failing banks. In both cases, the U.S. government, lacking a central bank, had no alternative but to rely on one private citizen.

This illustrates a powerful strategic principle: becoming the indispensable node in a critical system gives you leverage that no amount of competition can erode. Morgan's position transcended normal market dynamics because his failure would mean systemic failure. The modern parallel is 'too big to fail' institutions, but the principle applies at smaller scales too. Any business that embeds itself so deeply in its customers' operations that switching costs become existential has achieved a version of Morgan's moat.
