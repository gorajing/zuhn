Qualcomm's licensing model charged Apple roughly 5% of each iPhone's average selling price plus additional per-unit fees — estimated at $7.50-$17 per phone just for patent licenses, separate from the chip itself. Across 250M iPhones/year this approached $7.5B annually, about 20% of Qualcomm's entire chip revenue. Apple tolerated this only as long as there was no credible alternative. When Qualcomm pushed pricing further, Apple took two extreme measures: first bringing in Intel as a secondary supplier (which failed on technical grounds), then simply buying Intel's entire modem division and beginning the brutal multi-year project of building their own baseband modem.

Baseband modems are notoriously the single hardest chip in consumer electronics — Apple, one of the most technically capable companies in the world, has needed years and is still not shipping one. The fact that they started at all is the lesson. Any time your pricing makes your customer's cheapest option 'start a decade-long internal project to replace you,' you have crossed the threshold where the value you capture is smaller than the option value of your replacement. Qualcomm's CEO now openly forecasts Apple revenue going to ~zero in the chip business.

The operating rule: the right long-run price for a monopoly input is the price that maximizes what you extract without funding your own replacement. The extractive ceiling is not 'what the customer will pay' — it's 'what is cheaper than funding a five-year replacement program.' Most dominant vendors overshoot this line because the signal (customers leaving) lags the cause (over-extraction) by years.
