In a magnet below the Curie temperature, each atom influences only its immediate neighbors — effects die out within a few atoms. But at the critical temperature, local influences chain together: one spin nudges its neighbor, which nudges the next, creating correlations that span the entire material. The system becomes scale-free (domains of all sizes, from atoms to millions), fractal, and its size distribution follows a power law.

This critical state is 'maximally interesting' because it's the point of maximum unpredictability — the system is neither frozen in order nor dissolved in chaos. It has both 'elements of stability and persistence over time' and 'changes over time.' This pattern appears in markets (neither perfectly efficient nor completely random), in technology ecosystems (neither monopoly nor perfect competition), and in creative work (the sweet spot between formula and chaos). The practical insight: the most valuable and generative systems live at the edge of a phase transition. If your market, product, or organization feels maximally uncertain, that may be a feature — you're at the critical point where small actions can have outsized effects.
