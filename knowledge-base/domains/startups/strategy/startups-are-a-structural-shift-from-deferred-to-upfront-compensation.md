Graham's key insight is that the corporate ladder wasn't just a career path — it was a deferred compensation system. Employees did work early in their careers with the implicit promise of high-paying executive jobs later. Startups collapse that timeline: founders take the risk now and capture the reward now, rather than deferring both to an employer.

This reframe matters because it strips away the mythology around startup risk. The corporate path wasn't actually safe — projects got cancelled, industries disappeared (as with Graham's father's breeder reactor work), and the 1980s takeover wave wiped out the implicit debt companies owed to long-tenured employees. Both paths involve risk; they differ in who holds it and when it materializes.

For founders evaluating whether to start a company or take a senior corporate role, this framing clarifies what they're really trading. The question isn't 'safe job vs. risky startup' but rather 'deferred upside with institutional counterparty risk vs. immediate upside with market risk.'
