Most businesses that refuse to scale are hiding a failure of ambition. Benchmark is different: the product they sell to founders is 'every partner in the firm will have full context on your company and will take calls from you at any time.' That product has a hard ceiling around 5-6 partners. Adding a seventh partner doesn't grow the firm — it destroys the product. This is counter-intuitive because in most industries, adding capacity adds revenue. In high-touch expertise businesses, adding capacity can subtract value.

The lesson generalizes beyond VC. Acquired itself (noted in the episode) chose not to expand into a multi-show network because Ben and David's attention is the product. High-end restaurants, boutique law practices, small creative studios — all face the same trade-off. The question to ask is: 'does adding a unit of capacity multiply or dilute the core product?' If it dilutes, growth is value-destruction, and the discipline to refuse is a competitive moat because competitors assume you must be growing and will leave your niche uncontested.

Application: before celebrating a growth decision, ask what the added capacity does to the quality of the core output. If quality is tied to the founder's or the small team's attention, growing is the opposite of scaling. The courage to stay small is harder than the courage to expand — investors, peers, and the market all push the other way.
