Graham's 'conservation law' is a powerful corrective to romanticized startup narratives. If making a million dollars through a postal career means 50 years of steady stress, a startup compresses that same million-dollars-worth of pain into 3-4 years. You get a bulk discount but cannot evade the fundamental law. If starting a startup were easy, everyone would do it.

The payoff distribution makes this even more sobering: being 30x more productive doesn't mean earning 30x more. It means earning between 0 and 1000x, with the median at zero. Graham notes that even Viaweb's hackers were extremely risk-averse and would have preferred a guaranteed $1M over a 20% shot at $10M. The all-or-nothing nature is not a feature founders choose but a structural property of the startup game they cannot escape. Famous billionaires like Gates are outliers who combined ability with extraordinary luck (the IBM DOS licensing blunder), making them poor role models for expected outcomes.
