RuBisCo evolved when Earth's atmosphere had almost no oxygen. When photosynthesis itself flooded the atmosphere with oxygen, RuBisCo started accidentally slicing the wrong molecule roughly half the time — producing a toxic byproduct. Rather than evolving a better enzyme, plants compensated by producing enormous quantities of RuBisCo (an estimated 40 billion tons exist on Earth at any given time) and building elaborate detoxification pathways for the toxic byproduct.

This is evolutionary lock-in in its purest form: a solution entrenched itself so deeply that replacement became more costly than accommodation. The parallel to technology and startups is direct. QWERTY keyboards, TCP/IP, JavaScript, and dominant platform APIs all persist well past the point where cleaner alternatives exist — not because they're optimal, but because the ecosystem of workarounds, tooling, and expertise built around them makes switching prohibitively expensive. First-mover advantage creates moats that scale independent of underlying quality.

For founders and strategists, the implication is double-edged: building on top of entrenched but imperfect platforms is often rational even knowing their flaws, and achieving early category lock-in matters far more than achieving technical optimality.
