Zuckerberg's operating model, articulated by an observer and confirmed by him on stage: 'If we can learn faster than every other company we're going to win.' Two distinct values are stacked here — engineering throughput (more shipped iterations) and learning rate per iteration (better feedback loops, sharper hypotheses). Meta deliberately ships things 'almost at the line of being embarrassed' because polishing past the point of useful feedback wastes turns.

The corollary is that being early and rough beats being late and polished, *as long as the hypothesis you're testing is reasonable enough that users will engage*. If the product is so bad nobody touches it, you collected zero signal — that's the only failure mode of this strategy.

For any team setting up a development culture, the implication is to invest in the meta-systems that enable both halves: shipping infrastructure (CI, feature flags, observability — the kind of internal tooling Stat Sig was built to recreate) AND a feedback diet that converts deployments into knowledge. Without both, 'move fast' degrades into either thrash or theater.
