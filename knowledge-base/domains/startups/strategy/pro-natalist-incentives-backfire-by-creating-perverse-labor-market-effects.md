Sweden offers one of the world's most generous parental leave policies — 480 days of paid leave per child. While this has supported fertility rates, it created a significant second-order effect: employers became more hesitant to hire young women, who are statistically more likely to take extended leave. The intended beneficiaries of the policy became its victims in the labor market.

This pattern — where incentive-based solutions to structural problems generate side effects of comparable magnitude — recurs across domains. In startups, overly generous equity vesting can create retention but kill accountability. In product design, removing friction at one point often creates friction elsewhere. The lesson is that interventions into complex systems require modeling second-order effects, not just the target outcome. The cost of raising children has also risen steadily regardless of subsidies, suggesting the structural economics overwhelm incentive nudges.
