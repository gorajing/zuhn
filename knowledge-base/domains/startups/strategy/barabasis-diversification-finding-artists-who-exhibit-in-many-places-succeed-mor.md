Physicist-turned-network-scientist Albert-László Barabási studies the science of success. One of his findings (cited by Shin Soo-jung): when comparing two groups of artists — (1) those who exhibited only at a specific elite gallery or near-home academy, vs (2) those who spread their work across many places (home, Italy, Switzerland, varying styles) — the latter group had an overwhelmingly higher probability of becoming celebrated. Even when the elite-only artists were technically superior, they were less likely to be discovered. The principle Barabási distilled: 'Success depends on quantity — on how many attempts you make.' The mechanism: success in any high-variance field requires being discovered by the right person at the right moment, which is structurally random. Maximizing exposure to diverse contexts increases the probability that at least one match occurs. The implication for builders: don't optimize for the prestigious-venue play (one elite paper, one elite conference, one prestigious gallery, one Sand Hill VC firm); optimize for the diversification play (many venues, many publications, many channels, many investor types). The elite-venue strategy works in equilibrium when everyone agrees what 'elite' means; in fragmented attention markets, diversification dominates. The startup analog: don't only fundraise from top-tier VCs; talk to angels, family offices, strategic operators, and corporate venture arms too. The career analog: don't only network at conferences in your specialty; cross over to adjacent fields where your skills are rarer.
