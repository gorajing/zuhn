McDonald's illustrates the deadly timing trap: by waiting until the core business needed saving, the company faced a two-front war. The existing business model — which would normally finance experimentation — itself needed rescue, creating a resource conflict between survive-now and invent-the-future.

This is a generalizable principle for any company: experimentation is cheapest and most likely to succeed when funded by a healthy core business with margin to spare. Once decline sets in, every dollar spent on future experiments competes directly with dollars needed to keep the lights on. The psychological pressure also shifts — leadership becomes risk-averse precisely when bold bets are most needed. Companies should treat business model experimentation as a continuous discipline, not a crisis response.
