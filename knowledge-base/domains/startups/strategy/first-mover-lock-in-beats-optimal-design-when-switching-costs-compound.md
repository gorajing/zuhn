RuBisCo — the enzyme responsible for carbon fixation in every plant on Earth — fails at its job roughly half the time, grabbing oxygen instead of CO2 and producing a toxic byproduct. Yet 450 million years later, every photosynthetic organism still uses it because the entire metabolic architecture was built around it.

This is a powerful model for startup strategy: the first solution to capture an ecosystem doesn't need to be the best — it needs to be good enough while everything else gets built around it. Once downstream dependencies accumulate (other enzymes to handle RuBisCo's errors, metabolic pathways tuned to its outputs), replacement becomes structurally impossible. The same dynamic plays out in platforms, APIs, and technical standards. The lesson isn't to ship garbage — it's that speed-to-ecosystem-capture matters more than theoretical optimality, and that 'we'll fix it later' often becomes 'we'll build workarounds forever.'
