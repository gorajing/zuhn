After the 2011 earthquake exposed semiconductor supply chain fragility, Toyota distinguished between commoditized inputs (easily substitutable, like plastic resin) and critical-path inputs (semiconductors, with years-long facility construction times). For the latter, holding months of inventory isn't waste — it's insurance against inevitable disruption.

This principle applies beyond manufacturing. Any operation optimizing for efficiency must classify its dependencies by substitutability and recovery time. Buffers on easily-replaced inputs are genuinely wasteful; buffers on hard-to-replace, long-lead-time dependencies are structurally necessary. The failure mode is treating all buffers as equal and cutting them uniformly — which is exactly what most JIT adopters did, optimizing for quarterly metrics while building catastrophic fragility into their most vulnerable supply chains.
