In 2005, Putin publicly vowed on television to never raise the retirement age. For 13 years, he papered over the growing demographic imbalance with subsidies, bloated state employment, and pension increases (pensions more than doubled between 2012-2022). When the math finally forced his hand in 2018, the change had to be far more aggressive than gradual reform would have required — jumping retirement age by 5-8 years in a compressed timeline, versus the US approach of raising it 2 years over two decades.

This is the band-aid trap: every year of delay narrows the solution space and increases the required magnitude of change. The pattern maps directly to technical debt in startups, deferred pricing restructuring, or any situation where leaders avoid short-term pain. The compounding nature means the 'right time' for structural reform was always earlier than it felt comfortable. Putin's case is an extreme example, but the dynamic is universal: deferred structural problems don't stay the same size — they grow.
