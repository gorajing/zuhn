After WWII, America subsidized suburban mortgages, funded interstate highways, and rewarded mass-production homes while car makers, oil companies, and tire manufacturers lobbied for highways and actively dismantled streetcars. Europe nationalized healthcare, education, and transit; America subsidized suburban sprawl. The American Dream was elevated from cultural identity to ideological construct — home and car ownership as global proof that only American capitalism could deliver mass prosperity. This locked in car-centric infrastructure for generations, making dense transit-dependent development structurally impossible in most American cities.
