Costco's $60 membership fee should make customer acquisition fatally difficult, yet it selects for affluent households (average income ~$100k) who become loyal, high-spending patrons. Its warehouse layout should annoy shoppers, but instead makes them feel like deal-hunters—similar to how IKEA's DIY assembly creates pride of ownership. The bulk-only format should limit the customer base, but it attracts business owners (one-third of members) who generate two-thirds of sales.

This pattern—deliberately introducing friction that competitors avoid—creates positioning that's extremely difficult to copy. A competitor would have to accept short-term customer loss to implement mandatory fees, warehouse aesthetics, or limited selection. The strategic lesson is that obstacles don't need to be eliminated; they need to be converted. The key is ensuring each friction point filters for your ideal customer while simultaneously creating an experience that breeds loyalty.
