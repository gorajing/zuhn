The Great Schism of 1054 between the Orthodox and Catholic churches wasn't primarily about resources, territory, or power — it was about identity: the nature of the Trinity, the authority of the Pope, liturgical language. These identity-level disagreements proved far more durable than any territorial dispute. Nearly 1000 years later, the split persists.

This pattern repeats in organizations: co-founder splits over vision and values (not equity or compensation) are the hardest to repair. Company culture wars between engineering-first vs. sales-first identity are more destructive than budget fights. Open-source community forks driven by philosophical differences (GPL vs. MIT, cathedral vs. bazaar) outlast any technical disagreement. The lesson is that when you detect identity-level misalignment — disagreements about what the organization fundamentally IS — treat it as a potential permanent fracture, not a negotiable difference.
