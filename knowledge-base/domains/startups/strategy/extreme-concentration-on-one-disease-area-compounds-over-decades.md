Novo Nordisk is the 20th largest pharma company by revenue but the second largest by market cap. The explanation is concentration: 85% of revenue in metabolic disorders, 100 years of focused R&D on diabetes, and the deep process knowledge that comes from iterating on the same biological problem across generations of scientists. This enabled GLP-1 — a drug class built on decades of tuning how molecules absorb, persist, and clear in the human body, which is a harder-to-copy advantage than the molecule itself.

For 60 of those 100 years (1920-1980), type 1 diabetes was not even an attractive market. The company stayed anyway, and when type 2 diabetes and obesity exploded, they were the only player with the scientific depth to capitalize. The lesson echoes Sequoia's Don Valentine ethos — target a big market, then stay focused on it for decades. Most companies drift to diversification when their core market feels saturated; the counterintuitive move is to assume the market will grow under you if you pick right, and to resist diversification pressure even when industry wisdom demands consolidation.
