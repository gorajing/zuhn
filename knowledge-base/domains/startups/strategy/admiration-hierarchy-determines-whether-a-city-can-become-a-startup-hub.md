Graham makes a structural argument about why New York is unlikely to rival Silicon Valley as a startup hub: admiration is a zero-sum game within a city, and each city tends to focus on one dominant type. In New York, the dominant currency is wealth — a billion dollars impresses even if inherited. In Silicon Valley, the currency is power over the world through technology — nobody cares about inherited wealth.

This creates a second-class citizen problem: a startup founder in New York occupies a lower rung than a hedge fund manager, which produces a constant ambient discouragement. The same person in Silicon Valley would sit near the top. This isn't about access to capital or talent — it's about the psychological tax of operating in a status hierarchy that doesn't recognize your kind of ambition as the most important kind. Graham notes the empirical evidence: the ratio of New Yorkers to Californians in the Forbes 400 flipped from 1.45 to 0.83 between 1982 and 2007.
