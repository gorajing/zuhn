Graham observes that imagination is indivisible: it's hard to have odd ideas about technology without also having odd ideas about politics, and many technical ideas have inherent political implications. This means authoritarian regimes face a fundamental contradiction — they want the economic output of innovation but cannot tolerate the intellectual freedom that produces it. The 'back pressure' from political suppression propagates into technical fields.

This explains why authoritarian states can build factories for things designed elsewhere but struggle to produce the designers themselves. Singapore's energetic government intervention can make a port efficient but cannot coax startups into existence. The pattern suggests that civil liberties aren't merely a nice-to-have for innovation ecosystems — they're a load-bearing structural requirement. A state that bans chewing gum has a long way to go before it could create a San Francisco.
