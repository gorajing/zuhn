RuBisCo, the enzyme responsible for carbon fixation in photosynthesis, is remarkably inefficient — it confuses oxygen for CO2 roughly half the time, producing a toxic byproduct plants must actively manage. Yet plants never replaced it. Instead, they compensated by producing enormous quantities (an estimated 40 billion tons on Earth at any time) and evolving workaround pathways.

This mirrors startup strategy: the first adequate solution that achieves network effects or ecosystem lock-in often defeats technically superior latecomers. Plants went 'all-in on the RuBisCo strategy' and built their entire metabolic architecture around it, making replacement prohibitively expensive. The lesson is that speed to market with a good-enough solution, combined with aggressive scaling, creates path dependency that persists even when the original design is clearly suboptimal.
