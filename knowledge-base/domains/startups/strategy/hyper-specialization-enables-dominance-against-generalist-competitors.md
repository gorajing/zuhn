Phages succeed precisely because each one targets a single bacterial species with extreme precision, contrasted with antibiotics which indiscriminately kill all bacteria including beneficial ones. This mirrors the startup dynamic where hyper-focused products beat generalist incumbents within their niche.

The tradeoff is real: specialization means you can't address adjacent problems (a phage that kills E. coli is useless against Staph). But within the chosen niche, the specialized attacker has billions of years of co-evolutionary refinement — an R&D advantage no generalist can match. The lesson for strategy is that precision often beats power, especially when collateral damage from broad approaches creates its own problems.
