Photosynthesis evolved to produce chemical energy (ATP, NADPH, and ultimately glucose) for plant survival. Oxygen was literally described as 'the most useful useless byproduct in the history of useless byproducts' — a waste gas vented through stomata. Yet this byproduct transformed Earth's atmosphere, enabled the evolution of all aerobic life, and became far more consequential than the glucose that plants originally optimized for.

This pattern recurs in business and technology. Slack started as an internal tool for a failed video game. YouTube began as a dating site. AWS emerged from Amazon's internal infrastructure needs. The implication for founders is to pay close attention to what your system produces incidentally — the throwaway output, the unexpected user behavior, the data exhaust. These 'waste products' may represent your actual market opportunity.

The deeper principle is that complex systems generate emergent properties that their designers never intended. Optimizing solely for the planned output means ignoring potentially transformative side effects.
