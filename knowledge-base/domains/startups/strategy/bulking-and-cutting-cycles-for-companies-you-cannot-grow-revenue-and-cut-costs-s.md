    Cliff Weitzman's framework based on Speechify's actual trajectory:
    'Companies go through cutting and bulking cycles like bodybuilders do.
    There's a reason why cutting and bulking makes sense — you can't oscillate
    between the two in one week. You have to commit for like 6 months to doing
    one. For Speechify, it took us 4.5 years to find real product market fit.
    The product stays exactly the same. The vision today is very much the same
    as it was in the beginning. But in 2020 we started growing really fast. The
    companies succeed because you have a person who puts their beating heart
    into the product. You can only do one of those things at a time. The second
    you split your focus — you can't fundraise and sales at the same time. You
    got to do one. I started logging my time in 30-minute increments. 30% of my
    time I spent helping other people. 30% of my time I spent learning SEO. I
    decided: I'm not allowed to learn anything anymore. I'm only allowed to
    execute. The second I saw myself reading an article, I would close it and go
    execute.' The conclusion across nearly a decade: 'Any Harvard MBA can cut
    costs — if you're smart, you can do it. It takes a genius to grow revenue.
    That's why investors value exponential growth of revenue even for companies
    not making a profit. But it's very difficult to do both at the same time.
    You're clicking the gas and clicking the brake at the same time. Speechify
    just finished 4.5 years of running profitable. Now we're in a hyper-growth
    phase.' The structural argument: a company's organizational attention is
    finite. Growing revenue requires investing in: experiments that may fail,
    new channels with unknown ROI, hiring ahead of need, customer acquisition
    that may cost more than current LTV. Cutting costs requires the OPPOSITE:
    predictable systems, optimization of existing channels, headcount
    discipline, customer retention focus. The cognitive frames are inverted.
    Trying to hold both simultaneously produces a company that's mediocre at
    both. The corollary for founders: explicitly choose your phase. Communicate
    it to the team. Don't measure growth-phase teams on cost discipline. Don't
    measure cutting-phase teams on growth velocity. The corollary for boards:
    ask the CEO 'what phase are we in?' If they can't answer clearly, they're
    trying to do both and producing neither. Force a choice. The corollary for
    investors: stop demanding 'grow revenue AND improve margins' simultaneously.
    That's mathematically incoherent in most cases. Pick one expectation per
    phase. The corollary for individuals: same principle applies to personal
    finance (saving vs investing aggressively), career (skill-building vs
    earning), fitness (cutting vs bulking), education (depth vs breadth). The
    discipline of committing to one mode for 6-12+ months produces vastly better
    outcomes than oscillating weekly.

Cliff Weitzman's framework based on Speechify's actual trajectory: 'Companies go through cutting and bulking cycles like bodybuilders do. There's a reason why cutting and bulking makes sense — you can't oscillate between the two in one week. You have to commit for like 6 months to doing one. For Speechify, it took us 4.5 years to find real product market fit. The product stays exactly the same. The vision today is very much the same as it was in the beginning. But in 2020 we started growing really fast. The companies succeed because you have a person who puts their beating heart into the product. You can only do one of those things at a time. The second you split your focus — you can't fundraise and sales at the same time. You got to do one. I started logging my time in 30-minute increments. 30% of my time I spent helping other people. 30% of my time I spent learning SEO. I decided: I'm not allowed to learn anything anymore. I'm only allowed to execute. The second I saw myself reading an article, I would close it and go execute.' The conclusion across nearly a decade: 'Any Harvard MBA can cut costs — if you're smart, you can do it. It takes a genius to grow revenue. That's why investors value exponential growth of revenue even for companies not making a profit. But it's very difficult to do both at the same time. You're clicking the gas and clicking the brake at the same time. Speechify just finished 4.5 years of running profitable. Now we're in a hyper-growth phase.' The structural argument: a company's organizational attention is finite. Growing revenue requires investing in: experiments that may fail, new channels with unknown ROI, hiring ahead of need, customer acquisition that may cost more than current LTV. Cutting costs requires the OPPOSITE: predictable systems, optimization of existing channels, headcount discipline, customer retention focus. The cognitive frames are inverted. Trying to hold both simultaneously produces a company that's mediocre at both. The corollary for founders: explicitly choose your phase. Communicate it to the team. Don't measure growth-phase teams on cost discipline. Don't measure cutting-phase teams on growth velocity. The corollary for boards: ask the CEO 'what phase are we in?' If they can't answer clearly, they're trying to do both and producing neither. Force a choice. The corollary for investors: stop demanding 'grow revenue AND improve margins' simultaneously. That's mathematically incoherent in most cases. Pick one expectation per phase. The corollary for individuals: same principle applies to personal finance (saving vs investing aggressively), career (skill-building vs earning), fitness (cutting vs bulking), education (depth vs breadth). The discipline of committing to one mode for 6-12+ months produces vastly better outcomes than oscillating weekly.
