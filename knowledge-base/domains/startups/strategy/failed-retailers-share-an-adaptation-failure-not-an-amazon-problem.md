The 'retail apocalypse' narrative frames Amazon as the destroyer of physical retail, but the evidence tells a different story. The retailers that died — Sears (tried everything poorly), Toys-R-Us (dismissed the internet), J.C. Penney (lost touch with customers), RadioShack, Payless — all share a common trait: failure to adapt their core value proposition. Meanwhile, Ross, Lush, Aldi, Dollar General, and Costco thrived in the exact same competitive environment.

This pattern recurs across industries facing disruption. The disruption narrative conveniently externalizes blame ('Amazon killed us') when the actual cause is internal ('we stopped being relevant'). For founders and strategists, the lesson is that competitive threats don't uniformly destroy incumbents — they expose which incumbents were already failing to deliver genuine value. The question isn't 'how do I survive Amazon?' but 'what unique value do I provide that no one else can replicate?'
