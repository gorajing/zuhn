Smith identifies a recurring pattern in Chinese governance: aggressive, dramatic action that ignores long-term consequences, followed by rapid reversal once those consequences become clear. The one-child policy went too far and lasted too long; now China frantically promotes three children. Real estate was used as an economic shock absorber for decades; now Xi crushes the sector, causing economic chaos. Industrial crackdowns swing between extremes.

This 'oversteering' pattern applies directly to startup strategy. Companies that make dramatic pivots, aggressive market entries, or sweeping organizational changes — then reverse course when problems emerge — burn more resources and create more internal chaos than those making continuous smaller adjustments. The lesson isn't to avoid bold moves, but to build feedback loops that detect consequences early enough to adjust incrementally rather than waiting until a full reversal is the only option.
