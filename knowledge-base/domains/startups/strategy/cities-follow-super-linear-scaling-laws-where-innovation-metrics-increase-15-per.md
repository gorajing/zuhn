Geoffrey West's research in 'Scale' identified a striking mathematical regularity: knowing only a city's population, you can predict average wages, GDP, patent output, and other innovation metrics with 80-90% accuracy. These metrics increase approximately 15% per capita each time the city population doubles—a super-linear scaling law consistent across cities worldwide.

This has profound implications for startup location decisions. Cities aren't just convenient—they're mathematical engines of innovation whose network properties compound with scale. This same math explains why cities have been the most durable networks in human history (most major world cities have existed for thousands of years) and why the 10,000+ year trend of urban clustering is unlikely to reverse despite advances in digital communication. The bandwidth gap between online and offline interaction remains too great given how the human brain is optimized for in-person exchange.
