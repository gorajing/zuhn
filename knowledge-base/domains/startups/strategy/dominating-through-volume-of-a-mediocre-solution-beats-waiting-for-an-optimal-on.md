RuBisCo, the enzyme responsible for carbon fixation in photosynthesis, is famously inefficient — it confuses oxygen for CO2 roughly half the time, producing a toxic byproduct plants must actively detoxify. Yet it became the foundation of nearly all life on Earth because the organisms that evolved it simply produced massive quantities to compensate for its poor performance.

This mirrors startup strategy: a mediocre product shipped at scale often defeats a superior product that arrives late. Once RuBisCo was entrenched, plants were 'all-in' and couldn't pivot to a better enzyme. The same path dependency affects technical architectures and business models — early lock-in of a good-enough solution creates switching costs that make optimization nearly impossible, even when the original design becomes actively harmful in changed conditions (like rising oxygen levels).
