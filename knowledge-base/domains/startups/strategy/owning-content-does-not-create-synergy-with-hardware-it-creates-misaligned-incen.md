After losing the Betamax war, Morita concluded Sony needed to own content to never again be at the mercy of Hollywood. Sony bought CBS Records for $2B in 1988 and Columbia Pictures for effectively $6B in 1989. The CBS Records purchase became a great financial asset, generating $2B+ in annual operating cash flow for decades. But the strategic synergy thesis — that owning content would help Sony sell hardware — never materialized. The reason is structural: the hardware division wanted devices that played the widest possible range of content (including pirated, cracked, or foreign content) to maximize device sales, while the content divisions wanted hardware to enforce DRM and format restrictions to protect their catalogs. These incentives cannot be reconciled without one side losing. Sony spent decades trying and failing to make a unified strategy work.

The deeper lesson is that 'synergy' is usually a word people use when they cannot articulate a concrete mechanism by which two businesses make each other more valuable. For hardware and content, the concrete mechanism would require the hardware team to accept worse product decisions (restrictive formats, locked-down players) so the content team could charge more. That trade almost always makes both sides worse, because consumers route around it.

Apply this when evaluating any vertical integration deal pitched on content-plus-device synergy. The burden of proof should be on identifying the specific decisions the two sides will make differently as one company than as two — and whether those decisions actually help customers. For Sony, the answer was no. For Apple (which chose not to buy a studio), the answer was also no. Gaming is the closest exception because the content is the hardware in a more literal sense.
