Paine highlights how WWI generals kept running trench offensives for months past the point of diminishing returns, racking up hundreds of thousands of deaths for territory they couldn't hold. The British army that fought WWI on the main continental front for four years took twice as many casualties as the WWII army that fought a peripheral strategy — despite WWII being longer and larger in scope.

The operator's lesson: every campaign, product launch, fundraise, or hiring push has a culminating point where marginal input starts producing negative returns because you're depleting reserves the opponent (or the market) can exploit. The discipline is recognizing early that your initial gains were the whole prize, and that 'one more push' is usually ego, not strategy. Great WWII commanders were conditioned by WWI to pull back when tactical momentum stalled instead of doubling down.
