Evans revives his 'content isn't king / Netflix isn't a tech company' framing: Netflix is enabled by tech the industry built, but every question that matters to it — what shows, what to pay talent, whether to chase awards or buy sports — is a Los Angeles media question, not a San Francisco one. The same migration is happening with AI. What generative video means for Hollywood is a question Ben Affleck can answer better than a technologist; what AI does to a law firm's pyramid, margin structure, and hiring is a question for people who know what associates actually do and what clients are actually paying for. If you've never worked at a law firm or at Bain/BCG/McKinsey, you don't know what the associates do or what the client is buying, so you can't see how it reconfigures.

This points to a concrete sourcing and team-building heuristic for AI startups and investors: the leverage in vertical AI sits with deep domain insiders, because a huge share of how organizations actually work is implicit, undocumented, not in the training data, and not something anyone could draw you a flowchart of — which is precisely what consultancies get paid to excavate. The corollary is that point-solution adoption outside tech looks different from consumer AI: corporations succeed when they pose a specific bounded problem ('forecast our cash flow better') rather than handing users a chatbot and asking them to invent uses. Build with insiders, target named problems, and treat domain knowledge as the scarce asset rather than model access.
