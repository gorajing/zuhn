Germany and Japan both constructed elaborate ideological frameworks (racial superiority, co-prosperity) to justify what were fundamentally resource-acquisition campaigns. The ideology followed the strategy, not the other way around.

This pattern maps directly to business: companies facing existential resource constraints (runway, talent, market access) often develop grand narratives about 'disruption' or 'democratization' to justify aggressive competitive moves. Recognizing when a competitor's stated mission is actually post-hoc rationalization of a survival strategy provides better competitive intelligence than taking mission statements at face value. The insight also applies internally — founders should honestly distinguish between vision-driven and constraint-driven decisions.
