Coconote charged $99/year for a student app when conventional wisdom
insisted students would not pay meaningful subscription prices. The
premium pricing worked because it signaled reliability and trust —
students willing to pay for a study tool were serious about their
education and valued quality. Even more counterintuitively, when they
raised the price from $99 to $129, both user count and revenue
increased. The higher price reinforced the perception that the product
was worth investing in. The lesson: underpricing does not just leave
money on the table, it can actively signal low quality. If your product
delivers real value, price accordingly from the start. You can always
test price increases — you may find, as Coconote did, that demand is
less price-sensitive than you assumed.
