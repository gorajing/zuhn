Phil Knight's early Nike story is a masterclass in strategic overstatement. He invented the company name 'Blue Ribbon Sports' mid-meeting when asked by Onitsuka executives. Later, when they required an East Coast presence for a national distribution deal, he immediately claimed to have offices there — then scrambled to make it real after the meeting. Each bluff secured a deal that provided the revenue and credibility to build what he'd claimed.

This isn't pure deception — it's a calculated bet that you can deliver on promises by the time they're due. The pattern works because B2B partners often need to see scale signals before committing resources that would actually enable that scale. The risk is obvious: if you can't close the gap between claim and reality, you lose credibility permanently. But Knight's example shows that in early-stage business development, the willingness to commit to a vision slightly ahead of your current reality can create the momentum needed to catch up.
