'Ramen profitable' — making just enough to pay founders' living expenses — isn't about validating a business model. It's about hacking the investment process by eliminating existential dependency on outside capital. When you can survive indefinitely without investors, every conversation with them shifts from 'please save us' to 'here's an opportunity.'

This connects to Graham's broader emphasis on cheapness as a survival strategy. Most startups fail before making something people want, and they fail by running out of money. Ramen profitability removes the ticking clock, buying unlimited iteration time. It also boosts morale, creating a virtuous cycle: less financial stress means more focus, which means better product decisions, which means faster growth.
