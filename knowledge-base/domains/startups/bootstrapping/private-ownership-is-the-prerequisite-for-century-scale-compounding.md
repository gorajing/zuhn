Mars's fifth principle is Freedom, which concretely means: no public shareholders, no debt, no external board pressure. The family has refused photographs for decades and paid off a freelance photographer $20,000 to recover images from a Washington Post article they disliked. This extreme privacy isn't eccentricity — it's strategy.

Private ownership lets Mars optimize for metrics public companies can't, like replacement-cost ROTA and multi-year reinvestment cycles that depress current earnings. It lets them reinvest aggressively into R&D and custom manufacturing equipment, taking the benefit as lower taxes and compounding asset productivity instead of quarterly EPS growth. It lets them run a 10-year investment horizon on new categories (pet food, rice, eventually veterinary services) that would never survive the earnings call cycle.

The lesson generalizes beyond Mars: whenever a business advantage requires sub-optimizing the short term — brand building, category creation, talent investment, manufacturing quality — public ownership actively works against you. Complete ownership or board control (Meta via dual-class, Ikea via foundation, Mars via family) is the prerequisite, not an afterthought, for century-scale compounding.
