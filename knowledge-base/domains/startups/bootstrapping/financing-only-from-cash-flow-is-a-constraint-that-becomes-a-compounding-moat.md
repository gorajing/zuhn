The only outside capital IKEA ever took was a $63 bank loan Ingvar Kamprad used at age 12 to buy fountain pens. Every factory, every store, every market entry was funded by reinvested profit from the previous period. The comparison to Warren Buffett's early career is direct: both men treated every dollar leaving the business as a permanent loss of future compounding, and that discipline warped how they made decisions.

The non-obvious consequence is that bootstrapping shapes what kind of company you build, not just how fast you build it. IKEA could not afford to lose money on loss-leaders, so the hot dog policy required every cheap item to still be profitable. It could not afford to ramp supply faster than demand, so it developed obsessive inventory discipline. It could not afford splashy failures, so expansion was methodical (Japan 1975 failed and they pulled out; they didn't double down). And crucially, staying private let Kamprad structure ownership into a foundation that insulated the company permanently from public-market short-termism — a move impossible once you've taken institutional equity.

The catch: this only works with an 81-year time horizon. If you need to dominate a category in 5-10 years (most consumer tech, anything with a land-grab dynamic), bootstrapping loses. If the category will exist in 50 years and durability matters more than speed, it wins decisively. Founders should pick the model deliberately, not by default.
