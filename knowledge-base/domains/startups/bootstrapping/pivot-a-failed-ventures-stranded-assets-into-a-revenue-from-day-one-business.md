RunPod's origin is a case study in asset-recycling and community-validated bootstrapping. The founders' crypto-mining venture failed, leaving them with a basement full of idle GPUs. Rather than write off the hardware, they built a prototype of a GPU-rental platform and posted on Reddit: 'Does anyone want some free GPUs in exchange for feedback?' That single post seeded both their first users and their product direction, and they built in public with that community thereafter — reaching ~$120M ARR while being revenue-generating from the very start, which the presenter notes is rare.

Two transferable moves. First, a failed venture's stranded assets (hardware, data, distribution, a half-built tool) can be the cheapest possible starting capital for the next idea — the sunk cost becomes the wedge. Second, giving away spare capacity in exchange for feedback is a high-signal customer-discovery channel: it costs little when the asset is already idle, attracts users with genuine need, and converts directly into product feedback and eventual revenue. The lesson is to look at what a failure leaves behind as raw material, and to bootstrap demand by trading underused capacity for learning.
