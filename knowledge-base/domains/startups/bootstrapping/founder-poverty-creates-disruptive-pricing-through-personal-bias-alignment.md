Graham illustrates this with both Apple (Woz designed for himself because he couldn't afford more) and Viaweb (priced at $300/month, an order of magnitude below competitors, because that felt expensive to the founders). The pattern recurs across industries: someone builds something at 1/10th the cost, and incumbents can't follow because they don't want to think about a world where that price point is viable.

This is more than a pricing strategy—it's a cognitive advantage. Poor founders don't have to discipline themselves to think cheaply; it's their natural frame. This aligns with technology's inherent trajectory toward commoditization. Incumbents face the innovator's dilemma not just structurally but psychologically: their personal lifestyles make cheap solutions feel wrong. The tactical takeaway is that artificially maintaining a low burn rate and personal austerity isn't just financial prudence—it shapes product decisions in competitively advantageous ways.
