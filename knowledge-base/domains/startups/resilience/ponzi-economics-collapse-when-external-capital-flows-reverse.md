Madoff's scheme ran for at least 15 years (possibly since 1993) because bull markets generate surplus capital seeking returns. The 2008 crisis didn't gradually stress the operation — it killed it almost overnight when withdrawals exceeded inflows. This pattern applies beyond literal Ponzi schemes to any venture that uses new customer revenue to fulfill promises to existing customers without building underlying value. Hyper-growth startups burning cash to acquire users who never reach profitability, crypto yield protocols paying returns from new deposits, or real estate schemes dependent on perpetual appreciation all share this structural fragility. The diagnostic question is: if all new money stopped tomorrow, could existing commitments be honored? If the answer is no, you're looking at Ponzi economics regardless of intent.
