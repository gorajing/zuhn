The Comair implementation flight had one planned fly-by, one aborted landing, and one successful landing — routine in commercial aviation. But tabloids spun it as 'terrifying three attempts to land' and called the airport a 'folly' and 'farcical vanity project.' Much reporting was factually inaccurate (claiming it was impossible to land, that the airport was closed).

Years later, airport staff were still actively battling the negative perception. As one official noted, 'the sensationalist stuff that sells papers' travels faster than positive corrections. This is asymmetric information warfare: destroying credibility is orders of magnitude easier than building it. For startups, this means crisis communication isn't optional — the first narrative that reaches scale becomes the default, and you may never fully overwrite it. Proactive narrative control before a crisis is far cheaper than reactive reputation repair.
