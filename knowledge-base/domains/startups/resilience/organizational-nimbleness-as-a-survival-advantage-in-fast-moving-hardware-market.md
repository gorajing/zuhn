The video draws an explicit parallel to Nokia's demise — a company whose massive size created bureaucratic layers that fatally slowed its reaction time in the smartphone transition. Obi positioned its small size as a feature: the ability to quickly iterate designs and swap components when better options became available.

In hardware markets where component technology evolves rapidly, the ability to react quickly to new chipsets, display technologies, or manufacturing innovations creates compounding advantages. Each product cycle where a small company can adopt newer components faster than a large competitor widens the value-per-dollar gap. However, this advantage is inherently temporary — it lasts only as long as the company remains small and the market remains in flux. Once markets stabilize and scale economies dominate, nimbleness becomes less valuable than supply chain leverage.
