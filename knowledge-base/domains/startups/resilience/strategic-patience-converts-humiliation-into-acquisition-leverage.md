In 1999, when Ratan Tata approached Ford about selling Tata's motor division, Ford officials told him 'you do not know anything' and said they'd be doing Tata a favor by buying. The deal fell through. Less than a decade later, Ford couldn't make Jaguar and Land Rover profitable and was forced to sell both brands to Tata for $2.3 billion. Tata then turned both brands around successfully.

This pattern — where dismissal by incumbents becomes fuel for eventual dominance — appears repeatedly in business history. The critical enabler is strategic patience: Tata didn't react emotionally to the humiliation or abandon the automotive space. They continued building capability and waited for the right moment. The lesson for founders facing dismissal from incumbents or larger players is that the power dynamic can reverse surprisingly quickly, especially when the dismissive party is overextended and the dismissed party is building genuine capability.
