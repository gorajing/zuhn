South Africa's private security industry employs more people than the police and military combined. City improvement districts where businesses self-fund street maintenance, security, and sanitation have created European-standard pockets within a deteriorating state. Solar installation has doubled in a single year as those who can afford it disconnect from the grid entirely. This pattern is predictable and generalizable: when a critical state service crosses a reliability threshold, a private market emerges to serve those who can pay. However, this creates a dangerous feedback loop — once the wealthy have private alternatives, they lose their incentive to pressure government for improvement and their tax contributions effectively subsidize a system they no longer use. For founders, this represents a massive market signal: any geography where state services are visibly declining is a market for privatized alternatives. But the broader lesson is that parallel private infrastructure, while individually rational, collectively accelerates state decline by splitting the constituency that could demand reform.
