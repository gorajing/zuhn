By the early 1950s, Toyota was on the verge of collapse — WWII had devastated its production capacity and forced its founder's son to resign. Then the Korean War broke out, and the US military ordered over 5,000 vehicles in the first few months, single-handedly reviving the company. This pattern appears repeatedly in business history: companies survive not through brilliant turnaround plans but through exogenous demand shocks they couldn't have predicted. The implication for founders is twofold — during existential crises, survival itself (staying alive long enough for conditions to change) is the strategy, and the eventual lifeline often comes from a direction no one anticipated. This argues for extreme cost-cutting and optionality preservation during downturns rather than bold pivots.
