In 1991, one year before IPO, Schultz fought his two VC board members to create 'Bean Stock': 14% of every employee's base pay granted as stock options to anyone working 20+ hours a week — including every barista. The VCs said 'we're not doing that.' Schultz insisted it had to happen before the IPO so nobody would miss the initial strike price ($6, which is now ~$4,800 adjusted for splits per original share — an 800x return to the early part-timers).

The VCs predicted what actually happened: attrition dropped, performance rose, the brand elevated. But the deeper effect was cultural: that's the day Starbucks started calling employees 'partners' and writing titles in lowercase. The equity grant was the cause, not the symbol. It turned a retail labor force into people with literal ownership of the customer experience — which is why the customer-driven innovations (names on cups, custom drinks, the third place behavior) came from baristas, not from headquarters.

The generalizable lesson is that equity to frontline workers is qualitatively different from higher wages or better benefits. Wages buy labor; equity changes who is in the room when the company is deciding what to become. Most founders treat this as a marginal HR decision and give tiny grants to executives only. The Starbucks/Costco cluster suggests the opposite: the earlier and more broadly equity is distributed (especially to the people customers actually interact with), the more the culture compounds into a genuine moat. If your board resists, the resistance itself is a signal you're doing something structurally valuable.
