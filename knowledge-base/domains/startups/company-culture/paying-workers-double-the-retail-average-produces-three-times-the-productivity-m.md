Costco's 245,000 workers earn an average of $21/hour, with better health insurance and retirement benefits than the retail industry standard. In exchange, the company reports employees are three times more productive than the average retail worker. Low turnover reduces hiring and training costs, high engagement reduces theft and errors, and better customer service drives membership renewals.

This challenges the default assumption in low-margin businesses that labor is a cost to minimize. At scale, the productivity multiplier can more than offset the wage premium. The key variable is whether the work involves enough skill, judgment, or customer-facing interaction that quality of execution materially affects outcomes — if it does, paying for quality has compounding returns.

The Costco case also illustrates reputational compounding: customers who know workers are paid well feel better about spending money there, reducing the cognitive dissonance of 'getting a deal' at someone else's expense. Ethics can be a brand moat.
