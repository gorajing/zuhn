Jamsetji Tata founded the company in 1868 with explicit goals beyond profit — steel, education, hotels, hydroelectric power — and a caring attitude toward Indian society. Over 150 years and multiple leadership generations later, Tata Group still maintains this philanthropic orientation, returning wealth through trusts funding medical research, technology, education, and social welfare. This isn't just PR — it earned them #11 on Forbes' most reputable companies list in 2009.

The mechanism matters: Tata embedded values structurally through trusts and institutional practices rather than relying on individual leaders to perpetuate them. This suggests founder values survive generational transitions only when encoded into organizational structures, not just cultural narratives. For founders building companies today, the implication is that the structures you create to enforce your values matter more than the values themselves.
