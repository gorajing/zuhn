The conventional model in retail treats labor as the primary cost lever — minimize hours, minimize wages, minimize benefits. Costco inverts this: average wage of $21/hr (double the US retail average) plus better health insurance and retirement benefits, with reported employee productivity three times higher than retail norms.

The causal mechanism likely combines lower turnover (recruiting and training costs are high), higher morale producing better customer service, and selection effects — better wages attract more reliable workers. In an industry where the average supermarket has high SKU counts and complex restocking needs, a stable, skilled workforce compounds over time.

This is a meaningful data point for founders building operations-heavy companies: the 'cut labor costs' reflex may destroy more value than it saves if it produces churn, lower quality, and customer experience degradation. The Costco model suggests there's a high-wage, high-productivity equilibrium that many companies fail to find because they never try it.
