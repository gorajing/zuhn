The Acquired hosts observe that they cannot find a single Enron departure where the leaving employee did not extract a golden parachute, sweetheart asset purchase, or hush payment. Lou Pai cashed out $250M for a divorce. Rebecca Mark walked with $83M after losing billions on international water deals she never had a chance of making work. This pattern, they argue, traces directly to Ken Lay's pathological non-confrontationalism — he never said anything publicly confrontational to anyone, ever.

The mechanism is simple. When a CEO will do anything to avoid an uncomfortable conversation, every counterparty learns this and prices it into their behavior. Departing executives ask for more because they know they'll get it. Underperformers stop being managed out. Bad deals get rationalized rather than killed. The cumulative cost falls entirely on shareholders, who have no seat at the negotiation.

The lesson for boards and founders: confrontation-averse leadership is not a soft problem about culture, it's a hard problem about value capture. A CEO who cannot or will not say no — to a departing executive's demand, to a bad deal, to a bullying customer — is structurally biased toward giving away shareholder economics in exchange for personal comfort. This trait should be screened for explicitly in CEO selection and treated as a dealbreaker, not a quirk.
