Foroughi's diagnosis of why most current AI-driven layoffs won't deliver expected efficiency: when a team becomes bloated, the A-players are already long gone — they had options and left when the culture got slow. What's left is A-minus to B-plus people who like processoriented work, plus the B/C/D players underneath. Firing 50% of that mix leaves you with half-mediocrity, not half-of-anything-good. The only effective rebuild requires firing roughly 99% and starting from scratch — which is operationally easy for private companies and exceptionally hard for public ones.

AppLovin's actual approach in 2024-2025: cut 40-50% of teams in most departments, focused on processoriented orgs (HR went from 70-80 people to 15), retained only individual contributors who could 'just get stuff done.' The catalyst was a CTO who repeatedly asked 'why do we have these people, why do we have these processes' — forcing the company to revisit assumptions accumulated over 10 years.

For builders: this is the operational form of why most layoff-driven 'AI transformations' won't deliver. The CEO promising 30% efficiency gains via headcount reduction without addressing culture is making a category error. For Zuhn editorially: this is direct material on the structural difficulty of legacy companies adopting AI-native ways of working — it's not about adoption velocity but about whether the cultural fabric can support the shift, and most can't.
