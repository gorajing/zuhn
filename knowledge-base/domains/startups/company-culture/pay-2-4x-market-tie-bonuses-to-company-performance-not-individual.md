Forrest Mars knew that to scale without micromanaging, he needed the best people operating independently. Rather than giving equity (which would compromise private ownership) or paying high base salaries (which creates fixed costs), he paid Mars associates 3-4x the market rate for their role via bonuses tied to company-wide performance metrics. Today the multiple is closer to 2x, but Mars still pays at least 10% above industry and ties most compensation to company performance.

The only individual-performance element was a 10% bonus for never being late all year — everyone from the CEO down punches a time card. Otherwise, you rise and fall with the company. This creates psychological ownership without legal ownership: associates behave like partners because their downside (tough years mean bonus cuts) and upside (great years mean multiples of market) both track the company's fortunes.

Combined with an aggressively flat culture — no executive offices, no perks, no reserved parking, no doors on conference rooms — the compensation structure reinforces that everyone is an 'associate' with a stake in the collective outcome. The result is famously low turnover, deep tribal knowledge, and the ability to run operations with 30% fewer employees than competitors.
