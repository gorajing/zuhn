Asked about moats in AI labs, Midha argues that data isn't the real moat — culture is, and it's the one that compounds and determines everything downstream. But he immediately qualifies it: culture is not a durable asset but a brittle thing requiring daily tending 'like a garden.' He invokes the Bushido-attributed line that 'culture is not a set of beliefs, it's a set of actions' — if you stop taking the actions that demonstrate the mission matters to your team and the world, the culture starts to fray. His diagnosis of why so many well-capitalized AI labs can't ship and then bleed people is precisely this cultural decay. The system for keeping the garden tended ultimately comes down to knowing yourself: an authentic founder naturally makes the trade-offs that reinforce culture, which makes the culture effortless to maintain and very hard for outsiders to replicate. He uses Anthropic as the example of a mission-like zeal (safety, safety, safety) held accountable daily by both team and the world, which becomes a moat over time.
