Graham describes a specific cognitive skill he developed at Y Combinator: instead of defaulting to listing reasons a new idea won't work, deliberately switching polarity to imagine ways it could succeed. This isn't naive optimism — it's a trained response calibrated by seeing thousands of ideas in an environment where power-law outcomes make missed opportunities catastrophically expensive.

The key insight is that this polarity switch is trainable and compounds over time. Graham notes he's 'become quite good at it' through practice, suggesting it's not a personality trait but a skill. In any domain with heavy-tailed outcome distributions, the expected value of engaging seriously with unpromising-looking ideas far exceeds the expected value of correctly dismissing them. The asymmetry of outcomes means the optimist's occasional big win dwarfs the skeptic's many small correct predictions.
