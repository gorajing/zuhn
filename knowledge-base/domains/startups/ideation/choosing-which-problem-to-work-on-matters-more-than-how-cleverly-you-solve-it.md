Graham observes that even brilliant people are surprisingly conservative in choosing what to work on — they follow fashion in problem selection while being creative in solutions. This is backwards: the greatest contributions come from originality in problem choice, not problem solving. Copernicus and Darwin didn't solve existing problems more cleverly; they identified entirely different problems to work on.

The most overlooked problems fall into three categories: problems people think are fully explored but aren't (Dürer, Watt), problems that don't seem important enough but actually are (follow your curiosity), and problems in the dead zone around cherished but mistaken principles. The practical test Graham suggests: 'If you were going to take a break from serious work to work on something just because it would be really interesting, what would you do?' That answer is probably more important than it seems. Questions are underrated because schools treat them as unstable particles that exist only briefly before being answered, but a truly good question is a partial discovery in itself.
