Graham defines the startup essence as 'having brilliant people do work that's beneath them'—think Einstein designing refrigerators. Big companies try to match people to roles; startups gain leverage by having research-caliber minds solve immediate, prosaic problems like UI design or customer support workflows.

This framing resolves a common tension: smart founders often resist working on 'boring' problems because it feels like a waste of their abilities. Graham argues the opposite—applying extraordinary capability to ordinary problems creates wealth rapidly precisely because the problems are straightforward enough that raw intelligence can crush them. The difficulty isn't in the problem's complexity but in the founder's willingness to focus their abilities downward rather than upward.
