Graham describes a complete polarity inversion in how to evaluate new ideas: instead of defaulting to skepticism (listing flaws), switch to generative optimism (imagining paths to success). This isn't naive positivity — it's calibrated to environments where outcomes follow power-law distributions, meaning the cost of missing one great idea vastly exceeds the cost of being wrong about many mediocre ones.

Y Combinator's entire model depends on this skill. Reviewing thousands of strange-sounding proposals from unknown people every six months, partners must identify needles in haystacks where conventional dismissal would be catastrophic. The key insight is that this evaluative optimism is a learnable, practiceable skill — not an innate personality trait — and its economic returns cause it to spread naturally through communities that adopt it.
