Most people default to listing reasons a new idea will fail — it feels analytically rigorous and socially safe. Graham argues this polarity is exactly wrong for early-stage ideas. In environments with power-law distributions (startups, research, creative work), the cost of missing a great idea dwarfs the cost of spending time on a bad one. YC partners practice this switch thousands of times per batch cycle, treating each strange-sounding idea as a challenge to imagination rather than a test to be graded. This is not lowering standards — it's applying the right evaluation framework for the stage. The skill compounds: the more you practice constructive evaluation of early ideas, the better you get at distinguishing ugly ducklings from genuinely bad ideas.
