Graham argues that most people evaluate new ideas by listing reasons they'll fail, which feels intellectually rigorous but systematically filters out the highest-value opportunities. In a world with power-law distributed outcomes, the cost of missing one great idea vastly outweighs the cost of spending time on many bad ones. Silicon Valley has learned this through painful experience.

The practical technique is a 'polarity switch': instead of asking 'why won't this work?', ask 'how could this work?' This isn't about lowering standards — it's about redirecting analytical energy from destruction to construction. Graham notes this skill improves with practice, suggesting it's a learnable habit rather than a personality trait. The fact that it's 'extremely lucrative' means it tends to spread wherever people adopt it.
