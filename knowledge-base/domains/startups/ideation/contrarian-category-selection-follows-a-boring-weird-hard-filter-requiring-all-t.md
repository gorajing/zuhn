Across three decades, Kalanick has consistently entered categories that smart people told him were dead ends: a VC told him in 2001 that all software had been invented, the food delivery graveyard was full when he entered in 2018, and in 2026 every serious dollar chases software AI while he bets on atoms and mines. The pattern isn't contrarianism for its own sake — it's a three-part filter where boring repels attention-seeking founders, weird confuses pattern-matching investors, and hard creates natural execution barriers.

The critical insight is that two out of three isn't enough. A boring-but-easy category gets competed away. A weird-but-not-hard category gets copied once proven. Only the intersection of all three creates the conditions where extended stealth and operational compounding can work. The practical test: count how many smart people told you your category was a bad idea. If the answer isn't 'most of them,' the category may not be defensible enough.
