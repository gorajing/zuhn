Graham identifies a meta-pattern across economic history: an expensive thing becomes cheap to produce, volume explodes by orders of magnitude, and entirely new applications emerge that were inconceivable at the old price point. Steel in the 1850s, power in the 1780s, cloth in the 1200s, computers in the 1980s — each triggered cascading structural changes far beyond the original industry.

Applying this pattern to startups themselves is the essay's core insight. When starting a company shifts from a capital-intensive, permission-requiring act to something as accessible as getting a laptop, the consequences cascade: standardized deal terms, professionalized acquisitions, credential disruption, faster technology evolution. The pattern predicts that the most interesting effects aren't the obvious ones (more startups) but the second-order structural changes that emerge when volume increases by orders of magnitude.
