Graham distinguishes two types of insider rejection: ideas dismissed as impossible (which insiders have usually thought about carefully) and ideas dismissed as undignified (which they haven't examined at all). HP passed on the Apple II partly because it used a TV as a monitor — not because it couldn't work, but because it seemed low-class. Durer made masterpieces in engraving, a medium considered suitable only for devotional images.

This is a precise heuristic for opportunity identification: look for domains where the establishment's objection is about status rather than feasibility. When the criticism is 'that's not serious work' rather than 'that can't work,' you've found territory that's both fertile and uncontested. The cheap, lightweight, seemingly toy-like approach often turns out to be the growth vector precisely because cheap things spread faster and lightweight things evolve faster.

The implication for founders: actively seek projects that established players would find embarrassing to work on. The discomfort of the incumbent is the moat of the outsider.
