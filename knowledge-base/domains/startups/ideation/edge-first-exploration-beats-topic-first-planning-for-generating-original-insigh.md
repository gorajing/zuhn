Graham draws a direct analogy to trading: professional traders won't enter a position without an edge — a convincing reason they'll win more than they lose. Similarly, attacking an important topic without a way in produces generic output. The edge can be surprisingly small: merely having questions about something other people take for granted can be enough.

This applies directly to startup ideation and research. A systematically generated list of 'important problems' is worthless without personal edge on any of them. The input quality that drives edge comes from two dimensions: breadth (reading widely across unrelated topics, talking to idea-generating people, going places) and depth (actually solving problems in a domain). Graham specifically notes that talking to one insightful person (Robert Morris) generates more ideas than 20 new smart people — quality of intellectual input vastly outweighs quantity.
