Huberman makes an important pharmacological distinction: there are no drugs that increase focus per se — all current options (Adderall, modafinil, Wellbutrin, caffeine, nicotine) increase alertness, which gates focus. Sleep gates alertness. So stimulants are essentially a bypass for inadequate sleep, and they always exact a cost — either disrupted sleep (creating a vicious cycle) or cardiac strain from chronic sympathetic nervous system activation.

A WashU study showed that Ritalin and similar stimulants improve focus about as well as a good night's sleep — but most users aren't getting good sleep. Sunosi (FDA-approved for excessive daytime sleepiness) is highlighted as a potentially important new option — it hits dopamine, norepinephrine, AND serotonin for a gentler arc, and did well in ADHD trials.

The venture opportunity Huberman identifies: a peptide or compound that reduces 'cognitive noise' and enables deliberate attention allocation WITHOUT stimulating the sympathetic nervous system. Low-dose GLPs are being explored for this purpose. This would be massive in the tech/knowledge worker market where stimulant use (caffeine, nicotine, prescription stimulants) is endemic. Huberman himself acknowledges drinking a lot of caffeine and wishing for a non-stimulant focus enhancer.
