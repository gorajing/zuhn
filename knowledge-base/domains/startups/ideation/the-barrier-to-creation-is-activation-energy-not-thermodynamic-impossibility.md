Lane opens with a remarkable fact: mixing hydrogen and CO2 in a tube produces nothing, yet thermodynamically those two gases are less stable than living cells. Life isn't improbable in an energetic sense — it's the thermodynamically favored outcome. The barrier is purely kinetic: the activation energy required to get the reaction started. Hydrothermal vents provided the continuous electrical charge (the 'spark') that overcame this barrier.

This reframes entrepreneurial thinking fundamentally. Many valuable products and services are 'thermodynamically favorable' — users want them, the economics work, the technology exists — but they're kinetically blocked by coordination problems, regulatory friction, or missing catalysts. The founder's job isn't to prove the idea is viable (it often obviously is, like H2 + CO2 → life) but to find the specific catalyst — the continuous energy source — that lowers the activation barrier. Lane's framing also explains why some ideas keep failing despite being 'obvious': the catalyst hasn't been found yet, not because the idea is wrong. The right environment (like alkaline hydrothermal vents) makes the impossible inevitable.
