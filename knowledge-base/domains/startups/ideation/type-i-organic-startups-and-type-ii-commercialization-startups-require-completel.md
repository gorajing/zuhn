Graham distinguishes two startup types: Type I startups that evolve naturally from founders identifying opportunities (most software companies), and Type II startups called into being to commercialize scientific discoveries (most pharma companies). Type II startups are straightforward to create—fund research, commercialize what emerges—but they don't generate startup culture.

Philadelphia illustrates this perfectly: abundant Type II biotech startups but almost no Type I startup culture. This distinction matters for anyone trying to build or evaluate ecosystems. A city full of university spinoffs and commercialization offices may look vibrant on paper while completely lacking the social infrastructure that produces the next Google. The implication is that measuring startup activity by count alone is misleading—the type composition determines whether an ecosystem is self-sustaining.
