Graham (citing Lisa Randall) describes the experimental frame as a powerful psychological technique: if your project is an experiment, there is literally no such thing as failure — only results. A disproven theorem, a structural member that breaks under stress, a startup hypothesis that doesn't pan out — all produce genuine knowledge if the problem was sharply defined enough.

This reframe works because it changes what you're optimizing for. Instead of optimizing for success (which makes early failure terrifying), you're optimizing for learning (which makes early failure informative). The key constraint is that the problem must be sharply defined — vague experiments produce vague non-results. The practical move is to articulate what specific question your project answers before starting, so that even a negative outcome teaches you something concrete and transferable.
