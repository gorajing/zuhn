Most people evaluate new ideas by defaulting to criticism — listing reasons it won't work. Paul Graham argues this polarity should be completely reversed: the right response is to treat a new idea as a challenge to your imagination, trying to think of ways it could succeed. This isn't lowering standards; it's a fundamentally different cognitive operation.

This skill is learnable and gets better with practice. YC partners develop it by necessity — evaluating thousands of strange-sounding ideas every six months in a power-law distribution where missing one needle in the haystack is catastrophic. The result is that optimism becomes urgent rather than naive. Importantly, this approach is more economically accurate than skepticism: skeptics are more often right on individual predictions, but catastrophically wrong when predictions are weighted by outcome.
