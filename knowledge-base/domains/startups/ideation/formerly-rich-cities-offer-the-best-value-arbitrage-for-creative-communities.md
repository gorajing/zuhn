Graham identifies a specific real estate arbitrage: always-rich places are nice but expensive, always-poor places are cheap but grim, but formerly-rich-now-poor places have solid buildings and infrastructure at low prices. Pittsburgh's century-old architecture from its steel-era wealth creates 'palaces for cheap.'

This pattern applies beyond Pittsburgh to any city experiencing post-industrial decline — Detroit, Baltimore, Cleveland, and many European cities follow the same dynamic. For startup founders choosing where to build, this framework suggests looking for cities in the trough of a formerly-rich cycle rather than simply the cheapest cost of living. The physical environment matters because it shapes the daily experience that retains talent.
