Graham and Carmack both observe that the distance between early work and finished work in a given medium directly affects how many people attempt ambitious projects. When Quake mods were crude enough that a quick hack wasn't far from the official game, modding flourished and launched careers. When commercial game quality rose, the gap between prototype and polished product became so large that modding collapsed — until Minecraft's deliberately crude aesthetic reset the bar.

The principle generalizes: digital notebooks over stone carving, sketches over oil paintings, quick hacks over enterprise architecture. The key insight is that much of digital media's advantage isn't the software itself but the elimination of upfront commitment. You can start something new without implicitly promising to finish it. This is why 'just a sketch' and 'just a quick hack' are not self-deprecation but strategic framing — they give you permission to produce the ugly early version that every ambitious project must pass through.
