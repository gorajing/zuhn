Graham identifies a combination of perfect weather, beautiful mountains, and proximity to cosmopolitan San Francisco as 'much of the reason Silicon Valley grew up around this university and not some other one.' This challenges the common narrative that innovation clusters form purely around intellectual capital, government funding, or economic policy.

The implication is that talent attraction has a strong lifestyle component that policy-driven cluster attempts (Research Triangle, etc.) often underweight. People with options choose where to live based on quality of life, and once a critical mass of talent concentrates, network effects take over. This suggests that cities trying to build tech ecosystems should invest in livability and cultural amenity as much as in tax incentives or university research funding.
