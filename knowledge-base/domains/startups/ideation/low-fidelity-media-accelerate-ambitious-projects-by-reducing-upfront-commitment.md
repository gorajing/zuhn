The medium you choose to start a project shapes whether you'll survive its early phase. Drawing in a notebook feels like 'just a sketch' — carving in stone feels like a commitment. Digital tools compound this advantage not primarily through software features but by collapsing the gap between starting and having something to evaluate. The faster you get initial results, the faster you can see improvement rate, and focusing on rate of change rather than absolute quality is one of the most effective antidotes to early-stage discouragement.

John Carmack's observation about game mods illustrates the same principle at ecosystem scale. Quake's crude technical constraints meant quick gameplay hacks weren't far from the official game, spawning careers. As commercial quality rose, the barrier became insurmountable — until Minecraft and Roblox deliberately re-lowered it. The lesson generalizes: when selecting tools or formats for new work, optimize for speed-to-first-result and low psychological commitment over polish or capability. You can always upgrade the medium once the project has momentum.
