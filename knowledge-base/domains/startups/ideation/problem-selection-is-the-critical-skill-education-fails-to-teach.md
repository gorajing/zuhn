Graham observes that most Summer Founders applicants defaulted to obvious ideas (social networks, multiplayer games) while ignoring valuable unsolved problems in plain sight (like micropayments for newspapers). He attributes this to 15-20 years of solving problems other people set for them—grading requires standardized problems, so schools never teach problem selection.

The good news is that problem selection is learnable. Graham's core insight is that 'the hard part about figuring out what customers want is figuring out that you need to figure it out.' Once you see this—like seeing the rabbit in a duck-rabbit illusion—you can't unsee it. Hackers who add this customer-awareness to their existing technical ability become what Graham calls 'bronze'—an alloy so much harder than its components that it seems like a different metal entirely.
