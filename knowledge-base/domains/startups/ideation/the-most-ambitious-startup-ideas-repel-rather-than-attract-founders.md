Paul Graham observes that the relationship between startup idea quality and founder attraction is inverted at the top end. While moderately good ideas feel exciting, the truly massive opportunities — replacing email, building new search engines, transforming education — actively repel people. This happens because such ideas threaten the founder's identity: they force you to confront whether you're actually capable of executing something that enormous.

This creates a systematic market inefficiency. The ideas with the highest expected value are the least competed-for, not because people rationally assess them as bad, but because subconscious psychological filters screen them out before conscious evaluation even begins. The practical implication is that feeling intimidated by an idea is actually a positive signal, not a negative one. Founders should actively seek out ideas that make them uncomfortable rather than gravitating toward ideas that feel safe and achievable.
