Graham draws an analogy between programming language type systems and career systems: America's 'dynamic typing' for careers — where people often don't choose their specialty until after college — produces more startups than Europe's 'static typing' where career tracks are locked in during high school. The mechanism is that startup founding is inherently unplannable; no high schooler chooses it, and when forced to choose early, people pick conservative, well-understood occupations like doctor or lawyer.

American PhD programs illustrate this: most CS grad students are there to learn more, not committed to research careers, so they feel free to start companies. Even America's notoriously bad public schools may have a hidden advantage — they're so inadequate that students adopt an 'open mind by default' attitude, waiting for college rather than committing early. Graham argues he'd take the US system (bad high schools, great universities) over Europe's (good high schools, mediocre universities) every time, because it's better to make everyone feel like a late bloomer than a failed child prodigy.
