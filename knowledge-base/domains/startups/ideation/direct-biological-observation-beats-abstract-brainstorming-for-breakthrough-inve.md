Dr. Elliot Hawkes didn't invent the vine robot through systematic R&D or by iterating on existing soft robots. He watched an actual vine in his office slowly grow around a shelf edge toward sunlight over the course of a year, and that single observation became the core mechanism — tip-based growth that navigates obstacles by growing around them rather than pushing through them.

This pattern repeats across breakthrough inventions: Velcro from burrs, bullet trains from kingfisher beaks, self-cleaning surfaces from lotus leaves. The key differentiator is specificity — not 'nature is inspiring' as a vague philosophy, but observing a specific organism solving a specific problem and asking whether the mechanism transfers. The vine robot's elegance (a folded tube that everts under air pressure) maps directly to how vines extend from their growing tip. For founders and inventors, this suggests that time spent carefully observing biological systems solving your problem domain may yield more novel solutions than time spent ideating in a conference room.
