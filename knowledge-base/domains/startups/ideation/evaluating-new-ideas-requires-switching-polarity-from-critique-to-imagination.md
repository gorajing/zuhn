Most people evaluate new ideas the same way they evaluate finished products: by looking for flaws. This is the wrong frame entirely for early-stage work. Graham argues the correct approach is a complete polarity switch — not merely being less critical, but actively trying to think of ways the idea could work. This is what experienced investors and YC partners train themselves to do, because in a power-law world, the cost of missing one outlier outweighs the cost of being wrong about many mediocre ones.

Silicon Valley's cultural advantage isn't lower standards but this practiced polarity inversion. The skill is trainable and, because it's extremely lucrative, tends to spread. For anyone evaluating ideas — whether as an investor, manager, or collaborator — the practical takeaway is to catch yourself listing objections and consciously flip to imagining paths to success.
