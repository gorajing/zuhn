Graham argues that the space of startup ideas has been so thoroughly picked over that a startup generally has to work on something everyone else has overlooked. But this doesn't happen through conscious effort. Usually successful startups happen because the founders are sufficiently different from other people that ideas few others can see seem obvious to them. The innovation is unconscious at the moment of founding.

The Apple and Google examples illustrate this: Wozniak wanted his own computer (unusual in 1975), and Page/Brin could tell search engines weren't good enough (invisible to non-experts). In both cases, by the time the problem became obvious to everyone, the founders were already entrenched. The pattern is that technology changes rapidly enough that formerly bad ideas become good without anyone noticing — except founders positioned at the intersection of technical expertise and the problem domain.
