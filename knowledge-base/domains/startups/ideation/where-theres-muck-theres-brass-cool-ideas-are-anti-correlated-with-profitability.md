Graham cites the Yorkshire saying 'where there's muck, there's brass'—unpleasant work pays well precisely because few people want to do it. The inverse is equally true: work people enjoy (like building programming languages or putting art galleries online) pays poorly because oversupply of willing workers competes away the returns.

For startup founders, this means the 'coolness' of an idea is a negative signal for profitability. Graham argues you should aim to make money and maybe be cool, never to be cool and maybe make money. Making money is hard enough that it won't happen as a side effect of pursuing what's fun. This reframes idea evaluation: if an idea sounds exciting to build, that's a reason to scrutinize its economics more carefully, not less.
