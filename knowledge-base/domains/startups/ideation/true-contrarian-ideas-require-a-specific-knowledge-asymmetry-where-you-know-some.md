Hoffman distinguishes between being contrarian (easy) and being contrarian and right (hard). The test is whether a smart, informed person — not someone ignorant or crazy — would disagree with you. If no intelligent person would push back on your idea, it isn't actually contrarian. The critical follow-up question is: what specific thing do I know that the critics don't?

With LinkedIn, two-thirds of Hoffman's smart network said it would fail because network products have no value until they reach 500K-1M users. His specific knowledge asymmetry was understanding that people would join for aspirational reasons ('I believe in this vision' or 'I'm willing to play with it') before the network delivered its core value proposition. This wasn't about being smarter than the critics — it was about having a specific insight into user adoption psychology that they hadn't considered. The framework turns contrarian thinking from identity ('I'm a contrarian thinker') into evidence ('here's what I know that they don't').
