When selling Pensive's AI grading tool to professors, the founder discovered that feature-based pitches ('we use AI to grade papers') generated mild interest, but life-change narratives generated commitment. The winning pitch was: 'What if you pressed a button before boarding a plane, and by the time you landed, all your grading was done?' That framing converted 10 professors from a landing page alone.

This works because early-stage products are incomplete — customers can't evaluate features that barely exist. What they can evaluate is whether the transformed future is desirable enough to bet on. The founder frames this as 'selling the willing spec' — the customer's willingness to adopt is proportional to how vividly they can imagine the changed life, not how impressed they are by current capabilities.

This is especially relevant for AI products where the technology sounds impressive but the workflow change is what actually matters to the buyer.
