The bet is that some of the biggest companies of the next decade won't be software businesses at all — they'll be services companies (insurance carriers, law firms, audit shops) rebuilt from scratch with AI doing most of the work. The defining move is providing the outcome to the customer instead of building a tool the customer operates. This inverts the usual SaaS posture: in services-as-software the human is the interface to the customer and the product exists to let that human scale their work non-linearly. Founders evaluating an AI opportunity should ask whether they're delivering a finished result the customer accepts (and pays for as an outcome) versus shipping a co-pilot whose value depends on the customer's own competence and adoption. The former captures the labor budget directly; the latter competes in the crowded tooling market and leaves the hard last-mile work to the buyer.
