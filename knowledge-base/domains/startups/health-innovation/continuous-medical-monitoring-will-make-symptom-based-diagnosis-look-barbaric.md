Graham uses Bill Clinton's 2004 heart surgery as a striking example: even with the best medical care available, Clinton didn't learn his arteries were 90% blocked until he had symptoms. Graham argues we'll eventually know health metrics like arterial blockage as routinely as we know our weight, and cancer will 'show up on some sort of radar screen immediately.'

The key insight is that the primary obstacles aren't technical but institutional. The medical profession is structured around reactive care — patients present problems, doctors diagnose. Continuous monitoring inverts this model and faces resistance from 'thousands of years of medical tradition,' including legitimate concerns about false positives and incidentalomas. However, Graham argues these concerns reflect current limitations, not fundamental constraints. As scanning becomes routine and interpretation improves, what today causes panic (like discovering a harmless cyst) would be as unremarkable as knowing about a birthmark. This framing — the obstacle is institutional inertia, not technology — applies broadly to healthcare innovation.
