Most longevity companies operate within the existing sick care paradigm: wait for cancer to appear, then treat it. This approach yields diminishing returns because aging is a compounding process — by the time symptoms manifest, cascading damage is already extensive. The essay argues this structural mismatch explains why the longevity space suffers from lack of ambition.

For startup founders and investors, this reframe suggests evaluating longevity companies not by their disease-treatment efficacy but by whether they address root aging mechanisms preemptively. Technologies like cell reprogramming (Altos Labs, Retro Biosciences), stem cell-derived organ replacement (Renewal Bio), and programmable RNA therapeutics represent the 'health care' category that could produce non-linear gains. The sick care vs. health care distinction is a useful mental model for any domain where incremental improvement within the wrong paradigm yields diminishing returns.
