Anatomists developed standardized terms (anterior/posterior, medial/lateral, proximal/distal, superior/inferior) not out of academic pretension but because imprecise language in surgery kills people. A doctor can't tell a surgeon the patient has an 'achey belly' — they need to specify 'posterior to the heart, anterior to the vertebrae, inferior to the collarbone, superior to the stomach' so the surgeon knows exactly where to cut.

This principle transfers directly to any domain where multiple specialists must coordinate on complex systems. In software engineering, architecture decision records and ubiquitous language (from DDD) serve the same function. In startups, the lack of shared vocabulary around metrics, user segments, or product concepts creates the equivalent of 'achey belly' miscommunication — vague enough that everyone thinks they agree while actually meaning different things. Investing in precise shared terminology feels like overhead but is actually load-bearing infrastructure for team coordination at scale.
