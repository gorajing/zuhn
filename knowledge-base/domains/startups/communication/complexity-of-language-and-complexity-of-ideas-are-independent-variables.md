There's a common misconception that sophisticated ideas require sophisticated prose. Graham observes the opposite: specialists discussing abstruse topics with peers use sentence structures no more complex than lunch conversation. They use precise terminology, but only as much as necessary.

The mechanism is twofold. First, experts have less to prove, so they don't need linguistic signaling. Second, the harder the ideas, the less cognitive bandwidth can be wasted on parsing ornate language. Informal language is 'the athletic clothing of ideas'—it stays out of the way and lets the substance do the work.
