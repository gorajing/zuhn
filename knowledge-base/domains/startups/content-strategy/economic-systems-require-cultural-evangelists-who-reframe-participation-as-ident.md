Industrial capitalism required a cultural precondition: people had to believe that risk-taking, investment, and consumption were natural expressions of human identity rather than morally suspect activities. Writers employed by entities like the English East India Company actively publicized the idea that economies were controlled by markets and that humans were naturally rational economic actors. Even language shifted — 'individuals' didn't refer to persons until the 17th century, and 'career' only meant a horse's racing life until the 18th century.

This has direct implications for startup founders building new categories. The product alone is insufficient — you need cultural evangelists who reframe participation in your ecosystem as identity-level behavior. Salesforce didn't just sell CRM; it sold 'the end of software' as an identity. Crypto didn't just offer financial instruments; it sold 'decentralization' as a worldview. The insight from capitalism's origin story is that the most transformative economic shifts are preceded by narrative shifts that make new behaviors feel like expressions of who people already are, not impositions from outside.
