The ACX contest uses a two-stage structure: readers vote to select ~10 finalists, the editor posts one per week through summer, then readers vote for final winners. This isn't accidental — it's a deliberate hybrid that assigns crowd intelligence to the task crowds are good at (broad filtering and surface popularity) while preserving editorial judgment for the moment when taste and depth matter most.

Pure crowd selection suffers from popularity bias — accessible, agreeable content beats challenging or unusual work. Pure editorial selection suffers from filter bubbles and idiosyncratic taste. The hybrid exploits comparative advantage: the crowd handles breadth, the editor handles depth. This pattern recurs in contexts like open-source project governance (community proposes, core team merges), academic publishing (reviewers shortlist, editors decide), and talent competitions. The stage boundary is where power is allocated — so designing it intentionally matters.
