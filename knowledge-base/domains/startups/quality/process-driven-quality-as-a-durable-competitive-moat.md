The 1970s oil crisis forced American automakers to rapidly produce smaller, fuel-efficient cars. But their approach was cost-cutting, which degraded build quality. Toyota, having already developed Total Quality Control (TQC) — a systematic process encompassing staff satisfaction, continuous improvement, and rigorous standards — maintained quality regardless of vehicle size or production pressure. This gap became Toyota's durable moat for decades. The deeper lesson is that quality-as-process is resilient to external shocks while quality-as-outcome (achieved through spending more) collapses under cost pressure. For any company, embedding quality into the process itself rather than treating it as an output to be achieved through resources creates an advantage that compounds over time and survives market disruptions.
