Graham identifies a causal link between believing in quality standards and actually producing quality: Renaissance Florence's artists were intensely competitive, always trying to outdo one another 'like mathematicians or physicists today — maybe like anyone who has ever done anything really well.' The belief wasn't a useful illusion; they were right that greatness was achievable, and believing it enabled the effort required to get there.

The modern art school doctrine that 'the concept of good has been retired' — replaced by 'exploring your own personal vision' — actively prevents ambitious people from pursuing excellence. When the energy that would go into the work instead goes into intellectual-sounding theory ('My work represents an exploration of gender and sexuality in an urban context'), the quality of output drops while the quality of rationalization rises. This pattern applies well beyond art: any field where relativism about quality takes hold loses its ability to produce exceptional work.
