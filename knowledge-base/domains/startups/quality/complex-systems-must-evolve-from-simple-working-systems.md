John Gall's observation that 'a complex system designed from scratch never works and cannot be made to work' is one of the most consistently validated principles in engineering and organizational design. The implication is profound for builders: you must ship a simple version that actually works before layering on complexity. This contradicts the instinct to architect everything upfront.

This applies beyond software — to organizations, products, and even policy. The reason is that complex systems have emergent behaviors that cannot be predicted from their components. Only by starting simple and iterating can you discover and accommodate these emergent properties. Every successful complex system you admire (the internet, biological organisms, thriving cities) grew from something far simpler.
