Graham makes a counter-intuitive competitive argument: at the far end of the performance curve where returns are steepest, there is less competition than expected. This isn't just because exceptional work is hard — it's because the prospect of competing at that level is so intimidating that few people even try. The competition thins out not from failure but from self-selection.

This creates a double bargain. First, incremental effort at the far end yields outsized returns due to the steepness of the curve. Second, fewer people are competing for those returns than the reward would justify. The practical application is the startup principle of 'do things that don't scale' — paying ridiculous attention to tiny initial efforts because the compounding curve is steepest at the far end. The same applies to learning: the initial phase feels hopeless, but making the effort to get a toehold is disproportionately valuable because learning accelerates from there.
