Leonardo painted every individual leaf on the juniper bush behind Ginevra de Benci's head, even though it was just background framing. His effort on any part of a painting did not depend on how closely he expected anyone to examine it. The result: people walking by the portrait are immediately arrested by it before they even read the label. All those unseen details combine to produce something stunning.

The same principle applies to software. Inside good software, parts no one is ever supposed to see are beautiful too. This is not mere aesthetic preference — it's a strategy for emergent quality. Each individually invisible detail contributes to a cumulative impression that users experience as 'it just works.' This is why great makers are relentless like Michael Jordan: the payoff from obsessive craftsmanship is nonlinear and only visible in aggregate.
