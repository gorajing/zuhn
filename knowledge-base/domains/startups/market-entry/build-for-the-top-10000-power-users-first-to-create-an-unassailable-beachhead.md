Graham's search engine example illustrates a counterintuitive market entry strategy: a search engine used only by the top 10,000 hackers would be in a 'very powerful position despite its small size.' This echoes how Google itself started — as the search engine technical users preferred. The mechanism is that expert users are taste-makers; their adoption signals quality to the broader market and creates organic word-of-mouth that money can't buy.

This principle generalizes beyond search. In any category where quality is hard to evaluate, the opinions of sophisticated users serve as a proxy for everyone else. The tactical implication is that early-stage products should be 'excessively' optimized for power users rather than dumbed down for mass appeal. Constraints that serve the expert audience aren't limitations — they're features that create the initial wedge. If you can't get the long-term broad market without first getting the initial core, then anything that gets you the core is the right move, even if it seems narrowing.
