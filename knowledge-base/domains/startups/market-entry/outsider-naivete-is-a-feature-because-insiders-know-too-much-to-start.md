Wand explicitly states: 'There was zero chance I would have started a business knowing what I know now.' He describes a deliberate knowledge acquisition strategy — consulting McKinsey partners, getting agent licenses, attending conferences — but stopping at the point where he had 'enough information to get to conviction and not too much information and know-how that it would scare me.' This isn't ignorance; it's calibrated naivete.

This maps to a broader pattern: Uber wouldn't have started from within the taxi association, Airbnb wouldn't have started from hotel management. Deep industry expertise wires your thinking to existing constraints. Fresh outsiders see the customer problem without the legacy mental model of 'this is how everybody else is doing it.' The practical implication for founders: do enough research to validate the opportunity but resist the urge to become a domain expert before committing — the knowledge that makes you an expert is the same knowledge that makes you cautious.
