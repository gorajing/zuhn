Ballmer identifies the specific mechanism of incumbent failure: you get locked into your model. The fear says 'the old franchise is our birthright and permission to enter'; the confidence says 'if we just Windowsize this, it'll work.' Both push you to extend rather than start fresh, even when the new market has different physics (form factor, business model, developer base, monetization).

The honest test: can you describe the new market without referencing your existing product? If you can't — if every plan starts with 'we'll use our existing X to enter Y' — you're probably not building the capability the new market actually needs. Microsoft's phone shouldn't have used a Windows UI, shouldn't have started on Intel, and shouldn't have been licensed to OEMs the way Windows was. They knew this intellectually but couldn't escape the gravity.

The counter-move: consciously focus on the question, get all the wood behind one arrow (don't enter five new things with one team's capacity), and accept that new businesses usually require new hires and new cost structures, not a reassignment of existing muscle. Get in the weight room and build capability ahead of need.
