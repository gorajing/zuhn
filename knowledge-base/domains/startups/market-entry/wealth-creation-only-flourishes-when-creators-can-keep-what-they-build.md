Graham presents a natural experiment spanning centuries: Northern Italy 800 (off, warlords steal it) vs 1100 (on). England 1800 (on) vs 1974 (off, 98% tax on investment income). West Germany (on) vs East Germany (off). In every case, wealth creation appears and disappears based on retention incentives.

The implication isn't just about tax policy — it's a fundamental insight for startup ecosystems. At least 90% of work at even high-tech companies is unglamorous execution (support calls, training, installation). People will write operating systems for free because it's fun, but they won't do the unfun 90% without financial incentive. Founders choosing where to build should evaluate not just current tax rates but the trajectory of wealth-retention policy, as it takes roughly a generation for the effects to fully manifest.
