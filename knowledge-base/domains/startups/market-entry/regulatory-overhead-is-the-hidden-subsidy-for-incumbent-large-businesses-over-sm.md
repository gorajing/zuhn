Large chain businesses like CVS or Walgreens have already budgeted for expensive permits, renovations, and labor regulations. These fixed costs are trivial at scale but devastating for small independents trying to enter the market. This means that regulations ostensibly designed to protect consumers often have the perverse effect of consolidating market power among incumbents.

The Abundance movement's push to reduce regulatory overhead isn't purely deregulatory — it's pro-competitive. By lowering the fixed costs of starting and running a business, it levels the playing field between chains and independents. This framing resolves the apparent tension between 'pro-business' deregulation and 'anti-monopoly' competition advocacy — they're actually complementary goals when the regulations in question function as barriers to entry.
