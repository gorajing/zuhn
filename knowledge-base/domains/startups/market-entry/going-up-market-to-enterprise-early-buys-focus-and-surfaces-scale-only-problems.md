Coval focused on enterprise from the very beginning, even at the cost of smaller startups and wider adoption. The payoff was depth: getting close enough to understand customer pain well enough to build a solution that scales to tens of millions of calls. Hopkins values that enterprises have longer-term roadmaps and visions, which enables genuinely collaborative work, and she argues moving up-market is itself a strong product signal. Critically, Coval's core problem — coordinating hundreds of engineers working on one voice system, with 100+ people from a single Fortune 500 in the platform — is a problem you only hit at scale, so enterprise was where the real product lived.

The explicit counterweight: you don't want only enterprise customers, because then you stop learning from the fastest-moving AI companies. Her rule is to move up-market for focus but not too fast, and to retain frontier customers as a learning channel. This is a barbell, not a single bet.

Underneath the GTM choice is founder-market fit: she spent her career at Waymo building tools so hundreds of engineers could collaborate on scaling an AI system, so the enterprise-coordination problem matched her exact strengths. The transferable lesson is to choose your customer segment partly by where your own background gives you unfair advantage — and to treat the segment decision as a portfolio (enterprise depth plus frontier learning) rather than an either/or.
