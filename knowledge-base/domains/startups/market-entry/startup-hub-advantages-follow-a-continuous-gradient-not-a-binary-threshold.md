Paul Graham argues that if you accept a startup in a small agricultural town would benefit from moving to a city, the same logic extends all the way up the chain. The argument against moving from London to Silicon Valley is structurally identical to the argument against moving from a small town to London — both deny a gradient that nearly everyone acknowledges exists.

This matters because founders often rationalize staying in a second-tier hub by pointing to its absolute strengths rather than its relative position. The gradient framing forces an honest comparison: if Boston startups measurably benefit from being in Silicon Valley, then every other city's startups benefit even more. The decision becomes one of weighing relocation costs against gradient advantages, not debating whether the advantage exists at all.
