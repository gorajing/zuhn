The traditional startup playbook of building in one market and expanding globally is hitting tariff walls. European buyers are increasingly favoring regional vendors as U.S. imports become more expensive, with 'buy local' sentiment rising measurably. A European mobility startup reported having to delay U.S. expansion entirely because tariffs destroyed their margin structure overnight.

This forces a strategic reset: instead of global expansion, founders should consider distributed operations that sidestep tariff friction—such as final assembly or compliance operations in target markets. Non-U.S. startups may benefit from establishing U.S. operations, and vice versa. The key insight is that market entry timing and sequencing now must account for trade policy as a first-order constraint, not an afterthought. Startups that build regional strength first and expand carefully will outperform those that spread thin across tariff-exposed markets.
