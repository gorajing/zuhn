Pre-automobile towns in the Cumberland Valley naturally spaced at exactly 10-mile intervals — the maximum round-trip walking distance that allowed buying and selling in a day. No planning authority dictated this; the physical constraint of human walking speed created emergent, near-optimal spacing. This pattern applies to any market where a hard constraint caps how far users will go: delivery radius for restaurants, commute time for offices, latency for cloud regions. The implication for startups is that in constrained markets, there's a calculable maximum number of viable competitors per geography, and entering a market where the spacing is already saturated is structurally disadvantaged regardless of product quality.
