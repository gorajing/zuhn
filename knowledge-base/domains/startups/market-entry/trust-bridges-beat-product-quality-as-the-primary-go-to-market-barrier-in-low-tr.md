In Latin America and similar low-trust markets, consumers don't casually download apps and try them — they first ask whether the company and person behind it are trustable. This is fundamentally different from Silicon Valley where the barrier to trial is near zero. Founders must identify the specific trust symbols their target population respects (trusted companies, community leaders, local endorsements) and build 'trust bridges' by associating with those symbols.

The practical implication is that your first customer isn't just a revenue source — they're social proof infrastructure. That first client proves to the rest of the market that you won't run off with their money. This is why outsiders struggle to win these markets: they don't understand the local cultural trust intricacies, including which specific words and phrases trigger trust or distrust. Sales cycles are longer because trust must be built through multiple meetings, contacts, and recommendations before a 'yes' emerges — and a first 'no' may actually be a 'not yet.'
