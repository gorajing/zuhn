Modern startup orthodoxy prescribes finding a beachhead market, dominating it, then expanding. eDreams did the opposite: launching in Spain, Italy, France, and UK simultaneously. When the dot-com bubble burst, they strategically contracted to Spain and Italy to prove profitability, then re-expanded with confidence to 40+ countries.

This 'launch broad, contract to proof, then re-expand' pattern offers an underappreciated alternative. The initial broad launch generated market intelligence across multiple geographies that a single-market approach would have missed. The forced contraction during crisis became a feature, not a bug — it pressure-tested which markets had genuine product-market fit. This approach requires more initial capital but produces a more battle-tested expansion playbook than gradual geographic creep.
