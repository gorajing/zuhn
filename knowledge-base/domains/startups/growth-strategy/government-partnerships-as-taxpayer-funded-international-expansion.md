During WWII, Coca-Cola positioned itself as essential to soldier morale, promising every serviceman a 5-cent Coke 'wherever he is.' While losing money per unit, this created enormous goodwill and — critically — justified government funding for 64 overseas bottling plants. The technicians installing Coke plants were given military-equivalent priority status.

This pattern of crisis-aligned expansion is replicable: Coke secured sugar rationing exemptions by lobbying (even placing a Coke executive on the rationing board), used taxpayer money for infrastructure, and turned soldiers into brand ambassadors who introduced Coke to local populations worldwide. The strategy compressed what would have been decades of organic international expansion into just a few years. Today, only Cuba and North Korea don't sell Coke — a direct result of this wartime expansion playbook.
