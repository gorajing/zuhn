In 1999, Google signed Netscape to power its organic search results and insisted on a 'Powered by Google' tag at the bottom of every results page. The Netscape deal brought 3 million searches per day and came close to tipping over Google's infrastructure. By June 2000, the Yahoo deal doubled daily searches to 14 million and paid Google $7.2M in 2001 — enough to bridge the dot-com winter when venture capital was unavailable.

But the real value wasn't the revenue. Every Yahoo and Netscape user saw the Powered by Google logo and learned to associate Google with fast, relevant search. Within two years, Google had flipped the dynamic entirely: portals wanted the Google brand on their pages as a value proposition to their own users. It became the Intel Inside of search — an ingredient brand that incumbents voluntarily advertised for free.

For modern founders: when you have superior technology but no direct distribution, structure B2B partnerships to include consumer-visible attribution, even if you have to give the deal away to get it. The partner gets your tech; you get their user base's brand impressions for free. The revenue is the pretext; the distribution is the prize. This is why API companies insist on 'Powered by' footers and why Stripe Checkout shows its logo on every payment page.
