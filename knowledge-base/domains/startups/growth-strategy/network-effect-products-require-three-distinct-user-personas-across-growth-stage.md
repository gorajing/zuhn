Waze's growth unfolded across three stages with incompatible user needs. Stage 1 targeted early adopters who wanted broken, janky functionality they could help fix — showing GPS signal strength and raw map-editing tools. Stage 2 needed passive drivers for GPS data collection, incentivized through gamification. Stage 3 served mass-market users who wanted a polished app and never knew a community built it — requiring Waze to remove beloved power-user features.

The counterintuitive lesson is that over-polishing your product for early adopters is actually harmful. A 'super smooth, clean, beautiful product that just doesn't work' disappoints early adopters who want to find bugs and see their impact. Conversely, keeping raw functionality for mass-market users alienates them. Each stage demands you kill what made the previous stage work. This framework applies to any network-effect business where initial contributors create value that later users passively consume.
