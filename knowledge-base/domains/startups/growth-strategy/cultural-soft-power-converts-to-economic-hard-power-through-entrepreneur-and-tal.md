The global 'weeb' phenomenon demonstrates how organic cultural appeal translates into economic potential. 42% of American Gen Z watches anime weekly (vs 25% for NFL), Japanese food has become the most sought-after cuisine in major US cities, and Japan tops international brand perception rankings. This cultural magnetism is already driving concrete economic outcomes: Sakana AI's founders (Llion Jones, David Ha) moved to Japan partly because they loved the country; TSMC workers' desire to live in Japan may have influenced Kumamoto fab placement; and American tech workers increasingly discuss relocating to Japan.

The mechanism works through three channels: entrepreneurs drawn by lifestyle + opportunity (the 'Weeb Dream'), workers willing to accept competitive salaries for quality-of-life benefits, and financiers who follow both. Immigrants are almost twice as likely to start businesses as native-born Americans, and 55% of US billion-dollar startups had at least one immigrant founder. Japan's cultural appeal could tap this same entrepreneurial energy. The critical insight is that cultural soft power isn't just a tourism asset — it's a talent pipeline that can be deliberately converted into startup formation, technology transfer, and export capacity.
