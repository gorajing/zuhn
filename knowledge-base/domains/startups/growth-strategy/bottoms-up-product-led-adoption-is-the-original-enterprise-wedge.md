In the early 1990s, Microsoft realized it needed to sell to the enterprise for the first time and had no idea how. CIOs and IT departments actively hated PCs because employees would bring them in and create chaos. Microsoft had no enterprise sales force, no relationships, no executive briefing center, no channel partners. But they had something more powerful: thousands of employees inside every Fortune 500 company were already using Excel and Word for their work, often having bought retail copies themselves. Steve Ballmer's enterprise sales motion didn't start cold — it started with 'hey, thousands of people in your organization are already using Excel; let's have a conversation about how we can make that work better.'

The broader principle is that product-led growth, hyped in the late 2010s as a slack/Atlassian/Trello innovation, is actually the oldest enterprise sales pattern in software. Get individual employees to love your product first, let it spread informally inside organizations, then convert that grassroots usage into a top-down purchasing conversation with IT. The wedge isn't your sales pitch — it's the awkward fact that the company is already running on your product whether they have a contract or not.

The operational implication today: when you're an early-stage company trying to sell to large enterprises, you usually don't have the relationships or enterprise muscle to win head-on. But if you can make individuals love your product enough to drag it into work, you create the leverage point that turns into enterprise deals later. Microsoft's mistake in this period was that it took them years to realize this was their wedge — they wasted time trying to figure out enterprise sales from scratch when the answer was sitting in every employee's hands.
