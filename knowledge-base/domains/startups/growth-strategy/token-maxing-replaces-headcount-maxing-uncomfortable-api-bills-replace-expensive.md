Hu's structural shift: in pre-AI companies, headcount was the dominant capital expense and limit on speed. In AI-native companies, tokens replace headcount at 5-10x cost reduction. The trade-off: be willing to run an uncomfortably high API bill because it's replacing what would have taken a far more expensive and inflated headcount. One person with AI tools is what used to take a large engineering team.

The operational implication: the company KPI shifts from 'headcount' or 'revenue per employee' to 'tokens per outcome.' The best AI-native companies will be the ones token-maxing — using as much inference as possible to compress timelines and reach value-creating outcomes faster. Founders who feel guilt about API spend are still anchored on pre-AI economics; the right anchor is opportunity-cost-of-the-headcount-the-tokens-replaced.

For builders: this is operationally actionable today. Look at every workflow where you're hesitant about agent token cost; estimate the human-equivalent time and cost; in nearly all cases, agents win. For Zuhn editorially: this is consistent with Howie Liu's 'anchor agent pricing on human-equivalent cost' — same principle from the buyer side. Companies that internalize this faster will have lower per-outcome cost than competitors who treat AI tokens as discretionary spending.
