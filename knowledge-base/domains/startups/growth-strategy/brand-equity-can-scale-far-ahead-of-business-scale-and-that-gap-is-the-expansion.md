At 4-6 stores Starbucks was already receiving mail orders from across the US because tourists visiting the Pike Place Market were shipping beans back to every major city. Schultz noticed 'the equity of the brand was much larger than the size of the business' and treated that mismatch as permission — and pressure — to expand well beyond Portland Oregon (which was the founders' actual plan).

The pattern generalized: Starbucks became iconic in Tokyo before opening there (people lined up around the block on day one because 'anticipation of something unique' preceded supply), appeared unprompted in You've Got Mail, and developed a LA celebrity halo without any paid marketing. Each time, latent brand pull was the signal that the market was already ready.

Operationally this means: track the ratio between mindshare signals (unprompted mentions, inbound requests from new geographies, tourist behavior, media references) and actual footprint. When mindshare runs 10x+ ahead of footprint, the constraint on the business is your execution speed, not demand discovery. Founders who fail to notice this gap or who wait for 'proof' in each new market are being outrun by a market that is already there.
