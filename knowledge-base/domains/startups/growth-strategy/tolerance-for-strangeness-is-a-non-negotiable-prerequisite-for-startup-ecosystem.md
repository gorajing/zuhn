Graham makes the point that startups are inherently strange — they pursue ideas that most people think are wrong or pointless. A city that only tolerates conventional behavior will filter out the exact people who start transformative companies. Crucially, you can't be selectively tolerant, accepting tech weirdness while rejecting cultural weirdness, because they stem from the same disposition toward nonconformity.

This immediately disqualifies large portions of the US as potential startup hubs and explains why socially liberal cities dominate the startup landscape. Graham notes Pittsburgh's historical tolerance — born from waves of immigrants from twenty different countries — as a cultural asset that mirrors Silicon Valley's pragmatic tolerance. The lesson for any aspiring startup city is that cultural liberalism isn't a nice-to-have progressive value; it's a functional prerequisite for an innovation economy.
