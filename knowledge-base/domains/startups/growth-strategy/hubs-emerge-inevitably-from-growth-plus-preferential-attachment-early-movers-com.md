Barabasi and Albert showed that hubs (super-connectors like O'Hare airport, Google, or ATP in cellular metabolism) aren't designed -- they emerge inevitably from two simple rules: (1) networks grow by adding nodes over time, and (2) new nodes are slightly more likely to connect to already-connected nodes (preferential attachment).

The simulation showed that 'all it takes is a tiny bias when growing a network and hubs end up being inevitable.' O'Hare became the most connected US airport not through central planning but through a feedback loop: more airlines attracted more passengers, which attracted more airlines. For startups, this means early connection advantages compound dramatically. Getting to hub status early in a growing network (marketplace, platform, content ecosystem) creates self-reinforcing growth that becomes nearly impossible to displace.
