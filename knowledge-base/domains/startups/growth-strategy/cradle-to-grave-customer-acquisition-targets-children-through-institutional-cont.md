Coca-Cola's internal 'Cradle to grave' strategy explicitly aimed to make children into lifetime customers. They paid schools approximately $1 per student annually for exclusive vending contracts, with bonuses for higher sales volumes. Schools, often underfunded, actively promoted Coke products to students. A Coke marketing chief stated it's 'far easier to get them at a young vulnerable age' than convert adults later.

Beyond school contracts, Coke used collector's items (Barbie dolls, board games, toy trucks) marketed as 'adult collectibles,' Christmas ads tying Santa to Coke in children's minds, and product placement in family films to circumvent advertising-to-children regulations. Research showed Coke was one of the most recognized brands even among babies. This institutional distribution approach — embedding the product in environments where children have no alternative — is a pattern seen across industries from tech (schools adopting specific platforms) to food service.
