Chamath identifies a historical pattern: Apple and Microsoft were born in the 1970s (high rates + microprocessor + software OS), while Google and Amazon emerged in the 1990s (post-dotcom discipline + internet + e-commerce infrastructure). Each cohort had the 'trifecta' of simultaneous advances in bits (software platforms) and atoms (physical infrastructure) intersecting with the austerity of expensive capital.

The thesis is that cheap money periods produce many startups but few enduring platforms, while expensive money periods with genuine technological breakthroughs produce fewer but far more consequential companies. The 2020s may qualify: AI/compute advances (bits) coincide with energy transition and life sciences (atoms) during post-ZIRP rate normalization. The austerity forces founders to build efficient, profitable businesses from the start — and those that survive this filter with genuine platform innovation become generational winners.
