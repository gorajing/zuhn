Levie identifies a repeatable pattern for spotting enterprise startup opportunities: monitor when underlying enabling technologies (cost of computing, storage costs, network speeds, new form factors like tablets/phones) change dramatically while existing practices remain stuck in the old paradigm. The opportunity exists at the maximum delta between what's now possible and how things currently work.

He illustrates this with Box's founding: in 2004, storage was getting exponentially cheaper, browsers were becoming capable (Firefox), and internet speeds were increasing—yet sharing files remained absurdly difficult and expensive in corporate environments. PlanGrid found the same pattern: iPads created a perfect form factor for construction blueprints, yet the industry was spending $4 billion/year printing them. Levie also notes that most 'new' technologies are actually ideas that were tried 10-30 years ago but failed because enabling technologies weren't ready. Reading old business articles about technology reveals these recurring patterns. The practical implication: scan for industries where technology costs have dropped but workflows haven't updated, especially in enterprise sectors with decades of infrastructure investment that must eventually migrate.
