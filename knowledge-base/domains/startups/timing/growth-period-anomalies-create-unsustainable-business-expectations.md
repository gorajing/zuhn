From 2007 to roughly 2016, smartphones improved so rapidly that each generation offered genuinely transformative capabilities—better cameras, faster processors, new form factors. This created a historically unusual pattern of 1-2 year upgrade cycles that companies treated as the permanent baseline. As technology matured, average upgrade cycles stretched to 3 years in 2018 and 4 years shortly after. Companies that built their financial models around growth-era behavior (high unit sales, frequent upgrades) faced existential crises when the market normalized. Apple lost $450 billion in market cap in months when this reality became apparent. The lesson generalizes: any business enjoying rapid growth should distinguish between structural advantages and market-timing tailwinds, and plan for the inevitable maturation where upgrade rates normalize to economically and environmentally sustainable levels.
