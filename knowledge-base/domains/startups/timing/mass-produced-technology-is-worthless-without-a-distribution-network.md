Gutenberg invented how to make 300 copies of a book for the price of one, then printed 300 Bibles in a town with seven legal readers of the Bible. He went bankrupt. The bank that seized his press went bankrupt. His apprentices went bankrupt and fled to Venice — and Venice worked because it was the airport hub of the Mediterranean, so you could hand ten Bibles to each of thirty ships' captains and they would physically distribute them. Printing didn't become economically sustainable until roughly 1490, forty years after the press was invented.

Palmer's parallel: e-books technically existed from the 1970s when someone first typed a book into a computer, but there was no e-book market until the Kindle created a commodity way to buy and sell them. The commodity is several decades younger than the technology. The implication for founders: if you ship a mass-produced good into a world that doesn't have the hub, the fair, the marketplace, or the payment rail to absorb it, you will die of inventory even though your tech works. The move is either to build in a hub city (Venice, App Store, AWS) or to delay until someone else has built the distribution you can ride on.
