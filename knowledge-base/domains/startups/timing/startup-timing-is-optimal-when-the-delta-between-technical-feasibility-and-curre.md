Levie describes a pattern-matching framework for startup timing: track the raw enabling technologies (cost of computing, storage, bandwidth, device form factors) and compare them against how industries currently operate. When the gap between what's technically possible and what's actually being done is at its widest, that's the window for a startup.

Box was founded when storage costs were plummeting and browsers were improving, yet enterprises were still sharing files through physical copying and expensive proprietary systems. PlanGrid found the same pattern in construction: iPads existed with enough screen resolution and processing power to display blueprints, yet the construction industry was spending $4 billion annually printing them. The framework is retrospective but actionable: you can literally read old business articles from 10-20 years ago to find ideas that failed due to technical or economic constraints, then ask whether those constraints have since been removed. Many successful companies are 'repeating technologies that were tried 10, 20, 30 years ago' but with newly feasible economics.
