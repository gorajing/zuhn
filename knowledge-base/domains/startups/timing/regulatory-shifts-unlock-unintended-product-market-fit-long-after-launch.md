The Boeing 757 was designed in 1983 for domestic short routes as a 727 replacement. It sold adequately but never spectacularly, and Boeing discontinued it in 2004. Then in 2007, after regulations changed to allow twin-engine aircraft over oceans, airlines discovered the 757 was perfect for transatlantic service between smaller markets — filling seats reliably where larger planes couldn't.

This pattern generalizes beyond aviation: a product's most valuable use case may not exist at launch because the enabling regulatory, technological, or market conditions haven't materialized yet. Discontinuing a product based on current demand alone ignores optionality from future environmental shifts. For startups, this suggests monitoring regulatory pipelines as a source of latent demand for existing capabilities, and being cautious about sunsetting products that could find new life under changed conditions.
