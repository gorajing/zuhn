Kinoshita first demonstrated x-ray lithography in 1985, but was laughed off stage. Andy Hawryluk at Lawrence Livermore developed multilayer mirrors for nuclear fusion research and was similarly ridiculed when he proposed applying them to chip-making. The technology didn't become commercially viable until ASML combined: (1) multilayer mirror research from nuclear weapons labs, (2) laser-produced plasma from a completely different field, (3) tin droplet generation techniques, and (4) precision optics from Zeiss.

This pattern — where a breakthrough requires technologies that mature independently in separate domains — is a recurring feature of transformative innovations. The lesson for founders and investors: when you see an idea that requires multiple enabling technologies to converge, the timing question isn't 'is this possible?' but 'have all the prerequisite technologies matured sufficiently?' The pioneers (Kinoshita, Hawryluk) were right about the concept but too early for the convergence. The winner (ASML) was the one positioned to integrate all the pieces when they finally matured.
