When Qualcomm filed its CDMA patent in 1986, doing real-time encoding, frequency management, and decoding on a battery-powered handheld was flatly impossible. Everyone in the industry dismissed it as sci-fi. The Qualcomm team's insight was that 'most people didn't know about Moore's Law at the time' — they modeled forward two or three generations of transistor density and bet the company on a product that would become feasible right as they shipped it.

This same pattern appears across most transformative hardware companies: success came from correctly forecasting where Moore's Law (or an analogous compute/cost curve) would be at the time of shipping, not where it was at the time of design. If you only build for today's compute envelope, a well-capitalized incumbent will catch you. If you build for a compute envelope two generations out, you can ship a product that looks magical on launch day and is defensible because the design assumptions were made before competitors believed they were possible.

The inverse warning: many ambitious hardware bets fail not because the underlying physics are wrong but because founders mis-time the cost curve by a single generation. Forecasting the shipping-date cost structure — not today's — is the operative skill.
