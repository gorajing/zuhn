Harold Haas demonstrated Li-Fi transmitting HD video through a desk lamp in a 2011 TED talk. By 2015, Estonian office trials showed 1 Gbps real-world speeds. Yet over a decade later, Li-Fi remains a niche technology without mass consumer adoption. This isn't a failure — it's the normal cadence for technologies that require changes to physical infrastructure, manufacturing tooling, device hardware, and standards bodies.

This pattern repeats across hardware innovations: 5G was demonstrated years before commercial rollout, autonomous vehicles have been 'two years away' for a decade, and solid-state batteries have been in labs since the 2000s. Software founders accustomed to rapid iteration cycles systematically underestimate hardware deployment timelines. When evaluating or investing in physical-layer technologies, the right mental model isn't software's 18-month cycle — it's a 10-15 year horizon from compelling demo to mass-market product, with most value captured by patient capital that enters after technical risk is retired but before commercial scaling begins.
