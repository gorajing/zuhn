The 2008 bailout — where taxpayers funded $700B to rescue the banks whose recklessness caused the crisis, while executives kept billions in bonuses and faced no criminal consequences — generated 'complete outrage' and 'the beginning of mass distrust in institutions and governments.' The immediate products of this distrust were the Occupy Wall Street movement and Bitcoin's invention in 2009.

This pattern is instructive for founders and investors: moments of institutional failure create massive greenfield markets for trust-replacing technologies. The key insight is that the technology doesn't need to be better than what it replaces on every dimension — it just needs to solve the trust problem that the crisis exposed. Bitcoin was slower and harder to use than traditional banking, but it offered something banks couldn't: mathematical certainty without requiring trust in human institutions. For anyone timing market entry, watching for institutional betrayal events is a leading indicator of where the next wave of disruptive demand will emerge.
