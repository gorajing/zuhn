Byju's rose to $22B during the pandemic when edtech demand exploded, then crashed to under $1B when in-person learning resumed. The founder Ravindran went from Forbes-featured billionaire to zero net worth. He didn't lose his industry knowledge or teaching expertise — the market timing that elevated him simply reversed.

This is perhaps the most uncomfortable counterweight to founder-worship: you can be the right person, with the right product, in the right market, and still lose because the timing window closes. Dr. Waman's success similarly depended on a chain of lucky breaks — a mentor's 100 rupees, 50 interview rejections leading him to Mumbai, and a disruption opportunity in thyroid. Skill put him in position; luck determined the outcome. The implication: build optionality rather than betting everything on one timing window.
