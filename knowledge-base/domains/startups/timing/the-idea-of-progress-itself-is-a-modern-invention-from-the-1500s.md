Through most of human history, societies viewed history as cyclical or declining from a golden age. The ancients were seen as intellectual giants who could never be surpassed. It took the voyages of discovery and thinkers like Francis Bacon in the late 1500s to argue that new knowledge and invention could exceed ancient achievements.

Bacon's vision took 200 years to fully materialize in the Industrial Revolution. This historical perspective is crucial: the optimistic, progress-oriented worldview that feels like common sense today is actually a fragile cultural achievement that can be lost. The 20th century anti-progress backlash shows how quickly societies can revert to fatalism.
