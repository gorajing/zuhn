Roman state religion offered transactional gods and no afterlife — a value proposition that works when the empire delivers prosperity. As Rome declined and life worsened for ordinary people, the implicit promise broke: worship the gods, and things go well. Christianity offered an alternative value proposition (eternal afterlife, justice for the meek) that specifically addressed what the incumbent couldn't deliver.

This pattern recurs in technology adoption: incumbents are most vulnerable not when challengers are technically superior, but when the incumbent's core promise breaks. Mainframe computing was fine until distributed teams needed flexibility it couldn't provide. Traditional media was fine until it couldn't deliver personalization. The timing insight is that you don't need to be better at everything — you need to be better at the specific thing the incumbent just failed at.
