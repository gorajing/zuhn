Graham argues that fields have natural rhythms of golden ages and decline that are beyond individual control. You're far more likely to do great work in a field on the way up. But golden ages are never labeled in real time — they feel like 'just' smart people working hard on interesting problems and getting results. Their participants take the conditions for granted.

The practical implication is a single principle that both steers you away from brand-age work and finds golden ages automatically: follow the problems. If you're smart, ambitious, and honest with yourself, your taste in problems is the best guide available. Go where interesting problems are and you'll find other talented people have arrived there too. This reframes career strategy from trend-chasing to taste-following — and suggests that the moment a field stops feeling like genuinely interesting problem-solving and starts feeling like brand management, it's time to move on.
