Photosynthesis is explicitly described as 'complicated, inefficient and confusing' — yet no alternative energy-capture system has displaced it in 450 million years. The process involves four protein complexes, multiple electron carriers, a two-stage reaction system, and an enzyme (RuBisCo) that fails half the time. A rational designer would never build it this way. But it was first, it worked, and everything else evolved to depend on it.

In markets, this manifests as the QWERTY effect at biological scale. TCP/IP, JavaScript, the SWIFT banking network — all have known inefficiencies, yet they persist because the ecosystem built around them creates switching costs that exceed any individual actor's ability or incentive to change. The critical insight is that the threshold for 'good enough to create lock-in' is far lower than most builders assume.

For founders, this reframes the perfection-versus-shipping debate. The question isn't whether your solution is optimal — it's whether it works well enough that users will build dependencies on it before alternatives arrive. Photosynthesis won not by being good, but by being first and functional while everything else adapted to its outputs.
