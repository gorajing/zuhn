Costco makes 75% of its money from membership fees, not product markups. This creates a structural incentive alignment rare in retail: raising prices would generate pennies today but cost the company $60 per churned member next year. The result is a self-imposed 15% maximum markup (11% average) versus Walmart's 24% and Home Depot's 35%.

This model inverts the typical retail dynamic where companies maximize extraction per transaction. Instead, every operational decision — warehouse layouts, limited SKUs, forklift-to-shelf stocking — serves the singular goal of impressing members enough to renew at 90% annual rates. The membership fee acts as both a revenue source and a commitment device that makes the entire business coherent.
