Standard product and retail design removes friction to maximize conversion. Costco's design philosophy does the opposite: the confusing store layout forces customers to wander through more aisles, increasing basket size. The $60-$120 membership fee selects for an affluent demographic (average household income ~$100k) and pre-commits them to shopping there via sunk-cost psychology. The warehouse aesthetic communicates 'we cut costs everywhere to pass savings to you,' building trust.

Each of these 'obstacles' serves a dual function: it filters the customer base toward high-value segments and shapes behavior once inside. The membership barrier means Costco never has to compete for casual foot traffic — its customers are already committed.

For product designers, the insight is that friction is not inherently bad — it depends on where it is placed and what it communicates. Friction at onboarding can select for better customers. Friction during browsing can increase engagement. The question is whether the friction aligns with or works against the core value proposition.
