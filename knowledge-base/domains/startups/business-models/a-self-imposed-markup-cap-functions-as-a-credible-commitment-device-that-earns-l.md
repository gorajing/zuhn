Costco has a self-imposed rule: no item marked up more than 15% (14% for branded goods), yielding an average 11% markup versus Walmart's 24% or Home Depot's 35%. What makes this powerful is not just the low markup but its public, rule-based nature. Because customers know the cap exists, they don't need to comparison-shop — the constraint IS the trust signal.

This is a commitment device: by publicly tying its own hands, Costco removes the customer's need to verify fairness on every transaction. Compare this to dynamic pricing or 'sale' manipulation common at competitors, where customers rationally assume they're being gamed and behave accordingly (checking prices, waiting for sales, abandoning carts).

For founders: explicit, verifiable self-constraints on pricing or behavior can be more valuable brand assets than marketing claims. The constraint is credible precisely because violating it would be visible and costly to reputation. Companies that try to earn trust through messaging alone are competing against the customer's default skepticism; companies that encode trust into a verifiable rule eliminate the skepticism at the source.
