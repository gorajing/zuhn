Costco's strategy inverts conventional retail wisdom at every turn. The membership fee should repel customers — instead it self-selects affluent households ($100K average income) and triggers sunk-cost-driven loyalty. The warehouse layout should frustrate shoppers — instead it forces wandering that increases basket size and creates a treasure-hunt experience. The sparse selection should send customers to competitors — instead it eliminates decision fatigue and creates supplier leverage.

This pattern — where constraints become features — is a signature of businesses with deep strategic coherence. Each 'disadvantage' reinforces the others: the fee selects customers who value bulk buying, the warehouse format keeps costs low enough to justify the fee, and the limited SKUs ensure each item is high-quality enough to maintain renewal rates. Companies that try to remove friction everywhere end up competing on the same dimensions as everyone else. The most defensible strategies embrace friction that competitors won't tolerate.
