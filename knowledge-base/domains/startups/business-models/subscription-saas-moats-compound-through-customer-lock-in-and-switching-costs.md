Workday (HR/finance), ServiceNow (IT workflows), and Splunk (data analytics) all shared a common business model trait: subscription-based pricing with deep integration into enterprise workflows. Once adopted, these tools became embedded in daily operations, making switching prohibitively expensive and disruptive.

This contrasts with companies like Yelp ($1.2B) and Kayak ($1.8B), which operated in more transactional, consumer-facing markets with lower switching costs. The SaaS companies' recurring revenue provided predictable cash flows, enabling sustained R&D investment and product improvement cycles. The 2012 IPO class illustrates that the best businesses don't just acquire customers — they become infrastructure that customers can't easily remove. This insight applies directly to startup strategy: founders should design products that integrate deeply into customer workflows, creating natural retention through utility rather than just contractual lock-in.
