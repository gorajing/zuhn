Costco earns ~75% of its profit from membership fees, not product markups. This creates a fundamentally different incentive structure: raising prices or degrading quality costs the company $60–$120 per churned member next year, not just a few cents today. The business is only viable if customers renew, so every operational decision is filtered through 'will this impress members enough to renew?'

This contrasts with margin-driven retailers (Walmart, Amazon) where the company profits by extracting margin from each transaction — a model that can tolerate mediocre customer experiences as long as switching costs are high enough. Membership-as-business-model inverts this: the company cannot afford to disappoint because the revenue stream depends on sustained trust, not transaction volume. Founders building subscription businesses should internalize that the product IS the membership, not whatever the membership unlocks.
