By the time Lalit Modi launched the IPL, his BCCI international rights deal had consumed essentially all of India's ~$2-3B advertising budget allocated to cricket — perhaps 5% of the entire national ad market just for the international team. There was literally no remaining cricket-advertiser budget to capture, which seemingly capped the IPL's revenue ceiling before launch.

Modi's breakthrough was realizing the IPL wasn't competing with cricket at all. He explicitly designed it to compete with prime-time soap operas: 8pm matches, three-hour format, every night for two months, Bollywood owners (Shah Rukh Khan, Preity Zinta) in the stands, glamour, fireworks, dance performances. The target wasn't cricket fans — it was Indian women who controlled the household remote and were watching soap operas. This unlocked a completely new advertiser pool: by 2019 a beauty brand was sponsoring a male cricket team's jersey (a world first), and IPL viewership today is 50/50 male/female.

The principle: when you've maxed out the budget pool of your current customer category, don't push harder on existing buyers — redesign the product so it competes with something entirely different, attracting an audience whose advertising/spending budget you weren't previously eligible for. The same content (cricket) became access to a totally different P&L line item (general entertainment ad spend, beauty brand spend) by changing the framing, format, and surrounding spectacle. Founders fighting for incremental share should ask: which adjacent budget pool is my product's true competitor, and how would I have to redesign to be eligible for it?
