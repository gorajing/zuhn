Viaweb charged a flat fee of $300/month for large stores, which seemed reasonable until Frederick's of Hollywood — their highest-traffic user — generated exactly $300/month in bandwidth costs alone, making that single customer a break-even proposition before accounting for any other operational costs.

This illustrates a fundamental flaw in flat-rate pricing for resource-dependent services: usage follows a power-law distribution, and your most successful customers become your most expensive ones. The customer generating the most value from your product (proving product-market fit) is simultaneously the one destroying your unit economics. Modern SaaS companies solve this with usage-based pricing tiers, but the tension remains — pricing that captures value from heavy users often creates friction for the long tail of smaller users who drive adoption and word-of-mouth growth.
