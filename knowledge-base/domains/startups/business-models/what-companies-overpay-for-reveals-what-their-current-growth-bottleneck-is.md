The insight from Graham's essay isn't just historical — it's a diagnostic tool. If you want to understand what a company truly believes is rate-limiting its growth, look at where it consistently pays above-market rates. 1950s manufacturers overpaid for labor. 2000s tech companies overpaid for servers and bandwidth. 2020s AI companies overpay for GPU compute and ML talent.

This means that overpayment isn't irrational — it's a signal of strategic prioritization. A company that haggles aggressively on infrastructure but pays top-of-market for engineering is telling you it believes talent, not infrastructure, is the bottleneck.

For founders, this is a useful self-diagnostic: where are you chronically overpaying or accepting inefficiency? That's probably your real strategic priority, regardless of what your pitch deck says. For investors, tracking where portfolio companies spend lavishly is a more honest read of their strategic model than what leadership says in board meetings.
