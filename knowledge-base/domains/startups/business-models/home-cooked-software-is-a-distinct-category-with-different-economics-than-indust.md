Appleton defines home-cooked software as 'apps you make for the people you know and love' — built for yourself, family, friends, or neighborhood, requiring some skills but not tons, cheap or free to run, controlled by the maker, private, safe, and made with love and care. Examples she cites: a newborn breastfeeding tracker, a personal finance reconciliation tool, a custom diabetic glucose monitor interface. These are drastically different from professional industrial software in every dimension — scale (dozens of users vs millions), profit (none required vs hockey stick), complexity (simple CRUD vs enterprise), trust (intimate vs stranger-built), and values (care vs commercial). Software thinking has concentrated exclusively on industrial software for 30+ years; home-cooked software is a legitimate parallel category that has been structurally ignored because no one could make money from it.
