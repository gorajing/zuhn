Premium pricing rewards measurably more utility — bigger iPhone storage, faster chip. Luxury pricing rewards the opposite: you pay more precisely because the extra cost does not map to extra function. Hermès stretches this further by bundling art directly into products (hand-engraved scarves, theater-set window displays, Robert Dumas's woodblock-print origins) so that part of what you're buying is literally unevaluable. The strategic consequence is escape from comparison shopping. A Birkin is not competing against other $12,000 handbags on stitch-count or leather durability, because the buyer is also purchasing a piece of the Hermès dream — and no competitor can supply that specific dream. This is why luxury brands cultivate connections to fine art without becoming art galleries: they need enough utility to justify purchase, but enough art to exit the value-comparison arena. Any business trying to build pricing power should ask what unevaluable component it can bundle in alongside the functional core.
