When Nike signed Michael Jordan in 1984, Converse was already paying Magic Johnson and Larry Bird roughly $100K/year in flat cash for generic 'Weapons' shoes. Nike's structure — signature shoe, 5% royalty on gross Jordan-line revenue, royalty on incremental Nike basketball sales, stock options, guaranteed ad spend — was brilliant not because it was generous but because Converse literally could not match it. If Converse gave Jordan that deal, Magic and Bird would demand the same and the entire signature-shoe category would collapse Converse's economics. Nike had nothing to lose because it had no stars.

The pattern generalizes: the cleanest counter-positioning plays exploit structural constraints on incumbents, not just strategic inertia. When evaluating a bold move, ask not 'is this a good idea' but 'what would it cost the incumbent to copy this, given their current commitments?' The bigger their installed base of existing relationships, customers, or product lines, the more expensive symmetry becomes for them.
