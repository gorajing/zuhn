The episode makes the case that Microsoft discovered the best business model of all time, and that this required the combination of two distinct properties — neither of which is sufficient alone. The first is software's infinite replicability and zero marginal cost: once you've written it, the next copy costs nothing. Many companies had this property and didn't get rich. The second is being the lynchpin that everyone else in the ecosystem must integrate with: applications had to target DOS, hardware makers had to ship DOS, so Microsoft sat at the unavoidable point of integration. This gave them pricing power.

When these two combine, something extraordinary happens. Pricing power from lynchpin position raises the top line, while zero marginal cost means almost all of that revenue drops to the bottom line. Microsoft used IBM to generate demand for DOS and then used every other PC manufacturer (the clones) to capture the value that demand created. From end of 1982 to mid-1984, revenue more than tripled to $98M at near-100% gross margin. The implication for evaluating any modern business: don't get excited about software economics alone, and don't get excited about a strategic position alone. Look for the rare cases where both exist simultaneously. Most 'platform' companies have one or the other; the generational outcomes come from combining them.

A secondary point: even Bill Gates himself didn't fully appreciate this for years. As late as 1998 he argued that technology companies should be valued at lower multiples than Coke and Gillette because of disruption risk. He was wrong about his own company — when you have both lynchpin power AND software economics, you deserve a premium, not a discount.
