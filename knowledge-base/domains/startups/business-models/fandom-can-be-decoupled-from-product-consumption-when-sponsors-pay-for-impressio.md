Roughly half of F1's US fans never watch a live Grand Prix. They discovered the sport through Netflix's Drive to Survive, follow driver storylines on social media, and can name the constructors' champion — but treat real races as essentially a spoiler for the show they care about. Under Bernie Ecclestone's model, where monetization depended on paywalled broadcast rights and race-fee extraction, this audience would be worthless. Under Liberty's model, where team revenue is ~60% sponsorship, it's highly valuable: sponsor logos accumulate impressions across documentary footage, merchandise, social clips, and hospitality events, entirely independent of live viewership.

The counterintuitive lesson for media and entertainment founders: the traditional 'attention → ads' funnel assumes fans consume the product. Once you have cultural stickiness, sponsors will pay for association regardless of whether fans watch the live event. This makes ancillary content (documentaries, social, behind-the-scenes) not a marketing expense but a revenue primitive — it creates monetizable fan identity separate from game-day audience.

This also reframes what 'engagement' means. Tracking live viewers undercounts brand value when the sponsorship model prices impressions that happen in the cultural halo around the product rather than inside it. For any IP-driven business, ask: could our fans never consume the core thing and still be monetizable? If yes, invest heavily in the halo.
