Bardin observed that 'data is one of the most misused terms in the startup world.' Founders frequently claim data collection as a strategic asset without answering: valuable to whom? Sold how? Could the same value be achieved through manual work or hard-coded rules instead? At Waze, data was valuable only because it fed a real-time traffic product that users directly consumed — not because data existed in quantity.

Waze's data strategy had three deliberate tiers: owned raw map data (built by community contributors), GPS trace data (passively collected from drivers), and human-reported data (police sightings, hazards) that no algorithm could detect. The critical decision was owning all raw data from day one despite it slowing early growth — this created a defensible moat rather than depending on purchased data from fleet management companies whose truck-based GPS traces were poor proxies for consumer driving patterns. The framework for evaluating data strategy: Can you name the specific product or revenue it enables? Could you achieve that value without the data? If not, do you own the data or rent it?
