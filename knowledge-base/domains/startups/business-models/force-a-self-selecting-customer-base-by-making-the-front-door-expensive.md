The most counterintuitive Costco statistic: median household income of a Costco shopper is ~$125K versus Walmart's ~$80K and the US median of $71K. The lowest-priced retailer attracts the wealthiest customers. The mechanism is that paying $60 upfront (and committing to buy nuts in 2.5lb jars) is a tax that selects against cash-strapped shoppers. The ones who pay it anyway tend to have disposable income, garages, and the planning bandwidth to think in months rather than days.

This filter cascades into every other operating metric. Wealthier customers steal less. They spend more per visit. They feel ownership of the membership and want to 'use it,' which the endowment effect amplifies. They're more credit-worthy, which means Costco can negotiate from strength with Visa rather than being squeezed. They tolerate Spartan stores because they're optimizing for value, not status. Each downstream metric is a function of who the upfront fee filters in.

The playbook generalizes: any pricing structure that looks like a barrier (annual contracts, qualification gates, bulk minimums, paid trials) is also a customer-selection mechanism. Most founders treat friction as something to remove. Sometimes friction is the product.
