Counterintuitively, Costco's mandatory membership fee — which should make customer acquisition harder — actually improves customer quality and retention simultaneously. The fee selects for an affluent demographic (average household income ~$100k) and triggers sunk-cost psychology: once paid, members feel compelled to use Costco to 'get their money's worth,' reducing price comparison behavior. The result is 90% annual renewal rates, higher than cheaper digital subscriptions like Apple Music or Hulu.

Business owners (who buy in bulk) constitute one-third of members but two-thirds of sales volume — another selection effect the fee creates. The friction also generates brand perception: paying for access signals quality. This is a useful design principle for products and services: adding a qualifying step or cost at onboarding doesn't just generate revenue, it reshapes who shows up and how committed they are. The right barrier can dramatically improve unit economics without changing the product itself.
