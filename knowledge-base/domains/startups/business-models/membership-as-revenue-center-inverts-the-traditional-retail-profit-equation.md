Traditional retailers use loss leaders to attract foot traffic, then profit from marked-up goods elsewhere in the store. Costco inverts this entirely: its self-imposed 15% maximum markup means products are essentially sold near cost, while the $60-$120 annual membership fee is where real revenue lives. This creates a structural incentive to keep prices relentlessly low — raising prices would generate pennies today but cost the company $60+ per defecting member next year.

This model produces a 90% renewal rate with 94 million members, rivaling Amazon Prime's 100 million despite having only 700 physical locations. The insight generalizes beyond retail: any business that can shift its revenue center from transactions to access fees will find its incentives naturally aligning with customer satisfaction rather than margin extraction. The membership model also pre-selects for committed customers (average household income ~$100k), creating a self-reinforcing flywheel of high-value patrons who spend more per visit.
