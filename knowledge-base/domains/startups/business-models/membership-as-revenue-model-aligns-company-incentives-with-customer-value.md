Most retailers profit from markups on goods, creating an inherent tension: every price increase benefits the company at the customer's expense. Costco inverts this by deriving 75% of its profit from membership fees, with a self-imposed 15% maximum markup on all products. This means raising prices would generate trivial short-term gains while risking the $60-$120 annual renewal that constitutes the actual business.

The structural insight is that revenue-model design determines incentive alignment more reliably than corporate culture or good intentions. When renewal is the primary profit driver, every operational decision—from supplier negotiations to store layout—naturally optimizes for the metric that also benefits customers. This is why Costco's 90% renewal rate coexists with genuinely low prices rather than the loss-leader-then-gouge pattern common in traditional retail.
