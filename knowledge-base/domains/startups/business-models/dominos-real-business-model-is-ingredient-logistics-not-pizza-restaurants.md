Domino's is technically a logistics company disguised as a pizza chain. Individual franchises earn razor-thin 11% margins on pizza sales, and there isn't much cash flow when you're taking 5% of $8. But Domino's corporate generates billions in revenue by selling ingredients at markup to its franchise network. This is the same pattern seen across franchise businesses: the real money is in the supply chain, not the storefront.

This insight reframes how to evaluate franchise businesses. The consumer-facing product (pizza, burgers, coffee) is the distribution mechanism for a B2B supply business. Franchisees increase profits by opening more stores, which strengthens the overall network and increases ingredient purchasing volume. It's a flywheel where the franchisor wins regardless of individual store performance, as long as the network grows. The model explains why Domino's remains one of the cheapest franchises to open -- low barriers to entry maximize network expansion and ingredient sales volume.
