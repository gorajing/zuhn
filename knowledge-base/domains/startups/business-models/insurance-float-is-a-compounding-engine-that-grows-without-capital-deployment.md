The 2025 letter states: 'At year-end, our insurance float — the capital we hold to pay future losses and, in the meantime, invest for Berkshire's benefit — stood at $176 billion. That amount increased from $171 billion at the end of 2024 and from $88 billion at the end of 2015.' Float is the time-value-of-money difference between when premiums are collected and when claims are paid. When underwriting is profitable (combined ratio < 100%, like Berkshire's 2025 87.1%), the insurer gets paid to hold the float — negative effective interest rate capital. Berkshire's float has grown from $88B to $176B in a decade without any new shareholder capital deployed to achieve it — the growth came from premium volume, policy durations, and disciplined underwriting. That's a 2x compound in 10 years (~7% CAGR) on top of whatever returns the investment portfolio generates on the float itself. This is the single most underappreciated mechanism in Berkshire's long-term returns — most investors focus on the equity portfolio, but the float compound has been more consistent and less volatile. Applies to any insurance business evaluation: growing profitable float is worth more than the equivalent earnings growth from operating businesses.
