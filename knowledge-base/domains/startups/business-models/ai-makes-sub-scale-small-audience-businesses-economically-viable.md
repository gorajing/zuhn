The economic shift: historically, building software carried a high fixed cost (a 20-person team, months of work), so it only made sense to build for very large markets — you needed gazillions of users (Salesforce/cloud scale) to amortize the cost. Niche products for small audiences were economically irrational.

AI collapses that fixed cost. When one person can build in a fraction of their spare time what used to take a team, the break-even audience size plummets. Building a useful product for 200 people — previously absurd — now makes economic sense, because the cost to serve that small audience is trivial. This opens the long tail of software: vast numbers of small, specific, previously-uneconomical products become viable.

The implications for founders: (1) markets too small to interest VC-scale companies (or foundation-model labs — connects to the PM-Google 'pick verticals too narrow for big labs' insight) are now addressable by solo builders; (2) the constraint shifts from 'is the market big enough to justify the build' to 'can you reach and serve this niche profitably' — which loops back to the attention/distribution scarcity; (3) spare-time/side-project economics change — a nights-and-weekends product for a few hundred users can be worth building.

The time-sensitive caveat: this depends on AI build costs staying low relative to the value of serving small audiences, which is the current regime. The durable principle: when fixed costs of production collapse, the long tail of previously-uneconomical niches becomes viable — the same dynamic that played out in publishing, video, and commerce now reaches software.
