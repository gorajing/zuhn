Paris market gardeners (maraîchers) in the 1800s produced four times the food of conventional rows, maintained waiting lists of wealthy households, and generated six-figure equivalent incomes from 1-2 acres. The system was never disproven or outperformed at the per-square-foot level.

It was displaced because industrial agriculture optimized for a different metric: output per labor hour rather than output per square foot. Wide rows needed tractors, tractors needed chemical fertilizer, and commodity pricing needed consolidated buyers. Each link in the industrial chain made the French system look 'inefficient' -- but only if your goal was continental-scale production. For individual economic independence, the logic completely inverts. This is a powerful pattern for analyzing disruption: sometimes the 'disrupted' system was actually superior for the end user but incompatible with the business models of intermediaries who profit from dependency.
