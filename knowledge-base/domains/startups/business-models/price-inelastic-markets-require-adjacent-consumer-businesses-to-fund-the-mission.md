Despite SpaceX dramatically cutting launch costs from $400M to $80M, demand barely increased because governments—the primary customers—buy a fixed number of launches regardless of price. This is classic price inelasticity: the decision-makers aren't spending their own money and buy based on need, not price sensitivity. SpaceX's solution was Starlink, a satellite internet constellation targeting billions of consumers—a highly price-elastic market where lower prices drive massive adoption. This pattern generalizes: if your groundbreaking cost reduction doesn't stimulate demand because your market is inelastic, the strategic move is to build an adjacent business in an elastic market that leverages your core capability. The adjacent business funds the mission while the core business serves its original purpose.
