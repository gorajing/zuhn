F1's most underappreciated moat isn't its media reach or fan base — it's that the FIA, the global regulator of most motorsports, explicitly designates F1 as the pinnacle of the field. That designation is embedded in the very name 'Formula One.' Competing series like Le Mans, IndyCar, or rally racing can out-execute F1 on a given dimension (more passing, cheaper entry) and still cannot claim the top rung because a third-party authority has already assigned it.

This is a cornered resource in the 7 Powers sense, but earned through regulatory standing rather than a patent or exclusive contract. Normal capitalist forces are partially suspended: a rival cannot simply raise capital, build a better product, and take the title.

The generalization worth extracting: in any category adjacent to a standards body, accreditor, or dominant regulator, category designation is itself a competitive asset. Being 'the official X of Y' — recognized by the entity that matters — can be worth more than product superiority. Founders in regulated or institutionally governed spaces (finance, medicine, professional sports, academic publishing) should think explicitly about whether the relevant authority has designated a pinnacle, and whether that designation is contestable or fossilized.
