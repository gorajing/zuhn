Jim Sinegal taught Bezos the Costco model over coffee at a Barnes & Noble Starbucks: Costco makes essentially zero operating margin on merchandise and earns all its profit on membership fees. Once customers pay the fee, sunk-cost psychology kicks in—they feel compelled to 'get their money's worth,' which means routing all their shopping through Costco and becoming extraordinarily loyal. The membership also funds the low-prices-guarantee promise that makes the whole flywheel believable.

Prime flipped this slightly: Amazon still runs a profitable retail operation and earns ~$20B+ in Prime subscription revenue on top. But the psychological mechanism is identical. Customers pay $139/year upfront, then mentally consolidate all purchases onto Amazon to justify the fee. Competitors face an impossible challenge: they don't just need to offer lower prices, they need to be compelling enough to break a customer's active commitment device. The float benefit (getting paid before any transaction) is a bonus on top of the loyalty effect.

The broader principle is that voluntary commitment devices are often more powerful than coercive ones. Free shipping as a marketing promise is easy to defect from; free shipping as the reward for a $139 sunk cost is not. Any subscription product that gives users a recurring reason to consolidate behavior onto a single platform compounds over time in a way transactional products cannot.
