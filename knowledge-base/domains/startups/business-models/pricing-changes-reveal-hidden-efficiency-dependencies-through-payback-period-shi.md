The model demonstrates that dropping price from $200 to $150/month — a seemingly reasonable 25% discount — extends CAC payback from 7 to 10 months while acquisition costs remain unchanged. This 43% increase in payback period means the company needs significantly more working capital to fund the same growth, and each customer takes much longer to become profitable.

This insight matters because founders often treat pricing and acquisition as independent levers when they're deeply coupled through payback period. A discount strategy only works if it produces proportionally faster conversion rates or lower acquisition costs. Without that offset, discounting is effectively borrowing from future profitability to inflate current top-line numbers — a trap that looks like growth but erodes the business's ability to sustain itself. The financial model's cause-and-effect visibility makes this tradeoff immediately visible rather than letting it hide in disconnected spreadsheets.
