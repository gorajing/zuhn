Osterwalder's framework implies that business model selection is not purely a market-facing decision. When companies generate multiple business model prototypes and must choose which to pursue, they need to evaluate each option against their specific change constraints: financial resources, human capital, cultural openness, and existing systems that must be modified.

This creates a two-dimensional selection matrix where the best choice is not always the most attractive market option, but the one that optimally balances opportunity with executable change. A company might rationally choose a less radical model that it can actually implement over a theoretically superior model that exceeds its change capacity. This reframes business model innovation from a pure strategy exercise into a strategy-plus-operations discipline.
