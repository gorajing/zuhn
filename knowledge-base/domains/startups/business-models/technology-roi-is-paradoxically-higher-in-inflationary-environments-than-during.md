The conventional wisdom is that higher interest rates and inflation are bad for technology companies because they compress valuations and reduce available capital. While this is true for funding and multiples, Chamath argues the opposite is true for technology adoption and utility. When labor costs rise and raw materials become expensive, every dollar saved through automation, software efficiency, or process optimization is worth more.

This creates a counter-intuitive dynamic: the demand signal for technology is actually stronger during inflationary periods because the economic pain creates urgent need for cost reduction. Companies that previously could afford to be inefficient (because capital was cheap) now must invest in technology to survive. For founders, this means the TAM for cost-reducing technology expands during exactly the periods when fundraising becomes harder — creating an opportunity for capital-efficient startups that can demonstrate immediate ROI rather than promise future growth.
