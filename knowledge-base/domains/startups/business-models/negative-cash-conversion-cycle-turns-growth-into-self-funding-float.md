Amazon's defining financial insight was that e-commerce inverts the traditional retail cash cycle: a book can arrive in inventory, sell the same day, and Amazon collects payment within days via credit card—while they have 30-60 days to pay the publisher. Every incremental sale creates a float pool Amazon can deploy into fulfillment centers, logistics, and new categories. Michael Mauboussin called this 'cashflow.com' and Amazon has not raised material operating capital since paying off its 2000-era convertible debt around 2004.

The deeper lesson is that the cost of capital is not a fixed input—it can be structurally negative. A competitor financing the same growth with equity or 10% debt has to clear a hurdle Amazon doesn't. This is the same mechanism Buffett identified in insurance float: you get paid to hold other people's money, and over decades the compounding effect dwarfs any operational edge.

The caveat is that negative cash conversion only works if growth continues. If the music stops, obligations to suppliers come due with no new float arriving. Amazon survived because internet adoption kept growing through the dot-com crash; a business with the same structure in a stagnating market would look like a Ponzi scheme. The strategy requires conviction that your end market is genuinely expanding, not just a clever financial trick.
