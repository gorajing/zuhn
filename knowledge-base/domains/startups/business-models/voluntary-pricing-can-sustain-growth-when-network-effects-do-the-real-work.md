WhatsApp's $1/year subscription was famously unenforced — users who didn't pay typically kept access anyway. This worked because the real value wasn't the dollar but the growing user base. Each new user made the app more valuable for everyone else through network effects, which attracted investors willing to fund operations.

This reveals a counterintuitive pricing insight: in network-effect businesses, aggressive monetization is self-defeating. The fee existed more as a psychological anchor ("this costs something, so it has value") than as a revenue driver. The founders understood that their real product was the network, not the messaging feature, and priced accordingly. This pattern applies to any platform where the user base IS the product — optimize for adoption velocity, not per-unit revenue.
