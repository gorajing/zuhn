Paul Graham observes that fast-growing companies don't optimize for cost — they optimize for not being slowed down. eBay didn't win by paying less for servers; mid-century manufacturers didn't win by squeezing wages. In both cases, the bottleneck was reliable supply of a critical resource, and paying above-market rates was the rational move.

This has a direct implication for startups: whatever your current growth bottleneck is — engineers, cloud infrastructure, distribution — you should expect to overpay for it and not feel bad about it. The mistake is applying cost-optimization thinking to the thing that's currently rate-limiting your growth. Optimization matters most when you've captured the market, not when you're still racing to get there.

The corollary is that apparent 'windfalls' for suppliers (union workers in the 1950s, web consultants in the 1990s dot-com boom) are often temporary market inefficiencies tied to a growth phase, not permanent structural shifts. Sellers should plan accordingly.
