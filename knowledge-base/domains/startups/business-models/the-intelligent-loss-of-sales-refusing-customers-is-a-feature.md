Sol Price articulated this in the Fedmart days and Costco has held it as scripture: it is rational to lose individual sales if the simplification of refusing them generates compounding gains elsewhere. Carrying one pack size instead of three cuts SKU count, which compresses logistics, which reduces labor needs, which lifts wages and lowers shrinkage. The customer who needed 3 oz is gone — but every remaining customer gets a better product at a better price.

The principle inverts the standard retail instinct, which is to add SKUs to capture every adjacent dollar. Walmart carries ~120,000 SKUs. Costco carries ~3,800. Costco's revenue per supplier is ~10x Walmart's, which gives Costco buyers leverage no broad-line competitor can match. Suppliers will take the call because Costco might be 50% of their business; Walmart buyers can't say the same about any individual vendor.

For a founder, the discipline is to ask 'which customers is it intelligent for us not to serve?' and treat the answer as load-bearing strategy rather than a regrettable gap. The Acquired hosts apply this to their own podcast — selling 'a season' as a single SKU instead of building an ad sales team. Most companies need fewer products and fewer customers than they think.
