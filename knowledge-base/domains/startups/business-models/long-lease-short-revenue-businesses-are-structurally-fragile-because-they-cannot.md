WeWork signed average 15-year leases with landlords while charging tenants on short-term, flexible arrangements. During growth periods, this model works: high occupancy covers fixed costs with margin. But the model contains a fatal asymmetry — when demand contracts (recession, remote work shift, real estate downturn), revenue drops immediately while costs remain fixed for years. WeWork's subsidiary structure (isolating leases in separate entities to prevent cascading failures) was an acknowledgment of this fragility, not a solution to it. The model had 'gone bankrupt multiple times before' in other forms. This is a generalizable pattern: any business arbitraging the spread between long-term commitments and short-term revenue is implicitly betting that demand never contracts significantly during the commitment period — a bet that always loses eventually.
