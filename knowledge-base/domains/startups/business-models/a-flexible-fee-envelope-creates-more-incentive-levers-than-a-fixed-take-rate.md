Most platforms charge a fixed take rate and call it done. Visa's interchange structure is more sophisticated: the total fee on a transaction is a pool that gets allocated across the issuing bank, the acquiring bank, Visa itself, the card network tier (signature, infinite, etc.), and sometimes a co-branded merchant partner. When Visa wants to push adoption of a new high-spend card tier, it simply widens the envelope and directs the extra to issuing banks, who use it to fund more generous rewards for consumers, who then pressure merchants into accepting the higher-fee tier.

The genius is that no single dial sets the outcome. Visa can push behavior on any of five parties by adjusting how the envelope is split, without renegotiating the underlying contracts. This is why retailer-led alternative payment systems keep failing — they conceptualize interchange as a cost to eliminate rather than as a multi-party rewards pool, and they cannot match the customer-acquisition firepower that funding rewards from the issuer's share of interchange creates.

For any multi-sided platform designer, the lesson is to resist the temptation to set a fixed take rate. Design the pricing as a flexible pool from the start, with explicit levers that let you redirect value between participants as the market evolves. This preserves optionality for incentive engineering that fixed-rate platforms can never match.
