Costco stocks roughly 4,000 unique items versus Walmart's 140,000. Because suppliers compete intensely to be one of two choices in a category, they lower prices, adapt packaging, and accommodate Costco's logistics needs — including redesigning cashew containers to save 24,000 pallets annually, savings passed directly to customers.

This is a counterintuitive concentration effect: fewer options create more power. A platform or retailer that commits to a small number of partners gives each partner more at stake, which transfers negotiating leverage to the buyer. The same principle applies in B2B software procurement, agency vendor selection, or any context where being 'the one' is worth more than being 'one of many.'

For founders: deliberately limiting the scope of what you carry, support, or integrate can paradoxically increase your leverage with the vendors and partners you do choose. Scarcity of shelf space, not abundance, is the power position.
