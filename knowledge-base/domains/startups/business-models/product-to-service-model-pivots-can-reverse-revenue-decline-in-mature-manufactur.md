When low-cost international competition threatened Hilti's power tool business after 2013, the company didn't respond with cost-cutting or incremental product improvements. Instead, it reframed its entire business model from product manufacturer to service provider — 'tools as a service.' The result: net sales grew from CHF 4B to CHF 5.7B by 2018.

This case is a canonical example of the Portfolio Map's 'Renovation' stage — the critical fork where a declining business either dies or reinvents itself. The insight for founders and operators is that when a product business faces commoditization, the winning move is often not to compete on cost but to move up the value chain into services. The product becomes the delivery mechanism for an ongoing relationship rather than a one-time transaction. This pattern repeats across industries: Rolls-Royce (power-by-the-hour), Adobe (Creative Cloud), and now increasingly in hardware startups exploring subscription models.
