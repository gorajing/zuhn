At the 1969 National Conference on Packaging Waste, plastics executives recognized that plastic's permanence was an existential threat to industry growth. Rather than developing genuine solutions, they deliberately created and propagated a recycling system they knew didn't work economically—virgin PET cost 54 cents/lb vs 63 cents/lb for lower-quality recycled PET.

The key insight is that perception-of-solution is itself a powerful product. By giving consumers the feeling of guilt-free consumption, the industry removed the market pressure that would have driven real innovation. This pattern applies broadly: any time an industry creates a 'solution theater' that pacifies stakeholders without solving the underlying problem, it buys decades of delay at the cost of compounding the original problem.
