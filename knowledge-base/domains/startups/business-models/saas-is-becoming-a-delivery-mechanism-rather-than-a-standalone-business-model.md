The thesis that SaaS as a standalone business model is ending reflects a broader market reality: software margins are compressing as AI commoditizes feature development, competition intensifies, and customers resist seat-based pricing for tools that increasingly automate the seats themselves. The moat of recurring revenue and switching costs that defined SaaS for two decades is eroding.

For founders, this means building a SaaS product is necessary but insufficient. The defensibility must come from proprietary data, network effects, or embedded workflows that create value beyond the software layer. Investors are similarly recalibrating, moving away from pure SaaS multiples toward companies where software enables but does not solely define the business model.
