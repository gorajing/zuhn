Coke's structural advantage is that it has never needed to invest meaningful capital into bottling or distribution. The parent company sells concentrated syrup; thousands of independent bottlers around the world buy that syrup, mix it with water, bottle it, and distribute it locally — putting up their own capital, hiring their own employees, and bearing their own working-capital risk. Asa Candler bought the entire company for $2,300 in 1891 because there was no capital to invest in. The business has been a cash-flow bonanza since day one.

The pattern generalizes well beyond beverages: McDonald's franchising, AWS reselling compute, Uber not owning cars, Airbnb not owning rooms, Visa not lending money. The bet is the same — the brand and the standards are the scarce, defensible asset; the physical operations are commoditized and can be outsourced to partners who want the local margin. Coke kept enough control via long-term parent-bottler contracts and merchandising support that it never lost the brand even though it didn't own the trucks.

The trap to avoid is doing this prematurely, before the brand is strong enough to attract partners on the brand owner's terms. Coke could only outsource bottling because soda fountains were already begging for the product. The order is: prove the demand, then leverage partners' capital to scale distribution — not the other way around.
