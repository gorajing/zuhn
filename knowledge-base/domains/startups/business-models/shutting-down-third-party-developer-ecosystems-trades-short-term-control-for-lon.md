At Twitter's peak, 20-30% of users accessed the platform through third-party clients like TweetDeck, LaunchBar Pro, and Seesmic. These clients had driven early adoption by offering power users richer experiences than the official product. When Ev Williams pushed to funnel all users into first-party clients for a 'consistent and unified' experience, it seemed logical -- but it destroyed the innovation ecosystem.

Third-party developers had been Twitter's unpaid R&D lab, building features and experiences that attracted the most engaged users. Restricting API access removed this distributed innovation engine. The pattern is consistent across tech: platforms that close their ecosystems (Apple's initial App Store restrictions, Reddit's API pricing) face backlash, while those that maintain openness (Shopify, Stripe) benefit from compounding developer-driven innovation. The trade-off between control and ecosystem health is one of the most consequential strategic decisions a platform can make.
