Domino's initial franchise model charged a large one-time fee, which attracted franchisees motivated by the opportunity but not necessarily equipped to execute. When 32 new stores mostly failed in 1969, Tom restructured the entire approach: no upfront fee, but you must successfully manage an existing Domino's store for a full year first. This unconventional model filtered for operational competence rather than capital availability.

The result was dramatically better franchise performance. Franchisees who survived the trial year had proven they could handle the work, understood the systems, and had built enough capital from their management earnings to fund their own store. This is a powerful pattern for any platform or franchise business: selecting for demonstrated capability rather than willingness to pay produces better outcomes at scale, even though it sacrifices short-term revenue from upfront fees.
