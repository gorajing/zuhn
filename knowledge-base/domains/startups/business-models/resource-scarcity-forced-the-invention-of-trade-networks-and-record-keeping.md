The Fertile Crescent was agriculturally productive but resource-poor — no metal, limited stone, scarce wood. This forced Mesopotamians into extensive trade networks, which in turn created the need for record-keeping (cuneiform was invented to track wheat-for-goat transactions, not for literature). Trade complexity then drove the formation of the world's first territorial kingdoms to secure trade routes.

This inverts the intuition that innovation comes from resource abundance. The richest environments (like the Indus Valley with its predictable flooding) didn't produce writing first. Scarcity created coordination pressure, which created communication technology, which created administrative complexity, which created political structures. For startups, the parallel is clear: constraints breed invention, and the most durable infrastructure often emerges from solving logistical pain rather than pursuing abstract ambitions.
