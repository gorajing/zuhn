Adobe poured significant investment into developing Illustrator, Photoshop, and other medium-for-thought tools. Sketch and then Figma came along later, copied the best features without needing Adobe's original R&D investment, and began eating Adobe's market share. Marc Andreessen: 'True defensibility purely at the product level is really rare in [Silicon] Valley, because there are a lot of really good engineers... And then there's the issue of leap-frogging. The next team has the opportunity to learn from what you did and then build something better.' This is the public goods problem: tools for thought cost a lot to develop initially but are easy to duplicate and improve, so the companies that make the initial investment are penalized for their own success. Patents don't help (most patent activity is defensive; the truly original ideas are often not patent-protectable). Society underinvests in tools for thought because no one company can capture enough value to justify the research. Partial solutions: philanthropic funding (like computer animation from PARC → Pixar), or the Adobe model where the moat is training, marketing, and distribution rather than the tool itself, or the games-industry model where novelty-based short-term revenue captures enough value before cloning eats the market.
