ARM's business model demonstrates a powerful principle: by designing chip architectures and licensing them rather than manufacturing chips, the company decoupled its revenue growth from physical production constraints. Each design can be sold to multiple partners across multiple applications (mobile, IoT, networking), creating compounding returns on the same R&D investment.

This asset-light approach enabled ARM to capture 95% of the smartphone chip market while competitors like Intel bore massive capital expenditure for fabrication facilities. The model works especially well in markets where a standard architecture benefits from ecosystem effects — the more partners adopt ARM, the more software targets ARM, creating a flywheel that manufacturing-bound competitors cannot replicate.
