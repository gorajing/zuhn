Newspapers like Newsday, The Village Voice, and the New York Post derived roughly 30% of their revenue from classified ads — small print listings charging up to $250 each. This wasn't cosmetically related to journalism; it was the economic engine that made investigative reporting, feature writing, and full-time newsrooms financially viable. When the internet (especially Craigslist) offered free classifieds, it didn't just hurt one revenue line — it pulled a structural pillar that the entire editorial operation rested on.

This is a generalizable business-model risk: bundled offerings where one component is the true margin engine and another is the visible product. Cable TV bundles had the same structure (ESPN subsidizing niche channels). Insurance bundles it (healthy customers subsidizing sick ones). When digital disruption unbundles the profitable component, the 'public good' component collapses with it — not because it lacked value, but because it was never independently viable. Builders of bundled businesses should model 'what happens if the hidden subsidy disappears.'
