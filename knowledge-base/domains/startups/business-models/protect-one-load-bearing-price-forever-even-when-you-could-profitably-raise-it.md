Munger explains that Costco's governing norm is 'don't raise the market — get it low and keep it there forever.' When a new CEO tried to raise the $1.50 hot dog price, Jim Sinegal forbade it at the leadership level, not even as a board-level discussion. The dollar of missed margin is trivial; the trust signal that Costco-is-still-cheap is the entire brand.

This generalizes beyond Costco. Heinz can raise ketchup prices and nobody switches because the flavor is inimitable; Kraft cheese raises prices and the entire supply chain rebels. The lesson is that a few prices in a business are load-bearing and a few are not. The load-bearing ones anchor customer perception of the entire offering, and touching them for a small gain can unravel a much larger franchise.

The application for founders: identify the one or two prices customers actually notice and use as proxies for 'is this still the company I trusted?' — then protect them with religious discipline, even if spreadsheet logic says you're leaving money on the table. The spreadsheet can't see the anchor.
