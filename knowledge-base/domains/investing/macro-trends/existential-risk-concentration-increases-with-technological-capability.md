The Great Filter hypothesis implies that existential risk is not uniformly distributed across a civilization's timeline — it concentrates in a narrow window where technology is powerful enough to destroy but not yet distributed enough to provide redundancy. Nuclear weapons, engineered pathogens, misaligned AI, and atmospheric chain reactions all become possible at roughly the same developmental stage. This has investment implications: the next 50-100 years represent a bottleneck period where tail risks are systematically higher than historical base rates would suggest. Companies and assets building interplanetary redundancy, existential risk mitigation, or civilizational resilience infrastructure are positioned at what may be the most consequential chokepoint in human history. The asymmetry is stark — if we pass through, the upside is galactic; if we don't, nothing else matters.
