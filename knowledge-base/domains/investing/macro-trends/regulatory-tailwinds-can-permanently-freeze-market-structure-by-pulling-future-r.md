The standard reading of HITECH and meaningful use is that it was a massive tailwind for Epic and other EMR incumbents. That's true but understates what happened. The deeper structural effect was temporal: by dangling $44K-$64K per physician in one concentrated window, the government forced every hospital in America to run their EMR buying decision within a 3-5 year stretch. RFPs that would naturally have happened over 20+ years all collapsed into one moment. Whichever vendors had the best product in that specific window would win effectively permanently, because switching costs on a 'nervous system' implementation are enormous enough that no hospital rebids for decades.

This is not regulatory capture in the pejorative sense—Epic didn't write the law. It's better framed as a regulatory freeze: a market structure set in amber. Any hypothetical future EMR innovator, no matter how technically superior, lost their shot because there was no greenfield left. Every customer they could have won had already committed to a 20-year contract during the 2009-2014 window. The analogous dynamic applies to any government program that pays customers to adopt a category: rural broadband subsidies, EV charging infrastructure, solar installation credits.

The investing takeaway: when evaluating incumbents in regulated markets, ask whether there was a compression event that locked in the competitive set. If yes, the incumbent's moat is structurally stronger than the usual 'switching costs' story implies, because the flow of new greenfield opportunities is permanently reduced. New entrants can't win customers that already don't exist.
