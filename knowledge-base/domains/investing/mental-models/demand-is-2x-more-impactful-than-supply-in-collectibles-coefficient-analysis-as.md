From PokeDataDadGuy's fitted regression: coefficient on desirability ≈ 0.41 (every 1-point increase in desirability score yields 41% price increase). Coefficient on pull cost ≈ 0.19 (every 1-point increase yields 19% price increase). The ratio: 41/19 ≈ 2.16, meaning desirability is ~2x more impactful than supply. The asymmetry has implications for multiple participants: (1) Collectors deciding what to buy: a card that's slightly easier to pull but significantly more desired is a better bet than a card that's harder to pull but less desired. The intuitive 'rarity = value' heuristic overweights supply. (2) Pack openers deciding whether to chase a set: the desirability of the top cards in a set determines whether ripping packs has positive expected value. Easy-to-pull but high-desire cards have stable price floors; hard-to-pull but low-desire cards crash. (3) Card creators (Pokemon Company / Bandai / The Pokemon Company International): if you want to boost set sales, you control desirability through artwork commissions and character selection, not just through rarity tier design. The corollary for any creator/distributor in a collectibles market: invest 2x more in demand-side (artwork, story, character resonance, marketing) than in supply-side (controlled scarcity). The lesson generalizes far beyond Pokemon: in art markets, artist reputation and emotional resonance dominate gallery scarcity. In sneaker markets, cultural moments and athlete association dominate production runs. In wine markets, producer mystique and vintage narrative dominate yield restrictions. Wherever you can choose between making something scarcer vs making it more desired, the desired path produces ~2x the price impact. The trap: it's easier to manipulate supply (set hard limits, reduce production runs) than demand (which requires genuine cultural work). So creators default to supply manipulation, leaving the higher-leverage demand work undone.
