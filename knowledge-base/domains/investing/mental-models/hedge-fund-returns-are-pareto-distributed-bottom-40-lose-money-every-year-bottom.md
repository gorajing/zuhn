Tom Mock's empirical breakdown of the hedge fund industry: 'Hedge fund returns are Pareto-distributed. So like a chart of wealth — Elon's up there at the top, then a bunch of other billionaires, then it gets flat pretty quickly. In the hedge fund industry, that Pareto distribution right around the 40% mark — 60% profitable. The bottom 40% lose money every year. The bottom 25% will go out of business and be replaced by another 25% pretty much every year.' This is a structural feature of the industry, not a cyclical phenomenon. The replacement rate at the bottom is consistent regardless of market conditions because the underlying error pattern is consistent: bottom-tier managers fundamentally misunderstand markets. The implication for prospective managers: when you decide to start a hedge fund, your modal outcome isn't the Tudor/Lewis Bacon case — it's the 25%-fail-this-year case. The expected value calculation has to weight the long tail correctly. The implication for allocators: don't assume 'I'll find a good manager from the index because hedge funds average X% returns' — the average is dragged up by the top tail and the bottom losers are constantly being replaced (so the survivorship-corrected return is lower than reported averages). The pattern generalizes to ANY Pareto-distributed industry: startups (90% fail, 1% capture most returns), content creators (top 1% on YouTube capture most monetization), app stores (top 10 apps in a category capture most revenue), career outcomes in elite fields (PE partners, big-firm law, top medicine). The mental model: when entering a Pareto-distributed market, don't reason from the celebrated top. Reason from the median outcome AND tail risk. Most of the participants in Pareto markets are net-losers; the top is sufficient compensation for the average loser only if you have the specific advantages that select for the top tier.
