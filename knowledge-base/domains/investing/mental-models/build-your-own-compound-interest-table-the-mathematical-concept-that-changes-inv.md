Chamath's most actionable investment advice: manually build a compound interest table for your own situation. The example he uses: he showed a famous athlete's salary in a Google Sheet, took out taxes/manager/agent fees, ran the compound interest table — the athlete's mind was blown. Then said 'let's back this off and assume the salary was 1/1000th' — still blown. The point: compound interest power is universal but psychologically requires personal confrontation to internalize.

The behavioral effect: building the table makes you see the forks in the road clearly — short-term gains vs long-term true alpha. People who internalize this become like Buffett, allowing compounding to do most of the work; people who don't become 'too active,' buying and selling and changing their minds. Even Chamath admits he's been 'a little bit too active at times' despite knowing the math.

For builders and investors: this is a one-hour exercise that pays back across a lifetime. For Zuhn editorially: this is the operational form of why long-time-horizon thinking is hard — the math is universal, but the visceral understanding requires personal computation. Useful for any decision involving time horizons longer than a year (career bets, equity vesting, R&D investment, customer LTV models).
