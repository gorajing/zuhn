Blankfein credits Goldman's crisis survival partly to marking positions 'rigorously, religiously' while others didn't—and reframes mark-to-market as fundamentally a risk-management tool: 'mark-to-market is not just a P&L system, it's a risk management system, because that was our early warning that something was amiss.' The institutional design that made this work was a deliberate separation of powers: a 'bureaucracy' of controllers, organizationally independent from the well-paid traders and investors, owned the marks and won disputes by default.

The elegant test for a contested valuation was empirical, not argumentative: 'go out and sell something—sell a fraction.' When traders were forced to sell supposedly-AAA instruments, 'the bids vanished... and were much lower, then much lower'—revealing the true price before the loss could metastasize. Marking down to a sellable price even made positions easier to offload because the losses were already embedded in the books rather than lurking. This connects to his cheap-insurance principle: testing the market early, while it's still functioning, surfaces problems when mitigation is still affordable. The transferable lesson is that any valuation an organization relies on—of assets, of AI outputs, of projects—should be subjected to a real disposal or stress test, and the people who do the marking should be insulated from the people whose compensation depends on the marks being high.
