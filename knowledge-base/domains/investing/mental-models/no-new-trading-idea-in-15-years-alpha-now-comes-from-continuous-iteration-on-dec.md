Tom Mock's empirical observation after 35 years in institutional finance: 'I haven't seen a real new idea in trading in at least 15 years. Just better people being better at doing the things that we already know how to do.' Even high-frequency trading isn't genuinely new — 'they do math in a different way... look at this angle or that angle... change things around. Technology is advanced so people have faster chips. But no new ideas.' Crypto is 'a bunch of super super smart guys cosplaying at finance' but built for retail and producing no new institutional trading ideas. Tom's concrete example of what work actually looks like: he built an NLP system in 2003 that read newswires and traded the results (state-of-the-art for the era). It ran spectacularly for ~1 year, then sharp ratio declined. The work then: 'I needed to look at the results I'd produced and find better hedges. So instead of using the S&P for a hedge, I had to use sector ETFs. Performance improved. I got another couple years out of it. Now it's declining again. Now I get rid of the sector ETFs as hedges and start treating beta and gamma hedging separately. Maybe options hedging or whatever. Fine-tune the results so I'm getting more idiosyncratic risk than market risk.' Each iteration buys 1-3 more years. The strategy never stops decaying — the work is the iteration on the decay. The general lesson for any quantitative work (and broader strategy work): in saturated competitive domains, edge doesn't come from genius-level new insights. It comes from disciplined iteration on small advantages. The corollary for AI builders: don't bet your stack on finding a 'breakthrough.' Bet on a systematic process for incrementally compounding small advantages. The Goldman Sachs trading floor 'collectively makes money 365 days a year' — they almost never lose money on a full day. Not because they had a magical breakthrough, but because they 'built this decision-making structure that is always one step ahead of the competition in some small way and they manage the risks between them well enough.' Boring is the goal.
