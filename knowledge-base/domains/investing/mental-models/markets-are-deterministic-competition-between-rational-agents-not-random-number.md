Tom Mock's articulation of why the bottom 40% of hedge funds lose money every year: 'They treat the markets like a casino. They want to buy a lottery ticket or they want to find some trick that's right all the time like selling options premium, which looks right all the time until it isn't. They don't think about the larger structure of the market. In the top tier, the watchword — the unified field theory of finance — is all probability of discounted cash flows. You can print that on a T-shirt: future cash flows discounted to today. Fair value. If current price is above it, sell. Below it, buy. That's it.' The deeper structural insight: 'Markets are NOT a random number generator. They're a deterministic system where everybody is trying to outsmart everybody else. You need to appreciate that every inefficiency in the market is directly related to somebody else thinking they're going to do better than you by doing it. That's the real nut to crack. Quantitative finance is really about understanding their incentives, estimating them, modeling them, and producing a profit because you're able to successfully predict their behavior.' Practical applications: (1) When you see an opportunity that seems obvious, ask 'who is taking the other side, and why are they rationally choosing to?' If you can't answer, you're probably the antelope with a limp. (2) Crypto market makers who built high-frequency systems assumed markets were random and got crushed when volatility spiked because they didn't realize their mean-reversion strategies had a short-volatility bias. Tom: 'That happened to roughly 50% of crypto market makers on 10/10 [Oct 10, 2025].' (3) The trader's job isn't predicting prices — it's predicting OTHER TRADERS' decisions. The same mental model applies far beyond markets: any competitive system (sales, hiring, dating, content) where multiple rational agents are simultaneously optimizing — assume deterministic rational competition, model their incentives. Don't assume randomness.
