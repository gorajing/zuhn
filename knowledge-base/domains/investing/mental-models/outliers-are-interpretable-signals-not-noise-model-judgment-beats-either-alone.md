    PokeDataDadGuy's interpretive process when his model flags a clear outlier:
    'This is where I think there's somewhat of a limitation in the model that
    can't factor in everything, but also it's telling something.' Mega Charizard
    X has a low pull cost (2 — easier to pull than most top-chase cards) and
    high desirability score, but trades at $800 vs the model's $500 prediction.
    Three plausible explanations: (1) Overvalued — sentiment-driven bubble that
    will correct downward. (2) New-collector dynamic — many new entrants to the
    hobby are buying the card directly rather than ripping packs from the
    'Charizard-or-bust' set (where most other cards are low-value, making
    expected pack-opening value low). The model doesn't know about
    pack-buying-vs-direct-buying preferences. (3) Cultural moment — this
    specific artwork is being celebrated in social media and viral content,
    creating temporary excess demand. The investor's value-add: not in arguing
    whether the model is right or wrong, but in evaluating which of these three
    (or more) explanations is most likely. If (1) is right, sell or avoid. If
    (2) is right, hold — new collectors will keep coming. If (3) is right, hold
    short-term and sell when the cultural moment passes. The model doesn't make
    the call — but it frames the question precisely enough that judgment can
    engage. The general principle: clean quantitative models are most valuable
    not when they're correct in absolute terms but when their outliers are
    informative. A model that's 88% accurate gives you 12% of cases to
    investigate deeply — that's much better than 0% (no model) or 100% (perfect
    model that you have to trust without interpretation). The investment
    workflow: build the cleanest model possible → identify outliers → ask 'what
    does this outlier reveal about market dynamics I haven't modeled?' → make
    decisions based on the interpretation. The pattern generalizes to
    anti-fraud, scientific discovery, manufacturing QA, and any field where a
    baseline model can flag the cases where human attention is most valuable.

PokeDataDadGuy's interpretive process when his model flags a clear outlier: 'This is where I think there's somewhat of a limitation in the model that can't factor in everything, but also it's telling something.' Mega Charizard X has a low pull cost (2 — easier to pull than most top-chase cards) and high desirability score, but trades at $800 vs the model's $500 prediction. Three plausible explanations: (1) Overvalued — sentiment-driven bubble that will correct downward. (2) New-collector dynamic — many new entrants to the hobby are buying the card directly rather than ripping packs from the 'Charizard-or-bust' set (where most other cards are low-value, making expected pack-opening value low). The model doesn't know about pack-buying-vs-direct-buying preferences. (3) Cultural moment — this specific artwork is being celebrated in social media and viral content, creating temporary excess demand. The investor's value-add: not in arguing whether the model is right or wrong, but in evaluating which of these three (or more) explanations is most likely. If (1) is right, sell or avoid. If (2) is right, hold — new collectors will keep coming. If (3) is right, hold short-term and sell when the cultural moment passes. The model doesn't make the call — but it frames the question precisely enough that judgment can engage. The general principle: clean quantitative models are most valuable not when they're correct in absolute terms but when their outliers are informative. A model that's 88% accurate gives you 12% of cases to investigate deeply — that's much better than 0% (no model) or 100% (perfect model that you have to trust without interpretation). The investment workflow: build the cleanest model possible → identify outliers → ask 'what does this outlier reveal about market dynamics I haven't modeled?' → make decisions based on the interpretation. The pattern generalizes to anti-fraud, scientific discovery, manufacturing QA, and any field where a baseline model can flag the cases where human attention is most valuable.
