Chamath's most expensive lesson, costing him $5B: in November 2021, he saw Bezos and Elon Musk selling. He thought 'these are the two best capital allocators in the world; if they're selling, I should sell.' He sold a little but not nearly enough — because he was 'afraid of what people would think' and confused his 'growing fame with growing skill.' Then March 2022 came (Russia invades Ukraine, rate cycle, no liquidity), and his SPAC-heavy portfolio got 'absolutely obliterated.' The honest answer to 'when could I have changed my mind?' was November.

The rule he adopted afterward: when people structurally and fundamentally smarter than him do things against the grain of what he's doing, he immediately considers doing the same — and demands of himself a strong proof of why he shouldn't. The default action is alignment with smarter operators; deviation requires explicit justification.

The corollary: he can now see when things are 'just materially different' (wars, rates, commodity dumps) and re-underwrites everything from 'everything is wrong, why do I keep these positions?' This is harder than it sounds — most people start from 'why should I sell?' rather than 'why am I keeping?'

For builders and investors: build a watchlist of operators whose moves you'd update on. When they move against you, treat it as an action item, not noise. For Zuhn editorially: this is the operational form of 'epistemic humility' — knowing whose signals to weight more than your own.
