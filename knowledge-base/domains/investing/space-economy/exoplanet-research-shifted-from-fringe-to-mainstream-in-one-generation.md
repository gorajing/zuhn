When Sara Seager was in graduate school in the mid-1990s, the first exoplanet around a sun-like star was reported. At the time, the field was considered fringe — people thought it was silly to spend time on it. Within 30 years, astronomers have confirmed thousands of exoplanets with several thousand more candidates awaiting confirmation.

This trajectory illustrates a recurring pattern in science and technology: the line between mainstream research and completely crazy research is constantly shifting. Fields dismissed by consensus can become dominant within a single career span. The practical implication for investment and career strategy: frontier research that seems impractical today may become the default field of tomorrow. Willingness to work in uncomfortable spaces — what a journalist called being the Indiana Jones of astrophysics — is how paradigm-shifting work gets done.
