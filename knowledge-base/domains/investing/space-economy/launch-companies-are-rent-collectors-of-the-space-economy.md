Every company operating in space must pay for launch as a non-negotiable cost of doing business. This positions launch providers as infrastructure gatekeepers who collect 'rent' from the entire ecosystem above them. The analogy to landlords is structurally apt: as the space economy grows, launch demand scales with it, and the providers with locked-in capacity earn recurring revenue regardless of which individual space companies succeed or fail.

This is a classic picks-and-shovels investment thesis applied to space. Rather than betting on which satellite constellation or space manufacturing startup wins, investing in launch infrastructure captures value from the entire sector. The key risk is commoditization if multiple players achieve full reusability, which would compress margins — but the capital intensity and technical difficulty of launch create natural barriers to entry that limit the field to a handful of players.
