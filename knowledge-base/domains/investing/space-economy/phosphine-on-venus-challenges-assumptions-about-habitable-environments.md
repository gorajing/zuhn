Venus's surface is far too hot for any known life form, but 50km above the surface, temperature and pressure match Earth's surface conditions -- even though the clouds are made of concentrated sulfuric acid. The 2020 detection of trace phosphine (a gas produced by decaying organic matter on Earth) in Venus's atmosphere could not be explained by any known Venusian chemistry despite hundreds of pages of analysis.

While not proof of life, this challenges the assumption that habitability requires Earth-like surface conditions. If life can exist in sulfuric acid clouds, the number of potentially habitable environments in the universe expands dramatically. The broader insight is that our models of where life can exist are likely too narrow, constrained by the single example of Earth. Seager argues we may not yet have conceived of all the planet types and environments that could support life.
