Kipping presents a striking statistical argument: Earth-like planets around sun-like stars appear in only 1-10% of systems, but roughly 50% of sun-like stars have Neptune-to-Jupiter-sized planets in their habitable zones. If even one in five of those gas giants hosts an Earth-like or Mars-like moon, habitable moons would outnumber habitable planets in the universe.

JWST is the first telescope capable of detecting such exomoons, but telescope time competition is fierce (20:1 oversubscription). The economic implication for space-focused investment: the search for biosignatures will increasingly target moons, not just planets. Starship's cost reduction could enable mass-produced space telescopes dedicated to this search, fundamentally changing the economics of astrobiology from shared scarce instruments to abundant specialized ones.
