Every transportation network required a servicing layer before it could truly scale. Gas stations, tow trucks, and highway maintenance crews weren't glamorous, but they changed the economics of everything else on the road network. In space, companies like Starfish Space are building vehicles that can rendezvous with satellites to extend operational lives, reposition them, or safely deorbit them.

The economics are compelling: a single geostationary communications satellite costs hundreds of millions of dollars, so extending its life by even a few months fundamentally alters ROI. Across tens of thousands of satellites, servicing becomes an enormous market. More importantly, once on-orbit servicing exists at scale, operators can build more cheaply, plan more flexibly, and insure at lower cost — changing the economics of the entire space ecosystem. This is the layer that doesn't generate headlines but must exist before second and third-order effects can materialize.
