NASA and private sector estimates suggest a functional moon base could be built for $20-40 billion spread over roughly a decade — comparable to the International Space Station's cost or Germany's 2017 budget surplus. This reframes lunar development from a sci-fi aspiration to a tractable infrastructure project.

The investment thesis becomes compelling when you consider the downstream economics: lunar water can be split into rocket fuel, drastically reducing the cost of deep-space missions. A lunar orbital fuel depot would make Mars missions and asteroid mining economically viable. The colony becomes self-sustaining once it can export resources — Helium-3 for fusion, precious metals from impact craters, and in-situ manufactured goods — creating a flywheel where space infrastructure funds more space infrastructure.
