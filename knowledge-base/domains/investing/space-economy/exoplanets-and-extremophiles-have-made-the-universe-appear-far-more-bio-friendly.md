Over Tarter's career, two discoveries have transformed astrobiology. First, the discovery of exoplanets revealed that planets are ubiquitous — there are more planets than stars in the Milky Way Galaxy, whereas when SETI started, we only knew of nine planets (now eight) in our solar system. Second, extremophile biology discovered organisms thriving in environments that were taught as absolutely sterile — extreme temperatures, pressures, and chemical conditions.

These two game-changers make it natural to ask whether any of the potentially habitable real estate is actually inhabited. The instruments needed to detect disequilibrium chemistry in exoplanet atmospheres are still being built and will need another generation to reach sufficient capability. But the scientific case for searching has never been stronger. This represents a window where investment in detection technology could yield civilization-defining results within decades.
