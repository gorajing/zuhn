The Rare Earth hypothesis stacks up hundreds of narrow conditions required for life (specific moon size, ocean chemistry, land fraction, etc.) and multiplies them into near-zero probability. Kipping's critique is elegant: this model assumes a single path to life. If there are multiple independent paths using different chemistry, the calculation needs addition, not just multiplication.

Our sample size of one makes it impossible to know how many alternative paths exist. But the logical structure matters: adding even a few viable alternative biochemistries could dramatically increase the overall probability of life arising. This fundamentally changes how we should think about the Drake equation's output — it may systematically undercount by ignoring parallel paths we cannot yet imagine.
