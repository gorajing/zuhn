The Copernican (or Mediocrity) principle — the idea that we are typical observers in a typical location — is a powerful tool in astronomy. It correctly predicts that Neptune-like planets are common elsewhere. But Kipping identifies a critical failure mode: you cannot apply it to features connected to your own existence.

Earth's oxygen atmosphere, liquid water, and large moon are not necessarily common just because we observe them. The weak anthropic principle explains why: we can only exist on a planet with these features, regardless of whether three or three billion such planets exist. Using the Copernican principle to argue life must be widespread is therefore circular. This is a subtle but important reasoning error that pervades popular science communication about alien life.
