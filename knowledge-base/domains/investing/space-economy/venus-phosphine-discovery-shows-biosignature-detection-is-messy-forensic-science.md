In 2020, Seager's team reported trace amounts of phosphine gas in Venus's atmosphere. On Earth, phosphine occurs naturally in decaying organic matter. They presented 100 pages of chemistry demonstrating that known chemical processes on Venus cannot account for the detected phosphine. This does not prove life exists on Venus — it proves something unexplained is happening.

This illustrates the reality of biosignature detection: it will never be a clean life-detected signal. Instead, it is forensic science — real data requiring extensive interpretation to make the picture whole. The search for an Earth twin with oxygen and continents remains a generations-long project. Meanwhile, Venus's clouds at 50km altitude have Earth-like temperature and pressure, making them an unexpected candidate for life despite their concentrated sulfuric acid composition.
