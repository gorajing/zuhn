Kipping identifies a fundamental asymmetry in the search for extraterrestrial life: a single confirmed detection would answer the question, but no amount of negative results can prove we are alone. Even on the nearest planet, Mars, we can never check behind every rock, under every surface, inside every canyon.

This has practical implications for how we think about the search: it may be a journey spanning not decades but centuries or millennia, analogous to the 400 years between Galileo's telescope and modern exoplanet discovery. The most promising places to look for uncontaminated evidence are the icy moons of Europa and Enceladus, whose thick ice shells would prevent Earth-originated contamination from reaching their subsurface oceans.
