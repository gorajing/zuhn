Ackman demonstrates that $10,000 compounded at 20% for 43 years yields $25 million, but if you lose 50% every 12 years, the result drops to just $1.8 million. Meanwhile, a steady 15% return yields $4 million with no catastrophic risk. This asymmetry exists because losses require disproportionate gains to recover — a 50% loss needs a 100% gain just to break even.

This insight reframes the investing objective from 'maximize returns' to 'maximize risk-adjusted compounding.' It explains why Buffett's Rule #1 ('never lose money') isn't conservative advice but mathematical optimization. For practical portfolio construction, this means preferring businesses with lower volatility and more predictable earnings over high-flying growth stocks that can crater. The compounding math is unforgiving: protecting the downside is more valuable than capturing the upside.
