Enron's 7.5x revenue growth from 1996-2000 happened during the dot-com bubble; FTX happened during the 2021 crypto boom. The hosts argue this isn't coincidence: in bull markets, capital saturates the obvious opportunities until all that's left is going further out the risk curve into low-disclosure, high-complexity assets. FOMO replaces diligence. Asking questions becomes socially costly because the stock keeps going up and the asker looks like the fool.

The corollary is operationally useful: the absence of skeptical questioning in your investor base is a leading indicator of bubble conditions, not a sign of trust. When analysts stop pressing on metric definitions, when journalists write hagiographies six years running (Fortune named Enron most innovative six years in a row, including the year it went bankrupt), when no one wants to be the person who 'doesn't get it' — the conditions for a major fraud have arrived.

For allocators, this means counter-cyclical due diligence discipline: ask the hardest questions when everyone else has stopped asking them, and treat 'I don't fully understand how this works' as a veto, not a confession of ignorance. The opacity that everyone is willing to tolerate at the top is the same opacity that becomes the post-mortem headline.
