The argument inverts the usual bubble worry. Bubbles are typically characterized by excess supply collapsing prices and economics; AI today is the opposite — supply-constrained on compute, memory, data centers, and power, with serious data-center capacity unavailable at scale until late 2028 or early 2029 and buildout running roughly a year behind schedule. Scarcity, not glut, defines the current state, which makes a present-tense bubble unlikely. The CapEx-to-revenue math is offered as support: ~$5T of buildout returning $1–2T of revenue looks reasonable when the two largest model companies alone could exit the year near $200B of revenue run rate.

Crucially, the speaker bounds the claim in time: confident there's no bubble now, far less confident about three years out. The named mechanism that would flip the regime is a step-change toward massively smaller models — an algorithmic breakthrough that makes intelligence far less token-consumptive (the human brain as the efficiency proof-of-concept) — which could convert today's scarcity into oversupply. So the insight is really a conditional: supply constraint is the current bubble-vaccine, and the thing to monitor is anything that abruptly relaxes the constraint (an efficiency breakthrough), not rising valuations per se.

Confidence is medium because it rests on a forward CapEx/revenue projection and on continued supply discipline (e.g., TSMC restraint), both of which are uncertain, and the speaker himself disclaims confidence beyond the near term.
