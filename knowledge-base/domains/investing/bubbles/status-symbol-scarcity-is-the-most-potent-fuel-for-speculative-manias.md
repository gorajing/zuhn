The tulip bubble was not random — tulips had a specific set of properties that made them ideal bubble fuel. They were genuinely scarce (long growth cycles, brief bloom windows), visually conspicuous (displayed in gardens for neighbors to see), and served as status markers in a newly wealthy merchant society. This combination meant demand was driven by positional competition — you wanted tulips because your neighbors had them and you needed to signal equivalent wealth.

This framework — scarcity + visibility + status signaling — predicts which assets are most vulnerable to speculative manias. NFTs, luxury real estate, rare art, and limited-edition goods all share these properties. The key differentiator from utility-driven assets is that demand for status goods is reflexive: higher prices increase rather than decrease demand, because the price itself becomes part of the status signal. This inverted demand curve is what allows 20x price explosions in a single month.
