Josh Kopelman's Venture Arrogance Score (VAS) quantifies why mega-funds structurally underperform: a $7B fund aiming for 10% ownership would need to capture 50% of all exit value in its investing window — something no firm has ever achieved. Kopelman argues real venture funds rarely exceed a VAS of ~10%, and anything above that is essentially private equity disguised as VC. Empirical data confirms this: 91% of top-decile funds had assets under $250M, 73% were Fund I or II, and median top-decile fund size was ~$38M.

This creates a paradox for institutional LPs. When they back mega-funds, they're buying stability and predictability, not return asymmetry. Carta's 2018 vintage data makes this stark: sub-$10M funds posted 90th percentile TVPI of ~4.03x while $100M+ funds managed only ~1.67x at the same percentile. The structural explanation is simple — smaller funds write earlier checks, capture higher ownership, and don't need five unicorns to make the math work. A single billion-dollar exit that returns 10x for a $50M fund barely registers for a $500M vehicle.
