The AI investment thesis since 2022 has rested on a core assumption: building state-of-the-art models requires massive GPU clusters costing hundreds of millions of dollars. This created a perceived moat around companies like Nvidia, whose monopoly on training hardware (via CUDA lock-in and superior Linux drivers) made them the picks-and-shovels play of the AI boom.

DeepSeek R1 shattered this assumption by matching OpenAI's $200/month o1 reasoning model at under $10M in training cost, and the model even ran on consumer Apple M2 Ultra hardware. The market reaction was immediate: Nvidia lost the most value in a single day, followed by TSMC and Broadcom. This pattern—where a cost-efficiency breakthrough instantly reprices an entire supply chain—is a recurring feature of technology bubbles. The investment lesson is that hardware moats built on temporary software lock-in (CUDA) are fundamentally fragile when the algorithms themselves become more efficient.
