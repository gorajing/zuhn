Chinese property developers created a system where pre-sale deposits from buyers were used to start new projects rather than complete the ones already sold. This worked as long as urbanization drove perpetual demand growth (4.1% migration rate in 1996 falling to 2.1% in 2020). When migration slowed, the funding chain broke—developers couldn't service existing obligations without new pre-sales, leading to cascading defaults (Evergrande, Kaisa, Shimao, Yuzhou). The pattern is generalizable: any asset market where current obligations are funded by future sales rather than operating income is structurally fragile. The three red lines policy, intended to fix leverage, actually accelerated the crisis by cutting off the debt-refinancing lifeline that kept the scheme running. This mirrors how intervention in Ponzi-like structures often triggers the collapse it aims to prevent.
