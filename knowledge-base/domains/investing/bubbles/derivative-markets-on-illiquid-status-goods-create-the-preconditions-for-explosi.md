The tulip bubble was not simply about irrational exuberance — it was structurally enabled by a financial innovation. The Dutch created futures contracts allowing tulips to be traded continuously, even when no physical bulbs existed to inspect or deliver. This decoupled price from tangible supply, letting speculative momentum build across months without reality checks from actual bloom seasons.

This pattern recurs: whenever derivative markets are layered onto assets with constrained or opaque supply (CDOs on housing, crypto futures on limited-supply tokens), the same structural vulnerability appears. The derivative market grows faster than the underlying, price signals become self-referential, and collapse follows when a thin slice of participants refuse to pay the next increment. The tulip auction no-shows in North Holland were functionally identical to a modern liquidity crisis.
