Bernie Ebbers took out $408 million in personal loans from JPMorgan, secured by WorldCom stock, to fund yachts, a yacht-building company, and the largest privately owned ranch in the world. When the telecom sector declined and WorldCom's stock fell from $62 to $18, Ebbers faced devastating margin calls. The WorldCom board even gave him a $408M company loan to prevent him from selling stock and spooking the market—effectively making the company complicit in propping up the CEO's personal finances.

This structure creates a lethal feedback loop: the CEO's personal solvency depends on the stock price, so they're incentivized to inflate earnings by any means. For investors, heavy insider pledging of stock as loan collateral is one of the strongest red flags for potential fraud. Modern governance best practices now restrict or require disclosure of executive stock pledging, but the fundamental incentive misalignment persists wherever executives' personal wealth is overwhelmingly concentrated in a single stock they can influence.
