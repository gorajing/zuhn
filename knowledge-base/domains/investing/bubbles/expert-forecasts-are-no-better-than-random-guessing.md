Wharton Professor Philip Tetlock's analysis of thousands of expert forecasts found they performed no better than the average person's predictions — which are themselves quite poor. Nobody predicted the pandemic crash and 69% recovery in 2020, Russia's invasion of Ukraine, or the Fed's 500-basis-point rate hikes sending both stocks and bonds down double digits in 2022.

The future is dominated by random, unanticipated events that make year-ahead forecasting a 'fool's errand.' By the time news reaches you through any media channel, it's already priced into markets. Acting on headlines means trading on old information. The only rational response is to acknowledge uncertainty and build a portfolio that doesn't depend on predictions being correct.
