Perhaps the most striking detail in China's housing situation is that Beijing is fully aware of the bubble, understands the risks, and even knows the approximate solution set — slow lending, rein in local governments, introduce property taxes. Yet it struggles to act because real estate is so deeply intertwined with GDP that any corrective measure would risk significant economic deceleration.

This reveals an important pattern about systemic risk: the danger point isn't when no one sees the bubble — it's when everyone sees it but the system has become structurally dependent on it. The bubble transitions from a market anomaly into load-bearing economic infrastructure. At that point, the rational calculation shifts from 'how do we prevent a bubble' to 'how do we prevent the correction from being worse than the bubble itself.' This dynamic applies beyond housing — any market where awareness of overvaluation coexists with structural dependency on continued appreciation (tech valuations and startup ecosystem health, for instance) faces the same paralysis.
