During ZIRP (2008-2022), cheap capital allowed companies to grow without regard to profitability, creating inflated valuations across tech, biotech, SPACs, and crypto. When the Fed raised rates from near-zero to ~5%, the mathematical reality hit: unprofitable companies' future cash flows get discounted more harshly, rendering many effectively valueless. This created a clear divide between management teams who 'found religion' — cutting costs and pursuing profitability — and those trying to avoid reality.

The insight extends beyond one cycle: any prolonged period of artificially cheap capital breeds a generation of companies and investors who mistake access to funding for business quality. The correction, when it comes, is both a destruction event and a quality filter. VCs who delay marking down portfolios create long-term reputational risk, while founders who embrace austerity emerge stronger. The pattern is structural, not situational — it will repeat whenever monetary policy creates and then withdraws artificial abundance.
