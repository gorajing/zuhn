Confirmation bias drives investors to seek information that supports their existing view rather than challenges it. Ritholtz reframes proper investment research: for every position you want to take, actively seek the strongest arguments against it. What are the risks? What could go wrong? What alternatives exist?

If after genuinely trying to disconfirm your thesis you cannot find a compelling counter-argument, you have a much stronger basis for the investment than if you had simply accumulated confirming evidence. He argues you should be able to argue both for and against every holding in your portfolio — if you cannot, you have not done real research. This principle extends far beyond investing: in any decision domain, actively seeking disconfirming evidence produces more robust conclusions than confirmation-seeking.
