Barings Bank survived 230 years, world wars, and revolutions. The Queen had an account there. It could raise capital easily based on its name alone. But behind this facade, Leeson described the trading floor as 'tacky' compared to Morgan Stanley, with outdated equipment and furniture. More critically, the bank had systemic incompetence in its controlling functions — executives who were 'supposed to understand derivatives' but didn't, £100M in uncollected debts that fell through the cracks, and auditors who accepted a forged document with a personal fax header.

The gap between reputation and operational reality is a form of technical debt that compounds silently. Barings' prestige actually accelerated its demise because it attracted ambitious traders like Leeson while simultaneously providing the trust-based cover that made fraud possible. A newer, less prestigious bank would have had tighter controls precisely because it couldn't afford to rely on name recognition.

This pattern maps to any legacy institution — universities, media companies, governments — where historical prestige substitutes for current operational excellence. The collapse, when it comes, appears sudden but was decades in the making.
