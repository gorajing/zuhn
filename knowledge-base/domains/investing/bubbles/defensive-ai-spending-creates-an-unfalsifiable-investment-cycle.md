The current AI investment pattern follows a distinctive 'defensive spending' logic: organizations pour capital into infrastructure, talent, and compute not because they've validated returns, but because they fear being left behind if the technology does deliver. This creates a perverse dynamic where the act of investing becomes its own justification.

Once millions are committed, admitting that results are underwhelming becomes organizationally impossible—it would mean acknowledging wasted capital and strategic misjudgment. So spending continues to 'save face,' and the narrative of inevitability strengthens precisely because so much money has been deployed. By the time anyone pauses to evaluate actual outcomes, the sunk costs have locked in the trajectory. This pattern closely mirrors previous technology bubbles and suggests that current AI valuations may reflect collective commitment more than demonstrated value.
