The cost of college has doubled while the median starting salary has remained essentially unchanged — meaning students now pay twice as much for the same economic output. This has produced $1.76 trillion in national student loan debt, exceeding the GDP of all but the top 10 countries. Unlike other debt, student loans cannot be discharged through bankruptcy, creating a permanent drag on earnings during the critical family-formation years of the late 20s through early 40s.

This mirrors classic bubble dynamics: the price of an asset disconnects from its underlying value while demand stays high due to social pressure and information asymmetry. The 'customers' (18-year-olds) have never earned a dollar and cannot comprehend the scope of $250,000 in post-tax repayment with interest. The practical framework is Buffett's investment question: it's not whether education adds value, but whether the price you pay is justified by the value you receive. At current tuition levels, the answer is increasingly no for non-elite institutions.
