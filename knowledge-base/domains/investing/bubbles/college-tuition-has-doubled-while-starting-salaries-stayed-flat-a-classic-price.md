The data Hormozi presents shows a textbook price-value divergence: college tuition has doubled in recent decades while the median starting salary for graduates has remained essentially flat. The result is $1.76 trillion in national student loan debt — more GDP than all but the top 10 countries. This debt is uniquely punitive because it cannot be discharged through bankruptcy, meaning 18-year-olds are making irreversible financial commitments they cannot comprehend.

This pattern mirrors other bubble dynamics: the product's price detaches from its delivered value, sustained by artificial demand drivers (social expectations, employer credential requirements) and the absence of market corrections (non-dischargeable debt prevents the natural feedback loop where bad investments get written off). The Warren Buffett investment framework Hormozi applies — the difference between what you pay and what you get — reveals that the same degree from a mid-tier school is now a fundamentally different financial proposition than it was a generation ago, even though the social narrative hasn't updated.
