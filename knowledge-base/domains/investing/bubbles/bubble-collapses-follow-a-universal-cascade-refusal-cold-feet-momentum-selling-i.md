The tulip bubble's collapse mechanism is remarkably instructive: buyers in North Holland refused to attend routine auctions. This was not a dramatic event — it was an absence. That absence signaled that the marginal buyer had evaporated, which caused mild selling, which caused more selling, until prices fell 99% in weeks.

This cascade pattern — quiet withdrawal → signal → cold feet → momentum selling → implosion — is the template for nearly every bubble collapse since. The trigger is almost never an external shock; it's the internal realization that the next buyer may not exist. This has implications for identifying bubble risk: watch for declining auction participation, reduced transaction volumes, or lengthening time-to-sale as leading indicators, rather than waiting for dramatic news events.
