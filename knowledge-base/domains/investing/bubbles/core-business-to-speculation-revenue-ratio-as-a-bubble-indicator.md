During the late 1980s, Japanese manufacturers including Nissan earned more revenue from speculative investments in real estate and stocks than from their actual products. This represents a measurable, objective signal that an economy has shifted from productive growth to speculative mania.

This ratio — core business revenue vs. speculative/financial revenue — could serve as a practical bubble detection heuristic applicable beyond Japan. When companies whose competitive advantage lies in manufacturing, technology, or services begin treating financial speculation as a primary profit center, it indicates that asset price appreciation has become easier and more lucrative than actual value creation. The signal is especially strong when it appears across multiple companies in different sectors simultaneously, suggesting a systemic rather than idiosyncratic shift.
