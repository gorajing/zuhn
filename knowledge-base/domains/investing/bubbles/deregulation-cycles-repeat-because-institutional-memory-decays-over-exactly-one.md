The Glass-Steagall Act was created in 1933 to prevent the speculation that caused the Great Depression, then repealed in 1999 — roughly one human lifetime later. Credit default swaps were illegal for a century after crashing markets in 1907, then re-legalized in 2000. By 2022, the same CDO structures reappeared under new names ('bespoke collateralized synthetic obligations') using corporate debt instead of mortgages.

This pattern suggests financial regulation has a natural half-life tied to generational turnover. The people who remember why a rule exists retire, and the next generation sees only the constraint without the context. For investors, this means treating 'this time is different' claims with extreme skepticism — the instruments change names but the underlying dynamic of leveraged speculation on mispriced risk is structurally immortal.
