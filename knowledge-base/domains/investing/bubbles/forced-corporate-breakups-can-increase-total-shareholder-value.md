The dissolution of Standard Oil in 1911 was intended as punishment, but it became the most profitable event in Rockefeller's career. He owned ~25% of Standard Oil when it was split into 34 independent companies and received proportional stock in each. When these companies began public trading, investors valued them collectively at nearly 3x what Standard Oil had been worth as a single entity.

This happened because markets apply a 'conglomerate discount' — a single massive entity is valued less than the sum of its parts because investors can't easily assess the value of individual businesses buried inside a conglomerate. Once separated, each company could be evaluated on its own merits, attract specialized investors, and pursue focused strategies. This pattern has repeated with AT&T's breakup, and is relevant to modern antitrust discussions around tech companies. For investors, forced breakups of monopolies can be buying opportunities rather than sell signals.
