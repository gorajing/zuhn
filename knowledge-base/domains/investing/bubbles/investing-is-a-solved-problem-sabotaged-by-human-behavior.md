Ritholtz opens with a provocative claim: investing is a problem that has been solved. Average returns for equities, fixed income, and inflation are well-documented over long periods. The wild card that torpedoes returns is human behavior — our evolutionary wiring for threat detection, action bias, and emotional decision-making.

Our brains evolved over 2 million years to survive on the savanna: detect threats, act quickly, notice bad news, ignore good news. These instincts are catastrophic for investing, which requires patience, inaction during crises, and comfort with exponential math that evolution never prepared us for. Ritholtz frames this as 'off-label brain use' — we are using an organ designed for survival to make financial decisions it was never built for.
