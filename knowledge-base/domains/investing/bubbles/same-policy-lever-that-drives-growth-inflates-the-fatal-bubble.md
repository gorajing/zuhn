Japan's Bank of Japan used 'window guidance' — quietly directing commercial banks to lend into key sectors like shipbuilding, steel, and electric power — as a core mechanism of the post-war economic miracle. The same system was repurposed after the Plaza Accord shock to flood the economy with cheap credit, but this time capital flowed into real estate and stock speculation rather than productive industry.

This pattern — a growth lever becoming a bubble lever — recurs across economic history. The tool itself isn't the problem; the problem is that success breeds confidence in the tool, which delays the recognition that conditions have changed and the same mechanism is now destructive. By the time Japan's authorities capped real estate lending in 1989, land prices had risen from 14.2 trillion yen (1969) to 2,000 trillion yen.
