Manuel Corpas's articulation of the negative spiral that traps health markets for underrepresented populations: (1) Data invisibility — countries in the global south lack the infrastructure and budget to produce health data sets at the scale needed for genomic research. (2) Epistemic invisibility — without data, there's less knowledge about diseases prevalent in these populations. (3) Less research output — less knowledge produces fewer publications, fewer clinical trials, fewer treatment guidelines. (4) Structural invisibility — with less knowledge and less research, these populations become less attractive for further investment. (5) Loop back to data invisibility — without continued investment, no new data is produced. Each cycle compounds the previous one, widening the gap. The role of AI: it amplifies the spiral rather than correcting it. Models are trained on the existing biased data (predominantly European-ancestry whole-genome data, predominantly Western diseases like cardiovascular and metabolic). Deployed in clinical settings, these models give predictably worse predictions for non-European patients (Manuel cites African-ancestry genetic-risk models losing up to 80% accuracy compared to European-ancestry models for the same diseases). This isn't a fixable bug — it's a structural feature of training on a biased corpus. The implication for capital allocators: 'just deploy more AI' is the wrong intervention. The intervention has to be upstream — invest in producing data from underrepresented populations. Manuel works with Peruvian genome projects specifically for this reason. Investments that close the data gap (biobanks in the global south, indigenous genome sequencing initiatives, neglected-tropical-disease registries) have non-linear returns because they break the compounding spiral. The pattern generalizes far beyond health: any market with high-data and low-data segments creates the same spiral. AI deployment in underserved segments WITHOUT upstream data investment will amplify existing inequities. The corollary for impact-oriented founders: the highest-leverage AI ventures may not be application-layer products serving well-represented markets — they may be data-generation platforms that bring underrepresented data into the global training corpus.
