Vitalik argues that ETH the asset and Ethereum the ecosystem are synergistic: the stronger the application ecosystem, the stronger ETH as an asset, and vice versa. Naval reinforces this by noting that the biggest stores of value in the world aren't gold or currencies — they're factories, stocks, houses, and other productive assets. This challenges the Bitcoin-as-digital-gold narrative by suggesting that productive assets with usage-driven demand may be more durable stores of value than inert ones.

This framework extends to platform tokens and equity valuations more broadly. Assets that serve dual purposes — both as a store of value and as a functional component of a growing ecosystem — benefit from a flywheel effect. The EIP-1559 burn mechanism makes this concrete: as ecosystem usage grows, ETH supply decreases, increasing scarcity. This is structurally different from gold, which has no mechanism linking its scarcity to productive usage. For investors evaluating any platform token or equity, the question becomes: does holding this asset passively benefit from the ecosystem's active usage?
