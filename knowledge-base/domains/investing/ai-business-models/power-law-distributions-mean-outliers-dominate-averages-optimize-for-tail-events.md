Normal distributions have well-defined averages and standard deviations where 95% of data falls within 2 standard deviations. But in power-law distributions — which govern income, startup returns, earthquake damage, and viral content — the standard deviation is infinite. The more you measure, the bigger the average gets, because occasionally you hit an extreme outlier that skews everything.

The St. Petersburg paradox illustrates this perfectly: a coin-flipping game with infinite expected value that no rational person would pay much to play. The expected value calculation is technically correct but practically useless because it's dominated by astronomically unlikely events. This has direct implications for investing and startups: venture capital returns follow a power law where one investment (Facebook, Google) generates more returns than the rest of the fund combined. Optimizing for average expected returns using normal-distribution intuition will systematically underweight the importance of finding and capturing tail events. The practical rule: in any power-law domain, spend disproportionate effort on positioning for the extreme upside rather than optimizing the median case.
