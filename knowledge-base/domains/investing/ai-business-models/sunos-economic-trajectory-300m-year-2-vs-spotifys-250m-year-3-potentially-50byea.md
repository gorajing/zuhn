Chris Anokute's economic math comparing Suno's trajectory to Spotify's: 'Suno already had $300M in revenue in the last 12 months — two years into the game. Spotify three years in (2011) was $250M revenue. 15 years later, Spotify is $19B revenue annually. So if you compare: Suno surpassing Spotify on revenue at year 2 vs year 3. If the AI music platforms follow Spotify's trajectory — a 65x growth in revenue over 15 years — Suno could be $20B revenue by 2040. Spotify pays out 70% of revenue to rights holders ($15B/year currently). Suno following the same model would pay out $14B+. The broader AI music category combined could be $50B/year in payouts.' Plus the licensing deals being negotiated now: 'The deals are getting done right now.' The wealth-transfer implication: the music industry's revenue base is fundamentally shifting. Pre-2025: streaming (Spotify, Apple Music, YouTube Music) dominates revenue, with ~$15B/year flowing from DSPs to rights holders. Post-2030: AI music platforms become a major additional revenue source, possibly approaching or exceeding the DSP era. The combined rights-holder payouts could be 2-3x current levels. The complicating factors that make this prediction uncertain: (1) IP litigation outcomes — if courts rule training data must be licensed, AI music platforms have higher cost structures and slower growth. (2) Quality ceiling — Suno's growth depends on continued improvement in AI music quality. Current models are good for background and short pop but not for 'serious' music. (3) Platform consolidation — Suno may be acquired/IPOed before reaching full scale, with different economic structures post-acquisition. (4) Spotify's own AI music push — if DSPs integrate AI music creation, the AI-only platforms may not capture the full upside. The corollary for capital allocators: the AI music space is a credible $50B+ category in formation. Bets on infrastructure (Suno, similar platforms), on rights aggregators who license favorably to AI platforms (existing labels with smart deals), and on tools that help creators capture AI-generated revenue (distributors, copyright enforcement) all have asymmetric upside. The corollary for working musicians: secure your IP rights now. The pending wealth transfer is real but only flows to creators who properly own and license their material. Creators who signed away rights to labels in pre-AI deals may have minimal recourse. The corollary for the recording industry: the AI deal-making happening in 2025-2027 will set the structural terms for the next 15 years of industry revenue. The labels and platforms that negotiate well now will dominate; those that delay or refuse to engage will be marginalized. This is the most consequential industry negotiating window since the Spotify era.
