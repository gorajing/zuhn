Rory O'Driscoll's framework via historical analogy: 'You have to face this question every compute revolution. Microsoft in the 90s — they dominated. They were the operating system. They dominated broad horizontal application software for the consumer and knowledge worker. The office suite. They dominated networking. But there were hundreds of application software companies on top of that: Siebel, Vantiv, Scopus, Clarify, Banner, SAP that said we run on top of Microsoft. We build specifically for this vertical or this horizontal use case. Microsoft tried. They bought Great Plains. They never really made it happen. Fast forward a decade later: Amazon, same kind of question. Does AWS eat everything? I know lots of brilliant investors who passed on Snowflake because they said Amazon Redshift is just going to eat the lunch. And it turns out it was a $50 billion thing. The default is the Microsoft outcome which is broad horizontal — the equivalent of broad horizontal compute is now broad horizontal intelligence — and that's going to be provided maybe not by the monopoly like Microsoft but the oligopoly of Anthropic, OpenAI, maybe Gemini.' Concrete data point: 'Anthropic released 10 financial agent templates, killing a load of YC companies in the process. They're scheduled to come out with a legal product which will challenge Harvey and Lagora.' But: 'I don't think there's any chance that Anthropic is going to do applications in CX, and I will bet you a lot of money they're not going to go all the way in legal at the app layer. Harvey costs $150,000 per year per law firm. I don't want to roll that into my $200/month Claude if there's any risk.' What distinguishes horizontal apps (eaten) from vertical workflow apps (survive): (1) Horizontal apps are 'mark up this document' or 'analyze this data' — anything an individual user does once that doesn't require multi-system integration. Labs eat these via templates and skills. (2) Vertical apps are coordinated workflows across enterprise systems (DocuSign integration, audit trails, custom field schemas, regulatory compliance). Labs don't have time/focus/relationships to deliver these. The corollary for builders: choose your wedge carefully. If your product IS 'apply this AI capability to this document type for an individual user,' you're competing with horizontal apps and will be replaced by a feature release. If your product IS 'orchestrate the legal-document-review workflow for a 500-lawyer firm with deep integrations,' you're protected by the integration moat. The corollary for capital allocators: when evaluating an AI startup, the key question is 'is this a horizontal feature that will be subsumed, or a vertical workflow with enterprise relationships?' Funding the former is a betting against the lab roadmap; funding the latter is betting on the operating-system-vs-applications historical pattern. The corollary for AI labs: don't try to build vertical apps. Provide the foundation models, distribution channels, and developer-friendly templates. Let the verticals build on top. The labs that try to do everything (OpenAI's brief Fiji-as-CEO-of-Applications experiment, walked back to 'CEO of AGI') will lose focus and underperform vs labs that stay in their lane.
