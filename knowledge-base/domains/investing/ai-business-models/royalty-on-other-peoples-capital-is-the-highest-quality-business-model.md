Ackman contrasts capital-light royalty businesses (American Express, Coca-Cola's formula licensing) with capital-heavy businesses (General Motors), showing that GM's stock barely moved over 50 years despite massive revenue because every dollar earned had to be reinvested in factories. Meanwhile, Amex collects ~3% of every transaction without deploying the underlying capital.

This framework is directly applicable to evaluating modern businesses: software platforms, payment processors, and marketplaces that take a cut of transactions are the contemporary equivalents. The key test is: does growth require proportional capital investment? If a company can double revenue without doubling its asset base, it has the royalty-like economics that compound wealth. This explains why asset-light tech platforms command premium valuations — they're the Coca-Colas of the digital era.
