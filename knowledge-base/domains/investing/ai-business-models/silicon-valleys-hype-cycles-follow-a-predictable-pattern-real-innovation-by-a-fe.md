Modern MBA traces a consistent pattern across tech hype cycles. A handful of genuine winners (Facebook, Google, Netflix, Amazon) achieve extraordinary results with a technology (data/algorithms). Their earnings snowball in ways the public markets have never seen. This creates irresistible social proof: if data was the moat for these leaders, why couldn't a nimbler startup apply the same playbook to a smaller industry?

Thousands of startups adopted the narrative. Every S-1 filing from the past 15 years prominently features the word 'data.' Groupon claimed 9 petabytes of data. Wayfair captured 4 terabytes daily. Blue Apron said they could predict individual taste profiles. Stitch Fix claimed more data on shoppers than any department store. Yet after a decade, none demonstrated anything meaningful -- Groupon, Yelp, Blue Apron, Stitch Fix, Lending Club, Casper, OpenDoor are all either penny stocks or bankrupt. When big data died, Silicon Valley moved the goalpost: first to machine learning, then to AI. The narrative changes; the structure persists.
