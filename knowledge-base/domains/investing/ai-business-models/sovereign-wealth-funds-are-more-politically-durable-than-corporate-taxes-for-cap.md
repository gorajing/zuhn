The core insight is about mechanism design for political durability. Corporate tax rates are trivially reversible — Trump cut them in 2017, and any future administration can do the same. But a sovereign wealth fund that holds broad market equity creates a constituency effect: dumping those shares would crash the stock market, making reversal politically toxic even for anti-redistribution politicians.

This matters enormously in the AI context because if capital's share of income keeps rising (labor share has already been declining since 2000), the redistribution mechanism needs to be robust across decades of shifting political winds. The Alaska Permanent Fund model demonstrates this — once citizens receive dividend checks from state-owned assets, the program becomes effectively permanent. A national version funded by broad market equity rather than resource extraction would capture AI-driven returns regardless of which specific companies win.
