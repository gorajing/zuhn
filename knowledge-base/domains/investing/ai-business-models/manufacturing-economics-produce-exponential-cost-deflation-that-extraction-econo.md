The fundamental distinction between extraction economics (coal, gas) and manufacturing economics (solar) explains why solar's cost advantage is structural rather than cyclical. Extraction requires ongoing opex to continuously procure and burn fuel, creating a cost floor. Manufacturing follows a learning curve where each doubling of cumulative production reduces unit costs by a predictable percentage.

This pattern—where a manufactured technology displaces an extracted resource—is a powerful mental model for identifying future disruptions. Any industry currently dependent on extracting scarce inputs is vulnerable to a manufactured alternative that can ride a learning curve. The solar precedent shows this transition can take decades but once the cost crossover occurs, the manufactured technology's advantage compounds while the extracted resource's costs remain flat or rise.
