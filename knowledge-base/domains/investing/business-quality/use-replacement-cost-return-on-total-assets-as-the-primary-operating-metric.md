Forrest Mars read TG Rose's 'Higher Control in Management' and adopted return on total assets (ROTA) as Mars's north star metric — but with a twist. Instead of measuring profits against the book value of factories (which gets artificially cheap as assets depreciate), Mars constantly revalues its fixed assets at their current replacement cost and measures profit against that. Every division and factory targets roughly 18% ROTA, meaning every investment must pay itself back in ~5 years.

The discipline cuts both ways. If a unit is below 18%, it's under-earning its capital. If it's above 18%, it's either overcharging customers or under-reinvesting in advertising, R&D, and manufacturing — both symptoms Forrest considered dangerous because they erode the long-term quality-for-money position.

The approach produces dramatic efficiency outcomes: in 1990, Mars generated $429K revenue per employee versus Hershey's $228K, with 30% fewer employees. Replacement-cost ROTA forces management to think like owners continuously evaluating 'if we had to build this factory today, would this investment still clear the bar?' — a much stronger filter than accounting-book returns.
