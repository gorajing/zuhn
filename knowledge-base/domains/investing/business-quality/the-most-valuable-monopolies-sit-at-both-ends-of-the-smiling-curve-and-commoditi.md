Ben Thompson's smiling curve says the most valuable positions in a technology stack are at the extremes — upstream IP/design and downstream customer interface — while the middle (manufacturing, assembly) is commoditized. Qualcomm is one of the purest executions of this in history. They own the core CDMA and later 4G/5G patents (the IP extreme) and they design the silicon that implements those patents (the chip-design extreme). They deliberately exited the base-station business (sold to Ericsson) and the handset business (sold to Kyocera) the moment they had bootstrapped adoption, because those were capital-intensive, low-margin commodity steps that were dragging down returns.

Crucially, they only captured the ends because they temporarily held the middle long enough to prove the system worked — you cannot commoditize a layer that nobody else is building yet. The sequence matters: vertically integrate to seed the market, then shed the low-margin layers and collect rents on the high-margin ones. Today ~85% of Qualcomm's $44B revenue comes from fabless semiconductors and the rest from licensing at ~69% EBIT margins — a structure only possible because they understood which layers of their own stack to walk away from.

The investing lens: when evaluating a tech-stack incumbent, ask which layers of their own stack they deliberately do not occupy. The ones that shed commodity layers early are playing the smiling curve; the ones that cling to vertical integration everywhere are usually confusing revenue with profit.
