Dimon is explicit that JP Morgan has been slightly less profitable than leverage-maximizing competitors in the good years. The trade is deliberate: 'a little bit less in good years, but in 08 and 09 we were fine and they weren't.' Pre-2007 many banks ran 30x equity leverage; most went bankrupt. JP Morgan was positioned to buy Bear Stearns, WaMu, and First Republic out of crises precisely because it had refused those returns earlier.

The mechanism is compounding through survivorship: a firm that loses 0 in a crisis while competitors lose 50% does not need to make back that 50% — it starts the next cycle from a higher base and can buy distressed assets from weaker hands. Short-term ROE comparisons miss this because they don't penalize ruin probability.

For any capital-intensive business, the implication is to measure 'earnings through the cycle' rather than peak-year earnings, and to treat leverage-driven ROE as borrowed from future drawdowns. The companies that win decade-length races are usually the ones that refused to run at the industry's risk frontier.
