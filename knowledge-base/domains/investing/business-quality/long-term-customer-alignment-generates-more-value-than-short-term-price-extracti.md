Epic's pricing discipline looks irrational on a standard revenue-capture framework: they give away new modules to existing customers for free, have never raised MyChart's price, raise overall prices below inflation (~2% yearly), and probably capture less than 20% of the total value of a deployment once you include implementation consultants and internal admin staff. By aggressive SaaS standards they are leaving billions on the table.

But the Bezos framing they echo—'an irrational and irresponsible decision not to use us'—reveals the strategy. Every dollar of price restraint is a dollar that would otherwise appear on a CFO's switching-analysis spreadsheet. By keeping the number small enough to be ignored, Epic ensures that no customer ever has a rational reason to even start an RFP for alternatives. Across 607 customers and 47 years, they have lost exactly one customer for six months before that customer came back. That retention is worth far more than any plausible price increase.

The non-obvious lesson for evaluating 'wide moat' businesses: when analyzing a company with dominant market position, persistent under-monetization is often a feature, not a bug. It is the mechanism by which the moat is maintained. Investors who see 'pricing power not yet captured' should ask whether capturing it would destroy the conditions of durability. Epic could probably double its take rate; doing so would be the first domino toward losing customers who had spent decades never questioning the vendor.
