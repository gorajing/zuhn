When every other graphics card company in the 1990s let downstream partners write their drivers, Nvidia insisted on writing their own. This looked like unnecessary fixed cost overhead at the time — employing teams to support every OS, every board, every OEM. But it accumulated something rare: low-level systems programming talent inside a chip company. That talent base made CUDA possible a decade later. It also gave Nvidia control over the end-user experience when competitors were shipping broken driver stacks.

The long-tail payoff is that Nvidia no longer sells chips — it sells 'solutions' bundled with CUDA, 150+ SDKs, and proprietary libraries. Gross margins went from 30% in 1999 to 50% in 2014 to 66% today. That margin expansion is entirely attributable to the software stack, which is given away free but runs only on Nvidia hardware. Jensen's quote captures it: 'You say solutions, I hear gross margin.'

The lesson for evaluating hardware businesses: look for ones that took on 'unnecessary' software fixed cost early. Those costs aren't overhead — they're the beginning of a platform. Companies that outsource drivers, SDKs, and developer tooling are permanently at the commodity end of the smiling curve, while vertically integrated ones can eventually price like software.
