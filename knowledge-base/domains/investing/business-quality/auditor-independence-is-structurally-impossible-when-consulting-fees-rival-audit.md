Arthur Andersen made roughly $50M/year from Enron, split nearly evenly between audit/tax fees and consulting fees. Audit clients are sticky and slow to switch; consulting engagements can be terminated overnight. The math meant Andersen partners had a continuous incentive to keep Enron management happy on every accounting judgment call, because half their economics could disappear with one phone call.

This is not a story about bad apples — it's a story about an incentive structure that guarantees capture. No code of conduct, ethics training, or 'Chinese wall' between audit and consulting practices can survive a situation where the auditing partner knows that being firm will cost the firm millions in consulting revenue and possibly his career. The only fix is structural separation, which is exactly what Sarbanes-Oxley imposed after Enron's collapse.

The principle extends to any 'independent' validator paid by the entity being validated: credit rating agencies during the 2008 mortgage crisis, sell-side analysts whose firms underwrite the companies they cover, board directors whose fees come from the CEO they're supposed to monitor. When evaluating any third-party assurance, the first question should be: how much non-assurance revenue does the validator earn from this relationship, and how easily can the relationship be terminated?
