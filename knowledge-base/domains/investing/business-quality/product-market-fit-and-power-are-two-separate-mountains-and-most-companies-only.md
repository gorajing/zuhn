By 2004 Nvidia was the fastest semiconductor to hit $1B in revenue, had 83% of the discrete GPU market, and was in the S&P 500 — and still only commanded 29% gross margins. Every Moore's-law-driven performance gain was being competed away because their closest competitors shipped essentially identical commodity cards running the same Microsoft-controlled APIs. Top-line revenue and market share were masking the fact that the business had no durable pricing power.

The framework here (from Hamilton Helmer's Seven Powers) is that product-market fit and power are two separate climbs. Finding PMF means solving 'people will buy this.' Finding power means solving 'people will keep paying us a premium even when competitors exist.' The second problem is not a continuation of the first — it's a different invention. For Nvidia, power eventually came from programmable shaders + a proprietary developer platform (CG → CUDA) that created switching costs — but that was a second bet-the-company transformation, not a natural evolution.

The investing implication is that revenue growth and market share are lagging, unreliable signals of durable business quality. Gross margins and pricing trajectory are the real test of whether a company has crossed from PMF to power. Many venture-scale outcomes with large TAM produce companies that ship a lot of product and capture almost no economic profit.
