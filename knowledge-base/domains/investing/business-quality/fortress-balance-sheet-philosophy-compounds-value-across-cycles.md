JPMorgan's 'fortress balance sheet' philosophy appears conservative but is actually aggressive: by holding ~$40 billion in excess capital (earning only 4% after-tax) and $1.4+ trillion in liquid assets, the firm can deploy capital opportunistically when competitors are distressed. This produced 14.1% compounded annual returns since 2000 (vs 8.0% for S&P 500), 8 consecutive record revenue years, and the ability to absorb First Republic during a crisis.

The philosophy has specific operational elements: never run the company to maximize short-term stock price, maintain liquidity well beyond regulatory requirements, stress test far more scenarios than CCAR requires (weekly, not annually), and always prefer deploying capital at attractive prices over share buybacks at elevated prices. Dimon explicitly notes they have options for the $40B in excess capital but 'are clear-eyed that many asset prices, including bank stocks, are fully valued.'

The broader principle: strength in good times is the source of opportunity in bad times. Companies (and investors) who optimize for upside capture in good times leave themselves unable to capitalize on the best opportunities, which only appear during crises.
