Sinegal's 'ketchup at $1.03' parable captures the discipline: a 3% price hike would have added 50% to pre-tax income, but he refused because 'it's like heroin — you do it a little and you want a little more.' The cap is enforced not because Costco can't charge more but because charging more would erode the only thing that actually compounds: member trust that they are getting the absolute best price available anywhere. Suppliers know Costco won't pocket the savings, so they negotiate in good faith. Members know prices won't drift, so they renew at 93%. Wall Street rewards this with a multiple far above Walmart's despite lower nominal margins.

The deeper investing lesson is that 'latent margin' — profit a company could take but chooses not to — is itself a balance sheet asset. It funds durability instead of quarterly EPS. Charlie Munger's love of Costco isn't about the current P&L; it's about the certainty that the same business will exist in 30 years because management treats every dollar of foregone margin as an investment in the next decade of cash flows.

The trap most CEOs fall into is that capped-margin discipline looks identical to leaving money on the table on any single day. It only pays off when you refuse to cheat for 40 consecutive years.
