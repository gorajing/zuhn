China has spent tens of billions trying to build SMIC into a leading-edge foundry. The US is subsidizing TSMC Arizona and Intel Foundry with CHIPS Act dollars. Neither can close the gap at 3nm, and Acquired makes the case for why: the operators of these machines represent a tiny population of humans, and the process knowledge needed to actually get yield off an EUV line has to be learned in production, on real wafers, across many cycles. ASML will sell machines to anyone the Dutch government allows — but the machines don't know how to make chips, only TSMC's people do.

This is different from the moats most investors think about. Network effects can be bought out (Facebook buying Instagram), brands can be rebuilt (Apple), and scale economies erode when demand shifts. Process power that has been compounding for decades is uniquely unshortcuttable because the path-dependency is intrinsic to the technology — each generation of chips is built on yield lessons from the prior generation's manufacturing, and you can't skip steps. Hamilton Helmer's process power category is underweighted in most moat analysis, and TSMC is the cleanest example of why it matters more than branding or network effects for long-term margin durability.

Apply this when evaluating incumbents in any technical manufacturing business (batteries, lithography, precision optics, advanced materials): ask whether the learning curve is path-dependent and how many years of continuous operation a competitor would need to match current yield. If the answer is 'more than a decade,' the moat is real regardless of what capex competitors are announcing.
