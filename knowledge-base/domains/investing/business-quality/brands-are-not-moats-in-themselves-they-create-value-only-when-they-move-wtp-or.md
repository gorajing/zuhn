Mauboussin argues that brands are commonly but incorrectly cited as sources of sustainable value creation. Plotting Interbrand's 2024 top 25 global brands against their 2023 ROIC-WACC spreads reveals no reliable relationship: Samsung (#4 brand) has a near-zero spread, Toyota (#5) and Mercedes-Benz (#7) have modest spreads, while Apple (#1) and Microsoft (#2) have large positive spreads. A well-known brand does not always create value.

The analytical framework demands specificity: does the brand increase WTP by conferring status (Tiffany's $10,000 premium over Costco for an equivalent diamond ring), by reducing risk and search costs (Munger's Wrigley example), or by creating habitual purchasing? Does it lower WTS by securing better terms from suppliers (Walmart's shelf-space leverage)? Christensen's 'jobs to be done' lens applies: a brand is valuable to the degree that customers 'hire' it for a specific job. The proliferation of brands has made differentiation increasingly difficult.
