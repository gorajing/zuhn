James Bessen's research identifies three developments that allowed superstar firms to pull away: (1) massive investment in proprietary software as an intangible asset, growing faster than R&D or advertising spend; (2) this software enables both scale economies AND market differentiation simultaneously, breaking the traditional tradeoff; and (3) the software diffuses at a slower rate than past innovations because it is complex, customized, and firms have no incentive to share it.

This creates a compounding moat: proprietary software raises both minimum efficient scale and capital requirements for potential challengers. Companies invest roughly twice as much in intangible assets as tangible assets today. The firms with the highest intangible investment have achieved better ROICs, growth, and productivity than peers. GenAI may amplify these existing advantages rather than create new ones, as strategists argue it 'won't be a source of competitive advantage for any one of them' but will 'amplify existing competitive advantages.'
