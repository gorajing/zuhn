David articulates a blunt distinction that most founder and investor writing blurs: early-stage venture is an art of picking founders at the napkin stage where you're really just dreaming up what a market might look like, while growth-stage venture is an access business where the diligence is mostly commodity and the scarce asset is getting into the round. Acquired's whole investment fund thesis rests on this: they pre-qualify their sponsors editorially, and then they just ask to invest. The round is usually $300M so a couple million from them is trivial from a cap table standpoint, but it aligns incentives and lets them participate in outcomes they already identified through the show.

The implication for anyone thinking about venture as a career is that the two stages are almost unrelated skills. Early stage rewards founder judgment and conviction in the absence of data. Growth stage rewards relationships, reputation, and having something the founder wants beyond the check — which is exactly what Acquired's distribution and brand provide. If you don't have a reason to be let in, your diligence doesn't matter.

Apply this when evaluating whether to build a growth fund: what do you uniquely offer that makes founders give you allocation when the round is oversubscribed? If the answer is "great analysis," you don't have a business. If the answer is "I am the person they want on their cap table for brand, distribution, or a specific unlock," you do.
