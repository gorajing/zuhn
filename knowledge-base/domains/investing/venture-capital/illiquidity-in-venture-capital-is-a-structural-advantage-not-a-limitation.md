Venture capital's illiquid nature is often cited as a drawback compared to public markets, but it serves a critical structural function. By locking up capital for extended periods, VC funds can back companies through the long development cycles needed to build transformative businesses without pressure to generate quarterly returns.

This illiquidity premium is what enables VCs to capture outsized returns from power law outcomes. If VC capital were liquid, fund managers would face redemption pressures during downturns precisely when the best buying opportunities emerge. The lock-up structure forces disciplined, long-horizon investing that aligns GP and LP interests around genuine value creation rather than mark-to-market performance.
