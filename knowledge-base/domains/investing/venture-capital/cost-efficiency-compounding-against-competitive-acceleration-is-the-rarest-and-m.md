Classical competitive dynamics allow one primary advantage at a time: the market leader is faster-growing but more expensive (Uber vs Lyft), or the cost-efficient challenger grows slower (Costco vs Whole Foods). Anthropic's 2025-2026 position against OpenAI reverses this pattern: 3.3x revenue growth in 4 months AND a training-cost structure 4x more efficient. The compounding is bidirectional — cost savings fund more experimentation which drives more revenue which funds more compute which extends the cost lead. Jason Lemkin names this as a rare fact pattern that usually predicts near-certain long-term victory for the lower-cost challenger unless the incumbent can dramatically change one of the axes. OpenAI's response (management reboot, aggressive fundraising) is attempting exactly that — but management changes do not fix cost structure. The practical implication for investors and competitive-intelligence analysts: when you observe both-axis compounding, weight your probability of the challenger's long-term win substantially higher than consensus valuations suggest. Public-market analogue: OpenAI at ~$870B valuation versus Anthropic at ~$370B produced a short-OpenAI long-Anthropic trade that would have been obvious in the public markets.
