The mythology of venture capital celebrates the 'vision bet' — backing an unproven founder with no product. But Benchmark's best deal ever was the opposite: eBay was growing 10% month-over-month, was profitable, and was drowning in mailed-in checks when they invested. Pierre was 'de-risked on every metric that mattered and risky on every metric that didn't' — he wasn't a 'proper CEO', the product was ugly, the use case (collectibles auctions) sounded silly, and the technology was held together with duct tape. The deal was non-consensus because everyone else was pattern-matching on surface signals (CEO pedigree, category seriousness, UI polish) instead of the numbers.

This flips the usual risk model. Conventional VCs look for 'big vision + credible team, bet on traction later.' The highest-return pattern is the reverse: 'traction now + non-obvious packaging, ignore the packaging.' The bet isn't whether the thing will work — it already works. The bet is whether the thing working at small scale will work at large scale, which is a much narrower question with much better odds.

Application: when evaluating any opportunity — not just investments but hiring, career moves, product bets — distinguish between 'does the signal of success look respectable' and 'is the thing actually working.' Bet on actually-working. The opportunities with prestige-signal mismatch are precisely where mispricing concentrates, because prestige-driven actors have already self-excluded.
