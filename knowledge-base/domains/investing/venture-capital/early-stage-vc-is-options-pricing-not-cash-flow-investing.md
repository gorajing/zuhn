Classical Ben Graham investing values a series of cash flows discounted to present value. But early-stage VC has no cash flows — just a founder and an idea. The math people use to justify $20M valuations on napkins is voodoo when framed as cash flow analysis. Michael Mauboussin's reframe: stop trying to DCF and recognize that you're buying an option on a wide distribution of outcomes.

This explains three otherwise puzzling behaviors of VCs: (1) obsession with TAM — option value is most sensitive to the magnitude of possible upside, (2) portfolio diversification — wide outcome distributions require many bets to smooth variance while preserving asymmetric upside, and (3) why the industry tolerates 'irrational' prices — the question isn't 'what's this worth today' but 'what should I pay for an X% chance this becomes the next Apple.'

The critical corollary from Honam at Altos: don't mistake startups for lottery tickets. Founders have lives and families, and VC is a multi-turn game. How you behave when an option expires worthless determines your reputation in the next round.
