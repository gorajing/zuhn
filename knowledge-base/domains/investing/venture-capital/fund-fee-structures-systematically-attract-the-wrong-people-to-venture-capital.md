Munger argues venture capital is 'very poorly done' as a category, and the root cause is structural: fund fees provide a living whether or not returns materialize. 'You really shouldn't be in the business of charging extra fees unless you really are going to achieve very unusual results. And of course it's more easy to say that you can get good results than to actually get them. So it attracts the wrong people.'

The downstream effect is twofold. First, founders usually end up hating their VCs — Munger says 'by and large... people in the business doing the work, more than not they hate the venture capitalists. They don't feel they're their partner.' Second, endowments and LPs end up underperforming because fees compound against them even when the underlying picks don't compound in their favor. The world is full of ex-Goldman partners running billion-dollar funds charging two-and-twenty who generate handsome livings for themselves while their LPs get mediocre returns.

The implication is that fee structure is not a minor design detail — it's the selection function that determines who enters the industry. Fix the fee structure and you change the population of people willing to do the work. Munger notes endowments are starting to push back (some negotiating fees in half with extra pari passu co-investment), which changes who stays in the game.
