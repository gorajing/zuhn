In public markets, beta measures a stock's volatility relative to its index. Venture has no direct equivalent, but portfolio overlap between funds serves as a useful proxy. Social Capital's analysis showed peer firms were 1.7x to 3.1x more correlated to the broader VC pack than their own portfolio. When market conditions shift—like a rising rate environment—highly correlated portfolios get whipsawed together because their valuations were inflated by the same cohort of funds bidding each other up across rounds.

This reframes what many VCs assumed was alpha (skill in picking winners) as beta (riding the same wave as everyone else). A deliberately uncorrelated portfolio acts as a hedge: valuations are less likely to be artificially inflated by herd bidding, making TVPI-to-DPI conversion more reliable. The practical implication is that contrarian positioning isn't just a style preference—it's a structural risk management strategy.
