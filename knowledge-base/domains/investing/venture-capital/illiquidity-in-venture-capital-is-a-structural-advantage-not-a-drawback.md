Illiquidity is typically framed as a disadvantage — capital locked up for 7-10 years with no exit flexibility. But this framing misses that the illiquidity premium exists precisely because most allocators avoid or discount illiquid assets. The structural unwillingness of most capital to accept long lock-ups creates a persistent supply-demand imbalance that rewards patient investors.

This reframe matters for both LPs and founders. LPs who can genuinely tolerate illiquidity have a structural edge over those who need quarterly liquidity. For founders, it means VC-backed companies can pursue longer time horizons without the quarterly earnings pressure that constrains public companies. The illiquidity isn't a bug to be engineered away — it's the mechanism that enables venture-scale returns.
