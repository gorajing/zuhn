a16z's early-stage philosophy (rooted in Khosla Ventures' approach) explicitly rejects targeting a low loss ratio. Their historical early-stage funds carry a ~60% loss ratio — 60% of deals don't return capital — and they regard a peer's pride in 'never having lost money on a deal' as a horrible data point that reveals insufficient risk-taking. The framework: in any major space with multiple talented entrepreneurs, real tailwinds, and a positive technology view, pick the best founder and back the market leader. If the space works and you have the leader, excellent; if the space fails but you had the leader, no harm, no foul — that is the business. The only truly bad outcome ('the bad box') is when the space works and you backed the wrong company; those are the cases to scrutinize hardest.

This matters acutely in 2026 AI because recent loss ratios have been artificially low — single-digit percentages — as established firms mark up seed rounds months later. The speakers flag this as unsustainable: the laws of gravity will reassert and loss ratios will rise. An investor optimizing for a low loss ratio in this environment is mistaking a temporary markup regime for skill, and is structurally under-exposed to the power-law winners that justify the whole portfolio.

The transferable principle beyond venture: when returns are governed by power laws, minimizing the failure rate optimizes the wrong variable. The objective is maximizing exposure to the extreme right tail, which necessarily entails a high base rate of losses.
