The conventional view treats illiquidity as a cost that LPs must endure for potential upside. But illiquidity functions as a feature rather than a bug in venture capital: it acts as a commitment device that prevents panic selling during downturns, filters out short-term-oriented capital that would create misaligned incentives, and enables founders to build without quarterly earnings pressure. The illiquidity premium exists precisely because most capital cannot tolerate the lock-up periods — those who can are compensated with returns that liquid market participants structurally cannot access. This framing suggests that attempts to add liquidity to venture (via secondary markets or tokenization) may inadvertently destroy the very mechanism that generates excess returns.
