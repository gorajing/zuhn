Benchmark's religious commitment to equal economics looks like a fairness stance but functions as an extreme filter on who can join. Because there are no junior tiers and no way to 'notch down' to 90% effort, anyone who can't sustain peak output either self-selects out or is gently rotated into LP status. The obvious failure mode is mediocrity — if incentives flatten outcomes, free-riding dominates — which is why most firms that copied the structure drifted into the 'squishy middle.'

The non-obvious mechanism is that equal economics remove the secondary incentive of 'becoming senior,' which in normal firms poisons trust (Do I send this great exec candidate to my partner's portfolio company or hoard them for mine?). When no one can out-rank anyone, coordination costs collapse and the partnership can genuinely cooperate on deals. But this only holds if every partner actually operates at world-class level — the very thing most partnerships cannot guarantee.

Application: before copying a flat-economics structure, ask whether you can maintain a ruthless bar for who stays in the equal slot. Without that bar, flat economics is worse than hierarchy, because it flattens rewards without flattening effort. The right question isn't 'is this fair' but 'can we sustain the recruiting discipline this structure demands.'
