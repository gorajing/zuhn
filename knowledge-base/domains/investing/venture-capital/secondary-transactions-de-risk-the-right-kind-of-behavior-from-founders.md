When Benchmark led eBay's series A, Pierre Omidyar and Jeffrey Skoll had a competing 50M acquisition offer from Knight Ridder. For two people a year into a profitable side project, 50M is life-changing money — the rational individual choice is to sell. Benchmark reportedly structured the deal with equity-backed loans to both founders (~750K each), effectively giving them a partial secondary. Whether the exact number is right, the mechanism is clear: remove the founders' personal financial desperation so they can make the bet that's actually right for the company.

This was considered scandalous at the time — 'this looks like paying the founders to not sell' — and was criticized again during the clubhouse deal. But the arithmetic is overwhelming: if secondary unlocks a 1000x outcome instead of a 10x outcome, every dollar spent on founder liquidity returns hundreds. The risk isn't that founders 'get comfortable' — it's that they're forced to make a company-scale decision with personal-scale stakes on the table.

Application: in any deal where founders have a large personal-wealth gap and the company has a plausible generational outcome, secondary is a positive-expected-value instrument, not a perk. The question isn't 'should we let them take money off' but 'is the incentive misalignment we're letting persist worth more than the dilution?'
