Daniel Lubetzky deliberately avoids direct AI startup investments despite having the deal flow and capital to participate. His reasoning: the AI startup landscape will be a 'bloodbath' where a few companies become enormous but most fail completely. Rather than trying to pick winners in a sector defined by power-law outcomes, he invests in only six funds he considers world-class, each backing hundreds of companies.

This strategy exploits a structural asymmetry: in winner-take-most markets, the cost of missing the winner through direct investing is catastrophic, while the cost of paying fund management fees is modest relative to the diversification benefit. The approach also addresses the fund quality decay problem — Lubetzky notes that 'most funds descend into mediocrity' as they accumulate AUM and lose hunger, so he maintains extreme selectivity even at the fund level.

The broader principle extends beyond AI: whenever a sector has extreme outcome dispersion and low predictability of individual winners, delegating selection to disciplined specialist managers while maintaining the rest in index funds (he uses VTI) produces a barbell portfolio that captures upside without concentrated downside risk.
