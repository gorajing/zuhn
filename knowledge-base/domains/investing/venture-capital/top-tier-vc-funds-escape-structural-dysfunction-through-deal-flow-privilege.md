Graham observes that the few VCs he genuinely likes — Mike Moritz, John Doerr — work at the very top funds. His explanation mirrors a social-hierarchy dynamic: just as the most popular kids don't need to bully, the best VCs don't need to engage in the predatory behaviors that the fund model otherwise incentivizes. They get first pick of the best companies, which means their investments are more likely to succeed without heavy intervention.

This creates a self-reinforcing loop: top funds attract the best founders, produce the best returns, attract more top founders, and can thus afford to be founder-friendly. Lower-tier funds, lacking this deal-flow advantage, must compete harder, act more aggressively, and micromanage more — which further damages their reputation and perpetuates the cycle. For founders, this means the quality gap between top-tier and mid-tier VCs is structural, not just reputational.
