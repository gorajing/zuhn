Chamath Palihapitiya's $380M loss on Relativity Space after a recapitalization event—reportedly despite a $3B acquisition offer from Eric Schmidt—is a stark lesson in deep-tech venture concentration risk. Unlike software startups that can pivot quickly, deep-tech companies (rockets, biotech, fusion) have long development cycles where capital burns continuously and pivots are expensive or impossible.

The lesson is twofold: first, deep-tech portfolio construction requires more diversification than software VC because individual positions carry higher binary risk. Second, the decision to reject acquisition offers in favor of continued independence can be catastrophically wrong when the underlying technology timeline slips. For LPs and fund managers, this reinforces that deep-tech allocation should be sized for total loss scenarios, not expected-value outcomes.
