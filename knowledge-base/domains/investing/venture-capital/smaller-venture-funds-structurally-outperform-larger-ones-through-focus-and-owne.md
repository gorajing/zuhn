The persistent outperformance of smaller venture funds challenges the assumption that more capital means more power. Smaller funds can take larger ownership stakes in portfolio companies, maintain tighter thesis discipline, and move faster than institutional mega-funds constrained by committee structures and LP optics.

This structural advantage compounds: higher ownership means more meaningful returns from breakout winners, while tighter focus means fewer dilutive follow-ons in mediocre performers. The data from Q1 2025 continues to validate what practitioners like James Sedgwick-Heath have argued—staying small is a deliberate strategic advantage, not a limitation to overcome.

For LPs and allocators, this suggests optimal VC portfolio construction should include meaningful small-fund exposure rather than concentrating in marquee names. For fund managers, resisting the pressure to raise larger successor funds may be the highest-conviction move available.
