Venture capital returns are not normally distributed — they follow a power law where a small number of outlier investments drive the vast majority of fund performance. This means the difference between top-quartile and median funds isn't incremental deal quality but rather structural portfolio decisions: how many bets to make, how much to reserve for follow-ons, and whether to double down on winners.

This has direct implications for anyone evaluating VC as an asset class or building a startup. For investors, it means fund selection matters enormously — the gap between best and worst funds is far wider than in other asset classes. For founders, it means VCs are rationally incentivized to push for massive outcomes even at the cost of higher failure rates, because modest exits barely move fund-level returns.
