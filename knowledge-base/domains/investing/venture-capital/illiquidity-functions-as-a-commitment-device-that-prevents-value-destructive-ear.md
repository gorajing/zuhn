In public markets, liquidity enables investors to act on fear and impatience, frequently selling winners too early and crystallizing losses at the worst times. Venture capital's structural illiquidity removes this failure mode entirely. LPs cannot panic-sell their fund positions during downturns, and GPs cannot be pressured into premature exits by mark-to-market anxiety.

This forced patience aligns perfectly with the power law nature of venture returns, where the biggest winners often take 7-12 years to fully compound. The inability to exit early is not a bug but a behavioral guardrail that protects investors from their own short-term instincts. This insight extends beyond VC — any investment strategy where value creation is nonlinear and back-loaded benefits from structural illiquidity as a commitment mechanism.
