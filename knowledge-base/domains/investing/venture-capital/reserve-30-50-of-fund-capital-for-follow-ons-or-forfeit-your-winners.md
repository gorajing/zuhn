Fund architecture determines return capture more than deal selection. A $20M seed fund writing $500K checks cannot realistically participate in a $5M Series C allocation without consuming 25% of the entire fund. The structural solution is pre-planned reserves: setting aside 30-50% of committed capital exclusively for follow-ons. Firms like Khosla Ventures and Y Combinator's former Continuity Fund institutionalized this, with YC automatically exercising pro-rata rights up to a set valuation threshold. The alternative structures — opportunity funds (separate capital pools for breakout follow-ons) and SPVs (single-purpose vehicles that syndicate deal-by-deal capital from external backers) — give smaller funds flexibility without restructuring their core strategy. The key insight is that follow-on capacity must be designed into fund structure from Day 1, not improvised when a portfolio company breaks out.
