Nvidia was funded in 1993 alongside roughly 89 other graphics-card startups. Sequoia and Sutter Hill invested $2M at a $6M post-money valuation on a thesis that was wrong in almost every specific: wrong polygon primitive (quadrilaterals, not triangles), wrong cost assumptions (designed for expensive memory), wrong ecosystem strategy (fight Microsoft instead of adopt DirectX), wrong primary customer (Sega, who walked away). Only two of the original 90 funded startups survived, and the other one (ATI) was a Canadian bootstrapped company that wasn't even in the Silicon Valley funding frenzy.

The returns came from Jensen's willingness to scrap the original vision entirely — three separate bet-the-company reinventions. The investors were 'wrong intellectually and yet they were right' because what they actually bought wasn't the plan, it was the founder's capacity for ruthless self-examination under existential pressure. The plan was always going to be falsified; the question was whether the team would fold or rebuild.

The generalizable venture lesson: in proliferation-phase markets (many startups, ambiguous winners, rapid technology change), underwriting the thesis is futile because the thesis will be wrong. Underwrite the founder's capacity to survive being wrong. This also explains why waiting until consolidation-phase to invest produces lower returns despite lower risk — by the time the winners are legible, the mispricing is gone.
