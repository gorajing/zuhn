Conventional wisdom treats illiquidity as a cost that investors must be compensated for via higher returns. But in venture capital, illiquidity may actually be a feature that improves outcomes by functioning as a commitment device. When investors cannot easily sell their positions, they are structurally prevented from the behavioral mistakes — panic selling, short-term thinking, overreacting to temporary setbacks — that destroy returns in liquid markets.

This reframing has broader implications beyond VC. Any investment structure that locks capital forces patience, and patience is the scarcest resource in investing. The illiquidity premium isn't just compensation for inconvenience — it's partly a behavioral alpha generated by removing the option to make bad decisions. This principle connects to psychological research on commitment devices: sometimes removing choices improves outcomes.
