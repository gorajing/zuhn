The most consequential forces affecting VC behavior begin with limited partners, not general partners. LPs aren't betting on startups—they're betting on fund managers to deliver risk-adjusted returns across 10-year horizons. In that calculus, consistency matters more than outliers. A steady 2.5x from a scaled, brand-name firm looks more attractive than scattershot 5x-or-zero outcomes.

This LP preference is what designs fund strategy at scale. Institutions now allocate to large venture firms the same way they allocate to public equity or growth funds. The more a fund looks like a portfolio optimizer, the easier it is to justify on an investment committee slide. This makes consensus capital self-reinforcing: LPs want dependable innovation exposure, GPs want predictable downstream capital, and the loop tightens.

Meanwhile, emerging managers practicing conviction-driven VC face the toughest fundraising environment in over a decade. Even with strong DPI and realized alpha, many struggle to raise follow-on vehicles. When capital formation tilts toward scale, fewer ideas get funded and more bets look the same—not because GPs lack courage, but because the LP layer constrains where risk tolerance begins and ends.
