Quantum computing faces multiple simultaneous scaling constraints: hardware is expensive and fragile, only a small set of algorithms show advantages over classical approaches, and qubits remain highly error-prone. Cryptographically relevant attacks alone require thousands to tens of thousands of logical qubits with very low error rates — no existing system is close.

For investors, this means the gap between 'technically possible' and 'practically achievable' is where most value destruction occurs. The technology sits in a zone where headlines isolate breakthroughs or failures without showing how hardware, algorithms, and timelines interact. Informed positioning requires tracking concrete engineering milestones rather than theoretical proofs of advantage.
