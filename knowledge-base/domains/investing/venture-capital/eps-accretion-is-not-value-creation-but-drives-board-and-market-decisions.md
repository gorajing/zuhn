When an acquirer buys a company using stock, the pro forma EPS changes based on the combined earnings divided by the new share count. If the target's P/E ratio is lower than the acquirer's, EPS mechanically increases (accretion) — even if zero operational synergies exist. This is pure arithmetic, not value creation. Yet public markets reward EPS accretion with higher stock prices, and private boards use it as a key decision metric.

This creates a real strategic dynamic: deals can be structured specifically to manufacture EPS accretion through payment mix choices. A stock-heavy acquisition of a lower-P/E target is accretive by construction. Understanding this distinction — that EPS accretion is a feature of deal structure, not operational improvement — is essential for evaluating whether an acquisition actually creates value or merely rearranges accounting metrics. The model makes this visible by showing pro forma EPS for each scenario alongside the structural choices that produced it.
