Unlike public markets where investors can exit at any moment, venture capital locks up capital for 7-10 years. This is commonly framed as a disadvantage requiring a liquidity premium. But the illiquidity also produces structural benefits: it forces investors to truly back companies through adversity rather than selling at the first sign of trouble, aligns VC incentives with founder timelines, and prevents the short-termism that plagues public markets. The lock-up acts as a commitment device — investors who would otherwise exit early at a modest gain are forced to hold through the power-law inflection points that generate fund-returning outcomes.
