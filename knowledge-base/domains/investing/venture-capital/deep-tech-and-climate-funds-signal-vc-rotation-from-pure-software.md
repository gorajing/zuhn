The newsletter catalogs a notable pattern in recent fund launches: Sofinnova's €165M biotech fund, Soulmates' €50M climate fund, Motion's $100M maritime/logistics fund, and Pillar's $175M deep-tech fund all closed in the same period. This clustering isn't coincidental — it reflects a thesis shift among LPs and GPs that software's easy-money era is ending as AI commoditizes code.

The rotation makes strategic sense: deep tech companies have defensibility through patents, regulatory approvals, and physical infrastructure that AI can't easily replicate. For founders, this means capital availability is shifting — pure SaaS pitches face more scrutiny while hardware-software hybrid plays, biotech platforms, and climate solutions find more receptive investors. The trade-off is longer development cycles and higher capital requirements, which suits the patient capital these funds are structured to deploy.
