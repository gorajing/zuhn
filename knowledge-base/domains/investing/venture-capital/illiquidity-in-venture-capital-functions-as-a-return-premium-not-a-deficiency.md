While illiquidity is often framed as a drawback of venture investing, it actually serves as a competitive moat and return driver. The inability to easily sell positions forces VCs to hold through volatile periods where public market investors would panic-sell, capturing long-term compounding that impatient capital forfeits. It also creates a natural barrier to entry — only investors who can tolerate 7-10+ year lockups participate, reducing competition for deals compared to liquid markets. This 'illiquidity premium' is well-documented across alternative asset classes, but in venture capital it compounds with the power law: the biggest winners often take the longest to mature, and illiquidity ensures investors are still holding when the exponential value creation occurs.
