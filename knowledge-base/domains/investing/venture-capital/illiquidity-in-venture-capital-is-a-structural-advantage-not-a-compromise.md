Conventional financial thinking treats illiquidity as a negative that must be compensated with higher expected returns (the illiquidity premium). But in venture capital, illiquidity serves a deeper structural function: it acts as a commitment device that prevents both GPs and LPs from making value-destroying early exits. Because you cannot easily sell a VC fund position, investors are forced to hold through the volatile early years when most of the value creation happens. This aligns incentives with the power law — if you could sell your LP stake after year 3, you'd miss the 10x+ returns that typically materialize in years 7-12. The illiquidity also reduces the behavioral finance errors that plague public market investors, effectively protecting patient investors from their own impulses.
