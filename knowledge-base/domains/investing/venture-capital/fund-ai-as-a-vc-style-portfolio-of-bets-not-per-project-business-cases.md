Enterprise finance is 'wired for certainty': projects must justify themselves with committed benefits and predictable cost phasing before funding is unlocked. This framing kills AI projects prematurely because it asks 'can we justify this specific thing predictably?' rather than 'what now becomes possible, and what is the cost of NOT doing this?' Business cases assume three things are knowable up front — scope/solution, expected value, and cost/time — but with AI all three are typically learned by doing the work, and when execution cost for prototyping drops near zero, the game shifts from efficiency to unlocking entirely new capability categories. The speakers cite AI achievers seeing ~50% higher revenue growth than peers, driven by new offerings rather than cost-cutting (Walmart's trend scanner, JP Morgan productizing an internal tool). A VC doesn't demand fixed three-year payback on a single bet; they back a portfolio knowing most fail but the compounding winners follow a power law. The unit of justification should therefore be the portfolio of AI bets across the organization, not each project in isolation — and if the finance function can't think this way, that is where transformation should start, because everything else is downstream.
