Musk's companies are often misread as competing within existing categories — rockets, cars, chatbots. His actual thesis is that each is a module in an integrated system: solar energy + robotics + AI + global connectivity + space infrastructure. The value isn't in any single vertical; it's in the compounding surface area where they intersect.

For investors, this suggests a reorientation: the next decade's category-defining companies are more likely to emerge at the collision of AI and biology, robotics and logistics, or satellites and internet connectivity than from within any established software category. These intersections tend to be underfunded early because they don't fit existing thesis buckets.

This also has a practical implication for due diligence: the question isn't just 'what market is this company in' but 'what adjacent disciplines does this team draw from' — because the defensibility and surface area for value creation scales with the number of disciplinary inputs.
