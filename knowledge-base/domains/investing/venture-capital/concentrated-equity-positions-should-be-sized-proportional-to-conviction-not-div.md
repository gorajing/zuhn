The 2025 letter discloses Berkshire's four largest equity holdings with market value, cost basis, and percentage of company owned. The pattern is striking: Apple is 1.6% of Apple but accounts for $62B; American Express is 22.1% of Amex but only $56B market value. They paid $1.29B for the Amex stake decades ago; it's now worth $56B. Cost basis on all four concentrated positions: $9.09B. Current market value: $158.6B. The 17x compound was not about picking hot stocks — it was about identifying four businesses where the conviction was high enough to justify concentrated ownership and then holding them for decades. Modern portfolio theory would call this irresponsible concentration; Berkshire calls it the entire point. The contradiction is only apparent because modern portfolio theory optimizes for volatility reduction while Berkshire optimizes for absolute return over multi-decade horizons. The lesson: if your goal is maximum long-term compounding AND you have genuine informational advantage on specific businesses, diversification is a confession that you don't know where your edge is.
