Sequoia tracked partner votes for over a decade and discovered a consistent pattern: investments that received lukewarm consensus approval underperformed investments that generated strong conviction from some partners, even when others disagreed. Their solution was to remove the middle option entirely — no five-out-of-ten votes allowed.

This maps to a broader principle in decision-making: comfort and consensus often signal mediocrity. The best opportunities are polarizing by nature because they require seeing something the market doesn't. By forcing binary conviction, Sequoia structurally prevents the portfolio from filling with 'safe' bets that feel good but produce average returns. The discomfort of strong disagreement becomes a positive signal rather than a red flag.
