IRR is highly sensitive to the time dimension because it measures return per unit of time. In mezzanine deals where principal repayment is bullet-structured (all at exit), the lender's largest cash inflow is concentrated at a single point. Moving that point forward by one year compresses the same total return into a shorter period, dramatically boosting IRR. Moving it back dilutes the return over more time.

This creates a negotiation asymmetry: founders tend to negotiate hardest on rate (the most visible number), while lenders know that exit timing and fees often matter more to actual returns. Sophisticated mezzanine modeling reveals that a borrower who accepts a slightly higher rate but negotiates a longer tenor may actually pay less in IRR terms than one who gets a rate discount but commits to an earlier exit. This insight applies broadly to any financing where the return profile is back-loaded.
