Most discussions of startup exits focus on the headline valuation — a $1B IPO or a $500M acquisition. But the actual cash an investor receives depends heavily on structural details: IPO underwriter discounts (typically 5-7%), lock-up periods preventing share sales for 90-180 days post-IPO, and earn-out provisions in acquisitions that tie a portion of proceeds to future performance milestones.

This means two exits at the same headline valuation can produce dramatically different returns. An acquisition with a 30% earn-out component spread over three years will deliver far less present value than a clean secondary sale at the same price. Investors and founders who model only the top-line number are systematically overestimating their returns on exits with deferred or contingent components.

The practical implication is that exit scenario modeling — mapping every dollar to a specific point on a calendar — should be standard practice before negotiating any exit, not an afterthought. IRR captures what MOIC misses: the cost of time.
