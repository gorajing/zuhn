Most VC funds fail at what amounts to fifth-grade math: the power law means a single outlier return drives the entire fund. Mega-funds, by their structure, must deploy large amounts of capital across many deals, which dilutes their ownership in any single company. Smaller, sharper funds can concentrate ownership in fewer bets, maximizing their exposure to the asymmetric upside that defines venture returns.

This creates a structural advantage for disciplined small funds. While mega-funds chase brand recognition and deal flow volume, focused funds optimize for the only metric that matters in venture: percentage ownership in the companies that become massive outliers. The implication is that fund size itself can be an anti-pattern in venture capital when it exceeds the ability to maintain meaningful ownership stakes.
