Naval frames money not as wealth itself but as a transfer mechanism — social credits representing IOUs for value previously created. When you create value for society, you receive money as a promise of future reciprocity. However, this IOU system degrades through multiple channels: governments print extra IOUs (inflation), people steal IOUs (fraud), and people renege on IOUs (default).

This framework has practical implications for wealth strategy. Since money is a leaky abstraction of value, holding wealth primarily as money means accepting systematic erosion. Assets that earn while you sleep — businesses, software, productive real estate — are more durable stores of wealth because they generate ongoing value rather than representing a static claim on past value. This aligns with why sophisticated investors hold productive assets rather than cash, and why founders building equity in a growing business are often wealthier than high-salary employees.
