Sequoia made a 40x return on Apple in 18 months and sold their entire position for $6M before the IPO — likely the single biggest mistake in investing history. The same pattern repeats with Amazon: holding from 1997 IPO to 2010 returned ~10x, but Paul Graham noted that 99.98% of Amazon's growth happened after that point. Holding another decade turned 10x into 170x.

The key insight is that growth rate in any given year is irrelevant — what matters is how many years of compounding remain. Most of the value in a winner shows up in the 'out years' that look impossibly far away from the early vantage point. This is why VCs obsess over TAM: only globally massive markets give a company runway to compound for decades.

Applied: when evaluating whether to trim a winning position, the question is not 'has it run too far' but 'is the addressable opportunity exhausted.' If the market is still expanding, the position with 10 more years of compounding ahead is more valuable than current valuation suggests.
