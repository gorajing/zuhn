The puzzle: Medallion is closed to outside investors, so Rentech effectively charges itself 5% management fees and 44% carry. Why? The insight is that this is a structural value-transfer mechanism from long-tenured insiders (who have accumulated large LP positions in the fund over years of compounding returns) to the currently-active research team. Each year, roughly 49% of returns flow to the working team via carry and fees, and 51% to the long-tenured base. Over time, as a researcher's LP stake grows, their balance flips from GP-side to LP-side — exactly like academic tenure.

This solves the otherwise-lethal problem of a closed research organization with full code access: why don't the best young researchers defect to start their own Medallion? Because the carry structure already hands them roughly half of the economic value they create each year, in a way that's probably tax-advantaged versus arbitrary bonuses, and scales automatically with their contribution.

A prop shop with principal-owned capital and discretionary bonuses is a static arrangement — fine for Jane Street, but it requires ongoing judgment calls about who deserves what. Rentech's LP/GP structure makes the value transfer formulaic and self-executing. Any closed research organization with a proven cash engine should consider whether a fund-like structure could automate the old-guard-to-new-guard handoff better than manual compensation committees.
