Angel investing has three components: capital, access, and picking. Rachitsky's data shows over two-thirds of his biggest winners were already considered hot deals at the time of investment, and similarly, over two-thirds of the hot deals he invested in performed well. This creates a clear hierarchy: access to high-quality deal flow matters far more than the ability to differentiate between deals.

This inverts the common narrative that investing success comes from superior judgment. In practice, the most popular deals—already discovered and validated by the market of sophisticated investors—tend to perform well. The binding constraint for most angels is not analytical skill but whether they can get allocation in competitive rounds. As Jack Altman notes, choosing randomly from a pool of great deals beats picking expertly from average ones.
