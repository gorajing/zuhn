Unlike traditional investing where diversification reduces risk, venture capital operates under power law dynamics where a tiny number of investments generate virtually all returns. Sebastian Mallaby's research into firms like Sequoia and Benchmark reveals that the best VCs don't hedge their bets—they concentrate capital on their highest-conviction opportunities.

This has profound implications for portfolio construction. Fund managers who spread capital too thin across many deals may reduce variance but also cap their upside. The discipline required is counterintuitive: you must accept that most investments will fail while maintaining the conviction to make outsized bets on the few that could be transformative. Firm culture and decision-making structure often matter more than check size in enabling this approach.
