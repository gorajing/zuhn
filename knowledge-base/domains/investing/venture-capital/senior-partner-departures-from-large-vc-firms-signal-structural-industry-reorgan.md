The pattern of senior partners departing large VC firms is symptomatic of deeper structural tensions in the venture industry. Large funds require large deployments, which push toward later-stage deals and bigger check sizes — but the best risk-adjusted returns in venture historically come from earlier stages with smaller checks. Senior partners with strong personal brands and deal flow can often capture more economics by running smaller, focused funds.

This talent migration also reflects the changing nature of VC value-add. As AI tools commoditize pattern matching and market analysis, the differentiated value of a VC partner increasingly comes from personal network, brand, and founder relationships — all of which are portable. The institutional advantages of large platforms (brand, back-office, data) are eroding relative to the nimbleness and alignment advantages of smaller vehicles. This trend likely accelerates as the IPO market remains constrained and the pressure on large fund IRRs intensifies.
