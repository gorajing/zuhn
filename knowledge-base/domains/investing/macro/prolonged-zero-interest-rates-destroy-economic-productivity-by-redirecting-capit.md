After 2008, central banks held interest rates near zero for over a decade. This created cheap money that disproportionately flowed into financial speculation rather than productive economic activity — R&D, manufacturing, infrastructure. US GDP per capita never returned to its pre-2008 trend line, and Janet Yellen called productivity 'the most important factor determining continued advancements in living standards.'

The mechanism is straightforward: when risk-free returns are zero, capital chases yield in speculative assets (stocks, real estate, eventually NFTs). The real economy — where productivity gains happen through investment in better tools, processes, and human capital — gets starved. This creates the paradox where stock markets hit all-time highs while median wages stagnate and a whole generation becomes poorer than their parents. For anyone building a business or allocating capital, this means the macro environment structurally favors financial engineering over genuine value creation during prolonged low-rate regimes.
