Banks prefer mortgage and real estate lending because physical collateral makes defaults recoverable — if a borrower can't pay, the bank takes the house. This rational self-interest systematically starves productive investment: R&D, small business, manufacturing, and education receive less funding because they lack physical collateral and carry higher default risk.

The macro consequence is that economies become fragile shells of financialized assets rather than robust systems of productive capacity. Australia serves as a case study where decades of bank preference for housing created one of the world's largest property bubbles while productive investment stagnated. The insight is that money creation per se is not the problem — the allocation of credit is. An economy that directs bank-created money toward wealth creation (innovation, businesses, human capital) builds resilience; one that directs it toward asset speculation builds fragility that requires ever-larger bailouts to sustain.
