For most of modern history, the US has been a dominant agricultural exporter, running consistent trade surpluses in food. In 2024, that surplus flipped to a $32 billion deficit — a reversal that reflects the compounding of farm input cost inflation, water depletion in key growing regions, labor shortages, and demand patterns the domestic supply chain was not built to serve.

This is not a blip caused by a single bad harvest or currency move. It reflects long-run structural erosion: the average farmer is 58 years old, water tables in the Southwest and Great Plains are declining at roughly 10x their replenishment rate, and labor in non-mechanizable crops remains persistently short.

For investors and policy analysts, the trade deficit signals that the US food system's comparative advantage is narrowing. This creates both risk exposure (import dependency for essential goods) and opportunity (precision ag, vertical farming, alternative proteins, and ag-tech that reduces input dependency).
