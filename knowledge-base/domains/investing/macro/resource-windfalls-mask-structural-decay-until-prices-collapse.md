Paine highlights that 80% of Soviet hard currency earnings from 1973–1985 came from oil, which the regime used to import grain it couldn't produce, to prop up Eastern European satellites, and to fund the Red Army and Third World clients like Angola, Ethiopia, and Nicaragua. The oil income was a life-support system disguised as economic success; during this period growth statistics looked tolerable even as the underlying productive system decayed.

When oil prices crashed in the mid-1980s — the same window as Gorbachev's arrival — the Soviet budget lost its cushion, commodity-exporting satellites lost their export earnings, and the whole edifice collapsed within five years. The point isn't that oil caused the fall; it's that oil delayed the fall by 25 years and then made the eventual collapse catastrophic rather than gradual.

This is the deep pattern of resource curses: hydrocarbon or commodity windfalls remove the pressure to build productive, diversified economies, so the state never develops market institutions, human capital, or industrial capacity. When the windfall ends, there's nothing underneath. Applies equally to modern petrostates, VC-funded startups running on cheap capital, and companies riding a single hot product cycle. Watch for institutions where a single revenue stream is both enormous and hiding that nothing else works.
