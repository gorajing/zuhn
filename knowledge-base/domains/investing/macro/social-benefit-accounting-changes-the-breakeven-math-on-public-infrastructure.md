A study of California's proposed SF-LA high-speed rail found that social benefits—reduced carbon emissions, higher worker productivity, and fewer road casualties—totaled roughly $440M per year, almost exactly matching the subsidy needed for the line to break even. China's state-owned railway corporation is only slightly profitable and carries enormous debt, but the social returns in enabling 75 million people to commute to Shanghai within an hour are transformative.

This reframes how to evaluate public infrastructure: pure financial ROI is the wrong metric. The real question is whether the externality value exceeds the subsidy cost. This lens also explains why private markets systematically underinvest in infrastructure—they can't capture the diffuse social benefits that justify the spend.
