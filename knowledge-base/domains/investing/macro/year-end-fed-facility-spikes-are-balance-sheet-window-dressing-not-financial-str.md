The Fed's Standing Repo Facility (SRF) lets banks borrow cash overnight by pledging Treasuries — it's a pressure valve that caps interest rates during cash shortfalls. The Reverse Repo Facility (RRP) lets money market funds park excess cash overnight — it puts a floor under rates by providing a guaranteed safe alternative to private lending. Both spiking simultaneously looks paradoxical (some borrowing while others lend?) but follows directly from incentive structures.

On December 31, regulators take a snapshot of bank balance sheets. Holding cash inflates reported balance-sheet size, so banks move cash off their books via the RRP. Simultaneously, other institutions still need cash to settle trades but find banks unwilling to lend on the reporting date. The Fed's facilities fill the one-night gap. On January 1, all trades unwind and markets normalize. The lesson for investors: headline-grabbing Fed facility numbers at year-end should be interpreted as regulatory calendar effects, not stress indicators. The same logic applies to quarter-end repo spikes more broadly.
