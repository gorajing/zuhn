The historical pattern is clear: manufacturing share predicts military and geopolitical outcomes, often with a 20-30 year lag. Britain's manufacturing dominance in 1870 (31.8%) gave way to US dominance by 1913 (32%), and the US industrial capacity proved decisive in both WWIs. Manufacturing capacity converts directly into warships, munitions, and logistics — it is the physical substrate of sustained military power.

Current data makes this concrete: China now produces warships 200x faster than the US can, has 28.9% of global manufacturing vs. the US's 17.2%, and is deploying renewable energy infrastructure at a pace the US hasn't matched. These aren't soft indicators — they translate to hard military and supply-chain leverage in any prolonged conflict or economic confrontation.

For macro investors, the implication is that manufacturing capacity trends are a better leading indicator of long-run geopolitical power shifts than GDP, military budgets, or technology indexes — because manufacturing is the foundation from which all other forms of power can be rapidly scaled.
