Lane's core claim is that life on wet rocky planets is not contingent. CO2 is the only element that can form the complex bonds needed to build molecules spontaneously. Water is everywhere. Olivine—the mineral that produces alkaline hydrothermal vents when it reacts with water—is common in interstellar dust. Put these together and you get proton gradients across mineralized pores, which drive CO2 and hydrogen to react and produce Krebs cycle intermediates. The chemistry is so constrained that Lane expects 50% or more of wet rocky planets in the Milky Way to produce nucleotides, and a substantial fraction to produce bacterial-like life.

The meta-principle is that when the substrate constraints are deterministic and the search space has been thoroughly explored, outcomes converge no matter how different the starting conditions look. The illusion of contingency comes from looking at the surface variation rather than the underlying physics. Meteorites show utterly different chemistry from living systems yet still produce amino acids and nucleobases—the same subset of stable molecules keeps appearing because they're thermodynamically favored.

For long-horizon investing and strategy, this reframes how to think about 'anything could happen.' When underlying constraints are tight and have been searched hard, the range of plausible endpoints is narrower than it appears. Technology paths that seem contingent often converge when you look at what physics and economics actually allow. The useful question is not 'what might happen?' but 'what is the substrate forcing, and has the alternative space actually been searched?' If the answer to the second question is yes and no alternatives emerged, treat the apparent contingency as a costume over hidden determinism.
