Rogoff argues that the distinction between normal recessions and financial crises is not degree but kind. In a normal recession the economy dips and recovers to trend. In a financial crisis, the banking system stops lending to innovators and dynamic companies, business models break, and the economy resets to a permanently lower trajectory. Japan is the extreme case — Rogoff estimates they'd be 25-50% wealthier per person without the 1990s crisis. The US in 2008 permanently lost ~15% of potential GDP and we're still living in its echoes almost two decades later.

This was the central claim of Reinhart-Rogoff's 'This Time Is Different' (2009), for which they were mocked at the time. It has since become the consensus view. The mechanism is that financial repression, bailout culture, and damaged financial intermediation persist long after the acute crisis. Applied: when modeling sovereign risk or forecasting post-crisis growth, do not use normal recession recovery curves. Greece and Portugal confirm the pattern — positive growth resumes but the lost ground never closes.
