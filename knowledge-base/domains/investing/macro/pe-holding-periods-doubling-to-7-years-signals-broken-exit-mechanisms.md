Dimon highlights a striking structural shift in capital markets: private equity investments are now held for an average of seven years, 'virtually double what it used to be.' Close to 13,000 PE-backed companies have not gone public. When exits do happen, companies are 'sold, not to another company or taken public, but put in a new fund called a continuation fund.' This is essentially recycling assets within the PE ecosystem rather than returning capital through genuine exits.

The backdrop makes this more concerning: there has been 'nothing but a bull market since the great financial crisis,' yet PE firms still haven't taken advantage of these healthy markets to exit positions. Dimon notes it's 'hard to imagine what will happen if and when we have an extended bear market.' Insurance regulators are likely to eventually insist on more rigorous ratings or markdowns, leading to demands for more capital.

The U.S. has gone from 7,300 publicly listed companies in 1996 to just 3,900 today, while PE-backed companies have grown from 1,996 to 13,100. This migration of companies from public to private markets concentrates risk in less transparent, less liquid vehicles and deprives retail investors of access to these returns. It also means that when a credit cycle eventually arrives, the inability to exit quickly through public markets could amplify distress.
