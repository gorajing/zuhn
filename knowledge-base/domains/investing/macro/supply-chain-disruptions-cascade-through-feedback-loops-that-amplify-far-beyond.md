The 2021 shortage cascade illustrated how tightly coupled systems create amplification loops. Containers piled up at US ports because lockdowns reduced exports. Port congestion meant ships couldn't unload, doubling transit times. The congestion meant ports couldn't load empty containers for return to Asia. Truck driver shortages meant containers couldn't even reach ports for return. Each shortage fed the next.

For investors and operators, this means initial supply chain disruptions are poor predictors of eventual magnitude. The feedback loop structure — not the triggering event — determines how bad things get. Companies with buffers at critical chokepoints (like Toyota's semiconductor stockpile) break these loops rather than amplifying them. The implication is that supply chain risk assessment must map loop structures, not just individual failure points.
