Napier recommends emerging markets in the next cycle because of their lower debt ratios and sounder finances, but attaches a hard caveat: the rule-of-law screen. Avoid countries where trading rights aren't guaranteed, where the constitution is weak, or where the currency is overvalued (setting up for coercive revaluation). Russia is his cautionary case — foreign capital was effectively trapped after sanctions, an absorbing state that cannot be reversed. He explicitly excludes China for the same structural reason: cannot trust that invested capital will be convertible back. The heuristic he offers for practitioners: don't invest in a country you wouldn't live in during retirement. It encodes the property-rights and capital-convertibility constraints in a single intuitive test. The return differential from weak-institution emerging markets is never worth the tail risk of entrapment, because the loss is total and permanent when it happens.
