Australia and the US started from remarkably similar positions — similar land area, coastal-origin British colonies, westward expansion driven by gold rushes, planned capital cities. Yet Australia's 35% desert interior prevented the inland population centers that enabled American economic diversification. With just 25 million people clustered in five coastal cities connected by two-lane highways, Australia's economic complexity ceiling is far lower than America's.

The mineral wealth that geography provides becomes the path of least resistance: mining requires minimal population density and generates outsized export revenue. But this locks the economy into commodity cycles and concentrates trade partners among the largest industrial consumers. It's a geographic determinism that institutional quality alone cannot overcome — you need population density to build the diversified service and manufacturing sectors that reduce trade concentration.
