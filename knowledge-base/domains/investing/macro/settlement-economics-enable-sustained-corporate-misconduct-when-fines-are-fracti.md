Purdue's pattern of legal settlements reveals a structural failure in corporate accountability. The company earned $35B in OxyContin revenue while paying settlements that a senator described as 'an expensive license for criminal misconduct.' The $600M federal settlement in 2007, the $10M deceptive marketing settlement in 2004, and the $270M Oklahoma settlement in 2019 collectively represent a small fraction of total revenue. Each settlement included confidentiality clauses and no admission of wrongdoing, allowing practices to continue.

This creates a rational-actor problem: when the expected cost of misconduct (probability of lawsuit × settlement amount) is consistently lower than the revenue from misconduct, the economically rational decision is to continue. The Sackler family's strategy of settling just before testimony and sealing records under confidentiality further reduced the deterrent effect. The pattern suggests that percentage-of-revenue penalties, disgorgement of profits, and personal criminal liability for executives are necessary to change the incentive calculus — flat-dollar fines simply become line items in a cost-benefit analysis.
