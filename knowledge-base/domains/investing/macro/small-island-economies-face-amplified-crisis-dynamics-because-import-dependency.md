Sri Lanka's crisis accelerated faster than comparable debt crises in continental economies because its island geography created a single point of failure: foreign currency reserves. When reserves dropped from $7.6B to $50M, the country couldn't import fuel (all seaborne), food (rice imports replaced collapsed domestic production), medicine, or even printing paper for school exams. Continental economies in similar debt distress can often maintain some trade through overland routes, barter arrangements with neighbors, or pipeline energy imports.

For investors assessing sovereign risk, island economies deserve a structural risk premium beyond what debt-to-GDP ratios suggest. The crisis transmission mechanism is fundamentally different: rather than gradual degradation across sectors, island economies face a cliff-edge collapse when forex reserves hit zero. Sri Lanka went from 'concerning but manageable' to 'failed state' in roughly 18 months. Rising oil prices compound this vulnerability disproportionately because all goods arrive by sea. The practical screening heuristic: for island nations with external debt above 80% of GDP and tourism-dependent forex earnings, any shock to tourism revenue should be treated as a sovereign credit event in progress.
