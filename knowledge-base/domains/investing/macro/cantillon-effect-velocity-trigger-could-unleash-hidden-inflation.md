The standard objection to inflation fears post-QE has been 'where is the inflation?' The answer is that inflation has been sequestered in asset classes — housing, equities, bonds — rather than in consumer goods. Money velocity (the rate at which money changes hands in the real economy) has remained low because QE money circulates within financial markets rather than the general economy.

The latent risk is a velocity trigger: when large asset holders begin selling stocks and real estate to purchase real goods and services — whether from crisis, changing preferences, or generational wealth transfer — the trillions of dollars trapped in financial assets will flood into the consumer economy. This would cause rapid, visible inflation that monetary authorities may be unable to control because the money has already been created. The practical implication is that current CPI figures dramatically understate the inflationary pressure already built into the system, and investors should position for a potential regime shift from asset inflation to consumer inflation.
