Handmer offers a sharp reframing: the grid's economic value comes from performing spatial arbitrage — moving power from cheap generation sites to expensive consumption sites — with pumped hydro as the only meaningful temporal storage until recently. Batteries now do temporal arbitrage so well that the average distance an electron travels between generation and consumption is dropping rapidly. Per-capita battery allocation has gone from ~10 grams (cell phones) to ~100 kg (Teslas) and will continue rising 4-5 orders of magnitude.

This creates a structural death spiral for grid operators. Batteries get installed behind-the-meter — you don't need permission from the utility, and you can use them to avoid peak-priced grid power. Grid assets have high utilization only during stress events, which become rarer as batteries smooth demand. Meanwhile, operating costs (aging workforce, wildfires, permit battles, eminent domain litigation) keep rising. The result: peak-hour revenue falls, maintenance costs rise, grid capacity degrades, more customers defect. PG&E's recurring bankruptcy is the canonical example.

Investment implication: regulated utilities face structural headwinds that are obvious in retrospect but not yet priced in. Distributed energy players (battery manufacturers, behind-the-meter installers, off-grid industrial developers) benefit. Large captive loads like AI data centers, aluminum smelters, and industrial processes will increasingly build their own power plants — returning to the pre-grid pattern.
