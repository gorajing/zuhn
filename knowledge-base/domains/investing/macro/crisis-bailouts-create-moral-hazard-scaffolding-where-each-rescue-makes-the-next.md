The 2008 bailout — $700B in TARP plus $2.5T in Fed asset purchases — stabilized the system but created perverse dependencies. The biggest banks emerged even larger after absorbing failed competitors. Interest rates stayed near zero for a decade, and the financial system 'settled around' these abnormal conditions as its new baseline. When rates finally rose in 2022, 'everything seems to be falling apart' because the system literally cannot function at normal rates.

The pandemic response amplified this pattern: central banks responded 'much quicker with a lot of force,' printing trillions, which sent stocks to all-time highs and inflated speculative assets (NFTs, meme stocks). Each intervention is larger than the last because the system's tolerance builds — like an addict needing bigger doses. By 2022, total debt in the system exceeded 2008 levels. For investors, this means the long-term trajectory is toward either controlled deleveraging (painful but manageable) or eventual loss of confidence in the monetary scaffolding itself.
