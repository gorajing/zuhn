Harper makes two related points about pandemic risk that cut against intuitive reasoning. First, standard evolutionary theory says pathogens face a tradeoff: killing the host too fast destroys the transmission runway, so diseases should drift toward moderate virulence. Plague violates this spectacularly because it's vector-borne (fleas transmit even from corpses) and because humans are collateral damage — its real evolutionary niche is burrowing rodents in Central Asia, so it has zero incentive to be gentle with us. Any pathogen with a similar escape from the tradeoff is a tail-risk outlier.

Second, containability depends on subtle parameters that don't show up in lethality rankings. SARS-CoV-1 was more virulent than SARS-CoV-2 and was contained precisely because it made you obviously sick before you became infectious. COVID-19's several-day pre-symptomatic window was the specific quirk that made non-pharmaceutical interventions impossible. Lethality is the headline number; latency and transmission-route quirks are what determine whether a pathogen breaks containment.

The investment and policy implication: reasoning about pandemic risk from base rates of known diseases systematically underweights the real threat, which is the next contingent weirdness — prion diseases, fungal pathogens, engineered pathogens that happen to hit the right latency window. Biosecurity spending should be weighted toward detection and response capacity for the unknown outlier, not toward refighting the last pandemic.
