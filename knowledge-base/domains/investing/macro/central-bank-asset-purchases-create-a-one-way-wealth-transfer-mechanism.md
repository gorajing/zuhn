The Cantillon effect describes how the first recipients of newly created money benefit disproportionately — they can purchase assets before prices adjust upward. In modern QE, central banks create digital money and funnel it through banks and hedge funds into stock and bond markets. Asset owners see their wealth grow 420% since 1980, while wage earners face the eventual inflationary cost.

The mechanism is circular and self-reinforcing: central banks print money, give it to financial institutions at near-zero interest, those institutions invest in assets, asset prices rise, asset owners get wealthier. Meanwhile the debt created to fund this must eventually be repaid through taxation or inflation — both of which disproportionately burden those without significant asset holdings. This is not a side effect of monetary policy; it is the structural mechanism by which it operates.
