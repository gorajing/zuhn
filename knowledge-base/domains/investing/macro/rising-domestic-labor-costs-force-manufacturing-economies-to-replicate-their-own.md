China's manufacturing boom created a middle class that raised labor costs nationwide, making the country uncompetitive for the lowest-skill, lowest-cost production. Chinese firms responded by setting up manufacturing operations in Africa—one of the world's cheapest labor markets—effectively replicating the exact development model that built China's economy. This is a recurring macro pattern: Japan outsourced to China and Southeast Asia; the US outsourced to Japan and Mexico; Britain outsourced to its colonies. Each industrializing nation eventually prices itself out of low-skill work and must find the next cheap-labor frontier. For investors, this pattern is predictive: identifying which regions will absorb the next wave of manufacturing migration reveals where infrastructure demand, urbanization, and consumer market growth will accelerate. Africa's current position mirrors China circa 1990—massive untapped labor, minimal infrastructure, and early-stage capital inflows.
