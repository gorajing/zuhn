China's manufacturing sector grew GDP from $200B to $11T over 40 years, but this success created its own undoing. Workers who once accepted $3-4/hour now demand middle-class wages as Shenzhen real estate hits $1,200/sqft—higher than San Francisco. This is the classic middle-income trap mechanism: export manufacturing lifts a population out of poverty, but the resulting prosperity eliminates the labor cost differential that attracted manufacturing in the first place. The predictable consequence is a geographic cascade—labor-intensive work moves to Vietnam, Bangladesh, and India while China tries to move up the value chain. For investors, this pattern is highly predictable and repeatable: identify which countries are next in the cascade and position accordingly.
