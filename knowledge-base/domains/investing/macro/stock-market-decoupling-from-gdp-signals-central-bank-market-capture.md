Throughout the 20th century, stock markets roughly tracked economic output — they were measuring instruments for productive activity. The post-2008 divergence, where US stock market capitalization reached nearly twice GDP, reveals that markets have become disconnected from the real economy and instead function as the primary absorption mechanism for QE money.

This decoupling is visible in stark contradictions: April 2020 saw 23 million Americans lose their jobs while the stock market posted its best month since 1987. The mechanism is direct — central banks created trillions, lent it at near-zero interest to banks and hedge funds, and that money flowed straight into equities. For investors, this means traditional valuation metrics (P/E ratios, earnings growth) are secondary to monetary policy in determining market direction. The practical signal is to watch market-cap-to-GDP ratio as a measure of monetary distortion rather than economic health.
