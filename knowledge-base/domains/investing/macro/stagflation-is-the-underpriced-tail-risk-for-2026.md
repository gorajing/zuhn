Dimon outlines a convergence of inflationary pressures: $725B in AI hyperscaler capex (2026), $300B fiscal stimulus from the One Big Beautiful Bill, geopolitical commodity shocks from wars in Ukraine and Iran disrupting oil, fertilizer, and helium supply chains, and U.S. debt-to-GDP projected to hit 120% by 2036. While AI capex is long-run deflationary via productivity, it is short-run inflationary. Combined with high asset prices (household net worth at 560% of GDP vs 460% at 2006 peak) and historically low credit spreads, the setup is fragile.

The key mechanism: inflation slowly going up rather than down would force interest rates higher, which acts as 'gravity to almost all asset prices.' Falling asset prices change sentiment rapidly, triggering a flight to cash. Dimon notes that human nature hasn't changed — sentiment and confidence can shift rapidly — and that market makers are less able to intermediate in volatile markets than before, amplifying any liquidity crunch.

For portfolio positioning, this argues for maintaining dry powder and being skeptical of the consensus soft-landing narrative. The asymmetry is unfavorable: good outcomes are priced in, while the tail risks (stagflation, geopolitical escalation, private credit unwind) could compound.
