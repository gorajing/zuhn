The leveraged private credit market has grown to $1.8 trillion, comparable to the $1.5T high yield bond market and $1.7T syndicated leveraged loan market. Dimon catalogs the specific credit quality degradation: weaker covenants, more aggressive add-backs to projected performance, increased PIK (payment-in-kind, meaning interest accrued rather than paid in cash), more aggressive private ratings particularly in insurance companies, and more arbitrage. Critically, private credit lacks the transparency and rigorous valuation 'marks' of public markets.

This opacity creates a reflexive risk: if participants believe the environment will worsen, they may sell even before realized losses change, because they cannot trust marks. Actual losses are already 'a little higher than they should be, relative to the environment.' Late entrants to credit provision are likely to do a far worse job, and Dimon notes there has been no credit recession in a long time, breeding complacency.

The parallel to pre-2008 structured credit is instructive: opacity + leverage + weakening standards + new entrants who haven't seen a cycle = conditions for surprise losses. The $1.8T figure itself isn't systemic relative to $13T in total investment-grade bonds, but the opacity makes the actual risk impossible to size from outside.
