Rogoff argues default is off the table (we print our own currency), austerity is politically impossible, and financial repression Japanese-style won't work because foreign holders can't be forced to take below-market rates. That leaves inflation. The recent 2021-2023 inflation pulse already knocked ~10% off the debt-to-GDP ratio; the next one will need to be bigger. He also argues markets have too much faith in Fed independence — in a real crisis (war, pandemic, AGI shock), Congress and the President have many ways to override the Fed that look easy when pressure is high.

This has concrete portfolio implications: nominal long-duration Treasuries are asymmetrically bad in this scenario, while TIPS, gold, and real assets benefit. The mechanism runs through a shock catalyst — you don't get gradual inflation; you get a sudden spike when markets decide US debt 'is not to be trusted' and rates reprice. Rogoff's historical anchor: the post-WWII US debt-to-GDP didn't outgrow via productivity, it inflated from 70%+ to 20%+ through the 1970s inflation and prior financial repression.
