Chinese households hold approximately 70% of total wealth in real estate—a concentration level that transforms market risk into regime risk. Cultural factors amplified this: property ownership as marriage prerequisite, one-child policy creating 35M excess men competing for brides via material proof, and families collectively pooling savings for home purchases. This created a feedback loop where cultural norms drove demand, demand drove prices, and rising prices reinforced the cultural belief that property was the only safe store of value. The dangerous implication is that the government cannot allow a meaningful correction without triggering social unrest (mortgage strikes already spread to 300+ projects worth $150-300B), but cannot prevent one without perpetuating the bubble. This 'too concentrated to correct' dynamic applies beyond China—any economy where household wealth is overwhelmingly in one asset class (US equities, Canadian/Australian housing) faces the same policy trap where the cure is as dangerous as the disease.
