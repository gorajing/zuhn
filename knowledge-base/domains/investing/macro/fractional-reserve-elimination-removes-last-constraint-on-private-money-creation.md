Until March 2020, banks were required to hold at least 10% of deposits in reserve, creating a theoretical ceiling on money multiplication. The Fed eliminated this requirement entirely, meaning banks can now create unlimited digital money through lending with no reserve backing whatsoever.

This represents a qualitative shift in monetary architecture — the system moved from fractional reserve to effectively zero reserve. The practical implication is that the only remaining constraints on money creation are borrower demand and bank risk appetite, not any mathematical ratio. For investors, this means monetary expansion has no structural brake, and the distinction between money and debt has become purely notional.
