China's youth movements evolved from 'lying flat' (tang ping, 2021—reducing effort) to 'let it rot' (bai lan, 2022—complete disengagement), with youth unemployment hitting 20%. This progression matters because it represents a rational response to structural conditions, not a cultural mood: when housing costs require 50 years of average income and 20% of educated youth are jobless, reduced effort is economically logical. The government's response—cracking down on big tech, banning private tutoring—actually worsened the problem by eliminating high-paying sectors and educational pathways. For investors, youth disillusionment movements are leading indicators because they reflect a breakdown in human capital formation that won't appear in GDP for 5-10 years. The pattern may be emerging globally: wherever housing costs, credential inflation, and reduced opportunity coincide, similar movements appear (US quiet quitting, Japan's satori generation, South Korea's N-po generation). These are not cultural fads but rational adaptations to broken incentive structures.
