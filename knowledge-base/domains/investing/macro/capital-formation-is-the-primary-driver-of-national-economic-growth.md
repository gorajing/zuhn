Dimon makes a forceful case that capital formation is the central mechanism of economic growth, not a side effect of it. The data is stark: 80% of the $800B in U.S. business R&D comes from large companies, 70% of the $2.2T in total capex comes from the 1,000 largest public companies, and these investments drive both productivity and GDP-per-person growth. Big and small businesses are symbiotic — a billion-dollar plant that creates 5,000 jobs also generates 25,000 jobs in surrounding small businesses.

He draws on international comparisons to illustrate how policy shapes capital formation outcomes. Sweden's investment savings accounts with favorable tax treatment (no capital gains tax, just 1% annual tax on balance) have 'dramatically increased investment by retail investors into the Swedish stock market,' producing more unicorns and billionaires per capita than America. Australia's superannuation system is another success model. In contrast, U.S. policy uncertainty — with legal, regulatory, and tax policies swinging dramatically with each administration — 'definitely reduces the propensity for companies to invest and hire employees.'

The investment thesis: evaluate countries and policies primarily through the lens of capital formation incentives. The failure to pass immigration reform alone costs 0.3% of GDP annually. The 1% gap between potential 3% GDP growth and actual 2% growth, compounded over 20 years, represents an enormous loss in per-capita wealth.
