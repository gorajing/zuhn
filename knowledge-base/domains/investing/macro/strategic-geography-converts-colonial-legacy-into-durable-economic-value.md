Guam was acquired as a spoil of the Spanish-American War, a 19th-century colonial transaction. Yet its value to the US has only increased as the Pacific became the primary theater of great-power competition. Sitting within striking distance of every potential Pacific flashpoint while being sovereign US territory (not a base on allied soil subject to host-nation politics), Guam offers something no amount of money can buy: jurisdiction. The military is doubling personnel stationed there. This pattern — colonial-era acquisitions appreciating in strategic value — appears across geopolitics: British Indian Ocean Territory (Diego Garcia), French Pacific territories, and others. For investors, the analog is assets acquired for one thesis that become valuable for entirely different reasons, suggesting that geographic and jurisdictional optionality is systematically underpriced.
