Shih frames the fundamental difference between Chinese and American capital allocation as a distinction between output-maximization and profit-maximization. Chinese companies like BYD or CATL may themselves want to make money, but their capital base comes from a state banking system whose lending criterion is 'can you produce the strategic good the Party wants?' — not 'can you earn a return?' A robotics startup can borrow against the promise of shipping units, with explicit acknowledgment that profit is optional.

The outer-loop discipline comes from an expert panel at the Ministry of Industry and Information Technology, which is supposed to vet whether a project has real technology. In practice, corruption is widespread — for semiconductors, dozens of MIIT officials were arrested for rubber-stamping bogus projects for kickbacks. Shih's claim: for every state-financed success story, there are over a dozen failed cases, and the genuine Chinese tech champions (BYD, CATL, DJI, Xiaomi, High-Flyer/DeepSeek) were mostly privately funded at the start.

The financing for this system is a tax on savers: 1% deposit rates combined with capital controls (20K USD per year outflow limit) trap household wealth in the state banking system, which then redirects it to strategic sectors. This is why the same country can simultaneously dominate global EV manufacturing and have homeless migrant workers and degraded elderly care — the allocation system optimizes strategic output, not welfare.
