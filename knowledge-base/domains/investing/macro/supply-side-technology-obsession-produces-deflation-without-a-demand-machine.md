Kroeber identifies what he calls the 'materialist Achilles heel' of East Asian industrial-policy models: a deep conviction that physical stuff is what matters and that services/intangibles will magically follow. Chinese leaders share this with Silicon Valley techno-optimists. But the evidence is that modern high-wage economies run on intangible services demand, not industrial output.

China's re-regulation of fintech, internet platforms, telehealth, and private financial services (2020 onwards) choked off the natural growth of the services economy simultaneously with doubling down on semiconductors, EVs, and robotics. The result: companies making the great technology products have no pricing power because there's no aggregate demand. Deflation sets in. Households save more because employment growth stalls. This feeds back into further deflation — they are 'on the edge of a deflationary spiral right now.'

Application: for any economy (or company) whose strategy is 'get good at making the physical stuff and demand will follow,' watch for the demand-machine deficit. Effective growth requires both production and consumption infrastructure. The US strength is that consumer demand drives what gets made; China has spent 45 years building production and zero time building demand institutions, and is only now recognizing the gap. This is also a warning against treating services regulation and industrial policy as separate — they interact through macro demand.
