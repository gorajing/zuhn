Sri Lanka's trajectory from 2006-2019 looked like a development success story — GDP per capita doubled, 1.6 million people exited poverty, and the country achieved World Bank upper-middle-income status. But this growth was funded by external debt reaching 119% of GDP. The structural fragility was invisible during good times because debt service was covered by tourism revenue and export earnings.

When COVID eliminated tourism (a major source of foreign exchange for debt service), the entire edifice collapsed within two years. Foreign reserves went from $7.6B in 2019 to $50M by mid-2022. The lesson for investors evaluating emerging market growth: distinguish between organic productivity gains and debt-funded consumption. The telltale sign is the ratio of debt growth to GDP growth — when debt grows faster than the productive capacity to service it, the 'development' is borrowed time.
