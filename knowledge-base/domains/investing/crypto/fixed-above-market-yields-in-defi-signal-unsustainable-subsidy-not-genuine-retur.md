Terra's 20% fixed yield on UST deposits was the primary growth engine that attracted billions in capital. Unlike market-determined rates that adjust to supply and demand, this rate was artificially maintained through company subsidies — costing roughly $7 million per day. The fixed rate created a dangerous feedback loop: higher yields attracted more deposits, which required more subsidies, which made the system increasingly fragile to any disruption in confidence.

This pattern generalizes beyond Terra. Any protocol offering fixed yields well above prevailing market rates is effectively buying users with investor capital. The yield isn't generated by productive economic activity — it's a customer acquisition cost disguised as a return. The critical question for any DeFi yield is: where does the money actually come from? If the answer involves subsidies, token inflation, or circular mechanisms rather than real economic activity, the yield is a ticking time bomb.
