The Bitfinex hack demonstrates a paradox of crypto theft: while the decentralized nature of Bitcoin made the initial hack possible, the blockchain's transparent public ledger made the stolen funds nearly unusable. The 119,754 stolen Bitcoin were immediately visible to the entire crypto community, leading exchanges worldwide to blacklist the associated addresses. After six years, only 4% of the stolen Bitcoin was successfully laundered.

This creates an asymmetry that favors defenders over time. Traditional bank heists involve fungible cash that becomes untraceable once moved. Stolen Bitcoin, by contrast, carries a permanent history. Every movement is visible, every receiving address can be flagged, and the stolen coins become increasingly toxic as blockchain analytics tools improve. The very feature that makes crypto attractive to thieves — no central authority to freeze accounts — is offset by the feature that makes it hostile to them: no way to erase transaction history.
