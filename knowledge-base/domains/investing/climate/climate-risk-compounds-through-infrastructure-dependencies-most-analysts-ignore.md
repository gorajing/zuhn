The Panama Canal's locks consume millions of gallons of freshwater per transit — water that flows from Gatun Lake and must be replenished by rainfall. During droughts, the canal restricts ship weight (draft limits), reducing cargo capacity per transit and effectively raising shipping costs globally. Climate change is expected to worsen these droughts. What makes this insight valuable for investors is the non-locality: a rainfall deficit in a small Central American country directly impacts shipping costs between East Asia and Europe, container availability worldwide, and retail inventory timelines in the US. Traditional climate risk analysis focuses on direct physical exposure (flooding a factory, burning a warehouse), but infrastructure chokepoint vulnerability creates second- and third-order economic impacts that are harder to model and therefore more likely to be mispriced.
