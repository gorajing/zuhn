Chile's northern grid and Germany both experienced electricity prices dropping to zero or going negative when solar generation outpaced local consumption — with no transmission capacity to export the surplus. This pattern reveals a structural tension in solar deployment: generation scales modularly (add more panels) while grid infrastructure scales in lumpy, capital-intensive increments (new transmission lines, grid upgrades). Investors face asymmetric risk — consumer-side benefits arrive immediately while investor returns collapse. The lesson generalizes: in any technology deployment where production scales faster than distribution, oversupply destroys producer economics even as it delivers consumer surplus. This is the same dynamic seen in content markets, SaaS commoditization, and other zero-marginal-cost goods.
