Research quantifies the EV tipping point at $36,000 price, 291-mile range, and 31-minute full charge. Current EVs roughly meet the first two but fail the third. This reveals a general pattern in disruptive technology adoption: the product itself can be ready while the surrounding infrastructure remains the binding constraint. Investors and strategists evaluating disruption timelines should identify which adoption threshold is the true bottleneck rather than assuming the product's core performance metric is the gating factor.

The implication extends beyond EVs: any technology requiring new infrastructure (hydrogen fuel cells, autonomous vehicles, drone delivery) will hit a phase where product improvements yield diminishing returns on adoption because the ecosystem hasn't caught up. Capital allocation should shift toward infrastructure enablement once the product crosses minimum viability.
