Since 2012, solar cell costs have followed a predictable halving pattern every decade — dubbed Swanson's Law — driven by manufacturing scale and process improvements. This mirrors Moore's Law in semiconductors and gives investors a rare forward-looking cost curve in the energy sector. The practical implication is that solar investments can be timed against this deflation: infrastructure built today will operate against steadily cheaper replacement panels, and regions that delay adoption pay an ever-shrinking premium for waiting. The predictability also means that technologies competing with solar (coal, natural gas) face a moving target that gets harder to beat each year.
