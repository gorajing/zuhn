The pattern repeats across every biography Senra has read: Andrew Carnegie, John D. Rockefeller, Henry Clay Frick, and Izzy Sharp (Four Seasons founder) all describe recessions as their best opportunities. Sharp claimed in his autobiography that he gained 25-28% market share across cycles specifically by increasing marketing spend when competitors pulled back. Buffett and Munger deploy capital precisely when others are forced sellers. The mechanism is behavioral: human nature is loss aversion, so most operators reflexively cut discretionary spend during downturns, which creates an arbitrage for anyone who can hold the line or press harder.

This applies beyond large companies. Tigus (Michael Elnick) scaled up podcast ad inventory across Colossus's entire network in early 2023 precisely because other advertisers were retreating and rates were softening. The logic: if your unit economics work in normal times, they work dramatically better when your competitors vacate the distribution channel. Ogilvy's framing makes it sharper — if advertising is necessary to sell your product, it's not a marketing expense at all, it's a production cost. Cutting it during downturns is like cutting raw materials. The test of a disciplined capital allocator is whether their spending rises or falls when the market panics.
