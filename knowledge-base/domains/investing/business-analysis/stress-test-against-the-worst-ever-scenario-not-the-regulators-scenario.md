When Dimon took over JP Morgan's risk book, the bank's high-yield stress test assumed a 40% credit spread move because '17% was the worst ever and the market is more sophisticated now.' He forced the test to use the worst-ever number. In 2008 the spread hit 20% and the market for bonds vanished entirely. The lesson: you are not trying to predict tails, you are trying to survive them. Stress tests calibrated to recent memory or to regulator templates create false confidence — the Fed's scenarios are a floor, not a ceiling.

The operational discipline is to run many tests weekly (JP Morgan runs ~100) covering combinations — market down 50%, rates at 8%, credit spreads at worst-ever — and verify the firm survives each without needing a bailout or a capital raise. Acceptable outcome is 'worse results but we're still here,' not 'we hit our earnings target.'

This generalizes beyond banks: any business with leverage, long-tailed obligations, or fragile counterparties should anchor its scenario planning on historical extremes rather than smoothed averages. The rare event you cannot imagine is the one that kills you.
