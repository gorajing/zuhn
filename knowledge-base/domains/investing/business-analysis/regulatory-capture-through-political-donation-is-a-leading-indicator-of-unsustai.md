Enron was the single largest contributor to George W. Bush's campaign. Ken Lay personally recommended the chairman of the Federal Energy Regulation Commission. When California was being systematically robbed through energy market manipulation — costing the state $30 billion — Bush refused to intervene, and the FERC didn't act until the Senate forced them. The political investment wasn't incidental to the fraud; it was infrastructure for it. This pattern generalizes: when a company's government relations spending is disproportionate to its industry peers, it often signals that the business model depends on regulatory forbearance rather than competitive advantage. For investors, mapping a company's political spending relative to its sector — and especially tracking whether executives are personally close to regulators — is an underused due diligence signal. Heavy political investment is essentially an insurance policy against the scrutiny that would reveal the business doesn't work.
