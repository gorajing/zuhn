The same airline industry that offers $200 round-trip NYC-LA flights charges $500+ for a 130-mile Denver-Gunnison flight, purely because competition exists on one route and not the other. This demonstrates that industry-level concentration ratios (like HHI for the airline sector) can be misleading — what matters is competition at the specific product-market level. Five competitors on high-demand routes drive prices toward marginal cost, while monopoly routes allow massive markups. This principle extends beyond airlines: any business with geographic or network-segmented markets can have pockets of monopoly pricing power hidden within an apparently competitive industry. Investors should analyze pricing power at the route/segment level, not the industry level.
