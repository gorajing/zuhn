A scissor hub works by converging multiple inbound flights, swapping passengers, then sending planes onward to different destinations. WestJet's St John's hub converges flights from Orlando, Toronto, and Ottawa around 9:30-10 PM, then dispatches three planes to Dublin and London just after 11 PM. Three planes serve six city-pairs. St John's geographic position — only 4.5 hours from the British Isles — enables this further by allowing cheap single-aisle aircraft on transatlantic routes that normally require expensive widebodies. Similarly, Qantas uses Los Angeles as a scissor hub: three Australian cities converge by 6:30 AM, then one plane continues to New York at 8:20 AM. The pattern reveals that geographic positions dismissed as remote or peripheral can become strategic assets when reconceived as convergence points.
