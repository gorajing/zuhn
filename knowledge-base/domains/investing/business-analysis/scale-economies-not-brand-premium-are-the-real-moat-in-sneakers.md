The intuitive story about Nike is that it's a brand-power company commanding a Tiffany-style premium. The financials say otherwise: a Nike running shoe retails at the same $160-$180 as the equivalent Adidas or New Balance, and Adidas's gross margin (46%) has exceeded Nike's (44%) every quarter for a decade. Nike's actual power is scale economies — it's the biggest customer base, so it can afford to pay LeBron, Jordan, and Serena more than anyone else, which lets it control the pinnacle-athlete pipeline, which feeds the brand halo, which sustains the customer base. The flywheel is content-acquisition-driven, not pricing-driven.

This matters for evaluating any 'brand' business: test whether the company actually extracts a price premium over commoditized equivalents, or whether what looks like brand power is really just 'I reach for them first' from scale-driven ubiquity. Those are different moats with different durabilities. Pricing-premium brands (Hermès at 71% gross margin) can be small; scale-economy brands must stay enormous or the flywheel unwinds.
