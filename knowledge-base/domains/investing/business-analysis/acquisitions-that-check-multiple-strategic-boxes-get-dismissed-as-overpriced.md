YouTube at $1.65B in 2006 was universally panned — Google's own CFO said 'never again' about doing a stock deal that size, Wall Street called it Google tossing balls into the air like a drunken juggler, and the Acquired hosts themselves graded it a C on their 2016 episode. It lost ~$1B/year for its first several years (roughly a penny per view) on $30M of revenue. Every framework for evaluating it as an advertising business at purchase time said it was overpriced.

The regraded view (now A+): the acquisition simultaneously (1) solved a massive infrastructure and legal problem YouTube couldn't survive as a standalone, (2) defended Google's ad business from a nascent competitor accumulating the second-largest search volume on the internet, (3) gave Google a lock on the eventual shift of ad dollars from TV to digital video (which didn't happen until 2018), and (4) produced — unforeseen at purchase — the largest video training corpus on Earth for the AI era. Today YouTube does $50B+ in revenue, an estimated $8B in operating income, and Moffett Nathansson values it at $500B standalone. Google paid ~$6.7B all-in (purchase plus years of losses).

The generalizable mistake: evaluators score acquisitions on the scoreboard the seller is selling on. But the most strategically valuable deals are exactly the ones where the buyer sees 2-3 secondary and tertiary benefits the market ignores. When you see a deal that looks rich on the stated metric but is cheap on three other dimensions simultaneously — including optionality for eras that don't exist yet — the expected value is probably much higher than consensus. The asymmetry is huge: the downside is bounded by the purchase price, but the upside compounds across every dimension that plays out.
