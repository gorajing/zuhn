The Rajapaksa family's control of Sri Lanka mirrors a pattern seen in family-controlled conglomerates and authoritarian states: concentrating decision-making authority in a single family eliminates the adversarial feedback mechanisms that catch errors early. Mahinda as president, Gotabaya as defense secretary, Basil and Jamal controlling economic development and irrigation — each role reinforced the others rather than checking them.

The result was a compounding error cascade: debt-fueled infrastructure spending went unchallenged, populist tax cuts that eliminated 25% of revenue proceeded without opposition, and the catastrophic fertilizer ban faced no institutional veto. In corporate governance terms, this is the equivalent of a founder-CEO who also chairs the board and controls the audit committee. For investors evaluating sovereign or corporate risk, the key metric is not the quality of any single decision-maker but the strength of corrective feedback mechanisms. War-hero legitimacy (from ending the 26-year civil war) provided political cover that further weakened these mechanisms.
