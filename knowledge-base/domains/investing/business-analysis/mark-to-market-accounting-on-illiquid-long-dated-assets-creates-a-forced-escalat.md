Enron's adoption of mark-to-market accounting for long-dated energy contracts let it recognize 20 years of projected cash flows as Day 1 revenue. The fatal property of this scheme is that it leaves zero recurring revenue: every January 1 you start at zero, no matter how good last year's contracts performed. To show any growth, you must originate even larger deals than the prior year, which means accepting worse counterparties, padding projections more aggressively, and ultimately fabricating transactions with related-party SPEs.

The pattern generalizes well beyond Enron. Any business model that pulls future cash flows into present-period revenue — whether through aggressive contract recognition, MTM on private assets, or forward-flow agreements booked as one-time gains — creates a treadmill that gets faster every year. From the outside it looks like hypergrowth; from the inside, management knows the next quarter's number requires a 'bigger bomb' than the last. The fuse length is uncertain but the explosion is structural.

When auditing a high-growth company, the question isn't 'are these contracts real' but 'what does the next-year revenue base look like if no new deals are signed.' If that base is near zero, the business is a flywheel that requires constant escalation, and escalation under earnings pressure is how fraud begins.
