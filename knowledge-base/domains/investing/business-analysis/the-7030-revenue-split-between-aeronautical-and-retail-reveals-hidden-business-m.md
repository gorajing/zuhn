Heathrow's cost structure reveals a pattern common in infrastructure businesses: the core service (runway access, gates) generates enough to cover operating costs but not much more, while ancillary revenue (retail, parking, transport) provides the actual profit margin. Airline fees average $29 per passenger against $19 in costs, but after debt service and taxes, true profit is only $8.20 per passenger — and the retail $13.32 is what makes that margin comfortable.

This pattern repeats across infrastructure businesses: cinemas make money on concessions not tickets, razor companies on blades not handles, printers on ink not hardware. For investors analyzing infrastructure businesses, the question isn't whether the core service is profitable — it's whether the ancillary revenue engine is strong. A business that looks marginal on core economics might be highly profitable once you account for the ecosystem of secondary revenue it enables.
