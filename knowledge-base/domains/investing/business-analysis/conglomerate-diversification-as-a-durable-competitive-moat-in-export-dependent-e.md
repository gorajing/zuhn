Samsung's structure as a chaebol — a family-controlled conglomerate spanning electronics, construction, finance, military, shipbuilding, and hospitality — demonstrates how extreme diversification creates durability that no single-industry competitor can match. While Western business orthodoxy favors focus and core competency, Samsung's model shows that in export-dependent economies, controlling multiple value chains insulates against sector-specific downturns and creates internal knowledge transfer between divisions.

This model carries risks (capital misallocation, lack of accountability across units), but Samsung's trajectory from noodle trading to 17% of Korean GDP suggests the benefits of cross-industry learning and shared infrastructure outweigh the coordination costs when managed under a unified philosophy. The key enabler is that the businesses are 'commercially and legally independent but unified by a single philosophy' — loose coupling with cultural cohesion.
