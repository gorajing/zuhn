When Yann LeCun joined Facebook in 2013 to start FAIR, AI was being applied to profitable use cases within two years: ranking posts in the feed and selecting ads from inventory. That near-term ROI alone justified the entire research budget. But FAIR also turned out to be the foundation that let Meta build Reels fast enough to neutralize TikTok's algorithmic attack around 2020 — an existential competitive threat that could have collapsed the core business. And on a third horizon, FAIR positioned Meta as a credible player in the AI platform transition now unfolding with LLMs, giving them Llama and Meta AI.

The generalizable rule is that the strongest R&D bets are ones where the near-term application pays for the research in full, making the longer-term payoffs free optionality. Most companies that spend on AI today are speculating that use cases will materialize. Meta has been profitably deploying AI for a decade in advertising and ranking, so its ongoing infrastructure and research spend is guaranteed to have positive ROI — any future platform-scale payoff is pure upside. When evaluating a company's long-horizon investment, ask whether the near-term application alone justifies the spend. If yes, the research optionality is free and the downside is capped. If no, you are betting on an uncertain payoff with uncertain timing.
