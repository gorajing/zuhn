In 1997, four years before its collapse, Enron Energy Services rapidly fitted out a fake war room on the sixth floor of its Houston office, drafting secretaries and employees from other locations to sit at computer terminals pretending to trade. One administrative assistant was told to bring her personal photos to make her desk look real; she spent the visit talking to friends on the phone. The analysts left impressed by 'the very picture of a sophisticated booming business.'

This is a high-signal pattern. A real business with a real customer base does not need to manufacture the appearance of activity — the activity exists or it doesn't. The decision to stage the floor requires (a) knowing the real floor wouldn't impress, (b) caring enough about analyst perception to coordinate the deception, and (c) having a culture where dozens of people will participate without leaking. All three conditions correlate strongly with active accounting manipulation, because the same gap between perceived and real performance that motivates the staging also motivates the fraud.

The practical implication for analysts and diligence teams: surprise visits and conversations with employees one or two layers below the official tour are dramatically more informative than scheduled tours. When you take a scheduled tour, ask 'who else works on this floor on a normal Tuesday' and 'show me the actual production system, not the demo.' If management resists, that resistance itself is a finding.
