Amazon raised ~$2B in convertible debt during the go-go years and then spent the 2000-2007 nuclear winter building while competitors were laying people off and dying. Pets.com, Drugstore.com, Webvan—all dead. eBay survived but didn't advance. Amazon came out of the crash with six fulfillment centers (up from one warehouse), Prime, Marketplace, and the seeds of AWS. By the time capital became available again in 2007-2008, the moats were measured in miles, not feet.

The pattern is general: when your industry is frothy, capital is cheap, and everyone is bidding up the same inputs (talent, real estate, customer acquisition, M&A targets). When it crashes, the same inputs trade at distress prices and your competitors are distracted by survival. A well-capitalized player with conviction can buy durable advantages during these windows that would be impossible to acquire in normal conditions.

The prerequisite is having access to capital when others don't—which means raising more than you need during good times (even if it feels wasteful) and having the discipline to deploy it aggressively precisely when your instincts say to conserve. This is psychologically very hard; most boards and CEOs do the opposite, cutting during downturns and expanding during peaks, which is why the opportunity exists.
