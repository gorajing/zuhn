The conventional analysis of agriculture is that it's a commodity business where margins get competed away. Bollard complicates this: the top of the supply chain is an oligopoly of a very small number of processors (Tyson, Smithfield, Cal-Maine), and they constantly show up in antitrust and price-fixing scandals precisely because minimal coordination among a few firms can extract meaningful surplus.

The egg industry is the clearest example — it costs roughly 19 cents more to produce a dozen cage-free eggs than caged eggs, but retailers charge $1 to $2 more per dozen. They use welfare labeling as a price discrimination tool against less price-sensitive consumers. Cal-Maine, the largest egg producer, has seen its share price double on inelastic-demand price increases during egg-price spikes.

The investing lesson: 'commodity business' is a lazy label that can hide real pricing power. Look at concentration ratios, check whether the few firms at the narrowest part of the supply chain have repeated antitrust actions, and ask whether demand is inelastic enough that small coordinated supply restrictions translate to outsized profits. Margins get competed away only when the structure actually supports competition.
