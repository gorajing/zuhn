The Nvidia story between 2012 and 2016 is a case study in how long markets can take to price in a visible platform shift. AlexNet in 2012 was the Big Bang of deep learning, it ran on CUDA, and Jensen was explicitly telling investors at every earnings call that the future of the company was machine learning. Mark Andreessen said publicly in 2016 that every AI startup his firm saw was building on Nvidia and that he'd put his entire hedge fund into the stock. Brian Catanzaro's 2013 Stanford paper with Andrew Ng made deep learning on GPUs dramatically more accessible. All of this was in public view.

And yet the stock traded under $5 for most of those years and didn't hit its 2007 peak market cap until 2016. Semiconductor analysts thought Jensen sounded unhinged when he talked about neural networks on earnings calls. The compound return from the 2016 recognition point to the 2021 peak was still massive — you didn't need to be early, you just needed to believe the technical signal before the consensus analyst narrative caught up.

The pattern is durable: markets price what analysts can model with spreadsheets, and platform shifts produce non-linear growth that spreadsheet models cannot forecast. This creates a structural inefficiency where reading technical signals (who is developers building on? what is the default stack in research papers?) gives you years of alpha over reading sellside research. The discipline is trusting the technical signal when the consensus hasn't arrived yet.
