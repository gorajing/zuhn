    Jason Lemkin's framework for parsing recent SaaS public quarters: 'Monday vs
    HubSpot — they're both decelerating. They come out with a quarter and Monday
    trades up after being maybe the most beaten-down stock out there, right?
    trades up 20% and HubSpot down 18%. What's the difference? They're not much

    did that I can tell was for the next quarter they're still decelerating but
    at least they raised the guidance for real. HubSpot couldn't do it. HubSpot
    lowered their guidance. So if you're not accelerating, you're going to be
    destroyed.' The key reframe: the question isn't 'are you growing?' (almost
    every SaaS company is). The question is 'are you growing FASTER than the
    budget environment around you?' When AI spend is exploding (Goldman
    predicting 24x token consumption surge, Anthropic at $200B compute commits,
    enterprise IT budgets seeing 20-30% increase in software spend due to AI), a
    SaaS company growing at 15% is structurally LOSING share even though revenue
    is up. The market correctly prices this as decline-in-relative-position.
    'It's not that everyone's decelerating. The budget is accelerating and you
    are decelerating. Those aren't two good lines to cross over.' The 'minimum

    least raise your guidance to get some breath. Because at least it says, hey,

    to zero, we're not being destroyed by AI.' Rory: 'I think for many there is
    still a worry the terminal value is zero. I don't think that fear has gone
    away if you keep decelerating while budget is accelerating everywhere around

    not your historical trajectory. If enterprise AI spend is up 25% YoY, your
    guidance needs to either (a) raise growth rate substantially or (b) clearly
    show why your category is structurally moving share to AI in ways that
    benefit you specifically. Anything else, the market reads as terminal decay.

    anchor on company-specific growth rates. Anchor on growth rate relative to
    category budget growth. The corollary for analysts: build models that
    explicitly include 'category budget expansion' as a variable. Companies
    whose forecast assumes their share holds in an expanding pie (Monday)

Jason Lemkin's framework for parsing recent SaaS public quarters: 'Monday vs HubSpot — they're both decelerating. They come out with a quarter and Monday trades up after being maybe the most beaten-down stock out there, right? trades up 20% and HubSpot down 18%. What's the difference? They're not much further from each other on their agentic journey, which is early. All Monday did that I can tell was for the next quarter they're still decelerating but at least they raised the guidance for real. HubSpot couldn't do it. HubSpot lowered their guidance. So if you're not accelerating, you're going to be destroyed.' The key reframe: the question isn't 'are you growing?' (almost every SaaS company is). The question is 'are you growing FASTER than the budget environment around you?' When AI spend is exploding (Goldman predicting 24x token consumption surge, Anthropic at $200B compute commits, enterprise IT budgets seeing 20-30% increase in software spend due to AI), a SaaS company growing at 15% is structurally LOSING share even though revenue is up. The market correctly prices this as decline-in-relative-position. 'It's not that everyone's decelerating. The budget is accelerating and you are decelerating. Those aren't two good lines to cross over.' The 'minimum viable signal' for SaaS public stocks: 'At a minimum, you've got to raise guidance. Even if you're not accelerating like Monday, you've got to at least raise your guidance to get some breath. Because at least it says, hey, we're raising our guidance next quarter — at least it says we're not going to zero, we're not being destroyed by AI.' Rory: 'I think for many there is still a worry the terminal value is zero. I don't think that fear has gone away if you keep decelerating while budget is accelerating everywhere around you.' The corollary for SaaS operators: forecast for the budget environment, not your historical trajectory. If enterprise AI spend is up 25% YoY, your guidance needs to either (a) raise growth rate substantially or (b) clearly show why your category is structurally moving share to AI in ways that benefit you specifically. Anything else, the market reads as terminal decay. The corollary for investors: in evaluating public SaaS, the cleanest signal is the gap between revised guidance and budget environment trajectory. Don't anchor on company-specific growth rates. Anchor on growth rate relative to category budget growth. The corollary for analysts: build models that explicitly include 'category budget expansion' as a variable. Companies whose forecast assumes their share holds in an expanding pie (Monday) outperform companies whose forecast implicitly cedes share (HubSpot). The implication for SaaS startups: even private companies need to articulate the budget-trajectory story to investors and customers. 'We're growing 30%' is insufficient when the budget environment is growing 35%. The right pitch is 'we're growing 50% in a 35% budget-growth environment, so we're taking 15 points of share annually.'
