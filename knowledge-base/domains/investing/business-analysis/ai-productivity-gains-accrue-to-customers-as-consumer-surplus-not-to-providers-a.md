Evans's sharpest economic point cuts against the easy assumption that AI productivity translates into fatter margins. Using the Excel analogy: if a DCF takes a week, you do one or two; if it takes ten seconds, you do fifty — but you can't charge any more for it. A McKinsey/Bain/BCG analysis that dropped from a week to a day doesn't shrink the cost base or raise the price; the firm simply does five times more analysis and charges the customer the same. Investment banks already lived this — far more financial analysis, probably fewer people, same fees. The gain shows up as consumer surplus and competitive parity, not as captured value.

The mechanism is that these tools become competitive necessities: once everyone has them, the productivity gain is table stakes rather than differentiation, and markets compete it away. This is the Jevons-paradox 'price elasticity' button — making something cheaper can mean you do far more of it for the same money rather than the same amount for less. The investing implication is a caution against modeling AI adoption as automatic margin expansion: the harder, slower, and more valuable move is building a genuinely new revenue line (something previously impossible), not making existing knowledge work cheaper. Evans also notes the measurement trap — early benefits (better analytics, faster slides, better support) are real but hard to put a financial value on, which is why CFO surveys struggle to find clear ROI.
