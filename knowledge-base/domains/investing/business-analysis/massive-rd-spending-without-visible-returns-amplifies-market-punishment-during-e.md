Meta's Q4 2021 earnings revealed two simultaneous problems: Facebook's first-ever user decline AND $3.3B in quarterly losses from the Reality Labs metaverse division (totaling $8.3B for the year). Either problem alone might have caused a moderate selloff, but together they created a narrative of a company losing its core business while burning cash on an unproven bet.

This reveals a capital allocation principle: markets tolerate massive speculative spending only while the core business demonstrates strength. The moment the core falters, every dollar of speculative spending becomes evidence of mismanagement rather than visionary investment. For founders and CEOs, this means the sequencing of bets matters enormously — you need the core business to be undeniably healthy before the market will grant patience for moonshot spending. Zuckerberg could spend $10B/year on the metaverse when Facebook was growing; the same spending became a liability the moment growth stalled.
