Anchorage, Alaska is an unlikely logistics powerhouse: a small city with the world's fourth-busiest cargo airport. Its value comes purely from geography — it lies on the great-circle path between the US and Asia, making it an ideal refueling and sorting stop. FedEx and UPS use the mandatory fuel stop to also sort packages, combining two operations (refueling + customs/sorting) into one. Without Anchorage, they'd need expensive non-stop flights from every Asian hub to every American hub.

This illustrates how geographic position can be an enduring competitive advantage that no amount of capital can replicate. You can't move Anchorage. Competitors who want the same routing efficiency must use the same airport — turning a cost center (fuel stop) into a shared but irreplaceable sorting node. For investors analyzing infrastructure businesses, the question isn't just 'what does this facility do?' but 'is its geographic position structurally irreplaceable?' Ports, airports, and pipelines at geographic chokepoints carry durable value precisely because alternatives require orders-of-magnitude more cost to achieve the same connectivity.
