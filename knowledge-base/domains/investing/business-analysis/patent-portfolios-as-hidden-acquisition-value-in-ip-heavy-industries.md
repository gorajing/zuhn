Google's Motorola Mobility deal looks like a $9.6B loss on paper: acquired for $12.5B, sold to Lenovo for $2.9B. But Google retained Motorola's patent portfolio, which included thousands of patents covering mobile technology fundamentals. These patents served as defensive ammunition against Apple's aggressive patent litigation targeting Android manufacturers. The real calculus wasn't hardware revenue vs. acquisition cost — it was the value of protecting an ecosystem generating billions in ad revenue from existential legal threats. This pattern recurs in IP-heavy industries: the operating business is sometimes just a vessel for the intellectual property, and buyers who understand this can extract asymmetric value that doesn't appear on conventional acquisition scorecards.
