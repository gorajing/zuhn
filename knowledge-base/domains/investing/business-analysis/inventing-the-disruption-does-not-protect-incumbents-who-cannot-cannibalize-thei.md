The strongest version of the innovator's dilemma is when the incumbent literally holds the patent or prototype for the disruptive technology. Kodak patented the digital camera in 1977 but made so much money from film that they let it gather dust. Xerox built the Alto with GUI, mouse, and networking in 1973 but upper management — 'copier heads' in Jobs' words — couldn't see beyond their core business.

This pattern reveals that the bottleneck is never invention but organizational willingness to cannibalize. The R&D lab can produce the future, but if the business model depends on the present, the organization will suppress its own innovation. This is why startups with zero legacy revenue can outmaneuver incumbents who literally own the technology — they have nothing to lose by building around it.

For investors, this means holding a patent portfolio or research capability is not a moat if the company's revenue structure creates incentives against deploying it. Watch for the gap between what a company's labs produce and what its business units ship.
