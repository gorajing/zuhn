Russia's informal rule of granting overflight rights to roughly one airline per European country means legacy carriers like SAS, Air France, and Lufthansa hold exclusive access to the most efficient Europe-to-Asia routing. Budget airlines like Norwegian Air have been repeatedly denied despite legitimate operational need. This creates a competitive moat that looks like market dominance but is actually sovereign-granted exclusivity. The legacy carriers didn't earn this advantage through product quality or efficiency—they received it through first-mover diplomatic positioning. This pattern appears wherever a foreign gatekeeper controls access to a critical resource and distributes it selectively: the beneficiaries get monopoly economics without the usual antitrust scrutiny because the barrier originates outside the domestic regulatory framework.
