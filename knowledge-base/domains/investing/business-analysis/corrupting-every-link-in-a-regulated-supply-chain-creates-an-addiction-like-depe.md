Purdue's strategy was not simply aggressive marketing — it was systematic corruption of every node in the pharmaceutical supply chain. Distributors received rebates, pharmacists got refunds, patients received starter coupons, doctors were flown to 'seminars' (golf trips), academia received grants, medical journals earned ad revenue, and politicians received campaign donations. The FDA examiner who approved OxyContin's abuse-resistance claim left the agency and joined Purdue within two years.

This multi-node capture created a system where no single actor had sufficient incentive to blow the whistle, because every participant was financially entangled. The $900M spent on lobbying and political payments over nine years was not a cost — it was the cheapest possible moat. This pattern appears in other industries where regulated supply chains exist: when you can't compete on product superiority, you compete on system capture. The corrective signal (lawsuits, public outcry) took over 20 years to materially affect operations.
