By 1980 Nike had roughly 50% of the US running shoe market and assumed the fitness boom would keep powering running specifically. It wouldn't. Running was a cyclical fad inside a much broader secular fitness movement, and the next wave was aerobics — which Nike refused to touch on principle because it was 'unathletic.' Reebok, founded in 1979, built an entire company on that single wave, passed Nike by 1988, and forced Nike into years of reactive recovery that only ended with Jordan.

The lesson for anyone evaluating market leaders: the question is never 'is the trend continuing' but 'which part of the trend am I actually exposed to.' A company riding a cyclical segment of a secular movement will look dominant right up until the segment peaks. The warning sign is principled refusal to participate in adjacent expressions of the same underlying demand — that's not discipline, that's identity lock-in, and it's usually how incumbents hand market share to faster-moving rivals.
