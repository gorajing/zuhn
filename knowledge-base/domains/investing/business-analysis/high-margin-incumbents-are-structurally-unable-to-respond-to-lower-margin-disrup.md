The classic innovator's dilemma played out cleanly in cloud. Oracle, IBM, and HP sold bundled hardware/software licenses at 70-80% gross margins, with multi-year upgrade cycles, audit-based enforcement, and enterprise sales teams as the distribution moat. AWS targeted 20-40% margins with pay-as-you-go, self-serve pricing, and credit-card signup. For Amazon, operating a 2% margin retail business, this was a 10-20x margin improvement and wildly attractive. For Oracle, cannibalizing their license business to go there was a 3x margin downgrade.

The structural lock-in runs deeper than 'they should have been braver.' A high-margin incumbent has: (1) a sales comp plan built on large contracts, (2) a cost base that assumes those margins, (3) investor expectations calibrated to the same, and (4) the actual profit pool concentrated in the most vulnerable product line. Any serious response requires rebuilding all four simultaneously, which almost never happens in time.

When evaluating incumbents facing lower-margin disruption, the question isn't 'will they see it?' They will. The question is whether their structure allows them to respond, and usually it doesn't.
