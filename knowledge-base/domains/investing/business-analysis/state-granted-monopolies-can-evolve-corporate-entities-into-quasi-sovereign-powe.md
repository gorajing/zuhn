The VOC's trajectory from trading company to quasi-state is not an anomaly — it's the logical endpoint of granting a corporate entity both economic monopoly and enforcement powers. The Dutch government created the VOC to counter English trade dominance, giving it monopoly rights over Asian trade plus the authority to maintain armies, wage war, mint currency, and establish colonies. Within decades, the company employed 70,000 people (0.01% of world population) and operated as an independent sovereign entity.

This pattern has modern relevance: tech platforms with monopoly positions over digital infrastructure increasingly exercise quasi-governmental powers (content moderation, identity verification, payment systems). The VOC case suggests that monopoly + enforcement authority is an unstable combination — the entity either gets dissolved by the state (as the VOC was in 1799) or functionally captures the state. The key insight is that this trajectory is structural, not contingent on bad actors.
