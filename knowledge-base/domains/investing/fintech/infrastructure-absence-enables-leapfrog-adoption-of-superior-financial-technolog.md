Africa's mobile money revolution demonstrates a counterintuitive pattern: lacking infrastructure can be an advantage. With only 20% banking penetration in sub-Saharan Africa versus 92% in advanced economies, countries like Kenya and Zimbabwe had no incumbent systems to displace. M-PESA launched in Kenya in 2007 by piggybacking on existing mobile networks (80% phone penetration), and Zimbabwe's EcoCash reached 6.7 million users versus only 2 million bank accounts by 2017.

This pattern has broad implications for technology adoption. In the US, the proliferation of banks and credit cards actually holds back mobile payment adoption because users must coordinate on a single app (Chase Pay, Google Pay, Venmo, etc.) while existing infrastructure already works. In Africa, the value proposition was undeniable: send a text or take a two-day bus ride. Telecom monopolies further accelerated adoption by ensuring universal access through a single dominant platform.
