A call option on a $100 stock bought for $10 illustrates all three: if the stock drops to $70, you lose only $10 (vs. $30 owning the stock). If it rises to $130, your $20 profit on a $10 investment is a 200% return (vs. 30% owning the stock). And airlines use oil call options to hedge fuel cost spikes.

This structural asymmetry is why the derivatives market is several hundred trillion dollars — multiples of the underlying securities. Options allow financial engineers to decompose a single underlying asset into 5, 10, or 50 different risk-reward profiles tailored to individual preferences. The catch: during normal times derivatives add liquidity and stability, but during market stress they can amplify crashes when everything moves in one direction simultaneously.
