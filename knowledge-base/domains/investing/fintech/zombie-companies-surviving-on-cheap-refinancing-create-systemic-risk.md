A 'zombie company' survives not through operational profitability but through ability to refinance debt at favorable rates. During extended low-interest periods, these companies appear healthy because they can service their debt. When rates rise, they all face refinancing crises simultaneously.

This creates correlated failure risk: instead of individual companies failing as their specific business models deteriorate (which the economy can absorb), entire cohorts of debt-dependent companies face solvency crises at the same time. The second bankruptcy of Rite Aid within 8 months illustrates that some companies were never viable - they were just borrowing time.
