The hard part of mobile payments isn't building the app — it's hitting critical mass. The US market illustrates this perfectly: Chase Pay, Google Pay, Square Wallet, PayPal, Apple Cash, Cash App, Zelle, and Venmo all compete for the same users, and none can achieve universal adoption because existing payment infrastructure is 'good enough.'

Africa solved this through two mechanisms simultaneously. First, telecom monopolies like Safaricom in Kenya provided built-in universal distribution — every customer on the dominant network could transact with every other customer. Second, the urgency was existential: the alternative to digital payments was physically dangerous multi-day cash journeys vulnerable to theft. This combination of monopoly distribution and life-or-death urgency is nearly impossible to replicate in mature markets, which explains why mobile payment adoption in developing economies consistently outpaces developed ones.
