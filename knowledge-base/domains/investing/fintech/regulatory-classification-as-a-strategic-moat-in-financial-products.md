The entire airline loyalty economy depends on a regulatory classification: governments treat points as rebates (like a grocery store discount) rather than taxable income. This is despite the fact that points function like income — they're accumulated, have monetary value, and can be redeemed for goods. The rebate classification also enables the employer-employee exploit where companies pay for flights but employees keep the points tax-free, effectively creating an untaxed compensation channel.

This regulatory treatment isn't a minor detail — it's load-bearing infrastructure for a multi-billion dollar industry. If points were taxed as income, the entire value proposition would collapse. For investors and founders, regulatory classification is an underappreciated moat. Products that achieve favorable classification early (like points as rebates, or crypto as property rather than securities in some jurisdictions) gain structural advantages that are nearly impossible for regulators to reverse once an industry scales around them.
