China's demographic crisis illustrates a pattern common to slow-moving systemic risks: the problem is visible decades in advance but feels abstract until the tipping point arrives. The large worker cohorts currently generating high tax revenue will 'in a single day' shift from peak tax contributors to pension collectors, creating a fiscal cliff rather than a gradual slope.

This 'gradually then suddenly' pattern applies beyond demographics to climate change, infrastructure decay, and institutional rot. The key insight for decision-makers is that by the time a demographic crisis feels urgent, the window for effective intervention has already closed — children born today won't contribute economically for 18+ years. Japan, where adult diapers now outsell infant ones, demonstrates what the end state looks like.
