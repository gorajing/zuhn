Jacobsen uncovered a disturbing pattern: most presidents enter office understanding deterrence as a concept but avoiding the operational reality of nuclear command. Clinton, according to Panetta, didn't want to deal with nuclear issues because he had too many other priorities. Reagan stated in a 1983 press conference that submarine-launched ballistic missiles could be recalled — they cannot, nor can ICBMs once launched.

This creates a compounding risk: the person with sole authority to launch nuclear weapons often has the least operational understanding of the system. Panetta only grasped the full weight when he became Secretary of Defense and realized he'd be the person the president would turn to with 6 minutes to decide. The lesson extends beyond nuclear policy: the most consequential decisions in any system are often made by people with the least preparation for them, because the decision seems too remote to warrant daily engagement until the crisis arrives.
