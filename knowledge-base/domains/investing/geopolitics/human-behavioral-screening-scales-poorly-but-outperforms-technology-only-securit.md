Ben Gurion airport uses the same metal detectors as the US but adds layered human-behavioral screening: vehicle checkpoints a mile out, plainclothes officers watching check-in areas, mandatory passenger interviews with probing questions, and a 1-6 risk scoring system that determines screening intensity. The result is a perfect security record at one of the world's most targeted airports. However, this system processes only 20 million passengers annually (comparable to San Diego), relies heavily on racial profiling that most democracies legally prohibit, and requires passengers to arrive three hours early. The core insight is that focusing on 'who' rather than 'what' is more effective, but the approach has hard constraints—ethical, legal, and operational—that prevent direct transplantation. Countries like the US and Belgium have adopted partial elements (behavioral detection officers, security interviews) with less-trained personnel and diluted effectiveness.
