Angola's post-civil-war oil boom is a near-laboratory demonstration of the resource curse. Between 2002 and 2010, the economy grew 534%, yet the Human Development Index ranking improved by a mere 4 positions globally. The everyday Angolan saw almost no change in education, health, or standard of living while Luanda became one of the world's most expensive cities.

The mechanism is straightforward: when wealth flows from extractive resources rather than broad economic productivity, it can be captured at chokepoints by whoever controls extraction rights. The dos Santos family allegedly siphoned $24 billion through insider contracts, shell companies across 41 countries, and self-dealing schemes. This pattern recurs across Equatorial Guinea, the Central African Republic, Zambia, and resource-rich nations globally. The implication for investors is that GDP growth in resource-dependent economies is a misleading signal—institutional quality (independent judiciary, press freedom, transparent contracting) matters far more than commodity prices for predicting broad-based development.
