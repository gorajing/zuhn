Russia's military logistics are built around its vast state-controlled rail network—the third largest in the world with 20,000 of 21,000 locomotives government-owned. This works brilliantly for domestic mobilization but creates a structural vulnerability: once operations extend beyond railyards, the military must rely on truck-based material-technical support brigades with only 408 vehicles and 1,870 tons of capacity per combined arms army. Retired US Lt. Col. Alex Vershinin calculated this limits effective support to ~90 miles from supply dumps at best. This pattern is not unique to Ukraine—the same logistics weakness plagued the Soviet-Afghan War in the 1980s, suggesting it's a structural feature of the Russian military model, not a temporary shortcoming.
