Austria's plan at Rivoli was to attack Napoleon from multiple directions simultaneously. On paper, this would overwhelm him. In practice, the different columns couldn't coordinate timing (mountains and weather caused delays), and Napoleon — positioned centrally — defeated each column individually. The dispersion that was supposed to be Austria's advantage became their fatal weakness.

In business, multi-front attacks against a focused competitor often fail for the same reason: launching in multiple markets, targeting multiple customer segments, or pursuing multiple product lines simultaneously disperses resources. The focused competitor can concentrate on defending each one sequentially. The multi-front strategy only works if the attacks are truly simultaneous and coordinated — which is much harder than it looks.
