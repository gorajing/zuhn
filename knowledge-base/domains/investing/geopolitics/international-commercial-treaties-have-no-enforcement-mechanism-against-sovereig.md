The Cape Town Convention was specifically designed to solve the problem of cross-border aircraft leasing — ensuring that lessors could repossess aircraft regardless of which country they were physically located in. Russia was a signatory. But when facing the loss of the majority of its civil aviation fleet, Putin simply signed a law allowing Russian airlines to re-register foreign-leased aircraft as their own property. The treaty had no enforcement mechanism beyond the signatories' continued willingness to participate.

This has profound implications for any cross-border commercial arrangement that relies on international law rather than physical control. The framework works when everyone benefits from cooperation, but breaks the moment defection becomes less costly than compliance. For investors and operators, the lesson is that legal protections in international commerce are ultimately backstopped by incentive alignment, not enforcement power. Assets physically located in a foreign jurisdiction are only 'yours' to the extent that jurisdiction's government agrees — and that agreement is conditional, not absolute. The $10+ billion in seized aircraft represents one of the largest state-sanctioned thefts in modern commercial history.
