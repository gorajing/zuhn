A third-generation Palestinian refugee tells Lex that while he has no hope in his lifetime ('I'm 50, I have seven kids, no hope'), his neighbor frames resilience differently: 'Nothing lasts forever.' This historical perspective — that all occupations eventually end — provides psychological survival fuel even when present conditions offer no rational basis for optimism.

For geopolitical analysis, this reveals a structural dynamic: occupied populations sustain resistance not through short-term optimism but through historical pattern recognition. The implication is that occupations create their own inevitable opposition — the longer they persist, the deeper the generational commitment to their end. This makes permanent occupation an inherently unstable equilibrium, which has direct implications for risk assessment in regions with protracted conflicts.
