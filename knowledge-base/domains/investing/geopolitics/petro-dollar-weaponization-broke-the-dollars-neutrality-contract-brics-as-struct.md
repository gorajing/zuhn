Professor Jiang's structural diagnosis: 'The very basis for having the US dollar as the global reserve currency is that it would remain politically neutral — the Americans guaranteed seamless politically neutral international exchange. When the US sanctioned Russia, removed Russia from SWIFT, and ordered the Europeans to freeze over $200 billion in Russian assets, that broke the contract.' The structural reasoning: countries hold US dollars as reserves because they trust the dollar will remain useful regardless of political conflicts. Once the US demonstrated it would weaponize dollar access against geopolitical adversaries, any country that might find itself on the wrong side of US foreign policy faced an existential reason to diversify. China, India, Saudi Arabia, and many others are doing exactly this. The BRICS response: Russia + China + Iran + India + Brazil + South Africa form a trading bloc that uses gold as the medium of exchange (gold is politically neutral; nobody can weaponize gold). A planned Eurasian railway connecting Russia + Iran + China would route trade overland, bypassing American naval control of sea lanes. The world increasingly looks at this and asks: 'Am I better off in the American system (dollars that can be frozen) or the BRICS system (gold that can't)?' The US strategic response (per Jiang): invade Iran to retake control of Middle East energy → force countries to keep buying energy with dollars → preserve dollar reserve status. Hence the Iran conflict isn't really about Iran per se — it's about the petro-dollar system. Also: consolidate Western Hemisphere energy (Venezuela, Greenland, Canada, Mexico, Colombia) so the world MUST come to the US for energy regardless of dollar status. The corollary for investors: the dollar's reserve-currency premium is structurally weakening. Holding 100% dollar-denominated assets exposes you to the slow-rolling devaluation as alternatives emerge. The non-obvious hedges: physical gold (BRICS' preferred medium), strategic mineral commodities (rare earths, copper, uranium), and equities in countries credibly outside both blocs (Singapore, UAE, Switzerland). The corollary for any country considering its reserve composition: the cost of holding 100% USD has shifted from 'low — convenient' to 'meaningful — politically risky.' Even allies are quietly diversifying. The corollary for understanding US foreign policy: many seemingly disconnected moves (Iran war, Venezuela seizure, threats to Canada/Greenland, etc.) are coherent under the single goal of preserving petro-dollar dominance as BRICS structurally erodes it. The narrative framing matters less than the underlying logistics-of-energy logic.
