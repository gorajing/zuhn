Jin identifies a clear pattern in Chinese economic history: reform produces growth, growth slows, new reform produces the next wave. Agricultural reforms in the 1980s, special economic zones, WTO accession in 2001 — each triggered a decade of expansion. The current challenge is that reform pace has slowed in the last 15 years, with political security priorities increasingly overriding economic optimization.

This is actionable for investors: China has not reached close to its economic potential, meaning there is massive upside if reform resumes. The key indicator to watch is not GDP numbers but reform announcements — particularly decentralization of economic decision-making, financial system liberalization, and private sector enabling policies. The pattern suggests that any return to aggressive reform would trigger another strong growth cycle.
