Napoleon's Egyptian expedition was far from the glorious success portrayed in French newspapers. He abandoned his army, the fleet was destroyed at the Nile, and the campaign achieved none of its strategic objectives. Yet through careful propaganda — controlling dispatches, commissioning heroic paintings, suppressing bad news — Napoleon returned to France as a conquering hero. This manufactured reputation became real political capital.

In markets and business, narrative control operates the same way: companies that successfully shape perception attract the investment, talent, and partnerships that make the perception real. The gap between reality and narrative is bridged by the resources that narrative attracts. This is why investor relations, PR, and founder storytelling aren't vanity — they're strategic infrastructure that converts perception into actual competitive advantage.
