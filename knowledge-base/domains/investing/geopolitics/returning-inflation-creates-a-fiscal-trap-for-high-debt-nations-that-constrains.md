For decades, Japan managed its enormous government debt because near-zero inflation allowed near-zero interest rates, making debt service cheap. With inflation now above 2%, the Bank of Japan must raise short-term rates, dramatically increasing debt servicing costs. The government can borrow to cover these costs, but this grows the debt pile and erodes confidence, driving long-term bond rates even higher in a self-reinforcing spiral. Long-term Japanese government bond rates have already begun soaring.

The only genuine solution — cutting spending on elderly benefits to fund defense and manage the debt — is politically toxic in an aging society. This creates a structural tension that will define Japanese economic policy for the next decade. The broader pattern applies to any high-debt nation entering an inflationary period after a long deflationary era: the transition from free money to normal monetary conditions can trigger a fiscal crisis even without reckless new spending. For investors, this means Japanese government bonds carry significantly more risk than historical patterns suggest, and Takaichi's political capital may be the scarce resource that determines whether Japan threads this needle.
