Chamath admits he missed China's unprecedented growth period because he couldn't speak the language, didn't understand the political landscape, and felt like an outsider. In contrast, India's English proficiency, democratic alignment with the US, and favorable conditions for American entrepreneurs made it feel approachable.

This reveals a structural bias in global capital allocation: money flows more easily to culturally legible markets. India's English-speaking, democratically-governed population creates a natural on-ramp for Western capital that China never had. For investors, this means honestly assessing your own cultural fluency as a risk factor — not just the target market's fundamentals. For emerging economies, it means cultural accessibility to major capital sources is itself an economic growth lever.
