Kotkin's striking historical claim: constitutional orders in England, early America, and France were established with deeply restrictive franchises — only property-owning men could vote, women couldn't vote, slaves weren't citizens. This gave the constitutional order 'breathing space' to embed itself before the category of citizenship was gradually expanded over generations. The birth defects (slavery, disenfranchisement) were eventually corrected because 'the category of citizenship and the constitutional order were already embedded.'

When constitutional revolutions are attempted in the mass age — Russia, Mexico, Iran, China, Portugal between 1905-1926 — the constitutionalists briefly take power and are then swept aside by leftist or rightist mass movements. Rule of law 'isn't enough' for a mobilized peasantry and working class that wants land, bread, and national self-determination right now. The constitutional epoch of the early 20th century 'turned out to be a bust.' The few exceptions (West Germany and Japan under American occupation; Taiwan and South Korea under authoritarian industrialization) are not replicable templates.

This reframes democracy promotion and political reform: the assumption that any society can adopt constitutional liberalism through a democratic revolution is historically unsupported. For investors thinking about long-horizon political development, it suggests skepticism about narratives predicting smooth transitions to liberal democracy in currently authoritarian mass societies.
