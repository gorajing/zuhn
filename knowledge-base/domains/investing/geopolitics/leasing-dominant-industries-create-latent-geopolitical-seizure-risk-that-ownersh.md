Of Russia's 980 passenger aircraft, 777 were leased and 515 of those from foreign companies. When sanctions hit, those foreign lessors were either legally required or commercially motivated to terminate leases — creating an overnight crisis that an ownership-heavy fleet would not have faced. Russia responded by simply re-registering the aircraft under Russian law, effectively stealing billions in assets, but this only works domestically — these planes can never fly internationally again without facing seizure.

The broader principle is that leasing optimizes for financial flexibility (lower capital outlay, fleet scalability) but creates a dependency chain where asset control ultimately rests with foreign entities and their governments. Any industry that has shifted heavily toward leasing — equipment, real estate, IP licensing — carries this latent vulnerability. The Cape Town Treaty was supposed to make aircraft leasing enforceable across borders, but a sovereign actor simply chose to ignore it, revealing that international commercial law has no enforcement mechanism against a state willing to bear the reputational cost.
