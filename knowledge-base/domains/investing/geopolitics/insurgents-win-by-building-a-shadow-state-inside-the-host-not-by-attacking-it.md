Paine borrows Clausewitz's triad of passion (people), creativity (military), and rationality (government) and points out that states already have these institutions while insurgents must build them from scratch. Mao's innovation was to treat revolution as a construction project: propaganda teams, cadres, mass organizations, land-reform bureaucracies, and guerrilla forces were all scaffolding for a parallel state that would eventually replace the Nationalists from within.

This reframes how to read any insurgency — from the Viet Cong to ISIS to modern political movements. The question isn't 'can they defeat the incumbent's army?' but 'are they building the institutional substrate of an alternative state inside the host's territory?' Counterinsurgency that only targets the armed wing misses the real center of gravity: the shadow government, the propaganda apparatus, and the service-delivery network that peels loyalty away from the incumbent.

The same pattern shows up in nonviolent contexts — platform companies, religious movements, political machines all grow by building institutional substitutes inside an existing order rather than by frontal confrontation. The winners play the long institutional game, not the short coercive one.
