Austria's victory at Aspern-Essling was a genuine shock — Napoleon's first real battlefield defeat. But instead of pressing the advantage with an immediate counterattack while Napoleon's army was disorganized and demoralized, Archduke Charles hesitated, debating whether to seek peace or continue fighting. Napoleon used those six weeks to summon reinforcements, build vastly superior bridges, and return with 164,000 men for Wagram.

In business, this is the failure to press competitive advantage: a company that wins a patent suit but doesn't immediately lock in market position, a startup that gains traction but doesn't aggressively scale while incumbents are still confused, an investor who identifies a mispricing but doesn't size the position adequately. The window of advantage is always shorter than you think, and opponents who survive will always come back stronger.
