Before 1962, India was explicitly non-aligned, treated China with extraordinary generosity (recognizing it in 1950, signing a friendship treaty, lobbying for its UN seat), and was actively uninterested in militarization. Nehru saw India's identity as moral and post-colonial, not martial. China's decision to seize Aksai Chin during the Cuban Missile Crisis — when the Soviets couldn't intervene — was tactically brilliant and strategically cheap in the short run. China got the roads it needed and the territory it wanted.

But the second-order effects were ruinous. India doubled its army to 750,000 within a decade, created ten mountain divisions purpose-built to fight China, and has never stopped being angry. More importantly, it foreclosed the single largest counterfactual prize available to China in the 20th century: a China–India partnership. As Paine puts it, 'if China and India had teamed up, we would be in a completely very different world order now.' Instead, China bought a hundred square miles of plateau and lost a civilizational ally forever.

The generalizable principle: when evaluating any aggressive move — territorial, commercial, or competitive — the relevant question isn't 'can I win this?' but 'what kind of relationship does winning this leave me with?' Permanent enemies are extraordinarily expensive. The optionality you destroy by creating one often dwarfs what you gain.
