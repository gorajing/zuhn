Bolivia maintains a 5,000-person navy despite being landlocked since losing its coastline to Chile in 1884. Mongolia's navy consists of a single tugboat with seven sailors. These aren't irrational expenditures — they're sovereignty signals. Bolivia's navy reinforces its constitutional claim to sea access; Mongolia's preserves institutional capability.

This pattern extends beyond military: states routinely fund institutions whose primary output is legitimacy rather than function. Antarctic research bases (30 countries maintain them), embassies in marginal states, and national airlines that lose money all serve similar signaling purposes. For investors evaluating state-owned enterprises or sovereign spending, distinguishing functional expenditure from sovereignty signaling explains otherwise puzzling budget allocations.
