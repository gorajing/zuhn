China's investment in railroads, highways, and airports in Tibet serves a dual purpose beyond military logistics — it facilitates Han Chinese migration to change Tibet's demographic composition. The government recognized that a buffer zone whose population sympathizes with the adversary is worse than useless; it becomes a fifth column. By moving hundreds of thousands of Han Chinese into Tibet and building infrastructure to support them, China is engineering a population that would resist rather than welcome Indian or foreign military presence. This reveals a broader principle: geographic buffer value is a function of both terrain and population loyalty. States that control territory but not hearts resort to changing the population itself, treating demographics as a manipulable strategic variable rather than a fixed constraint.
