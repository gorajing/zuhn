China's South China Sea strategy perfectly illustrates how defensive actions can become self-defeating. The Philippines was actively distancing itself from the US, presenting China with an opportunity to win a crucial ally that could guarantee Pacific access without confrontation. Instead, China's aggressive sovereignty claims and island-building campaign pushed the Philippines back toward the US orbit. This created a worse strategic position than inaction would have — China now faces a more unified ring of hostile maritime neighbors rather than the fragmented alliance structure it could have exploited. The broader principle applies to any competitive situation: when your defensive moves are visible to potential allies and read as aggressive, the security gain from the defensive position is offset by the diplomatic cost of coalition-building against you. The optimal strategy often requires accepting short-term vulnerability to preserve long-term alliance optionality.
