China's high-speed rail, launched as a 2008 crisis stimulus, succeeded at its political objectives—employment, steel/concrete sector rescue, crisis avoidance—but failed economically. Routes connected secondary cities with insufficient demand, ticket prices exceeded population affordability, and by 2015 interest payments alone surpassed operating income. Total liabilities reached 5.9 trillion yuan (~$900B, 5% of GDP). The Belt and Road Initiative follows the same pattern at global scale: $52B in renegotiated debt in 2020-2021 alone, with borrower countries unable or unwilling to repay. Both projects share the same structural flaw: when the decision to build is political rather than economic, there's no feedback mechanism to stop expansion before liabilities become unserviceable. The government then faces a choice between writing off sunk costs (politically impossible) or doubling down (issuing another 300B yuan in railway bonds). This 'prestige infrastructure trap' is not unique to China—it appears wherever governments use infrastructure spending as a political tool rather than an economic one.
