Känzig and Raghavan (2025) modeled the economic impact of shipping chokepoint closures and found that a 10% shipping cost increase produces relatively modest inflation and GDP effects. Critically, during the Iran war the Strait of Hormuz closure spiked oil prices by ~50% but shipping costs barely increased — ships simply avoided the strait rather than paying more to transit it.

This distinction matters for scenario planning: the economic damage from chokepoint conflicts comes overwhelmingly from commodity supply restriction (inelastic demand meets reduced supply), not from shipping logistics disruption. Investors and policymakers should focus on which commodities flow through a threatened chokepoint and their demand elasticity, rather than on general trade disruption metrics.
