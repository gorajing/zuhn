One of the most widely accepted theories for the Indus Valley Civilization's decline around 1750 BCE is that the Indus River simply changed course — a single hydrological event that undermined the agricultural surplus supporting millions of people across hundreds of cities. No amount of standardized bricks or sophisticated drainage could compensate for losing the water source everything depended on.

This maps directly to modern portfolio thinking and geopolitical risk assessment. Economies built around a single resource (oil states), companies dependent on a single platform (App Store-only businesses), or infrastructure tied to one geographic feature all share the same structural fragility. The Indus Valley was arguably more technologically advanced than contemporary civilizations, yet its concentration of dependency on one river made it more fragile, not less. Sophistication without diversification is a trap.
