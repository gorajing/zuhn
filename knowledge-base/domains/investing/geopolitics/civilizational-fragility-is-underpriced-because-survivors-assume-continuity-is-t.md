The Younger Dryas event demonstrates that advanced cultures can be nearly completely erased by natural catastrophe — the Clovis culture vanished entirely, megafauna went extinct across continents, and if Hancock is even partially right, an entire civilization was lost with minimal archaeological trace. Yet modern civilization makes almost no serious preparation for similar events despite knowing the Taurid meteor stream still exists and Earth passes through it twice yearly. This is pure survivorship bias at civilizational scale: every society that exists today has, by definition, never been destroyed, so destruction feels impossible. The same bias operates in financial markets (the 2008 crisis was 'impossible' because it hadn't happened in living memory), in technology (companies assume their platform will persist because it has so far), and in personal risk assessment. The Göbekli Tepe builders' decision to deliberately bury their greatest achievement — possibly as a time capsule — suggests at least some ancient peoples understood this fragility and took preservation seriously. The practical correction is to weight catastrophic tail risks more heavily than your experience suggests.
