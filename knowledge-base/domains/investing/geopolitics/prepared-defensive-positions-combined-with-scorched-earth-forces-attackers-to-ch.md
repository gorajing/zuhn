Wellington built the Lines of Torres Vedras — massive fortifications across the peninsula north of Lisbon — in secret over a year. When Massena's French army advanced into Portugal, they found the countryside stripped of food (scorched earth) and then hit the Lines: impregnable positions they couldn't storm, couldn't outflank (sea on both sides), and couldn't wait out because there was no food. Massena's army starved for months before retreating, losing thousands without a battle.

In business, this is the defensive moat strategy at its most complete: combine an impregnable market position (regulatory barriers, patents, network effects) with systematic denial of the resources an attacker needs (talent poaching, exclusive supplier agreements, platform lock-in). The attacker faces a binary choice: abandon the attack or bleed out trying. The key is that both elements are necessary — a strong position without resource denial can be besieged, and resource denial without a strong position can be outflanked.
