Kotkin's sharpest line: 'Communism can fail at everything. It can starve the people, it can kill the people. It only has to do one thing to survive: suppress political alternatives.' During Stalin's collectivization, the peasants had no political alternative — it was 'return to tsarism or keep communism.' During Mao's famines, the same. Today in China, the CCP's market reforms were driven by society pushing back against the ideology grudgingly, not by the party's brilliance — but the party survived because it never permitted political liberalization.

This reframes how Leninist regime durability should be assessed. The usual analyst framework — tracking economic performance, popular satisfaction, elite cohesion — is measuring the wrong thing. The only variable that matters for regime survival is whether political alternatives can organize. A regime that is doing everything badly but has successfully atomized its society and suppressed opposition is more stable than a regime doing everything well but with a viable opposition.

For investors and geopolitical strategists, this suggests that betting on regime change in Leninist systems based on economic distress, corruption, or public anger is a losing bet. The relevant question is the state of the opposition infrastructure — parties, civil society, independent media, religious networks — not the state of GDP or approval ratings. It also clarifies why Xi's crackdowns on tech and civil society are rational from the regime's point of view even when they visibly damage the economy: the alternative would threaten the only variable the regime actually has to protect.
