The U.S. economy in early 2026 presents a case study in how aggregates can obscure: GDP growth looks like a normal expansion, prime-age employment is at multi-decade highs, and inflation is only slightly above target. By any bird's-eye reading, this is a healthy economy. Yet beneath those aggregates, two things are simultaneously true that have rarely co-occurred: productivity growth is running at near-historic highs while job creation has stalled.

This composition effect is a general warning about economic inference. Averages and headline indicators are deliberately constructed to smooth out variation — that's their purpose. But decision-making under uncertainty often depends on the distribution beneath the average. For investors, this means always asking what the aggregate is composed of. For founders, it means that 'the market is healthy' is an insufficient guide to whether demand exists in the specific segment you're entering. Drilling into the sub-indicators is almost always more informative than trusting the headline number.
