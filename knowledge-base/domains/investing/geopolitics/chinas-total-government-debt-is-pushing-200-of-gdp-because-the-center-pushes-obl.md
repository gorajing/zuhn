Shih's core claim on Chinese debt is a structural accounting critique: the reported 60-70% central-government debt-to-GDP ratio is an artifact of how China pushes obligations downward. When Beijing wants a bridge, an AI science park, or a semiconductor fab, it doesn't appropriate money — it authorizes a local government to issue debt and tells them to deliver. The debt sits on the local balance sheet but the project priority is centrally dictated.

His estimate of 120-140% of GDP in local government debt, stacked on top of 60-70% central debt, puts China's total government debt near 200% of GDP. The 10 trillion renminbi special refinancing bond issuance in late 2025 was 'an accounting exercise' — it swapped high-yield debt for low-yield debt without reducing the principal. Meanwhile, civil servants and teachers in poor provinces go unpaid for 4-5 months of the year because local governments can't service existing obligations.

This matters for anyone modeling Chinese GDP growth forward. Shih forecasts China plateaus at 1x-1.2x US GDP by 2040 (not the 2-3x consensus), explicitly because there is no room for a consumption stimulus — the debt overhang forces continued supply-side/investment-led growth, which accelerates overcapacity and trade-war backlash.
