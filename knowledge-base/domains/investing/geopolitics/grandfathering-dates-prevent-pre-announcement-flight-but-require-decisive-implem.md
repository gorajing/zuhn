A standard objection to wealth taxes is that the rich will simply move before the tax takes effect. California's proposal addresses this by fixing the taxable base to California residents as of January 1st of the proposal year, meaning any billionaire who had not already left by that date is liable regardless of subsequent moves. This converts an anticipated-elasticity problem into a fixed-cost problem — by the time the political debate is public, relocation no longer helps. Only six billionaires were publicly reported to have purchased out-of-state real estate or formed external LLCs in response, and most did so after the grandfathering date. Design implication: the grandfathering cutoff must be backdated to before public debate begins, or the tax loses its binding power. This also means wealth-tax proposals have a one-shot window — the grandfathering date can only be set once, and subsequent proposals cannot quietly rewrite it without signaling new elasticity windows. For policy design, combining a backdated grandfathering provision with a hypothecated revenue destination gives the tax both capture power and durability.
