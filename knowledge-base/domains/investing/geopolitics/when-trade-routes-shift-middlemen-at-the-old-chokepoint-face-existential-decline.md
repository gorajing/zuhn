For centuries, Venice's wealth depended on controlling the Mediterranean chokepoint of East-West trade. When Vasco da Gama sailed around Africa to India in 1498, he created an alternative route that bypassed Venice entirely. Spices could flow directly from Asia to Western Europe via the Atlantic. Venice's geographic advantage — its entire competitive moat — was suddenly irrelevant. The city's decline was slow but irreversible.

This is the most direct metaphor for platform disintermediation: when a new route connecting producers and consumers bypasses the incumbent middleman, the middleman's entire value proposition collapses. Travel agents bypassed by online booking, newspapers bypassed by social media, record labels bypassed by streaming. The key warning sign is the emergence of any alternative path that connects the same two endpoints without passing through your chokepoint.
