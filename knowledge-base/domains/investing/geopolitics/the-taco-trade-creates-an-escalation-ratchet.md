As markets learn to price in presidential reversals, each threat must be more extreme than the last to produce the bond and currency moves that historically cause policy retreats. This creates a game-theoretic escalation ratchet: the very mechanism that prevents damage (backing down) requires increasing the stakes each round.

This pattern has broader implications for any leader whose policy tool is threat-based brinkmanship. The market's adaptive expectations create a tolerance effect similar to drug resistance — each dose must be larger, and eventually the dose required may exceed what's survivable. For investors, the key risk is the round where the bluff gets called or the backing-down mechanism fails.
