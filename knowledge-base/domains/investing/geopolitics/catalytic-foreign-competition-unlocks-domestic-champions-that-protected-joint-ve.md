From the early 1990s, China required foreign auto companies (GM, VW, Toyota, Honda) to form 50/50 joint ventures with local firms, with the theory that Chinese partners would absorb technology and eventually kick the foreigners out. This failed massively. After 25 years, Chinese JV partners were still clipping dividend coupons with all technology and design coming from foreign partners. No global-competitive national champion auto makers emerged.

BY 2015, Beijing pivoted to leapfrogging into EVs, subsidizing BYD and the battery supply chain for a decade. BYD got good at batteries and software, but was still making unappealing cars — the sexy vehicles were the JV SUVs. In 2018, China made an unprecedented decision: allow Tesla to build a wholly-owned Gigafactory in Shanghai. Tesla cars started selling in 2019 and were immediately popular. BYD and competitors responded by hiring German car designers, upgraded consumer design dramatically, and by 2022 could compete with Tesla on both price and quality.

The lesson: forced technology transfer through JVs produces dependence, not capability. Direct competition from best-in-class foreign players creates the pressure that produces real upgrading. Kroeber argues the US should apply this in reverse — invite Chinese EV and industrial investment onto US soil if America is serious about revitalizing manufacturing. Application: for any protectionist policy that mandates local partnership, ask whether the partnership structure actually creates competitive pressure or just rent extraction. Real upgrading requires facing the best, not owning half of them.
