China's control of Tibet illustrates a counterintuitive principle: the most strategically valuable territory is often the least economically productive. Tibet's 13% of China's land area houses only 0.2% of its population, but it extends China's border to the Himalayas and creates an infrastructure desert that prevents India from advancing troops toward eastern China. Similarly, Mongolia's Gobi Desert creates such long supply lines that any land-based invasion from the north becomes impractical. This inverts the usual assumption that territory value correlates with productivity — in geopolitical strategy, emptiness itself is the asset because it functions as a natural moat against conventional military forces.
