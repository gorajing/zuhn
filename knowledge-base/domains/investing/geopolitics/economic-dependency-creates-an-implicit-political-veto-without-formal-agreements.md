Australia sends nearly 30% of its exports to China — more than the next three partners (Japan, US, South Korea) combined. This concentration means China doesn't need formal treaties or military threats to constrain Australian foreign policy; the implicit threat of economic retaliation (tariffs, reduced private-sector engagement) is sufficient. When a Reserve Bank study shows a 5% Chinese GDP contraction would cut Australian GDP by 2.5%, the asymmetry is structural: Australia cannot afford confrontation, while China can easily absorb the loss of Australian trade.

This pattern generalizes beyond Australia-China. Any entity — country, startup, or individual — that allows a single counterparty to control a dominant share of its revenue has implicitly surrendered decision-making autonomy. The dependency doesn't require malicious intent to become coercive; the mere possibility of withdrawal reshapes behavior.
