The 'Yuppie Fishtank Theory' — that building high-end housing absorbs wealthy residents who would otherwise compete for existing lower-cost stock — continues to accumulate supporting evidence. A detailed Honolulu study traced the vacancy chain from a 43-story luxury condo project and found its 512 units created at least 557 vacancies citywide, with some units generating up to four downstream moves. New residents left behind homes that were 38% cheaper per square foot than what they moved into.

Cities applying this principle are seeing measurable results: rents for older units have fallen as much as 11% in markets with significant new luxury construction, with some reaching 'affordable' designation levels. This directly contradicts the popular intuition that luxury housing causes gentrification and raises rents. The evidence at this point approaches what economists consider a 'scientific law' of housing markets: building more housing reduces rent, regardless of the price tier of new construction.
