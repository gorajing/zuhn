The Dutch government invoked the Goods Availability Act — legislation from 1952 originally designed for Cold War resource management — to seize control of Nexperia from its Chinese parent company Wingtech. The government suspended Chinese leadership, placed shares under custodial management, and gave the Minister veto power over any company decision deemed harmful to European supply chains.

This is significant because it demonstrates that Western nations are not just creating new regulations but reactivating existing legal frameworks that were never repealed. The trigger was coordinated pressure from U.S. officials who made clear that keeping a Chinese CEO would put Nexperia on the Entity List, effectively cutting it off from the U.S. technology ecosystem. For investors and operators in cross-border tech, this creates a new category of sovereign risk: any acquisition involving sensitive technology is now retroactively vulnerable to government seizure under laws that may have existed for decades but were never enforced.
