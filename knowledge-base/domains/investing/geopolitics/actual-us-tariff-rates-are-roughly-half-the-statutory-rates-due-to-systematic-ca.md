Gopinath and Neiman (2025) document a systematic gap between announced and actual tariff rates. The Trump administration used product exemptions (e.g., semiconductors at 9% actual vs. 24% statutory), company-specific deals tied to domestic manufacturing commitments, and USMCA compliance provisions to significantly reduce effective rates. Taiwan's actual rate was 8% despite a 28% statutory rate.

A 14% tax on imports that represent 14% of GDP amounts to roughly a 2% effective tax increase on the economy — significant but not catastrophic. Combined with the data center boom providing offsetting demand, this explains the economy's resilience despite extreme consumer pessimism. However, historical research suggests most tariff GDP impacts materialize after 1-2 years, meaning the full effects may still be ahead.
