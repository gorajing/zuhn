The naive response to population decline is that productivity growth will compensate — fewer workers each producing more. Smith identifies three reasons this fails as a strategy. First, every country is already trying to raise productivity, so there's no unused lever to pull. Second, Chad Jones (2022) models how a shrinking researcher pool directly reduces the rate of productivity improvement — the very mechanism you need to compensate is itself degraded by the problem. Third, empirical data shows aging workforces have measurably lower productivity at the state-industry level.

This creates a compounding feedback loop rather than a simple linear decline. The working-age-to-elderly ratio is already falling (from 5:1 to 4:1 in the US, 2:1 in Japan), meaning each remaining worker bears more eldercare burden while simultaneously being less productive on average and having fewer peers working on productivity improvements. For investors and planners, this means population decline scenarios are systematically underestimated because models typically treat workforce shrinkage and productivity growth as independent variables when they're actually negatively coupled.
