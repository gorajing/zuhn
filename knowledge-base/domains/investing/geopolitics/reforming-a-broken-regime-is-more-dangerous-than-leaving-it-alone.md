Paine identifies sequencing as Gorbachev's decisive error: glasnost and perestroika released political pressure while the command economy — the actual source of decay — remained untouched and in fact deteriorated further under half-reform. Price controls without private property created backroom corruption instead of markets, and loosened censorship surfaced grievances faster than the regime could absorb them.

China's Deng Xiaoping ran the opposite experiment: keep the Communist Party's political monopoly locked down, and aggressively liberalize the economy behind that wall. Thirty years later the CCP is still in power and China is rich. The lesson generalizes beyond regimes: any troubled institution considering reform must sequence economic/structural fixes before loosening the controls that keep dissent contained, or the opening itself becomes the collapse.

Practically, this is why incumbent leaders of failing organizations — companies, governments, parties — who 'decide to finally fix things' so often fall fastest. Reform signals weakness and invites challenge before the fix can land.
