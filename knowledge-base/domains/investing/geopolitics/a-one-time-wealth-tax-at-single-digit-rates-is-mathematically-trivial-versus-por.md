The arithmetic of a one-time single-digit wealth tax on large portfolios is asymmetric. At 7% annual growth, a 5% levy is recovered by normal returns within approximately 9 months — inside the range of ordinary market volatility. In the documentary's concrete example, Mark Zuckerberg's portfolio grew by more than the proposed tax amount over the 4 months it took to produce the video. Framing the tax as an 'existential threat' contradicts this arithmetic: if a billionaire's wealth grows back within a year, the tax is economically closer to a fee than an expropriation. This asymmetry is important for policy design and political communication: lifetime one-time wealth taxes at low single-digit rates generate substantial public revenue while being arithmetically invisible to the taxed party. The political ferocity of opposition is therefore not proportional to the economic impact on individual billionaires — it is a positional defense of the principle that unrealized capital is untaxable, not a response to the specific rate. This implies that marginal rate negotiation is the wrong axis: opponents will resist 5% as hard as 15% because the fight is about the tax base, not the rate.
