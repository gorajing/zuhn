Japan's inward FDI stock of ~$350 billion is low for its economic size, and the government has set an explicit target to double it to $650-700 billion by 2030. This isn't passive openness—it's a structural bet that foreign capital, technology, and operating know-how can rebuild industrial capacity faster than domestic systems alone.

TSMC's $17 billion Kumamoto investment gave Japan advanced 3-nanometer chip processing, the most advanced foundry production outside Taiwan. Rapidus is explicitly designed to pull in global partners rather than rely solely on domestic incumbents, reversing Japan's previous failed strategy of going it alone in semiconductors.

Poland validated this playbook over two decades: annual FDI inflows exceeding $40 billion at peak, total inward stock surpassing $330 billion, driving GDP per capita (PPP) close to Japan's despite starting far behind. The pattern is clear—countries that treat FDI as a technology transfer mechanism rather than just capital injection achieve faster industrial modernization.
