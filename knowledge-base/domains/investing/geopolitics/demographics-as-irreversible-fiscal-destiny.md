Between 1991 and 2021, Russia's pension system went from 'easy mode' (large contributor base, small recipient pool) to a 44% increase in recipients with slightly fewer contributors. The post-Soviet birth rate collapse created a demographic wave that is now hitting the pension system with mathematical certainty — fewer teenagers today means fewer contributors tomorrow.

The key insight is that demographic problems are uniquely intractable: unlike economic or policy crises, they cannot be reversed with legislation, spending, or reform. The only solutions (immigration or dramatically higher birth rates) operate on generational timescales. This makes demographic imbalances the most reliable long-term predictor of fiscal and political crises in pension-dependent states — and this pattern is not unique to Russia. Most of Europe faces similar or worse ratios, suggesting pension protests will become a recurring feature of global politics.
