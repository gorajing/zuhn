Zimbabwe's 2000 land reform forcibly seized farms from experienced white farmers and redistributed them to political supporters. The stated goal was colonial justice; the actual purpose was buying political loyalty. The results were catastrophic: total agricultural output halved between 2000 and 2009, with some farms producing only a tenth of previous yields. By destroying its two largest crops (corn and tobacco), the policy simultaneously wrecked the economy and the food supply.

The deeper lesson is about tacit knowledge — the operational expertise that experienced farmers carried couldn't be transferred with the land titles. This pattern recurs across many domains: nationalizing industries, forcing out skilled immigrants, or replacing experienced teams with loyalists. The productive capacity of an asset depends far more on the expertise of its operators than on its physical characteristics. This is why brain drain is often more economically devastating than capital flight.
