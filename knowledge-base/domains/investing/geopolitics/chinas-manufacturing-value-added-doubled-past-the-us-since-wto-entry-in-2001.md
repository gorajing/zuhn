Ferguson highlights a single statistic that reframes the entire geopolitical picture: in 2001 when China entered the WTO, American manufacturing value-added was more than twice China's. Today, China's manufacturing value-added is more than twice that of the United States. On a purchasing power parity basis, China's economy has been larger than the US economy for at least eight years.

This isn't just an economic statistic — it's a hard-power indicator. Manufacturing capacity translates directly into military production capability, supply chain resilience, and technological independence. Ferguson argues this reversal means US dominance 'can no longer be taken for granted' and that the administration's seemingly erratic behavior is actually a rational response to a position of relative weakness that most Americans don't perceive. The implication is that US foreign policy will increasingly reflect the behavior of a declining hegemon managing retreat, not a dominant power choosing its battles.
