Across 2,000 years, Chinese dynasties followed a remarkably consistent arc: a vigorous founder establishes order, successors consolidate and expand, later generations grow complacent and corrupt, and eventually the dynasty collapses — often to a new vigorous founder. The Mandate of Heaven provided ideological framing, but the underlying pattern was structural: centralized institutions accumulate inefficiencies and rent-seeking over time until they can no longer respond to crises.

For investors and strategists, this pattern is more actionable as a timing heuristic than a predictive model. You can't predict which specific crisis will topple an institution, but you can assess where it sits in the cycle. Early-stage institutions (startups, new regimes, reform movements) tend to be adaptive and resource-efficient. Late-stage institutions (legacy corporations, aging governments) tend to be rigid and resource-wasteful. The dynastic cycle suggests that betting against late-stage institutions during periods of external stress is a higher-probability play than betting on their reform.
