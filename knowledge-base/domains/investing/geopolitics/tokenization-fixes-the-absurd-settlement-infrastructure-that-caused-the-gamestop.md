The stock market settlement system evolved from T+5 (5 days to settle) in the 1970s to T+1 today, but the fundamental absurdity remains: when you buy a stock, you appear to own it instantly, but actual settlement takes a full day. During that gap, brokers must post collateral to cover price changes, which is what triggered Robinhood's trading restrictions during GameStop — not collusion with hedge funds.

Tokenization solves this by enabling instant settlement (T+0), plus 24/7 trading through holidays and weekends, self-custody immunity from broker outages, and more efficient securities lending (currently still done via Bloomberg messenger peer-to-peer). Robinhood is pursuing this through tokenization in the EU and Robin Hood Ventures (a closed-end fund) in the US. The transition from private to public markets also matters: Sarbanes-Oxley made going public expensive, pushing companies to stay private through Series G, H, K rounds — meaning retail investors are locked out of the highest-growth phase. 'Series D was called an IPO' 30 years ago.
