Paine introduces the concept of a 'cooperative adversary': not a country that wants to cooperate, but one whose institutions are so fragmented that a stronger rival can effectively script its decisions against its own interest. China from the mid-19th to mid-20th century was a textbook case — wracked by civil wars, warlord fragmentation, and rapid government turnover, it kept signing treaties it didn't understand, entering united fronts that got its cadres massacred, and trading its industrial base for empty promises. Russia's strategic communication apparatus exploited this ruthlessly.

By contrast, when Stalin tried the same script-writing against Hitler via the Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact, it failed — Nazi Germany had strong institutions, well-educated cadres, and Mein Kampf on the record. You cannot hoodwink a state that has the internal coherence to read signals clearly and act on them.

The generalizable lesson: institutional capacity is not just about domestic governance, it's a defense against being scripted by adversaries. For investors and strategists, the same pattern applies to firms and movements — an organization with weak internal coordination can be maneuvered by competitors, acquirers, or regulators into self-harming moves that a more coherent rival would simply refuse. Track state capacity (or institutional capacity) as a leading indicator of whether an entity can resist manipulation.
