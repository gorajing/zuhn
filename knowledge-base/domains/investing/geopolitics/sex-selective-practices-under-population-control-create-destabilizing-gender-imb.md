When families are limited to one child in a culture that strongly prefers sons, sex-selective abortion and abandonment of female infants create severe gender imbalances. China's sex ratio at birth reached 120 boys per 100 girls in some provinces (natural ratio: ~105:100). The aggregate result is an estimated 30-40 million 'surplus' men who cannot find domestic partners.

This surplus has measurable second-order effects: increased crime rates in areas with the worst imbalances, a marriage market that drives up bride prices and cross-border trafficking, and a large cohort of men with weaker family ties and social integration. For geopolitical analysis, this demographic creates both internal stability risks and external pressure — some analysts argue surplus male populations correlate with increased state willingness to engage in military adventurism, though this is debated. The broader lesson is that population control policies interact with existing cultural preferences in ways that produce severe unintended consequences that take decades to manifest and decades more to resolve.
