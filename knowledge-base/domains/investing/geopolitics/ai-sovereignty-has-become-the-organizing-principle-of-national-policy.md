The AI Index 2026 Chapter 8 documents that AI sovereignty — state agency over domestic AI capabilities — has emerged as a central organizing principle of national AI policy. More than half of newly adopted national AI strategies in 2024 came from developing economies. Sub-Saharan Africa (Ethiopia, Ghana, Nigeria), South and Central Asia (Sri Lanka, Nepal), and Latin America (Costa Rica, Jamaica) are entering the formal policy landscape for the first time. But infrastructure sovereignty is wildly unequal: as of 2025, China has 85 public or public-private AI supercomputers, Europe and Central Asia have 44 (up from 3 in 2018 via EuroHPC JU), North America has 41, East Asia and the Pacific have 27, Latin America and the Caribbean 8, Middle East and North Africa 3, and South Asia just 2. Data localization measures mirror the pattern — East Asia Pacific has 77, Sub-Saharan Africa 71, Europe 66, North America just 3. The geopolitical interpretation is that the world is bifurcating into high-sovereignty regions building domestic AI stacks (China, EU, parts of East Asia) and low-sovereignty regions that will depend on foreign AI infrastructure (most of the Global South, North America as a flow-first exception). National AI strategies document intent; supercomputer counts document actual capacity. The gap is huge.
