The Apollo program achieved its stated goal — landing on the Moon — but was cancelled shortly after because its real purpose was geopolitical: demonstrating American technological superiority over the Soviet Union. Once the Soviet space program stopped competing (after repeated failures and leadership changes), the political justification for Apollo's enormous cost disappeared.

In business, initiatives justified primarily by competitive pressure face the same vulnerability: if the competitor fails, pivots, or exits, the internal justification for the initiative evaporates. Companies that start projects to 'match' a competitor's offering often cancel them when the competitor stumbles. The lesson: programs should have independent value propositions beyond 'because our competitor is doing it' — otherwise they're as vulnerable as Apollo was to changes in the competitive landscape.
