For three decades after the Cold War, the US operated in a unique structural position: no peer competitors. This allowed unilateral rule-setting, institution-shaping to reflect American values, and the ability to project power globally without coalition-building. That structural condition — not any particular policy or military capability — was what defined the unipolar moment.

The mechanism of its ending is instructive: not American decline, but Chinese construction. China built comparable manufacturing capacity, military industrial output, and alternative institutions (BRICS, AIIB) that give non-Western states options outside the US-led framework. Once alternatives exist, the hegemon's leverage drops even if its absolute capabilities are unchanged.

This pattern has investment implications: the premium attached to US dollar assets, US-led institutions, and US rule-setting is structural — it depends on unipolar conditions. As multipolarity becomes more entrenched, that premium faces a slow structural headwind independent of US economic performance.
