Zimbabwe twice attempted technical fixes to its currency crisis — removing 10 zeros from banknotes in 2008 and 12 in 2009 — but both failed. The fundamental issue was that inflation had become self-reinforcing: prices rose largely because people expected them to. The government simultaneously undermined its own credibility by continuing to print money to fund the Second Congo War, spending $500,000 USD weekly on banknotes that were worthless by the time they arrived from Germany.

This reveals the dual requirement for stopping hyperinflation: you must both stop the mechanical cause (printing money) and restore belief in the currency's value. The second requirement is why hyperinflation recovery is fundamentally a political problem, not a technical one. Zimbabwe's inflation only stabilized after Mugabe's 2017 ouster — and promptly returned when the new government reintroduced the Zimbabwe Dollar, showing how fragile monetary confidence remains without deep institutional reform.
