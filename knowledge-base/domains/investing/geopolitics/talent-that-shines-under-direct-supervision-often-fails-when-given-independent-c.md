Napoleon's marshals present a natural experiment in delegation: the same men performed brilliantly under Napoleon's personal direction but often failed in independent command. Soult was a capable corps commander under Napoleon but lost repeatedly to Wellington in Spain. Bernadotte was effective in combined operations but unreliable independently. Only Davout and Suchet consistently succeeded without Napoleon's oversight.

This maps directly to the Peter Principle in organizations: promoting excellent executors to strategic leadership roles frequently fails because the skills are different. A great engineer doesn't automatically become a great engineering VP. A great salesperson doesn't automatically become a great VP of Sales. The critical skill gap is between executing within someone else's framework versus creating the framework itself. Organizations that conflate execution talent with leadership talent create Soults — people who are competent but inappropriate for their role.
