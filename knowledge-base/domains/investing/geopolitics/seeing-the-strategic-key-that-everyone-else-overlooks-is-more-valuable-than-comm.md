Everyone at Toulon was focused on attacking the harbor directly — a well-defended position that would have been enormously costly to storm. Napoleon, a junior artillery officer, identified that capturing Fort Mulgrave on a nearby promontory would make the harbor untenable for the British fleet. Instead of attacking strength, he attacked the leverage point. The fort fell, the British withdrew, and Toulon was liberated.

In business, the equivalent is identifying the one leverage point that unlocks an entire market or solves a complex problem: the single API that enables an entire platform, the one partnership that opens a distribution channel, the key hire that transforms team capability. Most organizations attack problems head-on with brute force (more spending, more features, more hiring). The rare strategic insight is identifying the leverage point where minimal investment produces maximum impact.
