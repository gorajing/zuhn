When the US joined Pakistan to the Baghdad Pact and extended a military aid treaty in the 1950s, the stated purpose was to wall off the Soviet Union from Middle Eastern oil. But Pakistan's primary adversary was India, not the USSR. The moment the tanks and planes arrived, they were implicitly pointed east, not north. Nehru's reaction was exactly what the rule predicts: 'You're arming the Pakistanis. Whom do you think they're going to shoot? It'll be us.' By 1965 Pakistani crews were using US tanks against India in the largest tank battle since WWII — not against Soviets.

The structural lesson: when you supply weapons to a regional actor, the weapons' ultimate target is determined by the recipient's threat model, not yours. If your enemy and their enemy are the same, alignment is automatic. If not, you have just rearmed a regional conflict that will burn your diplomacy on both sides — and you will eventually be blamed by both.

This applies well beyond 1950s South Asia: whenever aid flows to a country whose 'primary enemy' diverges from the donor's, expect the aid to be redirected toward the recipient's fight, expect the recipient's rival to treat the donor as an adversary, and expect the donor to get neither gratitude nor strategic leverage.
