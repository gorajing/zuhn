Minnesota's Northwest Angle exists because of a surveying error in defining the US-Canada border along the 49th parallel. The couple hundred residents must drive through Canada to reach the rest of the US. North Dakota may not have technically been a state until 2012 due to a missing sentence in its constitution. Tennessee's border with Arkansas still reflects a long-abandoned course of the Mississippi River, creating enclaves and exclaves.

These examples demonstrate that governance boundaries, once established, become load-bearing structures that accumulate dependencies — property rights, tax jurisdictions, electoral districts, infrastructure routing. Correcting the original error would require unwinding all downstream dependencies, which always costs more than simply accommodating the absurdity. This is the same reason legacy code survives in production, why corporate org charts retain vestigial structures, and why international borders rarely rationalize post-establishment. For investors analyzing geopolitical risk, the implication is that irrational-seeming boundaries are not fragile — they are among the most durable structures in governance.
