Smith draws a parallel between Xi Jinping at 72 and Stalin's terminal paranoia, arguing that aging autocrats who rose through patronage and backstabbing become increasingly focused on internal threats as they weaken. With no term limits and no successor, Xi may spend his remaining decade-plus in power fighting real or imagined internal challengers rather than launching external conflicts like a Taiwan invasion.

For investors and geopolitical analysts, this creates a counterintuitive risk profile: the probability of dramatic external military action may decrease while the probability of erratic domestic economic policy increases. Chinese companies and local governments may prove resilient enough to limit the damage, but the policy environment becomes less predictable. By the time a post-Xi leadership emerges in the late 2030s or 2040s, demographic decline and AI disruption may have changed the calculus entirely.
