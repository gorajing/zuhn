Napoleon's plan to split Wellington and Blucher was strategically brilliant — defeat each in detail before they could unite. But execution failed at every delegation point: Ney was slow at Quatre Bras, allowing Wellington to consolidate. Grouchy failed to prevent Blucher's army from marching to Waterloo, pursuing a phantom force instead. Neither marshal showed the independent judgment Napoleon's strategy required.

This is the principal-agent problem at its most lethal. A strategy that requires perfect execution by autonomous agents, without the leader being present to course-correct, carries catastrophic risk. In business, founders who design strategies requiring their personal involvement at every decision point create the same vulnerability — and those who delegate to lieutenants lacking strategic judgment face the same Waterloo.
