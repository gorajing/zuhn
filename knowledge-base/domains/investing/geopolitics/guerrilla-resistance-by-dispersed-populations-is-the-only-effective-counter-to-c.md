The Spanish guerrilleros invented modern insurgency: avoiding pitched battles, attacking supply lines, assassinating couriers and collaborators, and melting back into the population. No individual guerrilla action was militarily significant. But the cumulative effect was devastating: French forces couldn't concentrate because every garrison needed protection, supply lines required massive escorts, and intelligence was impossible to gather in a hostile population.

In business, this maps to how ecosystem participants resist dominant platforms: no single developer, creator, or supplier can challenge a platform monopoly directly. But distributed actions — forking projects, building alternatives, regulatory advocacy, standards organizations — create cumulative friction that gradually erodes the platform's advantages. The guerrilla principle: you can't win any individual engagement, but you can make occupation so expensive that the occupier eventually withdraws.
