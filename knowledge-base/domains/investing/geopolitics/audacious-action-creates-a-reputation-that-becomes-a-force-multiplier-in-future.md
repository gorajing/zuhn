After Lodi, Arcole, Rivoli, and other Italian victories, Napoleon's name carried psychological weight. Future opponents approached battles against him with fear and caution, often making defensive errors they wouldn't have made against an unknown general. His reputation became a force multiplier — the enemy was partially defeated by their own expectations before fighting began.

In business, brand reputation operates identically: companies with a track record of aggressive competition (Amazon entering your market, Apple launching a competing product) cause competitors to react with excessive caution or panic. The reputation itself becomes a competitive advantage separate from actual capability. Building a reputation for decisive, successful action early creates compound returns as future competitors are pre-intimidated.
