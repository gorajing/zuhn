Where America blocks new housing, infrastructure, and energy while subsidizing consumer demand (mortgages, healthcare, student loans), China bulldozes 500-year-old temples to build empty skyscraper complexes nobody lives in. The US can't rebuild fallen bridges; China builds bridges to nowhere. This isn't a minor policy difference — it shapes everything from real estate cycles to AI hardware capacity.

For investors and policymakers, the asymmetry matters: China's surplus capacity creates deflationary export pressure on the rest of the world, while its suppressed household consumption explains the chronically weak stock market and the reluctance of capital to stay in-country. Neither system is stable — both are pathological in opposite directions, and both are politically locked in by the constituencies they serve.
