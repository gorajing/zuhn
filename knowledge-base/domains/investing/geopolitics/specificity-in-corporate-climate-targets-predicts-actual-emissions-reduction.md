Stanford finance/AI talk on corporate climate target credibility: 11K companies, decade of CDP survey responses (50% of global market cap), state-of-art language models running on the data. Finding 1 (positive): specificity (quantitative numbers, proper nouns, action items) predicts emissions reduction. The coefficient is comparable to a 20% reduction in scope-2 emissions over 3 years. Finding 2 (negative): vague aspirational language ('net zero,' 'renewable') predicts INCREASED emissions when the target audience includes investors. Coefficient comparable to 2x the typical 3-year direct emissions trajectory. Interpretation: the rich companies that emit most can afford polished marketing language to investors, but their actual operations don't match the language.

Application: ESG investing using corporate disclosures as signal needs to filter for specificity vs. aspiration. The widely-used 'commitment to net zero' filter likely correlates with higher emissions, not lower. Investors should look for proper nouns, dates, dollar amounts, named projects in climate disclosures, not aspirational vocabulary.
