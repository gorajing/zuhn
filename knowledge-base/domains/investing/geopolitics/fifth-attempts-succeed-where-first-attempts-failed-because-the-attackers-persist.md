Austria's strategy of repeatedly sending relief armies to Mantua was individually unsuccessful but cumulatively stressful. Each relief attempt forced Napoleon to break off siege operations, march to meet the threat, win a battle, then return to the siege. Even though he won every time, the cumulative cost in casualties, time, and supplies weakened his position. Only Napoleon's exceptional tactical ability prevented this attrition strategy from working.

In competitive terms, even failed attacks impose costs on the defender: competitive product launches that flop still force the incumbent to respond, legal challenges that are dismissed still consume management attention, new market entrants that fail still drain the incumbent's resources in monitoring and response. The attacker's persistence can exhaust the defender even without a single individual success.
