Harris argues that when individuals accumulate enough power to get their own regulators fired and reshape information ecosystems, traditional institutional checks fail. He positions consumer boycotts (specifically of Tesla) as the only remaining lawful mechanism to apply pressure, while explicitly condemning vandalism and violence as counterproductive and victimizing innocent people.

This reflects a broader pattern in political economy: when regulatory capture occurs, market mechanisms become the accountability tool of last resort. The European Tesla boycott he references demonstrates that consumer coordination can move stock prices and board-level decisions even when government regulators cannot act. For investors, this creates a specific risk category — companies whose leaders take politically polarizing positions face consumer boycott risk that isn't captured in traditional financial analysis. The insight extends to platform economics generally: platforms owned by politically active individuals carry unique governance risks that compound over time.
