In 1848, revolutions erupted almost simultaneously across France, Germany, Austria, Italy, and Hungary. The speed and breadth of the upheaval stunned established governments. But the revolutionaries in each country had different goals (liberal constitution, national unification, social reform) and couldn't coordinate across borders. Once the initial shock wore off, conservative governments regrouped and defeated each revolution individually.

In business, simultaneous disruptive threats from multiple startups or new entrants often fail for the same reason: each pursues its own strategy without coordination, allowing the incumbent to address each threat sequentially. The incumbent's advantage is that it only needs to survive the initial shock — if the disruptors can't coordinate a sustained, unified campaign, the established player's resources and institutional resilience eventually prevail.
