The direct cost of Somali piracy (ransoms paid) is a fraction of its true economic impact. The full cost of $4.9-8.3 billion annually includes insurance premium increases, private security contracts, naval patrol funding, route diversions, and fuel costs from speed increases (an extra $88,000/day per ship just for going from 13 to 18 knots). These costs cascade through supply chains and ultimately land on consumers as an invisible 'piracy tax' embedded in the price of goods.

This pattern generalizes to all geopolitical instability: Houthi attacks in the Red Sea, Strait of Hormuz tensions, South China Sea disputes — each generates similar invisible cost layers. For investors, this means that the market impact of regional instability is systematically underpriced because the costs are diffused and invisible. Companies with supply chain transparency and route optimization capabilities have a structural advantage in absorbing these shocks. The insight also applies to startup founders building logistics or trade-finance products: there's real value in making these hidden costs visible and manageable.
