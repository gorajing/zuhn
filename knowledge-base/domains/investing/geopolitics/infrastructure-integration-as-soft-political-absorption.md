China's Greater Bay Area plan demonstrates how infrastructure investment can serve dual purposes: genuine economic efficiency and political absorption. By connecting Hong Kong and Macau to the mainland via high-speed rail and bridges, China creates economic dependencies that make the regions' separate legal and political systems increasingly inconvenient rather than protective.

This pattern — using bridges instead of walls — is harder for the international community to oppose because each individual project has a legitimate economic justification. The cumulative effect, however, is a gradual erosion of the practical separations that maintain autonomy. Mainland immigration checkpoints in downtown Hong Kong, unified insurance requirements, and shared transport systems all normalize cross-border governance in ways that formal legal changes would provoke resistance.
