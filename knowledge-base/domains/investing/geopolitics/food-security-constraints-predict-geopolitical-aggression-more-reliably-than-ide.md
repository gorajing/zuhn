Both major Axis powers faced genuine food security crises before expansion. Japan, as an island nation with limited arable land and a growing population, could not feed itself. Germany faced similar constraints exacerbated by WWI-era blockade memories. Both chose military expansion primarily to solve food equations, with ideology providing the narrative framework.

This insight applies to modern geopolitical risk assessment: tracking a nation's food import dependency ratios and agricultural land constraints may predict aggressive foreign policy moves more reliably than monitoring ideological rhetoric. Countries with high food import dependency and limited agricultural expansion options face existential pressure that transcends ideology. For investors, this means food security metrics (arable land per capita, import dependency ratios, climate vulnerability of domestic agriculture) deserve weight alongside traditional political risk indicators.
