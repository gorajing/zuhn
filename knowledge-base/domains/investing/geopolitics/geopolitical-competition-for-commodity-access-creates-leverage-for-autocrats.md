Within months of the civil war ending, China signed a $2 billion oil-backed loan with Angola while President dos Santos was invited to the Bush Oval Office. Angola became China's largest oil supplier while also courting American investment. This bidirectional courtship gave the dos Santos regime enormous leverage—if one power pushed too hard on governance reforms, the other was waiting.

This dynamic is a recurring pattern in resource geopolitics. When a country holds strategic commodities and multiple buyers compete, the seller can play buyers against each other, extracting maximum economic benefit while minimizing governance conditionality. For investors, this means resource-rich nations courted by competing powers are paradoxically more likely to maintain kleptocratic governance longer, because external accountability pressure is diluted by competition. The investment signal: when you see US-China bidding wars over resource access in a weak-institution country, that's a leading indicator of prolonged elite capture, not accelerated reform.
