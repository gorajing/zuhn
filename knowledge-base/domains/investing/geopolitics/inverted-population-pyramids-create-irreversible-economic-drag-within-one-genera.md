China's one-child policy created an extreme version of the dependency ratio problem: a massive cohort of elderly parents supported by a much smaller cohort of working-age children. By 2050, China is projected to have roughly 2 retirees for every 3 workers, compared to the 1:5 ratio it enjoyed during its economic boom. This isn't just a budget problem — it fundamentally constrains economic output, savings rates, and innovation capacity.

The critical insight is the irreversibility: even if fertility rose to replacement rate tomorrow, it takes 20+ years for newborns to enter the workforce. Meanwhile, the existing large elderly cohort draws increasing resources for healthcare and pensions. This creates a 'demographic debt' that compounds like financial debt — each year of inverted pyramid makes the eventual correction harder. For investors and strategists, this means China's demographic headwinds are not a problem that can be solved by policy changes; they are baked into the existing age structure for at least two decades.
