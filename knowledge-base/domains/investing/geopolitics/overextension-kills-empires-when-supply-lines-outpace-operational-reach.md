Napoleon invaded Russia with 600,000+ men, the largest army Europe had seen. But the fundamental problem was logistical: no army could be supplied across the vast distances of Russia using 19th-century technology. Disease, desertion, and starvation halved the army before any major battle. Napoleon waited in Moscow for a surrender that never came, hoping the enemy would capitulate before his logistics collapsed.

This pattern repeats in business: companies that expand faster than their operational infrastructure can support face the same death spiral. The revenue looks impressive during the advance, but the cost of maintaining overextended operations compounds invisibly until collapse becomes inevitable. The critical question is always whether your supply lines (cash reserves, operational capacity, talent pipeline) can sustain the position you're trying to hold.
