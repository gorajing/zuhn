The 'Grace Rule' established that products with less than 1% asbestos would not be regulated. This single definitional victory meant that contaminated vermiculite insulation — used in the World Trade Center and millions of homes — was legally marketed as 'asbestos-free.' The detection method (PLM) was also conveniently unable to detect fibers below the 1% threshold.

This is a structural insight about regulatory strategy: the most powerful form of regulatory capture is not lobbying against specific rules, but controlling the definitions and measurement methodologies that determine what counts. Whoever defines the threshold controls whether the problem officially exists. This pattern recurs in financial regulation, environmental standards, and technology classification.
