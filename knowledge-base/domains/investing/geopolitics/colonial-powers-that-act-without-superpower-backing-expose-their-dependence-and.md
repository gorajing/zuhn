The Suez Crisis was a moment of truth: Britain and France attacked Egypt successfully from a military standpoint. But the US, furious at not being consulted, threatened to sell British pound sterling reserves and block IMF loans. Britain, dependent on American financial support, was forced into humiliating withdrawal within days. The episode didn't cause British decline — it revealed and accelerated it by demonstrating that Britain could no longer act independently of American approval.

In business, this maps to companies that discover their independence is illusory: a firm that launches a major initiative opposed by its largest investor, platform partner, or regulator discovers that its apparent autonomy was conditional. The Suez pattern is: attempt independent action, face financial pressure from the entity you actually depend on, retreat, and emerge permanently diminished. This is why platform dependency is so dangerous — the platform partner can veto your strategy through economic pressure alone.
