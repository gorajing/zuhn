At each funding round, consensus declared the valuations were insane: Facebook at $100M was crazy, at $500M Greylock was 'stupid,' and Yuri Milner investing at $5B in common stock was 'dumb.' In hindsight, all were brilliant. Paying 100x revenue for Stripe in 2011-2012 seemed absurd. The best companies always look like you're paying too far ahead.

The investor's edge comes from one of three sources: (1) inside information — Milner had run a Russian social network and could calculate actual per-user monetization potential that others couldn't, (2) deep market understanding — knowing the customer or use case better than anyone, or (3) natural intuition calibrated by pattern recognition. The trap: many investors apply 'the best investments look expensive' logic to bad companies too, investing at high valuations in companies that don't have the same characteristics. The pattern is real but not universally applicable.
