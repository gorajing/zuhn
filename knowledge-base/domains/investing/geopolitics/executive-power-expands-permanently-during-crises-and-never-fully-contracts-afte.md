Lincoln expanded executive power during the Civil War, Woodrow Wilson during WWI, FDR during the Depression and WWII, and subsequent presidents during the Cold War and War on Terror. In each case, emergency powers were claimed as temporary but became permanent. The institutional apparatus (agencies, authorities, precedents) built for each crisis persisted and expanded.

In business, the same ratchet effect applies to management authority: powers claimed during company crises (centralized decision-making during turnarounds, expanded monitoring during remote work transitions, executive authority during competitive threats) rarely revert to pre-crisis norms. Each crisis justifies expanding authority, and each expansion becomes the new baseline. This is why organizations tend toward increasing centralization over time despite the efficiency of distributed decision-making.
