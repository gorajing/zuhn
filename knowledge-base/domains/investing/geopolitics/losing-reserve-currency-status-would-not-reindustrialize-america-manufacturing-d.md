A popular narrative claims the dollar's reserve currency status causes trade deficits that hollowed out American manufacturing. Krugman's analysis shows this is mostly wrong on two levels. First, the reserve currency story only partially explains trade deficits. Second and more importantly, trade deficits account for only a small fraction of manufacturing's long-term decline as a share of the economy.

The US manufactures trade deficit is ~4% of GDP. Eliminating it would raise manufacturing value-added by roughly 60% of that change (because manufacturers buy many services), yielding about a 2.5 percentage point increase — from 10% to 12.5% of GDP. Germany provides the definitive counterexample: despite running massive trade surpluses, it experienced the same huge long-term decline in manufacturing employment share. The UK's loss of reserve currency status during the World Wars didn't revitalize its manufacturing either — it became a manufacturing 'midget.' Industrial policy, not currency policy, is the lever for reindustrialization.
