The 1947 UN partition resolution attempted to solve the problem of two irreconcilable national communities by creating two states. The pattern that emerged — international body proposes compromise, one side accepts, other rejects, war follows — has recurred in multiple contexts. Even the Soviet Union's Gromyko initially supported a one-state solution but acknowledged that if the two communities couldn't coexist, partition was necessary.

For geopolitical investors and analysts, this pattern is diagnostic: when an international compromise is rejected by one side, escalation follows with near-certainty. The key variable is whether rejection stems from fundamental incompatibility of goals (eliminationist) or negotiating tactics (positional). In the Israel-Palestine case, both sides ultimately aborted the partition resolution — Israel through expansion, Arabs through military rejection — suggesting the goals were incompatible from the start.
