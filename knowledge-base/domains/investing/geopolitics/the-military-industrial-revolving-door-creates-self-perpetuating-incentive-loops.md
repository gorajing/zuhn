Gabbard describes the structural mechanics of war profiteering: defense contractors like KBR/Halliburton made trillions while paying imported workers $500/month and charging $35 per soldier per meal. The deeper systemic issue is the revolving door -- Pentagon officers and DoD civilians rotating between government and defense contractor positions, and Congressional staffers doing the same. The people writing contracts are the same people who later collect from those contracts.

This is a textbook example of incentive misalignment at institutional scale. The same pattern appears in other industries (banking regulators joining banks, tech regulators joining tech companies), but the military-industrial version has uniquely high stakes because the 'product' is war. For anyone analyzing geopolitical risk or defense-adjacent investments, understanding that war policy is partly driven by career incentive structures -- not just national security -- is essential context.
