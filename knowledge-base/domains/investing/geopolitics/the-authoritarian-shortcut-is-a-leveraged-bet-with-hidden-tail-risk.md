Singapore's success is essentially a leveraged bet that paid off. The absence of democratic accountability acted as leverage — amplifying the PAP's competent governance into world-class infrastructure, education, and healthcare delivered at unprecedented speed. But leverage works both ways. The same structural absence of checks that enabled rapid positive change also means a malevolent or incompetent successor could inflict catastrophic, irreversible damage.

This maps precisely to financial leverage and startup governance. A founder with unchecked authority can move fast and build something extraordinary — but the same concentration of power means a single bad decision can destroy the entire enterprise. The video argues Singapore 'took a massive gamble that happened to pay off' — the key phrase being 'happened to.' On a long enough timeline, any system without error-correction mechanisms will encounter an error it cannot survive. This is why investors and boards insist on governance structures even when the founder is brilliant: the expected value of unchecked power is negative over time.
