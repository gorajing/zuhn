Marmont surrendered his corps to the Allies in 1814, opening the road to Paris. His name became the French verb 'raguser' (to betray). But the reality was that Napoleon's cause was lost regardless — other marshals had already refused to fight, the army was exhausted, and the Coalition had overwhelming force. Marmont just happened to be the visible trigger for the final collapse. He received 100% of the blame for what was a collective failure.

In business, the person who triggers a visible failure — the executive who announces the layoffs, the board member who forces the CEO out, the analyst who downgrades the stock — absorbs reputational damage that should be distributed across many. This creates a perverse incentive: being the last to acknowledge a failing situation rather than the first. The messenger is always punished, which delays necessary action in deteriorating situations.
