When Takaichi declared Japan would defend Taiwan, China responded with war threats, tourism curbs, and a diplomatic isolation campaign accusing Japan of militarism. The result was the opposite of China's intent: Takaichi's approval soared to 60-77% overall and above 92% among young voters, while South Korea — previously antagonistic toward Japan over colonial history — rapidly moved to strengthen ties, producing the iconic joint photo-op of the two leaders.

This illustrates a recurring geopolitical pattern: coercive pressure campaigns against democracies tend to generate a rally-around-the-flag effect that strengthens the target. Authoritarian regimes consistently overestimate the fracturing power of threats because their own societies respond to coercion with compliance. In democracies, external threats create internal cohesion. The investment implication is that China's aggressive posture is systematically accelerating the formation of an anti-China coalition in Asia, making the regional security architecture more robust than conventional analysis suggests.
