Napoleon was actually losing at Marengo when Desaix arrived with fresh troops on his own initiative and launched a counterattack that reversed the battle. Napoleon then rewrote the official account to make it appear he had planned the whole thing. This self-serving attribution reinforced his belief in his own infallibility — a belief that would contribute to disastrous decisions in Spain and Russia.

In business, leaders who attribute lucky outcomes to their own skill build fragile confidence: they take larger risks because they believe they have a 'magic touch' that is actually survivorship bias. The CEO whose first startup succeeded due to market timing rather than product excellence, the investor whose returns were driven by a bull market rather than stock picking — both become overconfident in their abilities and take risks that probability doesn't support.
