Paine identifies a recurring American mistake: assuming warring parties can be brought to cooperate through coalition structures. Marshall spent 1946 pressuring Chiang Kai-shek to halt his advance and negotiate with the Communists — a delay that some historians argue cost the Nationalists the civil war by giving Mao breathing room. The same pattern repeated with Pakistan-India, with Iraqi factions post-invasion, and with every attempt to build power-sharing democracies among groups that want each other dead.

The diagnostic: parties are 'primary adversaries' when their political programs include the elimination (physical, ideological, or structural) of the other. Under that condition, proportional representation, coalition cabinets, and ceasefires are not paths to reconciliation — they are temporary pauses during which both sides rearm. Western diplomatic instincts formed in contexts of legitimate pluralism misfire badly when transplanted to zero-sum contexts.

The actionable lesson for any negotiator, investor, or founder facing factional conflict: first test whether the parties are primary adversaries or merely competitors with overlapping interests. If primary, don't waste effort on compromise architecture — either pick a side or stay out. Compromise is only available when both parties can live with the other's continued existence.
