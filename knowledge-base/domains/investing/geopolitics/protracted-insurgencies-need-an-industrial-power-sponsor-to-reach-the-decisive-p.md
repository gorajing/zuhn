Paine highlights Mao's explicit admission that defeating a strong enemy requires three conditions: progress by China, difficulties for the enemy, and international support. The third is non-negotiable: phase three — the conventional, war-winning phase — consumes industrial quantities of shells, trucks, and artillery that an agrarian base area cannot manufacture. Mao's decision to set his terminal point of retreat at Yan'an, on the Soviet border, was strategic: he was literally positioning himself to plug into a supply line from his 'big friend'.

This is the hidden dependency of every Maoist-style insurgency from Vietnam to North Korea: the phase transition from harassment to regime change requires an external industrial patron. Without one, insurgents are trapped oscillating between phases one and two indefinitely. Conversely, the counterinsurgent's highest-leverage move is not killing guerrillas but isolating the movement from its external sponsor — which is why interdicting the Ho Chi Minh trail, or cutting Soviet aid to China, was always the real strategic question.

The corollary for great-power competition: a proxy war is won or lost at the level of industrial supply, not at the level of battlefield tactics. Whoever can keep their proxy armed longer wins, regardless of local popularity.
