The strongest objection to wealth-taxing illiquid fortunes is that founders must sell super-voting shares to pay, losing corporate control and potentially triggering cascading market effects. California's proposal addresses this with a special provision allowing startup founders to defer payment until the company goes public. For post-IPO billionaires, a 5% one-time tax on a portfolio growing 7% per year is a 'rounding error' — the wealth grew back within the four months it took to produce the documentary. This matters for the general wealth-tax literature: the 'liquidity constraint' argument relies on a straw-man design where taxes are due immediately in cash on illiquid holdings. Real-world wealth-tax designs can include installment schedules, in-kind payment (stock transfer to the state), and deferral until liquidity events, all of which sever the link between the tax and forced asset sales. When opponents rely on liquidity-crisis framing, it is evidence they have no substantive objection to the non-liquidity-crisis version of the policy.
