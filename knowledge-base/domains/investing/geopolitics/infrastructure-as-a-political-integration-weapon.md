The $20B Lanzhou-Urumqi high-speed line can never break even—ticket revenues don't even cover electricity costs. China built it anyway, openly stating the goal was 'ethnic unity' in the Uyghur-majority Xinjiang province. The same logic drove the world's highest-elevation rail to Tibet and the politically contentious Hong Kong connection.

This reveals a framework for analyzing infrastructure investment: when a project's economics don't add up, look for the political calculus. Governments routinely deploy infrastructure as soft power—binding peripheral regions to the center, projecting sovereignty, and reshaping demographics. Investors and analysts who evaluate mega-projects purely on ROI miss the actual decision function driving them.
