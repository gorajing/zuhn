Both Nazi Germany and Imperial Japan faced genuine food production challenges — Germany had inefficiently organized small farms, Japan couldn't keep agricultural output ahead of population growth. Both chose territorial expansion (Germany's Hunger Plan targeting Poland/Ukraine/Russia, Japan resettling farmers in Korea) rather than deeper trade integration as Britain had done.

This pattern — choosing self-sufficiency through force over interdependence through trade — is a recurring strategic error. The British Empire, despite its many moral failures, survived precisely because it built trade networks rather than attempting total autarky. The post-war order (Bretton Woods, GATT, EU) was explicitly designed to make trade preferable to conquest. This insight applies beyond geopolitics: organizations that try to own every capability internally rather than building partnerships often overextend and collapse.
