Paine argues that the Chinese Communist Party historically rested on three pillars of legitimacy: ethical rule, economic prosperity, and territorial integrity. With ethical rule gone and economic growth faltering, Xi is left with the territorial card — hence the incursions into India, South China Sea island-building, and Taiwan pressure. Putin is playing the same hand with Ukraine after communism and Russian Orthodoxy failed as marketable ideologies. The Chinese Communist Party deliberately pivoted to jingoistic nationalism after Tiananmen in 1989, rewriting textbooks to focus on 'evil Japanese, evil Americans.'

The problem, Paine notes, is that nationalism is 'a very heady drink': it clouds leadership judgment (because criticism becomes unpatriotic), it repels the minority populations inside the empire who aren't part of the dominant ethnic identity, it frightens neighbors into coordinating counter-coalitions, and it makes de-escalation nearly impossible because backing down reads as national humiliation.

For analysts predicting regime behavior: when you see a regime losing its economic story, watch for a shift to territorial and nationalist narratives within 2–5 years. This is a high-confidence indicator of elevated geopolitical risk. The same framework explains why previously-stable countries suddenly become aggressive when their growth model breaks — the legitimacy math forces the move.
