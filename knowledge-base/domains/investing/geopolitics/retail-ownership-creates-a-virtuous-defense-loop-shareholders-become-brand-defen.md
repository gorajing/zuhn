Robinhood's IPO access product evolved from a favor-asking exercise (begging companies to allocate 1-2% to retail) to a sought-after feature (companies like Bullish allocating 20% to retail). The reason: stocks with retail followings were rewarded with higher multiples. Palantir and Tesla demonstrate the virtuous cycle — many Tesla stock bulls also own the car, creating organic brand advocacy.

Vlad argues this is especially critical for AI companies, which have the fastest product adoption in history but are simultaneously the most hated category (worse perception than social media) because people fear job loss. The solution: distribute ownership widely so that the public becomes stakeholders rather than opponents. 'The best way to not fight against something — if you're an owner of it, you'll defend it.' This mirrors the broader post-1971 insight: since decoupling from the gold standard, asset prices have inflated while wages stagnated, meaning the divide between owners and non-owners is the fundamental economic fault line.
