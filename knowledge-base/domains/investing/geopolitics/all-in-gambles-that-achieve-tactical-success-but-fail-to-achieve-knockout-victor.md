Germany's Spring Offensive of 1918 was a calculated gamble: use every available man to break the Allied line before American forces arrived in strength. It achieved stunning initial success — the biggest advances since 1914. But each offensive consumed irreplaceable reserves without achieving the decisive breakthrough. When the Allies counterattacked with fresh American divisions in July, Germany had no reserves to respond. Within four months, Germany was requesting an armistice.

The business parallel is the 'bet the company' move: committing all resources to a single product launch, market push, or competitive response. If it achieves total market dominance, it works. If it falls short of total victory — even achieving impressive results — the company has no reserves for the next competitive cycle. The window between 'great success' and 'total victory' is where most all-in bets die.
