Trump's immigration crackdown assumed kicking out immigrants would free up jobs for natives — a textbook lump-of-labor fallacy. The data showed the opposite: native-born unemployment rose while immigrant employment increased, because immigrants also create demand and produce goods. Similarly, tariffs meant to protect manufacturing ended up hurting it because manufacturing relies heavily on intermediate goods that tariffs make more expensive. Only 38.2% of manufacturing sub-sectors were still adding jobs, down from 47.2% a year prior.

The pattern repeats across the political spectrum. Progressive policies focused on redistributing 'resources' to politically connected nonprofits, or spending billions on projects like California High Speed Rail that create jobs but no actual infrastructure, suffer the same conceptual error. Countries don't get rich from resources — South Korea, Singapore, and Switzerland got rich without colonial empires, while Spain and Portugal with vast empires remained economic underperformers. Production and human ingenuity create wealth, not resource capture or redistribution.
