British naval doctrine required ships to maintain their position in the line of battle. Nelson, seeing the Spanish fleet was about to escape, broke formation and sailed directly into the enemy line without orders. His improvisation cut off the Spanish retreat and turned an indecisive engagement into a decisive victory. If he had followed procedure, the enemy would have escaped.

In business, organizations bound by rigid procedures in dynamic situations become predictable: their competitors can anticipate every move because it follows the playbook. Companies that empower frontline employees to improvise — deviating from process when the situation demands it — can exploit the gaps in their competitors' procedural behavior. The key is knowing when to follow the process and when to break it.
