China invested roughly $12 billion in loans and $3 billion in direct investment to Africa in 2015—modest sums for a global superpower—yet shifted the voting alignment of dozens of UN General Assembly members. The key insight is structural: the UN General Assembly operates on one-country-one-vote, meaning influence over many small nations outweighs influence over a few large ones. Countries recognizing Taiwan received 2.7 fewer infrastructure projects per year, while those voting with China received 1.8 more. This reveals a general principle: when institutions use equal-weight voting, concentrating resources on many small participants yields asymmetric returns compared to courting a few large ones. The same logic applies to standards bodies, trade organizations, and any governance structure where vote count matters more than vote weight.
