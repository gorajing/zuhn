The treaties imposed on China after the Opium Wars — extraterritoriality, forced port openings, territorial concessions — were experienced as national humiliation. This humiliation became central to Chinese identity and a driver of policy: the determination to 'never again' be vulnerable to foreign powers shapes Chinese military buildup, economic strategy, and diplomatic assertiveness 175 years later.

In business, deals struck when a company is desperate (unfavorable vendor contracts during cash crunches, partnership terms accepted during crises) create similar lasting grievances. The weaker party accepts the terms out of necessity but never considers them legitimate. When their position strengthens, renegotiation or termination is inevitable. Smart partners structure deals that both sides can accept as fair even in retrospect.
