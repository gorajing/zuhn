Historical pattern: oil shock → consumers flee to efficient vehicles → crisis ends → incumbents revert to high-margin gas guzzlers → next shock catches them unprepared. The 1970s oil crises enabled Japanese automakers to gain permanent US market share. Detroit recovered in the late 90s/2000s by pivoting to SUVs when oil was cheap.

Noah Smith argues this cycle breaks now because the alternative technology (EVs) has crossed a cost and performance threshold where it's simply better than ICE vehicles regardless of gas prices. Battery costs have fallen enough that the 'wait for cheap gas' strategy no longer works — EVs win on total cost of ownership even at low gas prices. Detroit's announced EV write-downs and scale-backs during a period of accelerating global EV adoption may represent the final chapter of this recurring strategic error.
