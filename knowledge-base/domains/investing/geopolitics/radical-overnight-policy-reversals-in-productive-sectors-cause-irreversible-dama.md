In April 2021, the Rajapaksa government banned all chemical fertilizer imports overnight, attempting to make Sri Lanka the world's first fully organic farming nation. The policy was reversed in November 2021 after mass protests, but by then rice production had collapsed, tea and rubber exports halted, and the country shifted from food exporter to food importer — draining the foreign reserves it desperately needed for debt service.

This illustrates a critical asymmetry in policy design: destruction of productive capacity is fast and the recovery is slow. Agricultural supply chains have biological cycle times (growing seasons) that cannot be accelerated by policy reversal. For investors watching emerging markets, overnight bans on critical inputs to established industries are a sovereign-level red flag, even if the government claims the policy is temporary or ideologically motivated. The damage compounds through secondary effects — food imports drain forex reserves, which weakens the currency, which makes all imports more expensive.
