Paine's diagnostic for alliance durability is surgical: track who is credibly threatening whom, not who shares ideology. The Sino-Soviet bromance — two great communist powers with a world-changing mission — shattered over some islands in the Amur River in 1969, to the point where Russia approached the Nixon administration asking whether it would be acceptable to nuke China. Mao's takeaway: 'The one that wants to nuke you is your primary adversary.' From that moment, the US could play the swing role and the entire Cold War balance reshuffled.

This explains why the supposedly unshakable Putin-Xi bromance today is actually fragile. The real question Paine poses is not whether it will end but when Xi decides he has enough leverage — China has nine times Russia's GNP, covets Siberian water and resources, and is already quietly peeling Central Asia away via Belt and Road. North Korea, not a coequal partner, is the template for Russia's likely future relationship with China.

The generalizable principle: when forecasting any adversarial realignment, weight existential threat dynamics far more heavily than ideological affinity. Allies with shared ideology and opposed interests drift apart; enemies with opposed ideology but converging threats (US-China vs USSR in the 1970s) can coordinate remarkably fast. Investors betting on geopolitical blocs should watch for threat-perception shifts, not ideology.
