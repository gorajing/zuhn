Samsung accounts for roughly 20% of South Korea's entire GDP — comparable to the government's own spending share of 20-25%. This concentration creates an asymmetric power dynamic: South Korea cannot survive without Samsung, but Samsung could theoretically restructure and relocate away from South Korea. The practical consequence is that the Lee family has been repeatedly convicted of serious crimes (bribery, corruption) and repeatedly pardoned — Lee Kun-hee in 2009 and Lee Jae-yong in 2022 — with justifications explicitly citing the company's economic importance. This pattern reveals that GDP concentration above a certain threshold effectively grants corporate leaders de facto legal immunity, not through formal exemption but through the pragmatic impossibility of enforcing consequences. For investors, this signals that in highly concentrated economies, the rule of law around corporate governance is structurally compromised regardless of the quality of the legal system on paper.
