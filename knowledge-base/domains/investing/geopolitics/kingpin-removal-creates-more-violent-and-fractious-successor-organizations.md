The Sinaloa cartel under El Chapo operated like a business: bribing local law enforcement, minimizing visible violence, and maintaining stable territory. When US law enforcement successfully dismantled its leadership, the organization fractured into approximately a dozen competing factions. This power vacuum was filled by the Jalisco New Generation Cartel (CJNG), whose operating model centers on extreme public violence as a control mechanism — shooting mayors, police chiefs, and civilians to establish dominance.

The pattern extends beyond drug cartels: removing a dominant stabilizing actor from any competitive ecosystem often produces worse outcomes than the original threat. The successor entities are typically more numerous, less predictable, and often more destructive because they lack the institutional knowledge and restraint that the original organization developed over time. This has implications for antitrust enforcement, geopolitical regime change, and competitive strategy — disrupting an incumbent can unleash chaos worse than the status quo.
