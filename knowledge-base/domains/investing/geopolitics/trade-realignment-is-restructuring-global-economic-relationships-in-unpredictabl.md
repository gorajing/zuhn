Dimon characterizes the direct impact of U.S. tariffs as 'only minor effects on inflation or growth' — just 'one straw on the camel's back.' But the real significance is the broader realignment: many nations are now analyzing how and with whom they should create trade arrangements, causing a fundamental reshuffling of economic relations globally. This is partly necessary for national security and resiliency, but the long-term effects are genuinely uncertain.

The context is a world where China's Belt & Road Initiative represents $1.2T in foreign direct investment, global foreign portfolio investment has grown from $8.4T (2010) to $25.5T into the U.S. alone, and foreigners hold $9.3T in U.S. Treasuries. Economic weakening of the world's democracies or fragmentation of their economic bonds 'could lead to truly adverse consequences.' Autocratic nations explicitly want allies less dependent on the United States.

The investment implication: the current trade disruption is not a one-off tariff adjustment but a structural shift in how global economic relationships are organized. Supply chain reshoring, 'friend-shoring,' and security-motivated economic policy will create winners and losers across sectors and geographies for years.
