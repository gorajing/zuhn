The Choi Soon-sil scandal reveals how informal power networks can completely subvert formal governance. Choi held no official position yet picked the President's outfits, edited speeches, made policy propositions, and — critically — influenced the National Pension Service's vote on the Samsung C&T/Cheil merger through her relationship with President Park. Samsung paid $36 million to Choi's front foundations (Mir and K-sports), and the state pension fund then voted in favor of a merger that objectively destroyed value for its beneficiaries — South Korean pensioners. This chain — corporation bribes shadow advisor, shadow advisor influences president, president directs state pension fund to vote against pensioners' interests — illustrates how concentrated economic power exploits informal governance channels. For investors evaluating political risk in any economy, the formal governance structure (democratic, authoritarian, etc.) matters less than the density and opacity of informal influence networks connecting economic and political elites.
