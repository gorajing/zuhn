The U.S. currently has compounding advantages in space: equator-adjacent launch sites, deep aerospace talent, mature regulatory infrastructure, active capital markets for space ventures, and a head start in reusable launch. But the historical pattern is unambiguous — every era's early geographic winner eventually faces challengers. China is investing heavily in reusable launch and orbital supercomputers. India's space program is growing rapidly.

The critical insight is the distinction between a head start and a moat. The U.S. must use its one-to-two-decade window to build durable institutional and infrastructure moats — not assume the lead is self-sustaining. History is littered with early transportation leaders (traditional dock cities that failed to modernize for containerization, for example) who mistook temporal advantage for structural advantage. The window for building moats is finite, and the cost of complacency is losing centrality in what may become the defining economic platform of the century.
