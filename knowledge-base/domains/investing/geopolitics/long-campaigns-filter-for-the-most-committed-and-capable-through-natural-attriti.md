The three-year march from Europe to Jerusalem acted as an extreme filter: disease, starvation, battle, and desertion reduced the crusading army by roughly 90%. The survivors were not a random sample — they were the most physically resilient, the most psychologically committed, and the most battle-hardened. This selection pressure created a force that was small but qualitatively extraordinary.

In business, this maps to the survivor effect in competitive markets: companies that emerge from brutal market conditions (recessions, regulatory crackdowns, technology transitions) are not random survivors — they are the most operationally efficient, the most adaptable, and the most strategically focused. The harshness of the filter improves the average quality of survivors. This is why post-crisis competitive landscapes are often dominated by exceptionally strong players.
