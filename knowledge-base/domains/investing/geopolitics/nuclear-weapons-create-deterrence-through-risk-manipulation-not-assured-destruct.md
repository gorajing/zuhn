Mearsheimer explains Thomas Schelling's manipulation of risk theory: in a MAD world, nuclear weapons aren't used for destruction but for signaling. NATO's Cold War doctrine was to use a handful of nuclear weapons — possibly in remote areas — not to destroy Soviet forces but to demonstrate resolve and put both sides on the 'slippery slope.' The message: 'The last clear chance to avoid Armageddon rests with you.'

This framework is critical for understanding modern nuclear risk. Russia's potential nuclear use in Ukraine would follow this same playbook — a few weapons in rural areas to 'spook everybody' through the threat of uncontrollable escalation. The terrifying reality is that humanity has zero empirical data on nuclear escalation dynamics, making every nuclear crisis a theoretical exercise with civilizational stakes. For risk assessment, this means nuclear conflicts are fundamentally different from conventional ones: outcomes are non-linear and unknowable.
