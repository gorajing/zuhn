Napoleon's Alpine crossing wasn't just about surprise — it was about strategic positioning. By arriving in the Austrian rear, he cut their supply lines and their route home. The Austrians were forced to turn around and attack Napoleon to survive, fighting on ground and at a time of his choosing. They couldn't ignore him or maneuver around him — they had to fight through him.

In competitive strategy, positioning yourself between a competitor and their critical resource (talent pool, key supplier, essential platform) creates the same forced engagement. They can't ignore you because you control their access to something essential. This is more powerful than direct competition because the competitor must respond on your terms rather than choosing when and how to engage.
