Paine frames Smoot-Hawley as 'half-court tennis' — the US raised tariff walls to protect domestic jobs during the Depression without modeling what the other side would do. Predictably, trading partners retaliated with their own walls. The result wasn't protected American jobs; it was a collapse in global trade that hurt everyone and pushed depressed economies toward radical politics.

Once fascists took power in Germany and Japan, the easy diplomatic solutions were gone. You cannot negotiate your way out of a situation where the counterparty is an ideologically committed military dictatorship. The error that matters isn't the war itself — it's the tariff policy years earlier that created the conditions.

This has direct application to contemporary trade policy: unilateral tariff moves without multilateral coordination invite retaliation, and the second-order political effects (radicalization of affected populations) may matter more than the first-order economic effects. The time to prevent a war is during the preceding depression's trade policy, not in the final negotiation.
