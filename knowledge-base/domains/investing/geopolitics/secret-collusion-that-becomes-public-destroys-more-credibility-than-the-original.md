Britain and France could have simply intervened in Suez openly, citing their canal interests. Instead, they secretly colluded with Israel: Israel would attack Egypt, then Britain and France would intervene as supposed 'peacekeepers.' This theatrical pretense was transparent to everyone, and its exposure added the charge of dishonesty to the charge of aggression. The cover-up was worse than the act.

In business, this is why manufactured justifications for competitive actions backfire: when a company creates a pretextual reason for a hostile action (pretending a price cut is about 'consumer benefit' when it's predatory, or claiming a partnership termination is about 'quality' when it's competitive), and the real motive becomes known, the reputational damage is double: the original act plus the deception. Transparent aggression is less damaging than discovered deception.
