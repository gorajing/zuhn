Smith's fourth factor — that Trump's interventions against Venezuela and Iran reveal potential Chinese military weakness — relies on an indirect but historically meaningful signal: if China were genuinely capable of defending its client states from U.S. coercive pressure, those states would behave differently under threat. When they don't, or when they capitulate, it reveals a gap between China's projected power and its actual capacity to project force at distance.

This logic has historical precedent: Soviet credibility in the Cold War was partly assessed by how proxy states and non-aligned nations hedged. When Soviet clients started making separate accommodations with the West, it was a leading indicator of systemic decline in Moscow's capacity to backstop its security guarantees.

For geopolitical risk analysis, this means that observing third-party behavior — how do Iran, Venezuela, and other Chinese partners actually act when facing U.S. pressure? — provides information about Chinese military reach that official statements and hardware counts do not. It's an indirect but real test of whether China's security umbrella is functional or aspirational.
