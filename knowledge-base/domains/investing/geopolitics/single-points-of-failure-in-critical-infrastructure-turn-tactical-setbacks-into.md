Napoleon's army was winning at Aspern-Essling until the Danube bridges were destroyed by Austrian fire-ships and floating debris. Suddenly, reinforcements, ammunition, and retreat routes were all cut off. The army that crossed was stranded — too small to win, unable to retreat, and running out of ammunition. The entire plan depended on one set of bridges, and when they failed, everything failed.

This is the infrastructure version of concentration risk: any system with a single point of failure will eventually experience that failure. In business, this means single-supplier dependencies, single-platform distribution (apps depending entirely on one app store), single-customer revenue concentration, or single-cloud-provider architecture. The lesson: always build redundancy into critical infrastructure, even if it seems expensive or unnecessary when things are working.
