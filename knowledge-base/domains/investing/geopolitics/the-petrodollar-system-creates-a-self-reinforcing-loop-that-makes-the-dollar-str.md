After the 1971 Nixon Shock ended gold-dollar convertibility, the U.S. needed a new mechanism to maintain dollar dominance. The 1973 agreement with Saudi Arabia (oil sold only in USD in exchange for military protection) created that mechanism. Since every country needs oil, every country needs dollars, creating constant structural demand.

The recycling loop is key: oil-producing countries earn dollars, then invest them back into U.S. government bonds, American companies, and real estate. This means the dollars flow back into the U.S. financial system, keeping interest rates low and enabling the U.S. to borrow cheaply. The system also gives the U.S. enormous coercive power through sanctions, since most international transactions flow through U.S. banks. Even hostile nations like Russia still use dollars because the switching costs are enormous -- disrupting contracts, hedging systems, insurance, and pricing models.
