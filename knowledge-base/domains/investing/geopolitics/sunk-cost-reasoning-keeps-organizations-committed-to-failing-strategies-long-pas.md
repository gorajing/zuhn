After the first day's catastrophe on the Somme (57,000 British casualties, negligible gains), rational analysis said: stop. But the battle continued for four more months. The reasoning was always the same: 'We can't stop now or the previous losses will have been for nothing.' Each day's casualties became the justification for tomorrow's attack. The sunk cost of 400,000+ casualties made withdrawal politically impossible.

This is escalation of commitment in its purest form, and it appears constantly in business: VCs doubling down on failing portfolio companies because they've already invested too much to walk away, companies continuing product lines that are clearly failing because of the development costs already sunk, executives maintaining strategies that aren't working because changing course would be an admission of error. The pattern is always: past investment creates political pressure to continue, which creates more losses, which creates more pressure to continue.
