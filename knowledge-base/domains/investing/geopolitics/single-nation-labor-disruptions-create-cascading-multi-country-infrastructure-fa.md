When French controllers struck on May 9, 2019, it didn't just affect French flights — it created a continent-wide crisis requiring Eurocontrol to coordinate with Spain, North Africa, Switzerland, Italy, Germany, and the UK days in advance. Airlines chose between 45+ minute delays or flying the 'Tango route' far west into oceanic airspace, turning 2-hour flights into 3+ hour journeys.

This illustrates a structural vulnerability in any tightly integrated multi-jurisdiction network: the most connected node can impose costs on the entire system through unilateral action. France's central geographic position in European airspace gives French ATC workers outsized leverage — their strike doesn't just ground French flights, it degrades the entire European network. The same dynamic appears in transnational supply chains, internet backbone routing, and financial clearing networks. For investors, this means labor risk in geographically or topologically central nodes deserves higher weighting than the node's proportional contribution would suggest.
