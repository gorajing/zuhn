Paine pushes back hard on the counterfactual that a larger Marshall Plan could have saved Nationalist China. Her distinction is between nation-rebuilding and nation-building: Japan and Germany had competent bureaucracies, functioning courts, literate populations, operational police forces, and modern economies before the war — aid dollars let them rapidly reconstitute machinery that already existed in institutional memory. China had none of that. High illiteracy, no indigenous administrative capacity, a century of warlord rule — cash could not conjure institutions where the human capital and procedural knowledge simply were not present.

This is why de-Ba'athification in Iraq and state-building in Afghanistan failed for structurally identical reasons: removing the existing regime and expecting the police to keep showing up assumed institutional depth that wasn't there. Institutions are slow-compounding social technology; they cannot be airdropped.

The investing and strategic implication: when evaluating any emerging-market transformation, foreign-aid bet, or regime-change hypothetical, distinguish sharply between 'rebuild' scenarios (where institutional memory exists and recovery is fast) and 'build' scenarios (where it doesn't and timelines stretch to generations). The two look similar in a strategy memo and have completely different expected values.
