The Tasmanian tiger was Tasmania's only apex predator — a keystone species with outsized ecosystem impact. After humans drove it extinct, the consequences cascaded: wallabees and kangaroos exploded in population, overgrazing shrubs and bushes, which impacted bird populations and changed the landscape. Without an apex predator to cull sick animals, the Tasmanian devil developed a transmissible facial cancer that now threatens its own survival.

This is a powerful model for understanding systemic fragility in any complex system — financial markets, supply chains, organizations. Removing a 'keystone' element (a market maker, a critical supplier, a key leader) can trigger non-obvious cascading failures that compound over time. The Tasmanian case is especially instructive because the damage was invisible for decades and is now potentially irreversible without radical intervention (reintroducing the predator). Many scientists believe no other living species can fill the thylacine's ecological role — some system components are truly irreplaceable.
