From 1900 to 2000, US grid power capacity grew 10x every 30 years. Since 2000, it has grown only 1.3x, flatlined at approximately 4,000-4,200 TWh. This dramatic deceleration coincides with surging demand from AI data centers, EV charging, and re-industrialization.

Chamath frames this as the fundamental constraint on civilization — energy powers everything from shelter to emergency services to technological progress. While media attention focuses on AI model weights and GPU availability, the physical infrastructure that makes computation possible is the actual bottleneck. This creates an asymmetric investment thesis: grid modernization, transmission buildout, and energy storage are likely to be more supply-constrained and therefore more valuable than the AI compute layer they enable.
