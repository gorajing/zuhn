Alexander didn't command from the rear — he led his Companion cavalry in person at every major battle, placing himself at the most dangerous point. He was wounded at least seven times. This wasn't recklessness; it was leadership through shared risk. His soldiers endured extraordinary hardships because they saw their king enduring the same conditions and dangers. The loyalty this created held the army together across deserts, mountains, and a decade of continuous war.

In business, leaders who visibly share organizational hardships (taking pay cuts during downturns, working alongside teams during crises, being present during difficult customer interactions) create the same loyalty multiplier. The asymmetry of modern CEO compensation — where leaders are insulated from the consequences their decisions impose on employees — is the opposite approach, and produces correspondingly weaker organizational commitment.
