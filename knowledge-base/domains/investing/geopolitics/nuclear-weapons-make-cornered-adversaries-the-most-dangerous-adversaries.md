Paine raises the 'cornered animal' dynamic specifically in the context of nuclear weapons: once a regime concludes it is doomed, the distinction between losing with grace and losing with maximum damage collapses. Whatever remaining destructive capacity they hold becomes rational to deploy, because there's no downstream cost to the actor making the decision.

This is why the entire logic of 'decisive victory' — push the enemy to unconditional surrender — interacts badly with nuclear arsenals. In WWII, demanding unconditional surrender from Japan was defensible because the marginal cost of additional war was bounded by conventional weapons. In a nuclear context, the same demand structure can produce infinite downside if the losing regime retains launch capability.

The investable takeaway: in any domain where the opponent holds asymmetric destructive capacity (not just nukes — think activist shareholders, whistleblowers, regulators, terminated employees with data access), leaving them a face-saving exit is not weakness. It's the dominant strategy when cornering them costs more than the concession.
