As high-quality language models proliferated and commoditized in 2024, the focus of AI competition shifted from the model layer to infrastructure. The U.S. leads in advanced chips and mass deployment (exemplified by xAI's 100,000 liquid-cooled H-100 cluster), while China leads in energy production with 9,000+ TWh generation, the world's largest coal fleet, dominant solar capacity, and extensive ultra-high-voltage transmission infrastructure.

This reframes the AI competition as fundamentally an industrial integration challenge rather than a software or research problem. The strategic implication is that AI primacy — which underpins both economic competitiveness and military strength — depends on solving energy production and distribution at national scale. For investors and policymakers, this means the most important AI investments may not be in model companies or chip designers, but in the energy and infrastructure companies that will power the compute layer.
