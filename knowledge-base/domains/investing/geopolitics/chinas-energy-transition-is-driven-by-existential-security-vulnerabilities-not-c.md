Most Western analysis frames China's clean energy push as climate policy or industrial strategy. The deeper driver is existential: coal (China's primary energy source) requires massive water, and China faces severe water scarcity — Beijing is among the most water-stressed major cities globally. Meanwhile, 80% of China's oil imports transit the Malacca Strait, a two-mile-wide chokepoint that any naval adversary could blockade.

This reframing matters for investors because it means China's renewable deployment is not vulnerable to the political reversals that plague Western clean energy policy. When energy transition is a matter of national survival rather than political preference, the commitment is structurally more durable. This makes Chinese renewable and battery companies more predictable long-term bets than their policy-dependent Western counterparts, despite regulatory risks.
