Britain's industrial capitalism was partly enabled by the 17th-century English Civil War, during which the crown could not enforce its complex licensing requirements and royal monopolies. This accidental deregulation persisted after the conflict, creating space for market experimentation that would not have occurred under stable authoritarian rule.

This pattern recurs throughout economic history: political upheaval disrupts incumbent regulatory capture, creating windows for new economic models. Modern parallels include how regulatory uncertainty in crypto emerged from governments being slow to classify digital assets, and how the post-2008 financial crisis regulatory vacuum enabled fintech innovation. For investors, the contrarian signal is that political instability in a region may indicate emerging market opportunities rather than just risk — the key is distinguishing between instability that destroys institutions versus instability that merely relaxes incumbent protections.
