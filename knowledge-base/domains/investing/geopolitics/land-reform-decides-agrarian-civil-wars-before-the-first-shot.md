Paine's comparative point is sharp: Mao's detailed peasant surveys led him to discover that 6% of the rural population owned 80% of the land, and that distributing that land was the single most powerful allegiance-generating lever available. The Nationalists couldn't match him because their own officer corps were the landlords — structural capture prevented the only reform that could have saved them. In Korea, Syngman Rhee did land reform immediately and North Korean southern insurgency collapsed; in Vietnam, the South never did and lost.

The insight generalizes beyond agrarian warfare: in any legitimacy contest, the incumbent who cannot distribute the one thing the population most wants — because its own coalition owns that thing — is already losing, even if the military balance looks favorable. Structural capture of the regime's base by the very asset class the population wants redistributed is a silent death sentence.

For investors and strategists reading a country risk situation, the diagnostic question isn't 'who has more troops?' but 'whose coalition blocks the one reform that would buy mass loyalty?' That's where the regime's expiration date is written.
