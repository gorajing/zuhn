Napoleon's genius at Friedland was not just tactical but positional: he attacked when the Russian army had crossed to the west bank of the Alle River with limited bridges for retreat. When the French assault broke the Russian lines, there was nowhere to go. Thousands drowned attempting to cross the river. The result was not just a victory but the complete collapse of Russian willingness to fight.

In competitive strategy, the equivalent is engineering situations where the competitor cannot retreat gracefully: forcing a bet they can't walk away from, creating switching costs that lock in their customers, or establishing a regulatory position that eliminates their fallback option. The most decisive competitive outcomes come not from fighting harder but from eliminating the opponent's ability to disengage and regroup.
