Western militaries use pull-based logistics where fighting forces request resupplies based on real-time conditions. Russia uses push-based logistics where resupply is centrally determined by leadership on predictable schedules. The push model works acceptably under resource constraints when conflicts follow predictions—leadership can strategically prioritize ammunition over fuel on less important fronts. But when Russia's assumption of a 3-5 day capitulation failed, the entire system broke down: tanks ran out of fuel, troops looted stores for food, and soldiers asked Ukrainian civilians for directions. The core lesson extends beyond military contexts: any system where resource allocation is pre-planned by central authority rather than responsive to ground-level demand will fail catastrophically when initial assumptions prove wrong.
