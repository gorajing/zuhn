Paine's framing is that Pearl Harbor succeeded at every measurable tactical criterion — surprise, execution, damage inflicted, Japanese losses — yet it was a strategic disaster because it instantly unified an isolationist America behind total war. The Japanese saw America's refusal to help Britain against the Nazis and inferred weakness, missing that isolationism is conditional, not constitutive. Once attacked, the same population flips to 'hell to pay' mode.

The generalizable lesson applies well beyond war: any aggressive action against a larger, quiescent adversary must be evaluated by whether it wakes them up, not by whether it succeeds on contact. In markets, competitive strategy, and geopolitics, the dangerous move is the one that looks like a clean win but changes the other side's utility function from 'tolerate' to 'destroy.'

The corollary: most strategic actors systematically over-weight first-order effects (what the strike does) and under-weight second-order effects (how the target's intentions change), because the second is harder to model and easier to rationalize away.
