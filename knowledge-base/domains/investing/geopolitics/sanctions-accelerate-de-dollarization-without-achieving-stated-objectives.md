Carlson observed Moscow two years post-sanctions: supply chains functioning, grocery stores well-stocked, infrastructure maintained, no visible economic collapse. Russia's wartime artillery production exceeds all NATO countries combined at a 7:1 ratio, indicating deep industrial capacity unaffected by financial isolation. Meanwhile, the sanctions forced nations into the BRICS economic bloc, accelerating de-dollarization.

The investment implication is asymmetric: the US weaponized the dollar's reserve currency status, which worked as a short-term pressure tool but sent a signal to every non-aligned nation that dollar-denominated assets can be seized. This creates structural incentive for diversification away from dollar reserves. The process of de-dollarization, which Carlson notes was 'inevitable ultimately because everything dies, including currencies,' has been 'accelerated exponentially.' For portfolio positioning, this suggests increasing allocation to hard assets and non-dollar-denominated investments as a hedge against gradual dollar erosion.
