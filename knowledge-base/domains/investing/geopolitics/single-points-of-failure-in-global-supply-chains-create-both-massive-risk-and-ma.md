There is exactly one company in the world that can make EUV lithography machines: ASML, located in Veldhoven, Netherlands. Every advanced chip — from AI accelerators to smartphone processors — requires these machines. The mirrors come from one supplier (Zeiss), and each mirror system takes months to produce. This means the entire global technology supply chain funnels through a single geographic and corporate chokepoint.

This concentration happened naturally through the economics of compound impossibility — the R&D costs were so enormous (billions over decades) that the market could only sustain one player. But it creates extraordinary geopolitical leverage. Export controls on ASML machines effectively control which nations can manufacture advanced chips. For investors, this pattern of natural monopoly in deep-tech supply chains represents both the highest-conviction investment thesis (the chokepoint holder captures enormous value) and the greatest systemic risk (any disruption cascades globally).
