After winning at Bassano, Napoleon didn't pause to consolidate. He immediately pursued the retreating Austrians, keeping constant pressure that prevented them from reforming. Each day of pursuit further disorganized the Austrian force until it effectively ceased to exist as a fighting unit. The pursuit created more strategic value than the battle itself.

In business, the equivalent is aggressively scaling after product-market fit, pressing a competitive advantage before competitors can regroup, or rapidly expanding after a successful launch. Companies that pause to 'consolidate' after winning an initial competitive battle often find that their opponent has used the breathing space to regroup, raise capital, and mount a stronger counter-offensive.
