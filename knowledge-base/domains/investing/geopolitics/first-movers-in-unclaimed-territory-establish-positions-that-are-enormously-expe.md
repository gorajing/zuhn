The Crusader States were remarkably small — thin coastal strips surrounded by hostile territory. Yet they survived from 1099 to 1291 (nearly 200 years) because the first movers built formidable castles at every strategic point, established alliances with local factions, and created institutional legitimacy through the Church. Displacing them required generations of Muslim unification and enormously costly siege campaigns.

In business, first movers in new markets or categories enjoy the same entrenchment advantage: they build brand recognition, lock in key partnerships, establish regulatory relationships, and create switching costs before competitors arrive. Displacing an entrenched first mover is dramatically more expensive than being the first mover. This is why timing of market entry matters more than being the best product — the cost of conquest versus the cost of establishment can differ by 10x.
