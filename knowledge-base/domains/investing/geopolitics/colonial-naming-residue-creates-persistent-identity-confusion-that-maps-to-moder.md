When West African nations gained independence, multiple chose to include 'Guinea' — the colonial name for the broader region — in their national names, creating Guinea, Guinea-Bissau, and Equatorial Guinea. This naming collision is not cosmetic; it creates real friction in trade recognition, investor pattern-matching, and diplomatic identity.

The pattern generalizes: colonial administrative boundaries and naming conventions persist as structural friction long after independence. Congo/DRC, Korea North/South, and the China/Taiwan naming dispute all show how inherited nomenclature becomes a vector for ongoing political contestation. For anyone analyzing emerging markets, recognizing which modern frictions trace to colonial administrative artifacts versus organic conflicts changes the assessment of resolution probability.
