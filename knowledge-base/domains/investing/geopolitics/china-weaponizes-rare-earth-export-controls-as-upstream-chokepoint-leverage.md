China added five heavy rare earths to its export-control list effective December 2025, extending April restrictions imposed in response to Trump's tariffs. This targets inputs upstream of semiconductor fabs, AI data centers, robotics companies, defense contractors, and EV manufacturers globally. The controls raise frictional costs (permits, delays, compliance risk) that cascade into capex timelines across industries.

This is qualitatively different from tariff warfare because rare earths have no quick substitutes — mining and refining take years to stand up domestically. The U.S. responded with executive orders to increase mineral production and legislation requiring Nvidia/AMD to prioritize domestic customers, but these are medium-term responses to an immediate chokepoint. The strategic lesson: whoever controls irreplaceable upstream inputs holds leverage disproportionate to the dollar value of those inputs.
