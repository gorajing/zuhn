By early 1807, Napoleon's army was exhausted from months of campaigning in Poland's freezing mud. A strategic withdrawal to winter quarters would have been the rational choice. But Napoleon couldn't afford to be seen retreating — his political power rested partly on the perception of constant forward momentum and invincibility. So he fought at Eylau under terrible conditions, nearly losing, and gaining nothing strategically.

This maps to business leaders who make decisions to protect their image rather than their company: continuing to pour money into failing products because admitting failure would damage their reputation, maintaining market presence in unprofitable regions because withdrawal would signal weakness, or refusing to pivot because it would mean admitting the original strategy was wrong. The cost of maintaining the illusion often exceeds the cost of the retreat it was meant to avoid.
