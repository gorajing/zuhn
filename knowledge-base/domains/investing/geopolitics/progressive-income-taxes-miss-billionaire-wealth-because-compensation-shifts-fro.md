California's billionaires hold about $2 trillion in wealth but contribute only 2.5% of the state's income tax base, because founder compensation is structured around unsold equity rather than salary. Mark Zuckerberg takes $1/year from Facebook; the rest of his compensation is stock he never sells. Raising the top marginal income tax rate to 95% would not touch that wealth. This creates a base-mismatch problem: the tax instrument (income) and the tax target (stock of accumulated wealth) are different variables. Any jurisdiction trying to tax the ultra-wealthy through the income code is solving the wrong equation. The only instruments that reach the stock of billionaire wealth are (1) a direct wealth tax on net worth, (2) a mark-to-market unrealized-gains tax, or (3) a consumption proxy like a luxury tax. This also explains why 'the rich already pay their fair share' claims are consistent with 'the rich are barely taxed' claims — both are true depending on whether you measure income or wealth.
