Rather than picking a single winner and funding it, China designates an industry a 'national priority' and instructs 31 provincial governments to nurture it. Those provinces in turn activate 300+ prefecture-level governments, each backing local firms hoping to produce the national champion. This tournament structure preserves competitive pressure while still concentrating national resources on strategic sectors.

The model avoids the classic central-planning failure of misallocating resources to the wrong horse — local officials have stronger information about regional firms. But it introduces a structural side effect: because many local governments simultaneously subsidize competing firms, the industry tends toward overcapacity once a winner emerges.

This is directly applicable to how platform ecosystems and accelerator programs work: structured competition among many entrants with prize concentration at the top outperforms selecting champions in advance.
