When Napoleon was exiled in 1814, his marshals revealed where their true loyalties lay: most immediately transferred allegiance to the restored Bourbons or simply retired. When Napoleon returned for the Hundred Days, some rejoined him and others stayed loyal to the new regime. The army's cohesion was entirely dependent on Napoleon's personal charisma and patronage — there was no institutional loyalty to sustain it in his absence.

In business, founder-dependent companies face identical risk: when the founder leaves, key executives, investors, and partners reassess their commitment. If their loyalty was to the founder personally rather than to the company's mission, governance, or equity structure, they defect to better opportunities. This is why institutional investors prefer companies with strong institutional governance over founder-personality-driven ones — personality loyalty doesn't survive founder departure.
