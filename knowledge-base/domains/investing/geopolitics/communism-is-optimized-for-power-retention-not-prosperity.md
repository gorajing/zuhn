Paine's core thesis is that the commissar system and party apparatus communists built are unmatched at the specific task of seizing power during warfare and maintaining it afterward. Mao killed tens of millions yet faced no successful coup or insurgency; North Korea remains a dynastic mess yet the Kim family stays in power; North Vietnam seized a three-harvest-per-year country and promptly engineered famine.

The same centralization, surveillance, and ideological control that prevent counter-revolution also destroy the price signals, property rights, and decentralized decision-making required for prosperity. This isn't a bug communists haven't fixed — it's the load-bearing feature. You cannot have both because the mechanisms trade off directly.

For investors and geopolitical analysts, this reframes China risk: don't expect the CCP to liberalize for prosperity's sake, because the party optimizes for its own survival first. Xi's recentralization is not a mistake or a phase — it's the system returning to its attractor state after a Deng-era deviation.
