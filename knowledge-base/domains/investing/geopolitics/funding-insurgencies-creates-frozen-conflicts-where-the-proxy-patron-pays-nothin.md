Paine walks through the insurgency-funding playbook as practiced by the US, USSR, China, India, and Pakistan — all of whom have done it. The logic is simple and amoral: if a rival has a restive minority, fund the insurgency. The rival now has to spend military and political attention there, which means they can't do things elsewhere that might matter to you. China funded the Naxalites, Mizo, Manipuri, and Naga insurgencies in India from 1962 to 1979. Pakistan funded Kashmiri insurgents. India allegedly funded Baloch and Pashtun insurgencies. The US funded Tibetans, Afghan mujahideen, and more.

The asymmetry is the key feature: the outside patron, 'if they're playing their amoral cards right, bears none of the costs.' All the costs accrue to the local population: horrendous deaths, no economic growth, warfare where you live, and hatreds that spike to the point where peace becomes impossible. These frozen conflicts develop 'veto players' who can derail any peace process with a single bombing — so 'all you have to do is send a certain number of package bombs and peace is not going to happen.'

The strategic lesson runs both directions. If you're tempted to fund an insurgency: the short-term pin is real, but you're creating something that will outlive your interest in it and will eventually blow back (ISI-diverted US money to Kashmir and eventually to Osama). If you're on the receiving end: recognize that frozen conflicts are engineered, not accidental, and that the veto players are the mechanism.
