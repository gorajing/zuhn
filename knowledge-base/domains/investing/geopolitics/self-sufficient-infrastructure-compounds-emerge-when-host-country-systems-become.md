The US embassy in Baghdad, physically as large as Vatican City with 16,000 peak staff, built entirely parallel infrastructure: generators, wells, water filtration, sewage treatment, fire station, independent internet uplink, and a phone network using New York and Virginia area codes. It even had its own apartments, swimming pool, tennis courts, and department store.

This pattern appears wherever organizations operate in environments where host infrastructure is unreliable or compromised. The key insight is that self-sufficiency isn't a linear cost increase — it's a step function. Once you cross the threshold where you can't trust local power, water, or communications, you must build everything redundantly, and the cost of operations permanently jumps by an order of magnitude. This has implications for evaluating companies expanding into unstable markets: the visible costs of market entry dramatically understate the true operational burden once the self-sufficiency threshold is crossed.
