Ezra Vogel's 'Japan As Number One' (1985) and Bill Emmott's skeptical rebuttal 'The Sun Also Sets' (1989) illustrate a recurring pattern: the strongest bullish consensus on a rising power tends to form at exactly the wrong time. Japan was at its financial and industrial zenith when Western observers were extrapolating permanent dominance; Emmott's contrarian warnings about financial fragility, demographics, and service-sector productivity were ignored until they proved correct.

The same dynamic appears to be playing out with China. Mainstream Western opinion has fully internalized the 'Chinese Century' narrative — surveys show most Americans believe China has already surpassed or will soon surpass the U.S. — at a moment when several structural cracks are beginning to show. The mechanism is straightforward: visible, recent evidence (Chinese EVs, infrastructure, trade surpluses) is highly legible and emotionally salient, while slower-moving structural weaknesses (demographic drag, industrial policy limits, autocratic fragility) are easy to discount.

For any long-duration investment or strategic planning, this suggests a meta-rule: the more universal the consensus on a geopolitical trajectory, the more carefully one should examine the contrarian case, especially if the counter-indicators are structural rather than cyclical.
