The standard debate about Chinese industrial policy focuses on whether Beijing can 'pick winners' sector by sector. Kroeber reframes this: China's real success came from two all-purpose enabling bets. First, 'informatization' (xìnxīhuà) starting in the 1990s — building networked infrastructure while Western commentators predicted the internet would end authoritarian regimes. They used it for both technological development AND social control, winning both bets.

Second, electrification. Most countries' electricity is only 15-20% of final energy consumption; China is now 30% and rising fast. They have 2x US generating capacity; their renewable capacity alone equals total US generation. This wasn't a climate bet — it started because factories needed power, then compounded through EVs, high-speed rail, and now AI data centers. Having the cheapest, most abundant electricity becomes a durable competitive moat for anything electricity-intensive.

Application: when analyzing a country's (or company's) strategic position, look past the visible product bets to the enabling infrastructure underneath. Asking 'what general-purpose capability is being scaled?' predicts durable advantage better than 'did they pick the right vertical?' The same logic explains why US internet dominance came from base-layer bets (ARPA, semiconductors, fiber) not from picking which apps would win.
