Matt DeFetz's research at Stanford: 193 Chinese-language media organizations globally, 14 million news articles, 25-year time span. Outlets that received PRC funding had pre-existing pro-PRC coverage before the money arrived — patronage isn't shifting their stance, it's selecting outlets whose stance already aligns. Patronage is associated with greater survival probability, so over time the funded outlets capture more market share. The market for Chinese-language news globally is reshaped not through direct control but through asymmetric survival.

Application: state influence on information ecosystems is harder to detect because it operates at the market-survival level rather than the message level. Counter-influence policies that focus on 'who is saying what' miss the actual mechanism. The same logic applies to other forms of soft power: subsidize aligned actors rather than control content.
