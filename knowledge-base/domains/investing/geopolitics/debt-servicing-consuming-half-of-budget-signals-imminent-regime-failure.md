By 1789, France allocated roughly 50% of its national budget to servicing existing debt — a ratio that made meaningful reform mathematically impossible. Every finance minister Louis XVI appointed failed not from incompetence but from arithmetic: when half your resources go to past obligations, you can't fund current operations or invest in structural change. The hailstorm that destroyed crops was the external shock, but the vulnerability was entirely self-created.

This threshold serves as a useful heuristic for evaluating sovereign risk, corporate health, or even personal finance. When debt service crosses roughly 50% of available resources, the entity enters a fragility zone where any external shock — crop failure, market downturn, customer churn — triggers cascading failure. The French case also shows that awareness of the problem isn't sufficient; Louis knew the system was broken and actively tried to fix it. The structural resistance from beneficiaries of the status quo (tax-exempt nobles) made reform impossible within the existing framework, requiring revolutionary restructuring.
