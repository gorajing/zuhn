Ney's courage was legendary — 'the bravest of the brave,' Napoleon called him. But his judgment was terrible. At Waterloo, he launched the French cavalry against intact British infantry squares without infantry or artillery support, destroying France's cavalry reserve for nothing. At Quatre Bras, his aggressive but poorly planned attack failed to take the crossroads. His bravery inspired men to follow him into situations that competent judgment would have avoided.

In business and investing, the Ney archetype is the charismatic leader who is decisive and bold but lacks analytical judgment: the CEO who commits to massive investments on gut feeling, the trader who doubles down on conviction without risk analysis, the founder who launches into markets without research. Their personal courage attracts followers and capital, but their lack of judgment means those resources are frequently destroyed.
