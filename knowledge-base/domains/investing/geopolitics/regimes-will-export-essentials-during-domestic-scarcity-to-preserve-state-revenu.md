This is one of the most counterintuitive points in the lecture: during the famine, China did not stop exporting food. The standard moral reading ('how could they?') misses the structural logic. For a revenue-constrained regime, grain was the only liquid asset with international demand; stopping exports would have halted arms imports, industrial inputs, and prestige projects. The regime's survival calculus prioritized state capacity over peasant lives — and the peasants who died were, not coincidentally, 'the least valued members of society.'

This pattern recurs: the Irish potato famine saw food exports from Ireland to Britain throughout; Ukrainian grain was extracted during the Holodomor; petrostates continue oil exports during domestic shortages. The invariant is that ruling coalitions protect their revenue base before they protect their marginal citizens.

Application for investors and analysts: when modeling regime behavior during crises, the first question is not 'what is humane' but 'what funds the state.' Revenue-critical exports will continue under almost any domestic condition. Predictions of policy reversal during humanitarian crises are usually wrong.
