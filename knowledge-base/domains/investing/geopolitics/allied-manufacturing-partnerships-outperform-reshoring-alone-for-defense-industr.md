The U.S. faces a structural mismatch: defense production bottlenecks (backed-up shipyards, thin munitions lines, aging workforce) cannot be solved by funding alone because new domestic capacity takes 3-7 years to permit and remains vulnerable to litigation. Meanwhile, China is rapidly outpacing U.S. production of ships, submarines, missiles, drones, and ammunition.

Japan offers an immediate force multiplier: world-class manufacturing capability, elite engineering talent, strong IP protection, and a regulatory system where approved projects actually get built without years of legal challenges. Japan already supplies roughly half the world's silicon wafers and photoresists, nearly half of industrial robots, and critical carbon fiber composites embedded across U.S. defense platforms. The lesson generalizes beyond defense: when speed matters more than self-sufficiency, leveraging allied industrial capacity beats waiting for domestic buildout.

Poland demonstrated this pattern after Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine—defense spending surged from 2.4% to ~4% of GDP, procurement timelines compressed from years to months, and foreign direct investment imported the manufacturing know-how needed to scale rapidly.
