Napoleon's first two days at Arcole were failures: direct assaults on the bridge were repulsed with heavy losses. But he didn't withdraw — he kept attacking, adjusting tactics each time. On the third day, a flanking maneuver finally broke the Austrian position. The first two days of 'failure' had actually exhausted Austrian reserves and revealed the flanking vulnerability.

In business, this is the value of iterative failure: products that don't find market fit on the first version, sales approaches that don't convert on the first attempt, marketing campaigns that don't work on the first try. Each 'failure' provides data about what doesn't work and exhausts the most obvious objections. Persistence through three iterations typically achieves what a single optimized attempt cannot, because iteration reveals information that planning cannot.
