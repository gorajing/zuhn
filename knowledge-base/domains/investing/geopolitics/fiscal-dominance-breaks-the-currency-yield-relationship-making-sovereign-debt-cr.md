In normal macro regimes, rising yields attract capital inflows and strengthen the domestic currency. Japan's January 2026 episode inverted this: a 6-standard-deviation move in 40-year JGB yields (to 4.2%+) was accompanied by yen weakness, because the yield rise signaled fiscal risk rather than growth. Investors fled the perceived weakness of a government whose debt issuance needs override the central bank's ability to control inflation — textbook fiscal dominance. The Bank of Japan holding rates at 0.75% while inflation stays above 2% means negative real rates persist even as long yields spike. This creates a structural disorientation for quantitative models calibrated on normal rate regimes. The practical implication for portfolio construction: in fiscally dominated environments, yield-based signals need to be filtered through debt sustainability context, not just rate levels. The JGB market is a canary for what could eventually happen in other high-debt developed markets if fiscal credibility erodes.
