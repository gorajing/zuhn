Only about one-third of final energy use in a modern economy comes from electricity — the rest (transport, high-temperature industrial heat, aviation, shipping) needs liquid or gaseous hydrocarbons. Handmer's Terraform Industries makes synthetic natural gas from sunlight and air, closing this gap. If synthetic fuels scale, the 'energy abundance' moat that China is building with solar becomes a total energy moat rather than just an electricity moat.

The geopolitical math is stark: China imports most of its oil from the Middle East via tankers on routes its navy can't defend (lacks blue-water Indian Ocean capability), and is surrounded by 15 mostly-hostile neighbors. The US, by contrast, has abundant domestic oil, natural gas, and two oceans for protection. Synthetic fuels let China sidestep its single largest strategic vulnerability using an input (solar electricity) it already dominates. Handmer notes Chinese projects are already working on this technology and is unsurprised they're taking it seriously.

Implications for investors and policymakers: (1) synthetic fuel technology is dual-use in a geostrategic sense — it's clean energy infrastructure that also neutralizes oil chokepoints, (2) export controls on synthetic fuel technology may become a consideration analogous to chip export controls, (3) the 'oil weapon' loses potency as synthetic fuels mature, which reshapes Middle East strategic calculus over 10-20 year horizons.
