Kilian and Zhou (2023) find that Europe and the UK experience significantly larger inflation bumps from oil shocks than the US or Japan. For developing countries like Pakistan and Uganda, the transmission mechanism is even more severe: global energy prices feed directly into local food prices, compounding hardship for populations with minimal economic buffers.

During the Iran war, this distributional asymmetry was stark — the US saw $4/gallon gas (annoying but manageable), while India faced panic buying, the Philippines declared a national emergency, and Australia considered fuel rationing. For investors and policy analysts, this means that 'modest global impact' assessments based on US or aggregate data systematically understate the severity experienced by the most vulnerable economies, where oil shocks can trigger genuine crises.
