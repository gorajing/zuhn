Micron's $100B megafab in Onondaga County—the largest semiconductor facility in US history—is receiving New York state support. xAI's $20B Mississippi data center qualified for the Mississippi Development Authority's Data Center Incentive, granting sales and use tax exemptions. In both cases, the government subsidy is not incidental but load-bearing for the investment decision.

This pattern reflects a structural reality in semiconductor economics: fab construction costs are so capital-intensive and timelines so long (5-10 years from groundbreaking to volume production) that private capital alone cannot justify the risk without public subsidy. This has been true since TSMC's Arizona fab negotiations and the CHIPS Act. The implication for investors is that domestic semiconductor capacity expansion is now a public-private co-investment thesis—and that political continuity of incentive programs is a real risk factor in any long-horizon semiconductor investment.
