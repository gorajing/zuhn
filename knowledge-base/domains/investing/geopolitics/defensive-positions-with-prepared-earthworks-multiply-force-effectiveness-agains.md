At Borodino, Kutuzov built extensive earthworks — the Raevsky Redoubt, the fleches — that forced Napoleon to attack prepared positions head-on. Each successive assault cost thousands of casualties. The Russians would lose a position, fall back to the next prepared line, and force another costly assault. Even when Napoleon won each local engagement, the cumulative cost was devastating.

This maps to competitive strategy: entrenched incumbents with prepared defensive positions (regulatory moats, switching costs, network effects, brand loyalty) force attackers into expensive direct assaults. Each position the attacker 'wins' (new market segment, regulatory approval, customer conversion) comes at a cost that compounds. The defender's strategy is not to win any single engagement but to make the total cost of the attack unsustainable.
