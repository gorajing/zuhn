Midha attacks the VC habit of boxing people as static types ('he's a researcher, not a founder'). He counters that publishing at the top of a field over decades — at a Berkeley or in a DeepMind — makes you 'an athlete of the mind' who has already demonstrated the leadership needed to earn an organization's trust, secure resources, and ship work into the world. Being a nominal CEO is easy; being a great one demands a performance level that elite, already-published scientists have proven they can hit (his existence proof: Dario Amodei, a scientist who went from zero to a near-trillion-dollar company in four years). The actionable rule: he would fund researchers who have already 'put soda out there' all day. The one real failure mode is not capability but desire — some great researchers simply don't want to be CEOs and primarily want to publish, which is fine but disqualifying for the seat. The CEO-specific requirement researchers must be willing to add is being confrontational up and down the entire stack — team, hiring, customers — not just confrontational about scientific convictions.
