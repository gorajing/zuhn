Chamath's diagnostic of his own 2021-2022 catastrophe: he was in a period where his SPAC portfolio was 'just ripping' and he had 'confused growing fame with growing skill.' He could tweet and markets would respond. He felt like a market mover. When the right move was 'cut everything, go to cash' in November 2021, he was 'too afraid to tell everybody' because of what they would think — the public position made him feel locked into the narrative.

The broader principle: public visibility in markets creates a self-reinforcing trap. People listen to you because you've made money; you've made money partly because they're listening; so you confuse the social signal with skill. The trap snaps shut when you need to reverse course publicly — the cost of being seen reversing exceeds the cost of being wrong while staying the course, and you choose the second.

For builders, investors, and public-facing operators: never let public position lock you into a thesis. The discipline: when you're considering reversing, the action item is reverse, the optional second is announce. Don't let announcement difficulty drive decision difficulty. For Zuhn editorially: this generalizes to any public-thesis bet. Founders who publicly commit to a strategy face the same trap — quietly pivot, then explain. Don't let the pivot wait on the explanation.
