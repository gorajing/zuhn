Tom Mock's most counterintuitive observation about the industry: 'One of the things I love most about the hedge fund industry is how unfailingly honest it is — sort of the opposite impression the media gives you. The reason: you're going to go to the same 500-2,000 institutional clients that buy into hedge funds. They know exactly what you're doing. They know how you're doing it. They can deconstruct your portfolio without knowing anything except the results. If you show me what your results are and tell me your general holding period, I can deconstruct absolutely everything you do.' The structural mechanism: (1) Allocator pool is small (~2,000 globally). (2) They know each other and talk constantly. (3) Modern attribution analysis lets you back out a strategy from public results — variance patterns, correlation to market factors, gamma profile, drawdown patterns. (4) If you lie about your method (e.g., claim 'we produce returns without short gamma' but your distribution looks short gamma), the deception is detectable. (5) Once detected, allocators don't just decline themselves — they actively warn their network. 'Not only should I not invest with you, I better call my friends who are allocators and make sure they don't either.' The asymmetry: honest mistakes are forgiven ('everybody expects you to lose money sometimes, even the best strategies lose 40% of the time'). Operational mistakes are forgiven if you're honest about them ('lose money in operational ways, generally people will forgive you'). Deception is unforgivable. The corollary for any small ecosystem with repeated interactions (VC, founders, allocators, executive recruiters, niche communities): the optimal long-term strategy is RUTHLESS HONESTY because the network is small enough that lies propagate. Founders who lie to one VC get blacklisted by the network. Operators who lie about their performance get blacklisted at executive search firms. The structural lesson: when interacting in a small repeated-game ecosystem, honesty isn't just ethically right — it's strategically dominant. The temptation to spin or hide is short-term optimization that destroys long-term position.
