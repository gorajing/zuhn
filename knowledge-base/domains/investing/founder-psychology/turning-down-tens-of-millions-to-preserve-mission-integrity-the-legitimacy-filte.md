    Jean-Baptiste Kempf's reasoning for refusing the offers: 'I refused dozens

    not moral and it was not the right thing to do. This is very important for

    reason is also because I did not feel that I'm completely legitimate to do
    that.' The structural argument: VLC's code was contributed by thousands of
    volunteers over decades. Many contributed because they trusted VLC was
    ad-free, community-built, free-as-in-freedom software. If JB had personally
    monetized VLC by introducing ads or selling out to a corporation, he would
    have: (1) Captured value he didn't create alone. (2) Broken the implicit
    contract with the contributor community. (3) Damaged the trust that made VLC
    dominant. The economically rational move (take the money) would have
    destroyed the asset (community trust). The 'legitimacy filter' (refusing
    what you don't feel legitimate to take) is structurally the right move for
    any community-built product. The same logic applies to: (1) Open source
    maintainers thinking about monetization — community trust is the asset;
    selling out destroys it. (2) Influencers with niche audiences — the audience
    is the asset; broad-monetization typically destroys it. (3) Founders
    considering selling — the team and customer trust is the asset; insufficient
    sharing of upside damages it. (4) Researchers commercializing academic work
    — the field's trust is the asset; private capture damages it. The corollary
    for what actually compounds: long-term trust is the rarest and most valuable
    commodity. Most operators are willing to trade short-term trust for
    short-term money. The few who refuse this trade build enduring positions.
    VLC has 6 billion downloads and ZERO ad spend because the trust position is
    unique. JB doesn't need to advertise because users know the project will
    never betray them. The legitimacy filter (refuse what you didn't earn alone)
    is the operational discipline that produces this. The corollary for AI
    startups: many AI products are built on top of foundation models built by
    labs (Anthropic, OpenAI). The legitimacy question: are AI app companies
    adequately recognizing/compensating the underlying labs? The economic answer
    is mixed; the legitimacy answer is that long-term ecosystem health requires
    fair distribution. Startups that aggressively capture value while denying
    lab dependencies will face long-term ecosystem backlash. The corollary for
    AI training data: the same legitimacy question applies to training on
    creators' work without compensation. Labs that train on uncompensated
    creator content while reaping massive valuation gains are running the same
    trade JB refused — short-term money for long-term legitimacy. The trade may
    produce short-term wins; it's structurally toxic to long-term ecosystem
    trust. The corollary for personal career: refuse offers that capture value
    you didn't earn alone. The discipline preserves your reputation, your
    authentic positioning, and your long-term capacity to deserve trust. The
    financial cost is real; the legitimacy gain compounds for decades.

Jean-Baptiste Kempf's reasoning for refusing the offers: 'I refused dozens of millions of dollars, yes, several times. I could be a multi-millionaire and be somewhere on the beach. But I did not do it because I thought it was not moral and it was not the right thing to do. This is very important for myself — to be like, I work for the greater good, I work for people. But the reason is also because I did not feel that I'm completely legitimate to do that.' The structural argument: VLC's code was contributed by thousands of volunteers over decades. Many contributed because they trusted VLC was ad-free, community-built, free-as-in-freedom software. If JB had personally monetized VLC by introducing ads or selling out to a corporation, he would have: (1) Captured value he didn't create alone. (2) Broken the implicit contract with the contributor community. (3) Damaged the trust that made VLC dominant. The economically rational move (take the money) would have destroyed the asset (community trust). The 'legitimacy filter' (refusing what you don't feel legitimate to take) is structurally the right move for any community-built product. The same logic applies to: (1) Open source maintainers thinking about monetization — community trust is the asset; selling out destroys it. (2) Influencers with niche audiences — the audience is the asset; broad-monetization typically destroys it. (3) Founders considering selling — the team and customer trust is the asset; insufficient sharing of upside damages it. (4) Researchers commercializing academic work — the field's trust is the asset; private capture damages it. The corollary for what actually compounds: long-term trust is the rarest and most valuable commodity. Most operators are willing to trade short-term trust for short-term money. The few who refuse this trade build enduring positions. VLC has 6 billion downloads and ZERO ad spend because the trust position is unique. JB doesn't need to advertise because users know the project will never betray them. The legitimacy filter (refuse what you didn't earn alone) is the operational discipline that produces this. The corollary for AI startups: many AI products are built on top of foundation models built by labs (Anthropic, OpenAI). The legitimacy question: are AI app companies adequately recognizing/compensating the underlying labs? The economic answer is mixed; the legitimacy answer is that long-term ecosystem health requires fair distribution. Startups that aggressively capture value while denying lab dependencies will face long-term ecosystem backlash. The corollary for AI training data: the same legitimacy question applies to training on creators' work without compensation. Labs that train on uncompensated creator content while reaping massive valuation gains are running the same trade JB refused — short-term money for long-term legitimacy. The discipline preserves your reputation, your authentic positioning, and your long-term capacity to deserve trust. The financial cost is real; the legitimacy gain compounds for decades.
