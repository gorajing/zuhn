Stu's framing of the most common AI failure mode (he calls it the slop cannon): the temptation to automate everything because automation is now cheap leads to mass-producing low-quality output. Examples: AI auto-replying to all LinkedIn DMs (now your AI is talking to their AI, no human ever sees value); content creators 'automating their entire podcast process in 15 minutes with no humans' (the resulting podcast is unlistenable slop); biotech companies that build mass-document-generation pipelines and then realize none of the documents can be trusted. The diagnostic: if your automation outputs something where you ask 'is this quality?' and the honest answer is 'I'm not sure,' you've built a slop cannon. The remedy is to automate selectively — automate the things that are right to automate (where verification is cheap and outputs are usable as-is), not the things where you'd traditionally need human judgment to QA each output. There's a parallel with Yash Tekriwal's 'automate the manual, never automate the important': both are arguing that automation should be applied surgically to the parts where humans add no judgment, not as a universal cost-reducer. The deeper point: in a world where everyone can build slop cannons, the scarce resource becomes things that are NOT slop — humans-in-the-loop, verified outputs, distinctive voice. The uncanny-valley aftereffect on social media (people instantly detecting AI-generated content and disengaging) is the market response to slop cannon proliferation.
