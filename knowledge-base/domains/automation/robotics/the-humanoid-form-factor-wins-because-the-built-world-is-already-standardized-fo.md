Every factory, warehouse, and home on Earth was designed around human physical constraints: bipedal locomotion, two hands, a certain reach envelope, a standing height optimized for counters and controls. Task-specific robots — conveyor systems, robotic arms, automated forklifts — require expensive facility redesign to accommodate their fixed form factors. A humanoid robot sidesteps this entirely by matching the form factor that infrastructure already assumes.

This is an underappreciated moat. It means deployment cost is dominated by the robot itself rather than by facility modification. It also means humanoids can operate across diverse environments (factory floor, reception desk, hospital corridor) with the same hardware, unlike specialized robots that are stranded in the environments they were purpose-built for. The insight generalizes: when a new technology can run on existing infrastructure rather than requiring new infrastructure, its adoption curve accelerates dramatically.
