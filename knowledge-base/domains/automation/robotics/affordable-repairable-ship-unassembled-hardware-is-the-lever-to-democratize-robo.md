Today's impressive robots — humanoids at $50K+, Waymo in the six figures — are too expensive to prototype with and too closed to adapt; no high school orders ten 50K humanoids for students. Marafioti frames Reachy Mini as a response: at $300-450, sold in bulk to schools, with the cheaper SKU dropping the Raspberry Pi and battery so you supply your own laptop or hardware.

The deliberate design choices encode the democratization thesis. Robots ship unassembled, so the owner's first act is building it — which means they then understand and can repair anything that breaks by just ordering parts. Everything (models, agents, pipeline) is open source, and the hardware is designed to stack with the team's other open robots (SO100/SO101 arms, the Kiwi wheeled base). The explicit goal is that interaction with robots be developed communally 'like computers were made in a bit more of a hacker fashion,' not guarded behind 50K machines. With 7,500 units shipped, the bet is that low price plus repairability plus openness compounds into a developer ecosystem the way the early PC did.
