The plan's most elegant move is deploying trillions of cyanobacteria to convert the atmosphere over thousands of years. No human workforce could match this — the bacteria self-replicate, self-repair, and self-distribute. They already proved the concept by converting Earth's original toxic atmosphere into one supporting complex life.

This is the deep pattern behind all scalable automation: instead of building bigger machines, deploy agents that replicate themselves. Software follows this pattern with viral distribution, open-source contribution, and AI agents that spawn sub-agents. The cyanobacteria approach works because each unit is simple, expendable, and self-replicating — the complexity emerges from scale, not from individual sophistication. This suggests that when facing a problem of planetary scale, the answer is rarely 'build a bigger tool' and almost always 'deploy a simpler agent that multiplies.'
