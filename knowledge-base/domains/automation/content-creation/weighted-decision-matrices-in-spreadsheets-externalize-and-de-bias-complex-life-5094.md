The career decision matrix project assigns weighted scores to job criteria (salary, location, growth, culture) and computes a total for each option. This simple structure does something psychologically powerful: it separates the act of defining what matters from the act of evaluating specific options.

Without externalization, humans suffer from attribute substitution — we replace hard questions ('which job maximizes long-term career capital?') with easy ones ('which job feels most exciting right now?'). The matrix forces you to commit to weights before seeing how options score, preventing post-hoc rationalization. This technique scales beyond career choices to vendor selection, feature prioritization, and any multi-criteria decision where stakeholders disagree on what matters most.
