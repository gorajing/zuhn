Creators often assume any viral video is good because 'exposure.' But the algorithm updates its channel-level fit model based on who engaged with your recent hits. If a one-off video goes viral with an audience outside your core — say a cooking creator whose political take blows up — the next video's sample will be skewed toward political viewers who will not watch a knife-skills demo. Those viewers bounce, sample data comes back bad, and the video flops despite being on-brand.

This creates a counterintuitive discipline: sometimes the right move is to kill or privately post an idea that would clearly go viral because the audience it would reach is wrong for your long-term trajectory. The cost of a misaligned viral hit is paid across multiple subsequent posts, not just the current one.
