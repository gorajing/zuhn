When the Egyptian military seized power and people were threatening Youssef not to speak against them, he called Jon Stewart in panic. Stewart gave him the most important creative advice of his career: 'If you're afraid of something, make fun of the fact that you're afraid of it.'

This principle operates on multiple levels. Comedically, acknowledging fear is inherently relatable and funny — the audience recognizes their own fear in yours. Psychologically, naming fear out loud reduces its power. Strategically, satirizing your own fear pre-empts the censor: they can't threaten you with something you've already publicly acknowledged being afraid of. Youssef built his entire career on this — leaving Egypt, rebuilding in America, and becoming the most fearless political satirist in the Middle East. The principle extends beyond comedy: in any domain where fear blocks action, publicly acknowledging that fear transforms it from a barrier into material.
