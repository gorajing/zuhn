At real-time speed, an underwater gunshot looks like a single violent splash. At 27,450 fps, it decomposes into three distinct, temporally separated phenomena: (1) cavitation bubbles forming along the barrel from the Bernoulli effect as propellant gases exit, (2) burnt gunpowder gas trailing behind the projectile as a visible sleeve, and (3) shock-wave-induced vapor forming independently of the other two effects.

This is a general principle about observation resolution: many 'single events' in any domain are actually stacked phenomena that only separate when you increase your measurement resolution by orders of magnitude. The lesson applies to debugging (log granularity), financial analysis (tick-level vs daily data), and scientific inquiry broadly—what looks like one thing at coarse resolution is often three things at fine resolution.
