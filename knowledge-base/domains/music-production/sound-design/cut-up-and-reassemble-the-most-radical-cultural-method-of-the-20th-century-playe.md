The documentary's opening thesis (Genesis P-Orridge / Coldcut): 'We discovered that the word could be cut up, that sound could be cut up, that everything to do with culture could be cut up and reassembled in ways that didn't exist before. That will be seen as the most radical important thing that happened this century.' The historical arc: Pierre Schaeffer's musique concrète studio (1948+) — splitting sounds, prolonging via reverberation, repeating, building 'a sort of alchemy that doesn't exist in orchestral music.' This evolves into hip-hop sampling, jungle's combination of dub + breaks + techno, mashup culture, ultimately into AI-generated content that recombines training-data patterns. Why this matters: pre-recombinant cultural production assumed a 'composer' creating from imagination. Recombinant culture assumes a 'curator/juxtaposer' selecting and arranging existing material. The shift is from creation-as-origination to creation-as-recombination. The corollary for any creator/builder today: the recombinant skill (recognizing what to combine, choosing the juxtaposition that produces meaning) is now more valuable than the originator skill (producing material from scratch). Generative AI accelerates this — the model is the ultimate recombinator, and the human's value-add is curation/selection/juxtaposition (Sandy Lee's authenticity-not-originality, Yash Tekriwal's 'manual creative process must precede automation,' Codex masterclass's 'extract concepts and repackage'). Electronic music's 80-year history is the rehearsal for what every creative domain is now living through. The deeper implication: the analytical mode (Descartes — solve problems by breaking into pieces) trained Western culture to decompose. The recombinant mode (electronic music, AI, modern content) requires the inverse skill — putting decomposed pieces back together into meaningful wholes. We're good at the first, bad at the second. The cultural shift now is learning the synthesis skill at scale.
