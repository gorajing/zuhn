Chris Anokute's direct empirical observation as a veteran A&R: 'I've seen major Platinum, number-one Billboard charting producers who are musicians and have produced some of the most credible records — and I won't say their names — I've seen them with my own eyes using Suno as a tool. And these are the same people who were kicking and screaming a year ago when Timbaland said he's using Suno and he said how dare you?' The pattern across creative industries: (1) New creative tool emerges (Suno for music, generative image AI for visual art, autotune for vocals, CGI for film, sampling for music in the 1980s). (2) Public position from established professionals: 'this is destroying our art form, real artists don't use this, anyone who does is a fraud.' (3) Private practice: established professionals quietly adopt because the tool works and the productivity gain is real. (4) 12-24 months later: industry-wide normalization, eventually the same people who attacked the tool become advocates. (5) The 'late majority' adopters loudly criticize the early adopters who attacked the tool — historical revisionism. The predictive insight: in any creative industry experiencing AI tool adoption, the public discourse will dramatically understate adoption rates because professional reputation depends on appearing 'authentic.' The reality is professionals adopt tools that work, regardless of their public stance. The corollary for industry-watchers: don't predict adoption rates from public statements. Use indirect signals — tool revenue growth, hiring patterns at AI-tool companies, anonymous surveys, behavioral indicators (Suno had $300M revenue year 2 — that doesn't happen with hobbyist users only). The corollary for individual creators: stop trying to figure out what's 'allowed.' If a tool helps you ship work that resonates with audiences, use it. The public discourse will catch up to your practice within 12-24 months. The corollary for tool builders: your most valuable customers are the ones who can't publicly admit they use you. Build for them. Help them work efficiently while preserving their public identity. Suno's success comes partly from being usable by professionals who never publicly admit usage.
