From the documentary's interview with Roland engineers and Chicago house producers: 'The TB-303 was originally priced at $600-700. It was trying to mimic acoustic instruments [bass guitar], but it didn't. The 303 lost its value, and that's why a lot of these guys in Chicago, in Detroit and Germany could pick up these things in the used market for $50 to $20.' The TR-909 had a similar story — Roland's attempt at digital-sampled drum sounds that 'did not sound incredibly great' compared to acoustic drums. Both machines bombed in their intended market. The squelchy 303 became the defining sound of acid house ('Acid Tracks' by Phuture in 1987, named after the machine). The 909 kick + hi-hat became the defining sound of techno. 'You can't listen to a house track without hearing a 909 drum kit in it.' Why this matters as a pattern: products designed to mimic something existing are evaluated against the original. They lose. But the FAILURE MODE (the way they sound 'wrong') has its own character that creative users discover. Once the failure-mode-as-aesthetic catches on, the failed product becomes the cult tool. The pattern recurs widely: (1) Auto-Tune was designed to correct pitch — used by T-Pain and Kanye West to introduce the iconic robotic vocal sound. (2) The Yamaha DX-7's harsh FM synthesis sounded 'wrong' compared to analog warmth — became the defining sound of 80s pop. (3) Lo-fi compression sounds 'wrong' compared to clean recording — became an entire genre. (4) Glitch art (corrupted-image aesthetics) emerged from data errors that creative users embraced. (5) GPT-3's hallucinations were failures-of-accuracy that creative users now mine as fiction generators. The implication for product builders: don't optimize away the 'wrong' character of your product. Failed-as-intended-design can be cult-as-creative-tool. Document the unusual outputs, share the failure modes — your most valuable users may be the ones who fall in love with what your engineers were trying to fix. The implication for buyers/investors in creative tools: study used markets and DIY communities. The next major creative shift is often using cheap-because-failed gear in ways the original designers never intended.
