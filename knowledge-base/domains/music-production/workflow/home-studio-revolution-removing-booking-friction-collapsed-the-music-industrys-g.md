From a producer interviewed in the documentary describing the shift: 'I just like the thing of getting out of bed and going and getting your breakfast, then come back, and then you're straight in the studio. You don't even have to go down the road and get on a bus to go to a studio. You're just there. It's hands-on, straightaway. You wake up in the middle of the night and do a track. That's what sort of revolutionized it for me.' The framing matters: pre-home-studio, recording required (a) studio booking (gated by money, social access, label sponsorship), (b) engineer scheduling (gated by professional networks), (c) instrument access (gated by gear ownership). Each gate was a structural barrier limiting WHO got to make music. Cost was only part of the gate — most aspiring musicians could scrape together studio fees occasionally. The deeper barrier was friction: leaving home, scheduling, negotiating, traveling. Home studios eliminated all of these. The producer wakes at 3am with an idea and records it 60 seconds later. The friction approaches zero. The total volume of music produced explodes. The result: labels lose their gatekeeping function (any producer can release directly). The economic surplus shifts from labels (capital allocators of studio access) to platforms (Spotify, YouTube — discovery allocators of attention). The general pattern: GATEKEEPING THROUGH FRICTION is far more powerful than gatekeeping through cost, because cost can be overcome with savings/lending but friction structures who-even-tries. The same pattern is now playing out across creative domains: (1) AI image generation eliminates studio-booking friction (model, location, scheduling) — every laptop owner can produce stock-quality images. (2) AI writing eliminates editor-queue friction — every operator can produce decent-quality copy without negotiating with an editor. (3) AI video eliminates production-team-scheduling friction. (4) AI coding agents eliminate developer-availability friction — anyone can have software written immediately. The implication for any industry: identify what FRICTION (not what cost) gates participation. Whoever automates that friction wins the next decade's value capture. The corollary for incumbents: if your business model relies on customers tolerating friction (scheduling, queueing, negotiating, traveling), automation will route around you — even if your raw service quality remains higher than the automated alternative.
