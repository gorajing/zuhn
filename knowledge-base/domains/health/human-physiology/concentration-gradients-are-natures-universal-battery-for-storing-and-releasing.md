In photosynthesis, the thylakoid membrane accumulates protons on one side through water-splitting and active pumping via the cytochrome complex. This concentration gradient — protons desperately wanting to equalize — drives ATP synthase like water through a turbine. The membrane doesn't store energy as a molecule; it stores energy as a difference in concentration.

This is the same mechanism that powers human cellular respiration in mitochondria. Understanding this pattern reveals why membrane integrity is so critical to health: anything that disrupts membrane gradients (toxins, oxidative damage, certain drugs) can catastrophically affect energy production. It also explains why the phospholipid bilayer structure — good at maintaining concentration differences — is one of the most conserved structures in all biology. Energy in living systems isn't primarily about fuel molecules; it's about maintaining differences across barriers.
