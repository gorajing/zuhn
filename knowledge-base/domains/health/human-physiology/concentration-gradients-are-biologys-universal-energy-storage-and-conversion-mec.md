In photosynthesis, the thylakoid membrane accumulates protons from water splitting and active pumping by the Cytochrome complex, creating a concentration gradient. Protons then flow through ATP synthase 'like water through a turbine,' converting potential energy into ATP. The video describes this as 'charging the thylakoid like a battery.'

This chemiosmotic mechanism is not unique to photosynthesis — it's the same principle used in mitochondria for cellular respiration, in neurons for signal transmission, and in kidneys for filtration. Phospholipid bilayers are 'really good at maintaining concentration gradients,' making them the universal hardware for biological energy storage.

Understanding this pattern reframes how we think about biological energy: it's not primarily about chemical bonds but about organized differences across boundaries. This has implications for understanding fatigue (gradient dissipation), nutrition (electrolyte balance maintains gradients), and even why cell membrane integrity is so critical to health — damage the membrane, lose the gradient, lose the energy.
