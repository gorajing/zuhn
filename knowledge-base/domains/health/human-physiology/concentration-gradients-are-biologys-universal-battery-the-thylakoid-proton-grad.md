In photosynthesis, the thylakoid membrane accumulates protons to create a concentration gradient that drives ATP synthase — essentially charging and discharging a molecular battery. This exact same mechanism (chemiosmosis) appears in mitochondria during cellular respiration, and the broader pattern of concentration gradients across membranes powers neural action potentials, muscle contraction, and kidney filtration.

Biology reuses a remarkably small toolkit of fundamental mechanisms across radically different contexts. Phospholipid bilayer membranes maintaining concentration gradients is one of the most universal patterns in all of biology. This convergence suggests that when a mechanism is truly effective at the physical level, evolution rediscovers and repurposes it rather than inventing alternatives — a principle that parallels how successful software patterns (pub/sub, queues, caches) appear across every domain of computing.
