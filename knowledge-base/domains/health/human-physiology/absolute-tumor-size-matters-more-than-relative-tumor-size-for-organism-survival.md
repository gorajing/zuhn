Cancer lethality depends on tumor burden relative to body mass, not absolute cell count. A tumor requiring the same number of cell divisions to form represents a lethal 10% of a mouse's body weight but a trivially small fraction of a whale's. This means large animals get a passive defense simply from scale — they can tolerate numerous small tumors that never grow large enough to impair function. Combined with hypertumor dynamics that prevent any single cancer from dominating, large organisms may be perpetually riddled with tiny, self-limiting cancers. This insight about absolute-vs-relative thresholds applies broadly: the same absolute disruption can be catastrophic or negligible depending on the scale of the system it occurs in.
