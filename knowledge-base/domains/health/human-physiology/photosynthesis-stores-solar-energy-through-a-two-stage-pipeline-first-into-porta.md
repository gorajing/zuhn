Photosynthesis splits into two distinct stages with different functions. Stage 1 (light-dependent reactions) converts photon energy into two portable chemical carriers: ATP and NADPH. Stage 2 (the Calvin Cycle) spends those carriers to fix CO2 into G3P (glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate), a versatile 3-carbon molecule that plants convert into glucose, cellulose, or starch depending on need.

This two-stage architecture — capture energy into intermediate carriers, then spend carriers on construction — is a universal pattern in biological energy systems. Respiration uses the same carriers in reverse. The intermediate buffer (ATP/NADPH) decouples energy supply from energy demand, allowing each stage to operate at its own rate and respond independently to changing conditions. G3P as the universal output means one pipeline serves all downstream needs.
