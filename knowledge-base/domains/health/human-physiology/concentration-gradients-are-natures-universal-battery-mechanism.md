The light-dependent reactions of photosynthesis don't directly create usable energy. Instead, they charge the thylakoid membrane like a battery by pumping protons inside through water-splitting and the cytochrome complex. ATP synthase then harvests this gradient as protons push through it, converting ADP to ATP.

This chemiosmotic mechanism — building a concentration gradient across a membrane and then letting ions flow through an energy-harvesting enzyme — is identical in principle to how mitochondria generate ATP during cellular respiration. It's the universal pattern of biological energy conversion, evolved independently in multiple contexts. Understanding this gradient-based energy storage illuminates why membrane integrity is so critical to cellular health and why many toxins and drugs target membrane transport proteins.
