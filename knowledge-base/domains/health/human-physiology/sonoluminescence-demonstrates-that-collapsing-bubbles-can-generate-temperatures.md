Sonoluminescence occurs when a rapidly collapsing bubble compresses its interior gas so violently that temperatures exceed 10,000 Kelvin—hotter than the surface of the sun—producing a brief flash of light. This phenomenon, governed by the Rayleigh-Plesset equation for bubble dynamics, demonstrates that extreme energy concentration can emerge from ordinary fluid mechanics.

The broader insight is about energy focusing: a diffuse input (sound waves) can be concentrated by geometric collapse into an output many orders of magnitude more intense. This principle appears across domains—from focused ultrasound in medical therapy (using cavitation for tissue ablation) to implosion-based designs in engineering. The human body itself experiences cavitation effects in joints and blood vessels, making this physics directly relevant to understanding biological mechanisms like joint cracking and potential vascular damage from rapid pressure changes.
