The mechanism of laser tattoo removal hinges on a race condition between energy delivery and heat conduction. When a picosecond pulse hits an ink particle, it heats one face almost instantaneously. That face thermally expands while the opposite face remains cool and unchanged. The resulting internal stress gradient literally tears the particle apart from within.

This is why pulse duration matters more than raw power — a slower, more powerful beam would simply heat the entire particle (and surrounding tissue) uniformly. The picosecond timescale is specifically chosen to be faster than heat can conduct across a microscopic particle. The same differential-expansion principle appears in geology (freeze-thaw cycles cracking rocks), engineering (thermal shock in ceramics), and materials science (rapid quenching creating specific crystal structures). Speed of change, not magnitude of change, determines whether something breaks.
