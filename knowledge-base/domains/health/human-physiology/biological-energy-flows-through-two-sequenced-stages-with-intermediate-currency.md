The light-dependent reactions and the Calvin Cycle are physically and temporally distinct: the first happens on thylakoid membranes using photon energy, and converts it into ATP and NADPH. The second happens in the stroma and consumes those molecules to build carbohydrates from CO2. The two stages don't interact directly — they communicate only through the intermediate energy currencies.

This architecture is biologically universal. Mitochondria run the same separation: the electron transport chain charges ATP, and metabolic pathways spend it. The intermediate currencies (ATP, NADPH, FADH2) act as a buffer layer that allows production and consumption to operate at different rates, locations, and in response to different signals.

Practically, this means cellular energy levels are relatively stable even when inputs fluctuate — a charged ATP pool absorbs variation. It also means that drugs or conditions affecting ATP synthesis (e.g., mitochondrial dysfunction, cyanide poisoning) are rapidly fatal because they collapse the buffer that every energy-requiring process depends on.
