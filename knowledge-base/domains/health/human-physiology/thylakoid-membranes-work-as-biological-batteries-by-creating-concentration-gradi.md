The light-dependent reactions don't directly create usable energy. Instead, they charge the thylakoid membrane like a battery: protons from water-splitting and the cytochrome complex accumulate inside, creating a concentration gradient. Protons then push through ATP synthase (driven by their desire to equalize concentration), and that mechanical force packs inorganic phosphate onto ADP to create ATP.

This chemiosmotic principle — first proposed by Peter Mitchell — is universal across biology. Mitochondria use the same trick in reverse during cellular respiration. The key insight is that biological energy systems are fundamentally indirect: they convert energy into concentration imbalances, then harvest those imbalances. This is why membrane integrity is so critical to cellular health — a leaky membrane is a dead battery.
