After an infection is cleared, most immune cells self-destruct to conserve resources. But memory T cells remain in lymph nodes, ready to recognize the same pathogen instantly on re-encounter. The second response is so fast the infection is typically eliminated before symptoms even appear. The body pays a high cost once — inflammation, cell death, energy expenditure, days of sickness — and then amortizes that investment across a lifetime of future encounters.

This is one of biology's clearest demonstrations of learning as investment. The first encounter is expensive and painful, but the encoded memory makes every subsequent encounter nearly free. This maps directly to organizational learning, skill acquisition, and system hardening: the first incident is costly, but if you encode what you learned (runbooks, antibodies, muscle memory), the cost of future encounters drops to near zero. Systems that lack this memory mechanism — that treat every encounter as novel — pay full price every time.
