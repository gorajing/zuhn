When abdominal muscles are consciously braced, they form a rigid barrier that distributes and absorbs kinetic energy across a wider surface area. The internal organs experience significantly less force because the muscular wall acts as a shock absorber. This is visible in high-speed footage where a prepared subject's abdomen deforms minimally compared to an unprepared hit.

This principle extends beyond combat sports — it applies to any scenario involving sudden physical impact. Car accidents, falls, and sports collisions all involve the same biomechanical reality: the body's preparedness at the moment of impact determines how much energy reaches vulnerable internal structures. Houdini's fatal punch succeeded not because the force was extraordinary, but because his muscles were completely relaxed when it landed.
