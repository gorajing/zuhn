In photosynthesis, energy isn't stored directly as ATP right away. Instead, the electron transport chain pumps protons into the thylakoid, creating a concentration gradient. Protons then flow back out through ATP synthase — like water through a turbine — generating ATP. The membrane itself is the battery; the gradient is the charge.

This chemiosmotic mechanism (discovered by Peter Mitchell, Nobel Prize 1978) operates in both photosynthesis and cellular respiration. It reveals that biological energy systems are fundamentally about maintaining differentials across barriers, not just breaking chemical bonds. This has implications for understanding fatigue, mitochondrial dysfunction, and why membrane health (influenced by lipid composition, oxidative damage, and temperature) is so central to cellular energy production. When membranes lose integrity, the gradient collapses and ATP production fails — regardless of fuel availability.
