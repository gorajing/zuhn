Peto's Paradox reveals that cancer rates don't scale linearly with cell count — blue whales with 3,000x more cells than humans barely get cancer. One explanation is that large animals evolved redundant tumor suppressor genes, meaning more independent mutations must occur in the same cell before it turns cancerous. This is nature's version of defense-in-depth: each additional suppressor gene is another lock that must be picked. The tradeoff likely exists (possibly accelerated aging or slower wound healing), but the principle demonstrates that scaling any complex system requires proportionally greater investment in integrity checks — a pattern visible from biology to software architecture.
