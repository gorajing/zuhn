The immune system deploys neutrophils as aggressive fighters that kill bacteria but also destroy healthy cells in the process. To prevent runaway damage, neutrophils are programmed to self-destruct after approximately five days. Similarly, once an infection is cleared, most immune cells lose the chemical signals that sustain them and undergo apoptosis rather than persisting unnecessarily.

This reveals a deep design principle: any sufficiently powerful defense mechanism must have built-in self-termination. The immune system solves the 'who watches the watchmen' problem not through external oversight but through intrinsic expiration. The cost of maintaining aggressive defenders beyond their usefulness exceeds the cost of rebuilding them from scratch when needed again.
