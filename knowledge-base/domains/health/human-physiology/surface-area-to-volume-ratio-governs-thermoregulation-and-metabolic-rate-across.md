As animals shrink, their surface area grows relative to their volume, meaning they lose heat disproportionately fast. This is why mice have heart rates around 600 bpm while elephants hover around 30 — smaller animals must burn energy far faster just to maintain body temperature. The same geometric principle explains why children are more vulnerable to hypothermia than adults.

This constraint cascades through all of physiology: caloric needs per unit mass, organ proportions, lifespan, and even the viability of certain body plans at certain sizes. It's a reminder that geometry imposes hard physical constraints that no amount of biological engineering can fully overcome — evolution must work within the mathematical reality of how surface area and volume scale differently.
