While most fertility discourse focuses on women delaying childbearing, there is a parallel and underappreciated crisis on the male side. Population-level testosterone in men is declining approximately 1% per year, and this trend persists even after controlling for age, health, and lifestyle factors. Low testosterone is directly associated with impaired sperm generation.

The implications are compounding: a 30-year-old man today has roughly 20% lower testosterone than a 30-year-old from the previous generation. This means both sides of the reproductive equation are under simultaneous pressure — women's biological fertility window is being pushed later by social and economic forces, while men's reproductive capacity is being degraded by environmental or systemic factors that aren't fully understood. The environmental causes (likely endocrine disruptors, microplastics, or lifestyle shifts) represent a potentially addressable intervention point, but only if the problem receives adequate research attention.
