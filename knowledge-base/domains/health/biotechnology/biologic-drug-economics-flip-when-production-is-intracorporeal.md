Biologics today are expensive largely because they require sterile biomanufacturing, cold chain, and repeated dosing — each unit of therapy bears the full weight of a factory-to-patient supply chain. A biohybrid implant front-loads the cost into one device containing living producer cells; ongoing per-dose costs approach zero, but new dominant risks emerge: how long the cells stay viable, how the immune system responds, and how reliable the bioelectronic control stays in vivo. This reshapes both pricing models (possibly annualized or outcome-based) and the R&D scoreboard (cell longevity becomes the bottleneck metric).
