Kimmel's controversial contention: lipid nanoparticles and viral vectors are both fundamentally limited. Viruses are always somewhat immunogenic (your immune system fights them), and no single viral species infects all tissue types, so each new indication requires re-engineering. LNPs have inherent size and biodistribution constraints — they're mostly confined to tissues that naturally take up fat (liver), with engineered targeting still limited to a subset of cell types. Both are fine for the next several decades of work, but neither is the endgame.

The biological precedent already exists: the immune system is exactly a body-wide, context-sensitive delivery platform. T cells and B cells patrol nearly all tissues (ex: brain, eye, joints are 'immune-privileged' exceptions), use AND-gate circuitry to detect specific combinations of environmental signals, and release cytokine or cytolytic payloads at the correct location. CAR-T therapy is an early proof of concept — it engineers only the detection half of that system, leaving the payload native. The next generation engineers both halves: cells with custom receptors detecting disease signatures and custom payloads of reprogramming TFs or arbitrary nucleic acids.

The critical enabling property is that cells are big and carry billion-base-pair genomes — unlike AAVs (capped at a few kilobases) or LNPs (size-limited mRNA cargos), engineered cells can encode arbitrarily complex logic and large payloads. They also persist for years after engraftment, so you get chronic responsive therapy from a single administration rather than repeated doses. This bifurcates the opportunity map: viral-vector companies will address the immune-privileged compartments their immunogenicity doesn't break, while engineered-cell therapies will cover the rest of the body — a complementarity that could span all of medicine. It's worth noting this is explicitly Kimmel's 'bet my second company on it' hypothesis, not consensus.
