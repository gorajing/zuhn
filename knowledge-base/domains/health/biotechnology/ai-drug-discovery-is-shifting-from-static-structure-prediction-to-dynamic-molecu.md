AlphaFold won a Nobel Prize for predicting the 3D structure of proteins — a massive scientific achievement. But static structure prediction has a ceiling: proteins are dynamic, and many of the most important drug targets (like KRAS in cancer) only expose binding sites briefly as they flex and move. Earlier models saw a smooth surface; those transient pockets were invisible, which is why aggressive cancers and neurodegenerative diseases were labeled undruggable.

IsoDDE's advance is simulating this molecular motion to reveal cryptic pockets. This is the difference between a photograph and a video — static prediction vs. dynamic modeling. The practical consequence is that a class of disease targets previously considered inaccessible becomes tractable for drug design.

The competitive dynamic here is also notable: Deep Origin presented comparable results months earlier at a conference, suggesting this is a competitive frontier with multiple labs converging on the same capability simultaneously. That compression of research timelines is itself a signal about where AI is accelerating scientific discovery most.
