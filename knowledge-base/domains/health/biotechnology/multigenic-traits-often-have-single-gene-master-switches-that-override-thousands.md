GWAS typically attributes complex traits like height to thousands of small-effect loci, suggesting that modifying them requires editing most of the genome. Church argues this picture is misleading: growth hormone alone produces extreme stature variation and is used clinically for seven treatments. Similarly, a single transcription factor can often convert a stem cell into a specific neuron type. Biology is forgiving because development runs at a 'high programming level' where small rule changes cascade into large morphological differences.

The practical implication is that the search space for meaningful gene therapy is much smaller than GWAS implies. Rather than trying to nudge thousands of variants by a fraction of a percent each, the high-leverage play is finding the master switch — one regulator whose perturbation produces the desired phenotype. This is why direwolf de-extinction required roughly 20 edits rather than millions, and why single transcription factor recipes reliably produce specific cell types. It reframes gene therapy from an additive statistical problem into a search for levers.

This insight generalizes beyond biology: in any complex adaptive system described by noisy high-dimensional statistics, the research strategy should not be to replicate the statistical distribution but to hunt for the control points that evolution or the system itself uses. The aggregate statistics are downstream symptoms, not the underlying machinery.
