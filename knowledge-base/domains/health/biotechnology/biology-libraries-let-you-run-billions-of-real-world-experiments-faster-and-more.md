AlphaFold can predict a protein fold to fractional Angstrom precision yet fail to predict whether a single alanine-for-serine substitution destroys function. Simulation stacks inherit compounding assumptions: QED to quantum mechanics to molecular mechanics — each layer introduces approximation error. Biology sidesteps this entirely. When you barcode and screen 10^17 physical variants, you are not simulating; you are running the actual physics, which is by definition 100% accurate to the real system.

This inverts the usual 'simulate first, then build' research pipeline. Rather than computing which protein to make, use AI to design a library that excludes obviously dead variants, then let physical selection identify winners, then feed the results back into the model. Evolution did this naturally at millions-of-years timescales; modern labs compress the same process into an afternoon, and each round guides the next. It's a hybrid where AI filters the initial space and wet lab provides ground truth.

The strategic implication for materials science, drug discovery, and any high-dimensional design problem: if you can make libraries cheaply, physical screening is not a slower backup to simulation — it is often the faster and strictly more accurate tool. The rate-limiter is not compute but the ability to synthesize, barcode, and read out diverse candidates.
