When tattoo ink is injected into the dermis, the body's immune system immediately tries to remove it. Macrophages attempt phagocytosis — engulfing and digesting foreign particles — but tattoo ink particles are simply too large (on the order of microns) for a single macrophage to consume. The particles become trapped in the dermal layer, surrounded by immune cells but never cleared.

This reframes tattoo permanence as a physical constraint rather than a chemical one. The ink doesn't bond to cells or tissue in any special way; it's simply too big to be carried away. This particle-size threshold is a recurring pattern in biology: the immune system's effectiveness is bounded by physical dimensions, not just biochemical recognition. The same principle explains why asbestos fibers and certain industrial nanoparticles cause chronic inflammation — they frustrate the phagocytic machinery.
