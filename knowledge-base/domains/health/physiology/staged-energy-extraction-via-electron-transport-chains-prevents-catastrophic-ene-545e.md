When a photon excites an electron in PSII, that electron holds significant energy. If released all at once, most would dissipate as heat — useless to the organism. Instead, the electron passes through a chain of protein complexes (PSII → cytochrome complex → PSI), losing energy at each step in controlled amounts that drive useful work: pumping protons, generating ATP, producing NADPH.

This staged extraction principle appears across engineering: turbine stages in jet engines, voltage step-down transformers, and multi-stage rocket separation all extract or convert energy incrementally for the same reason. The biological version evolved because organisms that wasted less energy at each conversion step had a survival advantage. For system designers, the implication is clear: when processing any high-energy input (information, capital, raw materials), a multi-stage pipeline with controlled extraction at each stage will capture more total value than a single dramatic conversion.
