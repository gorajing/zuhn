Laser tattoo removal is not a single-mechanism process. The laser shatters ink particles into sub-micron fragments, but those fragments don't disappear — they must be cleared by the immune system. Macrophages engulf the now-small-enough fragments and transport them through the lymphatic system to the liver for processing. This is why removal takes multiple sessions spaced weeks apart: each session fragments more particles, and the body needs time between sessions to clear the debris.

This two-phase pattern — where one system reduces a problem to a scale where a different system can handle it — appears across many domains. In engineering, coarse grinding followed by fine polishing. In software, compilers do macro-optimization, then runtimes handle micro-optimization. In business strategy, disruptive innovation breaks an incumbent's market position, then execution captures the resulting opportunity. The general principle: when a problem spans multiple scales, effective solutions often chain specialized mechanisms rather than seeking a single universal one.
