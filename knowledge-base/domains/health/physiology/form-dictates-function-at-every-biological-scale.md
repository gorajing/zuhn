The principle of complementarity of structure and function states that a biological structure's form determines its capabilities. Heart valves are shaped to prevent backflow. Bones are rigid to provide structural support. Red blood cells are tiny and disc-shaped for efficient oxygen transport through capillaries. This principle holds at every level of organization — from molecular shape determining enzyme binding, to tissue architecture determining organ function.

This isn't just a biology lesson — it's a powerful cross-domain heuristic. In engineering, software architecture, and organizational design, form constrains function in exactly the same way. When you encounter a system that isn't performing well, examining its structural constraints often reveals the root cause faster than analyzing its behavioral outputs. The inverse is equally useful: if you want a specific function, design the structure that makes that function inevitable rather than trying to force behavior from an incompatible form.
