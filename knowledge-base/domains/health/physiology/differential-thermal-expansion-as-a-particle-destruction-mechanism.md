Laser tattoo removal works through an elegant physics mechanism: pulses so short (trillionths of a second) that heat only penetrates the surface layer of an ink particle before the pulse ends. The heated outer shell expands while the cool inner core does not, creating enormous mechanical stress that shatters the particle into fragments small enough for macrophages to engulf.

This is differential thermal expansion exploited as a precision tool. The key insight is that the pulse duration must be shorter than the thermal relaxation time of the particle — the time it takes heat to conduct from surface to core. If the pulse were longer, the entire particle would heat uniformly and simply get hot without fracturing. The same physics principle applies broadly: ultra-short energy pulses can selectively destroy targets based on their thermal diffusion properties, a concept used in everything from kidney stone lithotripsy to semiconductor manufacturing.
