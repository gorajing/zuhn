The light-dependent reactions don't directly produce usable energy. Instead, they pump protons (H+ ions) into the thylakoid lumen — from water splitting and the cytochrome complex — creating a steep concentration gradient. This gradient is potential energy stored as chemical disequilibrium. When protons flow back through ATP synthase (the only exit), the enzyme harvests that flow to attach inorganic phosphate to ADP, producing ATP.

This 'charge then discharge' pattern appears throughout biology: mitochondria use the same mechanism in cellular respiration, neurons maintain sodium/potassium gradients for signaling, and kidneys use concentration gradients for filtration. Understanding this pattern reveals that biological energy management is fundamentally about creating controlled imbalances — not about individual chemical reactions. The phospholipid bilayer's ability to maintain these gradients is what makes complex life possible.
