Kimmel's three-part argument: (1) baseline hazard rates during most of human evolution were extremely high — infectious disease, predation, injury meant very few individuals reached ages where aging mattered, so the gradient signal flowing back to the genome for longevity alleles was negligible. (2) Kin-selection creates an active regularization pressure against longevity: an aged individual consuming calories while contributing less fitness is a net drag on genome propagation compared to two young adults taking their place. (3) Optimization constraints — mutation rate caps step size, population size caps parallel variants, and most selection pressure was consumed by infectious-disease resilience.

The engineering implication is direct: in biology, the first question when trying to modify a phenotype is 'did evolution spend a lot of time optimizing this?' If yes, your job is insanely hard. If no, low-hanging fruit exist. Aging falls firmly in the 'evolution didn't try hard' category, which is why even primitive interventions (single-target small-molecule drugs) already produce massive health benefits.

This reframes longevity pessimism as unfounded: the fact that we aren't already extremely long-lived isn't evidence that the problem is hard — it's evidence that evolution never ran the experiment. Contrast with intelligence: the fact that it took evolution billions of years to reach human-level intelligence is itself weak evidence that intelligence is either hard or was heavily constrained — arguments which generalize directly to how to reason about AI scaling.
