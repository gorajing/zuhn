Church directly counsels his gene therapy companies to avoid rare diseases and focus on common/age-related conditions because rare recessives already have a cheaper, more effective solution: preconception genetic counseling. The Dor Yeshorim community program has run this since 1985 with clear success. At ~$100 per genome, screening is less than zero-cost after accounting for averted lifetime caregiving, medical, and opportunity costs — at least 10x ROI, which he calls 'a no brainer from a public health standpoint.'

The reason this underrated intervention stays underrated is behavioral, not economic. People don't think about reproduction when dating, don't think about rare genetic disease when pregnant (because 3% feels like 'not me'), and experience the trolley-problem asymmetry where inaction feels blameless even when it has the same expected outcome as a bad action. Historical parallels include resistance to seatbelts and smoking cessation — interventions whose base rate benefit took decades of cultural acceptance to unlock. The eugenics confusion also suppresses adoption, even though the eugenics problem was state coercion, not voluntary information provision.

Strategic implication for biotech capital allocation: gene therapy's sweet spot is age-related and common diseases where screening cannot help, and genetic counseling's sweet spot is rare recessive disease where it dominates on cost per DALY averted. Companies that conflate these are building expensive solutions where cheaper ones already exist.
