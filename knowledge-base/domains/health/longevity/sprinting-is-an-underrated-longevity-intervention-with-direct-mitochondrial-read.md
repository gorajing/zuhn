Johnson flags sprinting as 'one of the most underappreciated longevity therapies' — consistent with the literature showing that maximal-effort short-duration exercise drives mitochondrial biogenesis, improves VO2max, and is associated with lower all-cause mortality independently of lower-intensity cardio volume. The practical point is that most longevity protocols over-weight zone-2 cardio and resistance training and under-weight sprints because sprints are unpleasant and feel less 'scientific.'

The secondary point is the biomarker angle: sprint time (60m, 100m, or repeat-sprint ability) is a cheap, non-invasive readout of mitochondrial function that can detect meaningful changes from interventions like mitochondrial transplantation in a way that resting biomarkers cannot. For anyone running self-experiments on longevity interventions, adding a sprint time to the monthly panel is a zero-cost addition with high signal on mitochondrial-mediated effects.
