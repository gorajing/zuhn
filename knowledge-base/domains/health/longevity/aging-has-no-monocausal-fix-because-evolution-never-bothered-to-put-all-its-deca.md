Kimmel pushes back explicitly on longevity proponents who frame aging as having a single master switch. The evolutionary argument that explains why evolution didn't fix aging also implies aging is multi-causal: if aging were monocausal, even weak selection pressure (because occasionally a grandparent's extra years did help propagate genes) would have partially addressed it. The persistence of aging across all complex metazoans argues the causes are distributed across many molecular layers — epigenetic drift, proteostasis, metabolic dysfunction, clonal dynamics, extracellular matrix decay, etc.

The practical consequence is the opposite of the 'aging is solved when we find the one cure' framing. Realistic expectation: the first epigenetic reprogramming medicines (NewLimit and similar) will add a specific, bounded number of healthy years by addressing one mechanism — not reverse aging wholesale. A patient on a first-generation reprogramming therapy still experiences decline elsewhere, just delayed or offset in the domains the therapy touches. Future longevity will look like stacking: a hepatocyte therapy, a hematopoietic therapy, an elastin-polymerization therapy (Kimmel notes skin sag is likely extracellular, not cellular, so it needs a separate approach entirely).

This has major implications for investing, for patient expectations, for regulatory framing (each therapy is a separate approval with its own indication), and for how the field should market itself — promises of a 'cure for aging' set a bar the evolutionary biology itself predicts can't be cleared in a single shot.
