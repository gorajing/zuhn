The fastest path to human longevity drugs is repurposing existing medications with known safety profiles. Rapamycin, originally an antifungal from Easter Island soil bacteria, targets cellular metabolism by inducing autophagy — the process of consuming and recycling damaged proteins. In 2009, it became the first drug shown to slow aging in mammals, extending late-life mouse lifespan by 10-15%.

Metformin, a diabetes drug, also shows signs of slowing human aging. The proposed TAME trial would track whether metformin-takers get cancer, dementia, and heart disease later than controls. The key advantage of repurposed drugs is that decades of safety data already exist, compressing the typical drug development timeline. Steele estimates the first approved longevity drug could arrive within 5-10 years with adequate funding.
