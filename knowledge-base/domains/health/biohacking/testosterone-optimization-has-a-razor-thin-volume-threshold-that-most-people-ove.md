Research from Duncan French at the UFC Performance Center identified a remarkably narrow optimal protocol for testosterone release: 6 sets of 10 repetitions on compound movements (squats, deadlifts, chin-ups) with strict 2-minute rest intervals, performed at most twice per week. The striking finding is that increasing to 10 sets of 10 — just 4 more sets — not only eliminated the testosterone benefit but actually shifted the hormonal response toward catabolic cortisol pathways.

This demonstrates what Huberman calls the 'exquisite sensitivity' of the nerve-to-muscle-to-hormone connection. The practical lesson extends beyond testosterone: the relationship between training volume and hormonal response is non-linear with sharp inflection points. More volume doesn't mean more benefit — it can mean the opposite. This principle likely applies beyond testosterone to other exercise-mediated hormonal responses, suggesting that the popular 'more is better' approach to training volume may be systematically counterproductive for hormonal optimization. Sessions should be capped at 60 minutes; past 75 minutes of high intensity, testosterone drops and cortisol rises regardless of protocol.
