When muscles exhaust their oxygen supply during intense exercise, cells don't simply stop producing energy. They switch to anaerobic fermentation, which produces only 2 ATP per glucose molecule compared to 38 with full aerobic respiration — a 95% efficiency loss. The tradeoff is lactic acid buildup, which causes muscle soreness and fatigue.

This is a textbook example of graceful degradation: the system accepts massively reduced performance rather than catastrophic failure. In humans, fermentation produces lactic acid; in yeast, it produces ethanol (the basis of all alcoholic beverages). The existence of this fallback pathway suggests evolutionary pressure for 'always-on' energy production, even at terrible efficiency. This pattern maps directly to engineering: systems that degrade gracefully under resource constraints (reduced bandwidth, partial outages) outperform those that fail completely.
