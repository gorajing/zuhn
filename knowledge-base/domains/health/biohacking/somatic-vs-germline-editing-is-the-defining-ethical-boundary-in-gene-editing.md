The most important distinction in gene editing is between somatic editing (non-reproductive cells, changes die with the individual) and germline editing (embryos, changes are inherited by all future generations). Somatic editing has safety concerns but is comparable to other medical treatments. Germline editing is qualitatively different — embryos cannot consent, changes are permanent and heritable, and errors propagate through entire population lineages.

Doudna argues that for severe single-gene heritable diseases like Huntington's, germline editing could become ethically necessary once safety is proven — 'it would almost be unethical not to use it.' But the 2018 case of CRISPR-edited babies for HIV resistance was premature: the technology hadn't been tested in animals, safe alternatives existed, and the edits were unclean, creating chimeric children. The framework: the severity of suffering must be weighed against available alternatives, the precision of the technology, and the permanence of the change.
