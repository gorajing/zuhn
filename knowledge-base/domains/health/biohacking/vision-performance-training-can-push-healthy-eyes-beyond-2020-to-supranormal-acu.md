Most vision research focuses on restoring impaired vision to normal baselines. Stanford's new Vision Performance Center is exploring the opposite direction: can we train normal vision to become supranormal? Athletes already demonstrate faster visual reflexes and higher visual acuity than the general population, suggesting the visual system has untapped performance headroom.

One concrete protocol uses strobe goggles that black out vision for fractions of each second (1/30th, 2/30ths, 3/30ths), forcing athletes to perform complex visual-motor tasks (like catching a basketball) with only 70-90% of normal visual information. When the goggles are removed, performance measurably improves — the brain has learned to extract more signal from the same visual input. This has implications beyond athletics: could similar training improve night driving performance and reduce accidents? The principle mirrors overload training in physical fitness — train under constraint, perform better without it. This is still early-stage research, but the biological basis (neuroplasticity of the visual cortex) is well-established.
