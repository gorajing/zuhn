The common belief that lactic acid causes muscle burn and fatigue is scientifically backwards. Humans don't even produce lactic acid — they produce lactate, which serves three beneficial functions: (1) it buffers acidity in oxygen-deprived muscle, actually reducing the burn sensation; (2) it acts as an alternative fuel source allowing continued muscular contraction without oxygen; and (3) it functions as a hormonal signal that travels to the brain, heart, and liver to improve their function.

This reframing has practical implications: the burn during exercise is a signal that lactate is arriving to help, not a warning to stop. Huberman recommends dedicating approximately 10% of workout time to burn-inducing intensity specifically to trigger lactate's hormonal signaling benefits for brain health. The mechanism works through lactate improving astrocyte function in the brain, which supports debris clearance and synapse formation. Deep breathing during the burn is critical — oxygen is required for lactate to fulfill its buffer and hormonal roles.
