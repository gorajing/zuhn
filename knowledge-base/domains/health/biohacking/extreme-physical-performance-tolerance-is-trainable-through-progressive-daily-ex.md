Major Michelle Curran (Mace) revealed that G-tolerance is not an innate gift but an acquired adaptation. The Thunderbirds train for 4 months over winter, flying twice a day, progressively building their bodies' ability to sustain 9G turns while maintaining cognitive function — talking on radio, hitting precise parameters, flying 200 feet above ground at nearly 90 degrees of bank. Critically, this tolerance decays: if Mace hasn't flown for a few weeks, pulling G's becomes noticeably harder.

This maps directly to the progressive overload principle in any performance domain. The key details are: (1) twice-daily exposure, not just once, (2) months of sustained practice, not weeks, (3) the adaptation is perishable and requires maintenance. This challenges the notion that some physical feats require innate ability — Mace described 9G as 'substantially worse feeling' than 7G but achievable through training, while Destin with no progressive buildup couldn't sustain 7.2G. The gap between 'can survive it' and 'can perform complex tasks during it' is the training delta.
