De-extinction headlines focus on woolly mammoths and direwolves, but the most transformative application of the same technology is genetic rescue of living species. The black-footed ferret population crashed so severely that every living ferret is a first cousin — dangerously low genetic diversity. Scientists are using gene editing to introduce diversity from 100-year-old museum specimens back into the living population.

Even more striking: the northern quoll in Australia faces extinction because a single letter of genetic code makes them fatally vulnerable to invasive cane toads. Scientists are working to edit that one letter, making the entire species resistant. Similar approaches could make coral reefs heat-resistant or key species disease-resistant. As one scientist put it: 'De-extinction science is conservation science and we absolutely have to use it.' The rebranding insight: the most valuable technology often gets obscured by its most dramatic application.
