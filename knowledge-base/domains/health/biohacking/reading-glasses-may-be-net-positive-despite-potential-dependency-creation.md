There's a common belief that wearing reading glasses creates dependency and weakens the eye muscles, leading people to avoid or delay using readers. Dr. Goldberg identifies this as a false economy: while there's plausible evidence that exercising the lens-focusing muscles by avoiding readers could marginally slow presbyopia progression, this comes at the cost of feeding blurry information to the retina and brain.

The retina and visual cortex benefit from receiving the sharpest possible signal. Chronically undercorrecting vision to 'exercise' the eyes may preserve some lens flexibility while degrading overall visual processing quality. The practical recommendation is counterintuitive: just wear the readers, enjoy your best vision, and accept that your prescription will gradually increase from +0.5 to about +2.5/+3.0 by your mid-60s regardless. This is a broader pattern in health optimization — sometimes the intervention that feels like a crutch is actually the net-positive choice when you account for the full system (lens + retina + brain), not just the local component.
