Berg reframes microneedling using a soil analogy: dense, compacted scar tissue around follicles prevents oxygen, nutrients, and signaling molecules from reaching the follicle environment. Microneedling physically disrupts this barrier, allowing the follicle to receive the inputs it needs to restart hair production and reactivate dormant stem cells. This is why it pairs naturally with topical treatments — the needling creates channels for active ingredients to penetrate. This framing also explains why massage is less effective than microneedling: massage improves circulation but doesn't disrupt the scar tissue barrier. The mechanism predicts microneedling will fail in fully cicatricial (scarred) alopecia where follicles are destroyed, and succeed in early-stage miniaturized but living follicles.
