The blue-light-blocking glasses industry exploded during the pandemic as screen time surged, but Dr. Goldberg finds no evidence that blocking blue light provides meaningful eye health benefits. The critical distinction is between UV light (genuinely harmful — accelerates cataract formation, damages the retina and ocular surface) and blue light (adjacent on the spectrum but without demonstrated eye damage at normal exposure levels).

The good news: UV blocking is already standard in virtually all modern plastic eyeglass lenses — consumers don't need to pay extra for it. Blue blocking, however, may actually be counterproductive by interfering with circadian rhythm entrainment, which depends on blue-wavelength light reaching the retina during daytime hours. This is a pattern worth recognizing: consumer health products often exploit proximity to a real risk (UV damage) to sell protection against a non-risk (blue light), creating a market from misattributed anxiety. The practical takeaway is to ensure UV protection (standard in glasses, available in sunglasses and brimmed hats) and skip the blue blockers.
