The Krebs Cycle's primary output is not ATP directly — it's the charging of electron carrier molecules NAD+ and FAD into their energy-rich forms NADH and FADH2. Each glucose molecule produces 10 NADH (worth ~3 ATP each) and 2 FADH2 (worth ~2 ATP each) that feed the electron transport chain. These carriers are derived from B vitamins (niacin and riboflavin), which explains their presence in energy supplements.

This reveals that NAD+ availability is a potential rate-limiting factor for energy production. If NAD+ is depleted, the Krebs Cycle cannot pass energy to the electron transport chain, regardless of glucose availability. This is why NAD+ precursor supplementation (NMN, NR) has gained attention in longevity research — declining NAD+ levels with age may throttle the entire energy production pipeline at the carrier level rather than the fuel level.
