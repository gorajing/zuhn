Neuralink's expansion from 1 to 21 human trial participants revealed that real-world surgical data from the first patient (Noland) was more valuable than years of animal testing for improving outcomes. Signal quality improved in 18 of 20 subsequent cases, demonstrating that brain-computer interfaces benefit enormously from iterative clinical feedback loops.

This mirrors a broader pattern in frontier technology: lab conditions can only approximate reality so far, and the fastest path to reliability is controlled real-world deployment with tight feedback cycles. Neuralink's plan to triple electrode count to 3,000 and their new VOICE trial targeting 140 words-per-minute speech restoration suggest the clinical learning rate is accelerating.
