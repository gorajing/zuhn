Cardiac output (heart rate × stroke volume) stays constant at rest regardless of fitness level — what changes is the ratio. As endurance training strengthens the heart, stroke volume increases (more blood per pump), so the heart compensates by beating less frequently. Galpin considers anyone over 70 bpm at rest to be insufficiently physically fit, regardless of normative ranges that extend to 80+ bpm.

Importantly, maximum heart rate does not increase with fitness (it's not a useful proxy for conditioning), and cardiac output only adjusts per acute energetic demands. The practical implication is that sub-60 bpm resting heart rate is achievable through any consistent training modality and serves as a reliable, free, daily-measurable indicator of cardiovascular health. The adaptation also means that at any given submaximal workload, a trained person operates at a lower heart rate — the definition of improved efficiency.
