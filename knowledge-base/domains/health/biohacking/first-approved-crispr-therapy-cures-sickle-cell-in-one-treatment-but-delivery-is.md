Victoria Gray became the first US patient to receive a CRISPR-based sickle cell treatment. The therapy activates fetal hemoglobin production, suppressing the disease-causing mutation. Patients who have received it have not had another sickle cell crisis since treatment — a life-transforming result.

However, the current therapy requires extracting blood, editing cells in a lab, and transplanting them back via bone marrow procedure. This makes it expensive and requires hospitalization, putting it out of reach for most of the global population affected by sickle cell. The next frontier is in-body delivery — getting CRISPR molecules to specific cell types without extraction. Cells have surface 'zip code' molecules that could guide delivery, inspired by how viruses naturally target specific cells.
