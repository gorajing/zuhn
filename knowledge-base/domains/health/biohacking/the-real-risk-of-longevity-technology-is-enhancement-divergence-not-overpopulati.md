The standard fears about longevity — resource depletion, overpopulation, calcified power structures — miss what Vitalik considers the deeper risk: enhancement divergence. If people can choose different cognitive, physical, or perceptual enhancements, the result isn't a homogeneously upgraded humanity but a diverging population of incompatible sub-types, each optimized for different environments or value systems.

This is structurally different from inequality in wealth or opportunity. Wealth differences create hierarchy but leave a shared human substrate. Enhancement divergence could dissolve that substrate. Two enhanced humans might not just disagree — they might literally perceive and reason about the world in ways that are mutually incomprehensible. Vitalik notes that this could enable new forms of complementarity (specialists who think completely differently working together) but is more worried about coordination failure.

The policy implication is that access equity (making enhancements available to all) is necessary but not sufficient. Even universal access doesn't prevent divergence if people make different choices. This suggests the deeper design challenge is not distribution but interoperability — maintaining enough shared infrastructure that divergent humans can still coordinate.
