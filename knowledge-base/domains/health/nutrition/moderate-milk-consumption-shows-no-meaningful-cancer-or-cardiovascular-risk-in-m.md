While individual older studies occasionally found associations between milk and breast, colon, or prostate cancer, comprehensive meta-analyses have failed to replicate these findings at moderate intake levels. The only consistent signal is a possible increased prostate cancer risk above 1.25 liters per day — a quantity far beyond typical consumption. Similarly, no impact on heart disease, stroke, or total mortality has been found. The calcium in milk may even be protective against colon cancer, though this effect could come from calcium in general rather than milk specifically.

This pattern — scary individual studies contradicted by larger meta-analyses — is a recurring theme in nutrition science and worth internalizing as a heuristic for evaluating food health claims.
