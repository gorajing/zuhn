A network meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials found that substituting non-nutritive sweeteners (NNS) for sugar-sweetened beverages improved multiple health markers including adiposity and HbA1c. Surprisingly, NNS beverages slightly outperformed even water substitution for reducing adiposity, suggesting a mild appetite-suppressant effect from sweet taste without calories. The epidemiological data associating artificial sweeteners with negative outcomes is confounded by lifestyle factors (people who drink diet soda may have other unhealthy habits).

Norton emphasizes the hierarchy of evidence: while individual studies on gut microbiome effects or insulin conditioning responses from artificial sweeteners generate headlines, the aggregate RCT evidence shows clear net benefit when NNS replaces sugar. The one study (Dana Small's lab) suggesting sweetener-glucose pairing could condition insulin responses had significant design limitations. For practical purposes, artificial sweeteners are a legitimate tool for people transitioning away from sugar-sweetened beverages, and the fear around them is disproportionate to the evidence. Norton notes anecdotal reports of people losing 50-100+ pounds simply by switching from regular to diet soda.
