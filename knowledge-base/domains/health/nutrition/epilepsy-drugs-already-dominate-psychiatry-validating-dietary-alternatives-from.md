Palmer makes a compelling institutional-logic argument: if psychiatry already borrows freely from epilepsy pharmacology — Depakote, Tegretol, Lamictal, Topamax, gabapentin, Valium, Klonopin, Xanax are all anticonvulsants repurposed for psychiatric use in tens of millions of patients, often off-label without rigorous psychiatric RCTs — then using the ketogenic diet, which has a century of evidence as an epilepsy treatment with 85% efficacy rates, is merely extending an existing pattern.

The ketogenic diet was developed in 1921 specifically to mimic fasting's anti-seizure effects as a sustainable long-term treatment. Early results showed 50% seizure freedom and another 35% with >50% seizure reduction. Even today, for treatment-resistant epilepsy (30% of patients), it achieves ~33% seizure freedom. This argument reframes the ketogenic diet not as alternative medicine but as mainstream neurology being applied to psychiatry — exactly what the field already does with drugs.
