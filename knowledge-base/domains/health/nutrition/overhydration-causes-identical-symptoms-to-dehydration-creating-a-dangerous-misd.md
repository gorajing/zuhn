Hydration follows a hormetic curve: both under- and over-hydration degrade performance. As little as 2% bodyweight loss from dehydration reduces shooting accuracy and increases perceived exercise difficulty. But excessive water intake dilutes blood sodium concentration (hyponatremia), producing identical symptoms — brain fog, confusion, GI distress, irritability — which people misattribute to dehydration and compensate by drinking even more water.

Galpin describes a CEO case study: a 170lb woman drinking 250-260oz of water daily had chronic brain fog, sleep disruption, and was consuming 8 cups of coffee to compensate. Simply reducing her water to ~180oz (roughly 1oz per pound of body weight) resolved her sleep, focus, and cognitive issues within 2 days. The diagnostic framework is simple: if you're waking to urinate more than twice per night with large volumes of clear urine, you're likely overhydrated. The baseline target is half an ounce per pound of body weight for daily intake, with the Galpin equation (bodyweight in lbs ÷ 30 = ounces every 15-20 minutes) for during exercise.
