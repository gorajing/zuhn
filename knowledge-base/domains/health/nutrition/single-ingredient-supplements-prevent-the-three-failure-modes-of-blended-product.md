Galpin identifies three distinct failure modes that make blended supplements inferior to single-ingredient formulations. First, interaction blindness: if a blend causes side effects or doesn't work, you have no idea which of 10-20 ingredients is responsible — you might permanently discard a supplement that actually works for you because it was paired with something that didn't. Second, economic perversion: manufacturers systematically under-dose the most expensive active ingredients in blends while padding with cheap fillers like caffeine, because consumers can't verify individual ingredient quantities.

Third and most alarming, label accuracy degrades dramatically in multi-ingredient products. Studies pulling supplements off shelves have found melatonin products containing 500-1000x the labeled concentration — explaining why some people react well to melatonin while others are devastated by it. Third-party testing certification partially mitigates this, but single-ingredient formulations inherently have better label accuracy. The practical implication: build your supplement protocol from individual ingredients you can dose, adjust, and evaluate independently. This also enables the three-category framework (fuel/stimulant/fatigue blocker) to function properly.
