Milk contains hormones, but at concentrations so low that you'd need to drink roughly 5,000 liters to match the hormonal dose of a contraceptive pill — and even then, your digestive system would destroy most of them before absorption (which is why oral medications need protective coatings). Pesticide and antibiotic levels are regulated with thresholds set well below harmful levels, and milk exceeding these limits is pulled from shelves.

This is a useful case study in how fear-based reasoning ignores dose-response relationships. The mere presence of a substance (hormones, antibiotics) triggers alarm, but the relevant question is always concentration relative to biological effect thresholds. This principle applies broadly to food safety and chemical exposure debates.
