There's a metabolic inversion between exercise types and their fuel sources. During high-intensity exercise (HIIT, weights, sprints), the body primarily burns glycogen. But for up to 24 hours after, post-exercise oxygen consumption rises and the body preferentially oxidizes fat over glycogen. The reverse holds for moderate-intensity cardio: it burns more fat during activity but more glycogen afterward. When you sum total fat burned (during + after), high-intensity training followed by moderate activity or just normal daily life produces superior fat oxidation. The optimal protocol Huberman derives: train at high intensity for 20-60 minutes (depleting glycogen), then transition to moderate/low-intensity activity or just normal daily movement. Training fasted further accelerates the switchover point — normally at 90 minutes of moderate exercise, fasting plus preceding high-intensity work brings this forward significantly. This explains why a friend's strategy of 'breathe hard for 30 minutes then go about your day' is surprisingly effective.
