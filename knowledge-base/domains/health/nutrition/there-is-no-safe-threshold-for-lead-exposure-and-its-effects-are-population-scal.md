Lead's danger mechanism is uniquely insidious: it mimics calcium in the body, so there's no efficient elimination pathway, and it accumulates in bones where it can continue poisoning for years. It breaks down myelin sheaths around axons and prevents neurotransmitter release, causing permanent cognitive and behavioral damage — especially in children.

The population-scale numbers are staggering. A 2022 study found that over 170 million Americans (more than half the population) were exposed to high lead levels in early childhood, with those born 1951-1980 disproportionately affected. The aggregate cognitive loss: 824 million IQ points. 20th-century Americans had 1,000 times more lead in their bones than pre-industrial mummies. The CDC's acceptable blood lead level dropped from 60 to 3.5 micrograms per deciliter as evidence accumulated, and current consensus is there is no safe level.

The crime correlation adds another dimension: preschool blood lead levels predict crime rates 20 years later, a pattern replicated across the US, Britain, Canada, and Australia. A study of 340 teenagers found those arrested were 4x more likely to have elevated bone lead. Lead may have shaped the 1970s-1990s crime wave and its subsequent decline. Current estimates still attribute 500,000-900,000 deaths per year to lead globally.
