The anti-seed-oil narrative rests on two pillars: epidemiology showing associations between seed oil consumption and negative health outcomes, and a plausible mechanism (polyunsaturated double bonds oxidizing under heat causing inflammation). However, when you control for confounding variables and look at human RCTs directly comparing polyunsaturated to saturated fat substitution, the effects on inflammation are neutral and effects on cardiovascular markers are neutral-to-positive.

Norton introduces the concept of 'overall energy toxicity' — the idea that overconsumption of total calories is the dominant health risk, dwarfing the effects of any individual macronutrient or fat type. Seed oils have indeed contributed to the obesity epidemic, but only because people added more oil (calories) to their diets over the last 20-30 years, not because of any unique toxicity of the oils themselves. The practical recommendation: consume mostly mono- and polyunsaturated fats, limit saturated fat to 7-10% of daily calories, but don't obsess over eliminating any specific fat source — focus on total energy balance instead.
