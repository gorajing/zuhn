The conventional medical model is fundamentally designed for acute care and symptom management rather than chronic disease prevention or reversal. Medical students receive minimal nutrition education (often less than 20 hours total) while spending years learning pharmaceutical interventions. This creates physicians who instinctively reach for prescriptions rather than dietary protocols.

The naturopathic principle -- remove impediments and the body will heal -- produces results that pharmaceutical approaches cannot for conditions like autoimmune disease, metabolic syndrome, gut disorders, and hormonal imbalances. The emerging approach of functional medicine attempts to bridge this gap, but systemic change in medical education is needed. Individual agency through dietary experimentation remains the most accessible path to root-cause health improvement.
