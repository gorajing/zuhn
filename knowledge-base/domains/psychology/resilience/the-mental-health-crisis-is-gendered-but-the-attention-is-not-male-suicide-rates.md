Reeves presents stark data: men account for 80% of US suicides and 70% of drug poisoning deaths. Since 2010, suicide rates among young men under 30 have risen by almost a third, representing a shift from the pre-2010 pattern where middle-aged men were the primary group affected. Drug poisoning deaths among men increased sixfold since 2001, with the excess deaths equaling exactly the 400,000 US deaths in World War II.

Despite these numbers, public attention disproportionately focuses on teen girls' mental health. The share of male therapists has more than halved — only 1 in 5 psychologists and social workers are men — making it harder for men who already resist seeking help to find someone they can talk to. Reeves argues this attention asymmetry is not a competition but a dangerous blind spot: solving it requires recognizing the crisis is gendered in both directions.
