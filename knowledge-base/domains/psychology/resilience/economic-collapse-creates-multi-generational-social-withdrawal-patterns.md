The documentary traces a direct line from Japan's economic collapse to the hikikomori phenomenon: young people who faced immense cultural pressure to succeed but encountered no opportunities withdrew from society entirely. Decades later, 63,000+ middle-aged and elderly Japanese remain shut-ins, many still living with aging parents, having never re-engaged with society.

This challenges the assumption that economic recoveries heal social damage. The psychological impact of entering adulthood during a severe crisis — especially in cultures with strong achievement expectations — can produce permanent behavioral changes that outlast the economic conditions that caused them. The interaction between cultural pressure to succeed and economic inability to do so creates a trap: the shame of failure prevents the re-engagement needed for recovery. Japan's experience suggests that economic policy must account for these social cascades, not just GDP recovery.
