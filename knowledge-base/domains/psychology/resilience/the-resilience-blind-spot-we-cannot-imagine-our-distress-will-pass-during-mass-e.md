Bonanno coined the term 'resilience blind spot' to describe a phenomenon he observed after 9/11, Hurricane Sandy, the Japan tsunami, and COVID-19. During large-scale disasters, shared anxiety is amplified by media coverage, social conversations, and the visible distress of others. This creates social contagion where everyone's upset feeds everyone else's upset.

The blind spot occurs because of affective forecasting bias (identified by Dan Gilbert): when experiencing strong emotion, humans cannot imagine it's ephemeral. We feel like the distress will last forever. This is compounded during mass events by the shared nature of the experience — it seems impossible that something affecting everyone this deeply could be temporary. Yet for most people (roughly two-thirds), it is temporary. The blind spot leads to overcalibrated policy responses, excessive mental health interventions, and a cultural narrative that disaster inevitably produces widespread lasting trauma — when the data shows the opposite.
