The instructor's core message: 'Take one decision after another, slowly thinking it through, that gets you closer and closer to the light.' This reframes panic not as fear (everyone was afraid) but as the breakdown of sequential processing. The marines who failed didn't fail because they were more scared — they failed because they tried to do everything at once (unbuckle AND remove flak jacket AND find exit), losing their reference point in the process.

The antidote is mechanical sequencing: grab seat, find exit, unbuckle, pull out. Each step fully completed before the next begins. This maps to any overwhelming situation — startup crises, medical emergencies, emotional breakdowns. The feeling of being 'in over your head' is almost always the feeling of trying to solve a compound problem simultaneously rather than sequentially. Forcing yourself to identify and complete just the next single step is not merely calming — it is the actual mechanism of survival. The calm follows the sequential action, not the other way around.
