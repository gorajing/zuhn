Perfect self-calibration sounds optimal in theory but fails in practice because ambitious projects face compounding headwinds: other people's skepticism, your own harsh judgment of early results, and the inherent lameness of anything at 20% completion. Slight overconfidence acts as a counterweight to these forces, not as delusion but as error correction.

This explains why the most successful people across fields tend to be slightly overconfident rather than perfectly calibrated. The asymmetry matters: underconfidence kills projects that would have succeeded, while slight overconfidence merely extends effort on some projects that won't. Given power-law distributions in outcomes, the cost of killing a potential winner far exceeds the cost of extra effort on losers.
