Most people assume the optimal level of confidence is perfectly calibrated accuracy. Graham argues this ignores that creators face asymmetric headwinds: external skeptics, internal self-doubt, and the genuinely unimpressive appearance of early-stage ambitious work. Slight overconfidence acts as a counterweight to these forces, producing more accurate net behavior than perfect calibration would.

This reframes overconfidence not as a character flaw but as a functional adaptation. The people who accomplish the most aren't necessarily better at estimating their abilities — they're better at sustaining effort through the phase where evidence seems to confirm their work is mediocre. The implication is that if you notice you're systematically abandoning projects early, the fix may not be better judgment but deliberately looser self-evaluation standards during the initial phase.
