Hormozi observes that the most destructive aspect of failure isn't the failure itself but the surprise and emotional devastation that comes from not expecting it. When people frame success as the default state and failure as an anomaly, each setback triggers a disproportionate emotional response.

Reframing failure and success as predictable cycles — where downturns are expected features, not bugs — builds emotional resilience that preserves decision-making quality during hard times. This connects to stoic philosophy and the psychological concept of 'stress inoculation': exposure to expected adversity builds capacity, while unexpected adversity depletes it.
