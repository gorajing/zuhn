Paul Graham identifies fear of making something lame as the single largest obstacle to doing great work. This fear is not irrational—early versions of ambitious projects genuinely look unimpressive, even to their creators. But the fear is disproportionate to the reality: a lame-looking early version of an ambitious project is far more valuable than it appears, because it sits on a trajectory toward something great.

The critical insight is that most people never even reach the stage of making something embarrassing. They self-censor before starting. This means the binding constraint on great work isn't talent or knowledge but psychological tolerance for the gap between current output and final vision. Overcoming this requires specific tactics: overestimating the importance of the work, slight overconfidence, curiosity-driven framing, and surrounding yourself with peers who can distinguish ugly ducklings from baby swans.
