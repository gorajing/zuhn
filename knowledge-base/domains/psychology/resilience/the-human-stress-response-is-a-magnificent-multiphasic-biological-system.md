Bonanno describes the stress response with genuine admiration for its sophistication. When threat is detected, sensory signals pass through the midbrain to the amygdala to the hypothalamus, triggering a cascade: blood to extremities, increased glucose, dilated pupils, heavier breathing, and a shift from reflective to automatic processing. Non-essential functions go offline.

If the threat persists, cortisol release escalates through epigenetic changes in brain structures, ramping up the response further. Crucially, the system has a built-in brake: some cells have high cortisol affinity (respond immediately and powerfully) while others have low affinity (respond later, initiating shutdown). This creates a graceful arc lasting up to two hours. Long-term narrative memory is blocked during this period because it is not needed — the brain focuses only on survival-relevant fragments.
