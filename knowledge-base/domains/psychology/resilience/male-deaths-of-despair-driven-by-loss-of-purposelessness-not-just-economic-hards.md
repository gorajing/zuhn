Deaths of despair from suicide, overdose, and alcohol are three times higher among men than women, with suicide rates rising sharply among middle-aged and younger men. Reeves cites Fiona Chan's research finding that the most common self-descriptions men used before suicide attempts were 'worthless' and 'useless'—language of purposelessness rather than poverty.

The opioid epidemic illustrates the compounding effect: death rates are higher partly because male users are isolated and alone. This connects loss of family role, loss of labor market status, substance use, and social withdrawal into a single reinforcing pattern. The insight is that economic interventions alone won't solve this—men need a culturally recognized sense of being needed, which requires redefining male purpose beyond the obsolete breadwinner identity.
