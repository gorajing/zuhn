Great projects universally pass through a stage where they look unimpressive even to their creators. The psychological barrier isn't irrational — early work genuinely does look bad — but most people either never start or quit during this phase rather than pushing through to the quality that lies beyond it. This means the primary bottleneck for ambitious work is emotional tolerance for producing bad output, not technical ability. The practical implication is that any technique that temporarily suppresses your quality judgment (overestimating importance, treating work as a sketch, framing it as an experiment) directly increases your capacity for doing great work.

This reframes creative blocks as judgment-calibration problems rather than talent problems. You don't need to become more skilled to start; you need to become better at recognizing that early-stage ugliness is a necessary phase, not a signal to quit.
