Graham's Octopart example illustrates a powerful psychological mechanism: after appearing in Newsweek labeled as future billionaires, the founders transformed from 'lighthearted, cheery guys' to 'grimly determined.' Graham estimates that if every YC startup had such public exposure, the success rate would hit 90%. This suggests the binding constraint on startup success isn't intelligence, market selection, or even effort — it's the psychological option to quit.

This is essentially a commitment device operating through loss aversion. The founders aren't working harder because of what they might gain — they're working harder because of what they'd lose (social standing, identity). This maps to broader research on pre-commitment as a decision-making tool: burning ships, making public pledges, and eliminating escape routes all exploit the asymmetry between loss aversion and gain motivation.
