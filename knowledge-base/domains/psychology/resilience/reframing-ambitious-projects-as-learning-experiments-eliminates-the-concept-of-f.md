The experiment framing is commonly dismissed as motivational fluff, but Graham and Lisa Randall argue it's epistemically sound. When a problem is sharply defined — proving a theorem, testing a structural member under stress — failure directly produces knowledge. The theorem is false, the member can't bear that load. This isn't consolation; it's real information that narrows the search space for future work.

The practical power of this reframe is that it changes the expected-value calculation for starting ambitious projects. If success yields a great outcome and failure yields genuine learning, the downside is bounded and positive. This makes it rational to attempt projects with low probability of success but high information value — exactly the kind of risky early-stage work that most people avoid because they frame failure as pure loss.
