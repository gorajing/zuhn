Bonanno's decades of research reveals a finding that was initially met with indifference and pushback from the clinical community: approximately two-thirds of people exposed to potentially traumatic events (violence, life-threatening situations, sexual violation) show a resilient trajectory — they continue functioning relatively normally afterward. They may get upset in the moment, but they maintain the capacity to concentrate, work, and maintain intimate relationships.

The remaining third splits into three patterns: recovery (struggling significantly but gradually returning to baseline over months to a year), delayed distress (initially struggling then getting progressively worse), and chronic distress (deeply traumatized with long-lasting symptoms, occurring in fewer than 10% of cases). This framework contradicts three pervasive misconceptions: that anything difficult is traumatic, that trauma inevitably causes lasting damage, and that we carry hidden traumas that continue harming us. The clinical community was so resistant to these findings that Bonanno's colleagues initially wouldn't let him use the word 'resilience' in publications.
