Perfectly calibrated self-assessment sounds optimal in theory, but in practice ambitious projects face skepticism from two directions simultaneously: other people's dismissiveness and your own harsh judgment of early results. Graham observes that across many fields, the most successful people are slightly overconfident — not delusionally so, but enough to keep moving through the ugly early phase.

This works because overconfidence is not operating in isolation. It's compensating for systematic undervaluation of early work. If you overestimate both the importance of your project and your ability to execute it, those errors can cancel out the equally real errors of judging a 20%-complete ambitious project as worthless. The net expected-value estimate ends up roughly correct despite both components being wrong.
