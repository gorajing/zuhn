When the last plane leaves McMurdo in February, 45 people at South Pole Station are completely cut off until November—no medical evacuation, no resupply, no communication-enabled rescue. Fuel would freeze within minutes if a plane attempted landing at -67°F winter temperatures. This hard cutoff creates a qualitatively different planning regime than environments where help is merely expensive or slow. Every calorie, medical supply, and spare part must be pre-positioned. Personnel selection must account for the impossibility of evacuation. The psychological insight is that partial connectivity creates an illusion of safety that degrades preparation quality. When you know rescue is impossible, you prepare differently—and often better—than when you believe help is 'probably' available.
