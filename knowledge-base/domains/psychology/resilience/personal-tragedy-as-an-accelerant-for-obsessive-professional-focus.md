Morgan's wife Amelia died of tuberculosis just four months after their wedding, leaving him a widower at 24. According to historical accounts, he never fully recovered emotionally, but the loss triggered an obsessive immersion in his work that persisted for the rest of his life. This pattern of channeling grief into professional intensity appears repeatedly in the biographies of transformational figures.

The insight cuts both ways. On one hand, profound loss can strip away the distractions and half-commitments that prevent most people from achieving exceptional outcomes. On the other, Morgan's later nervous breakdowns and struggle with public scrutiny suggest that unprocessed grief doesn't disappear; it compounds. For founders and ambitious individuals, the lesson isn't to seek tragedy but to recognize that when it inevitably arrives, the impulse to bury yourself in work can be productive in the short term but requires eventual reckoning.
