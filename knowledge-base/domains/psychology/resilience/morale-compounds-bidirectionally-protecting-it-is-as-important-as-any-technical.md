Graham frames morale not as a psychological nicety but as the physical and emotional basis of all ambitious work. It compounds via work: high morale produces better output, which further raises morale. But the cycle runs in reverse too — poor work demoralizes you, making it harder to produce good work.

This has several tactical implications. First, when stuck, switch to easier work to restart the positive cycle rather than grinding on the hard problem while demoralized. Second, the biggest mistake ambitious people make is allowing setbacks to burst their morale all at once — you can inoculate against this by explicitly treating setbacks as part of the process. Third, morale is ultimately physical: exercise, sleep, and diet directly affect your capacity for great work. The metaphor Graham uses is depth-first search: 'Never abandon the root node' — never let setbacks panic you into backtracking more than necessary.
