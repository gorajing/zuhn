Great work almost always begins as something embarrassing. Paul Graham argues this isn't a bug but a structural feature: the more ambitious the project, the wider the gap between its early state and its potential. Most people never start because they can't tolerate this gap, and most who start quit during it.

The practical implication is that feeling embarrassed by your early output is actually a positive signal — it means your taste exceeds your current execution, which is the prerequisite for improvement. The danger is treating that discomfort as evidence you should stop rather than evidence you're in the necessary ugly-duckling phase of something potentially great.
