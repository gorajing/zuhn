Gupta argues that the human internal environment responds to inputs like a tuning fork — if exposed to truth on a regular basis, something inside internalizes it and it becomes the new norm. The brain literally rewires itself based on exposure without any deliberate work, psychotherapy, or medication required. Naval extends this to wealth creation: hang around Silicon Valley or entrepreneurs long enough and you'll be in the tech business.

The key nuance is titration — any environment (including beneficial ones like Silicon Valley) contains both benefits and detriments. The practice is to surgically titrate out what's unwanted and titrate in what's desired, fine-tuning like a buffet. This is actionable and concrete: curate your information diet, social circle, and physical environment with the same rigor you'd apply to a business strategy. The mechanism is passive absorption rather than active effort, which means the quality of inputs matters more than the intensity of practice.
