Paul Graham observes that the biggest filter separating people who do great work from those who don't is not ability but willingness to push through an ugly early phase. Every ambitious project—startups, research, art—goes through a stage where the output looks embarrassing even to its creator. Most people never start, and most who start quit here.

The practical implication is that feeling embarrassed by early work is not a signal to stop—it's a predictable stage in the process. Recognizing this phase as universal (not personal failure) changes the decision calculus. The question shifts from 'is this any good?' to 'is this improving at a rate that justifies continued effort?' This reframe alone can prevent premature abandonment of projects that would have succeeded with more runway.
