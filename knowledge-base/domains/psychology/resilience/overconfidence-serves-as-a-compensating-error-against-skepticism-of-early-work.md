People who are slightly overconfident about their abilities consistently outperform those with perfectly accurate self-assessment. This seems paradoxical until you account for the opposing errors it corrects: other people's skepticism and your own premature self-criticism. The overconfidence isn't globally beneficial—it's specifically useful because it counteracts the universal tendency to judge early work by finished-work standards. Similarly, overestimating the importance of your project compensates for underestimating the value of a 20%-complete version. The math works out: wrong confidence times wrong importance can yield correct expected value. This reframes overconfidence not as a bias to eliminate but as a calibration tool for the specific context of creative and ambitious work.
