Most motivation frameworks require positive expectations: persist because it will pay off, because you'll succeed, because things will improve. Beckett removes this requirement entirely. 'I can't go on. I'll go on.' acknowledges simultaneously that the situation is impossible AND that you'll continue anyway.

This is more psychologically honest than optimistic persistence: sometimes situations genuinely are bad, progress genuinely is unclear, and meaning genuinely is absent. The ability to continue functioning without positive expectations is a more robust form of resilience than hope-dependent persistence, because it works precisely when hope doesn't.
