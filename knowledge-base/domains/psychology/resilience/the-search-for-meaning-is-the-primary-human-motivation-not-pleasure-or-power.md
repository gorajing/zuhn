Frankl presents survey data showing that 78% of American students identified 'finding a meaning and purpose in life' as their main concern, while only 16% said 'making a lot of money.' He contrasts this with the European stereotype that Americans are just out to make money — the data tells a different story.

This connects to his logotherapy framework: if you don't recognize a person's will to meaning, you make them worse. You make them dull and frustrated. But if you presuppose that even in a so-called criminal, drug abuser, or juvenile delinquent there is a 'spark of search for meaning,' and you recognize and presuppose it, you elicit it from them. The therapeutic and leadership application is the same — treating someone as if they have a meaningful purpose activates that purpose. Ignoring or dismissing their need for meaning contributes to the very dysfunction you're trying to fix.
