Rubin observes that in the digital age, where any sound or instrument can be summoned instantly, infinite choices don't produce better music. He advocates deliberately imposing constraints — like deciding to paint using only green and red — because the limitation forces the creator to solve problems in unexpected ways. When anything is possible, nothing demands creative ingenuity; when options are restricted, every choice becomes more meaningful and inventive.

This principle extends far beyond music production. In software engineering, constrained architectures often produce more elegant solutions than unlimited flexibility. In writing, strict forms like sonnets or haiku force precision. The mechanism is that constraints shift creative energy from the paralysis of selection ('what should I choose from infinite options?') to the productivity of problem-solving ('how do I achieve my vision within these boundaries?'). Rubin's insight suggests that when facing creative blocks, the counterintuitive move is to add more constraints rather than seeking more freedom.
