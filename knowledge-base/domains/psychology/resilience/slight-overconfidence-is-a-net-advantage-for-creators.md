Perfectly calibrated self-assessment seems optimal in theory, but in practice creators face two headwinds: their own harsh judgment of unfinished work and other people's skepticism. Slight overconfidence counterbalances both forces, producing a net-accurate assessment of expected value even though the individual estimates (of ability and of project worth) are each wrong.

This reframes overconfidence not as a cognitive flaw to eliminate but as a useful compensating error. Hardy noted that good work is not done by humble men — exaggerating the importance of your subject and your role in it corrects for the systematic undervaluation of early-stage ambitious work. The practical implication is to cultivate slightly inflated confidence deliberately when starting new projects, rather than trying to be perfectly objective.
