James Lovell's explanation of why the crew never panicked is striking in its rationality: 'We could have bounced off the walls for about 10 minutes, and when we finished we'd be back where we started from.' This reframes composure under pressure not as an emotional achievement but as a time-allocation decision. Panic is a pure waste function—it consumes minutes without generating information, options, or solutions.

This framing is more actionable than the typical 'stay calm' advice because it gives a concrete reason: panic has zero ROI on time. For founders facing existential crises—failed launches, lost key customers, funding falling through—the practical version is: you can spend 10 minutes catastrophizing, but you'll still need to solve the same problem afterward. Skipping the panic phase isn't about suppressing emotion; it's about recognizing that the problem doesn't change while you're panicking, so you might as well start solving it immediately.
