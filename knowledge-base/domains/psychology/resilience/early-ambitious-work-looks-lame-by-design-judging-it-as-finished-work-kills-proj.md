Paul Graham observes that the biggest barrier to great work isn't lack of ability but the fear of producing something lame. Every ambitious project goes through a stage where it doesn't look impressive even to its creator. Most people never start, and of those who do, most quit during this ugly-duckling phase because they judge early work by the standards of finished work.

The key reframe is that early work is a special category requiring different evaluation criteria. A project that's 20% complete toward an ambitious goal is categorically different from a mediocre finished project, yet our instincts treat them the same. Recognizing this as a cognitive error—not a character flaw—makes it addressable through specific techniques like overestimating the goal's importance to compensate for undervaluing partial progress.
