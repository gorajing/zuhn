Both Harris and Goldberg identify a specific mechanism of social control: forcing people to lie about obvious facts (like who started the Ukraine war) isn't about the specific claim — it's about establishing that 'truth will not save you.' Once someone publicly affirms a falsehood, they become invested in maintaining it, making them more compliant with future demands.

Goldberg connects this to the Stalin-era dynamic where 'you never want to be the first person to stop clapping.' The mechanism works because each act of compliance raises the psychological cost of future dissent — you'd have to explain not just the current disagreement but why you went along with everything before. This creates what Goldberg calls a situation where 'silence is taken as approval and consent' and 'breaking that silence gets punished.' The insight applies beyond politics to any hierarchical environment where challenging authority carries escalating personal risk.
