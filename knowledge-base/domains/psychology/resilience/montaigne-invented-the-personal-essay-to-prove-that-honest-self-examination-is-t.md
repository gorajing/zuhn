Montaigne retired from public life at 38 and spent a decade in his tower library writing essays that became the foundation of modern non-fiction. His method was radical: instead of reasoning about human nature abstractly, he observed himself — his fears, contradictions, bodily functions, sexual thoughts, and daily inconsistencies — and reported them honestly. The results were more philosophically profound than centuries of abstract theorizing because they captured how humans actually think and behave, not how they should. His core principle: 'Once you stop caring about appearing consistent, wise, or impressive, genuine insight arrives.' This is the philosophical ancestor of both psychoanalysis and modern mindfulness — truth through honest observation, not intellectual construction.
