Malice draws a culinary analogy for comedy: it's easy to make bacon taste good, but making obscure, bitter ingredients palatable takes real skill. Similarly, making people laugh about emotionally charged or dark subjects requires more craft than safe comedy. Joan Rivers (quoting possibly Churchill) said that making people laugh is giving them a 'little vacation.'

This insight extends to any creative or communication domain: the ability to handle difficult material with grace — whether in comedy, business communication, or content creation — is a differentiating skill. Malice's deeper point is that policing what can be funny restricts one of humanity's most important coping mechanisms. When his North Korean guides displayed humor despite having nearly all freedoms removed, it demonstrated that laughter is an irreducible human need that persists even in the most oppressive conditions.
