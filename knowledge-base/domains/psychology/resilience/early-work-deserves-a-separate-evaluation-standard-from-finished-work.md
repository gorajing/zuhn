Most people never start ambitious projects because they judge the inevitable rough early phase by the standards of polished final output. This is a fundamental category error—early work is a different type of artifact than finished work, and conflating the two systematically selects against the most ambitious projects (which have the roughest early phases). Graham argues that humanity simply hasn't developed cultural customs for evaluating early work because creating new things was historically too rare to require them.

The practical implication is that you need to consciously switch evaluation modes. When looking at something in its first 20%, the right question isn't 'is this good?' but 'could this become good?' Silicon Valley's outsized returns come partly from institutionalizing this switch—treating early ideas as challenges to imagination rather than claims to evaluate. The skill of seeing potential in ugly ducklings is learnable, and studying the genuinely feeble first steps of eventually-great projects can calibrate your judgment.
