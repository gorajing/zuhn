Most people assume the optimal level of confidence is perfectly accurate self-assessment. But Graham points out that creators face skepticism from two directions simultaneously: their own harsh internal critic and external dismissal from others. Slight overconfidence acts as armor against both, creating a net assessment closer to reality than perfect calibration would produce.

This reframes overconfidence not as a cognitive bug but as a compensating mechanism. The implication is that if you're working on something ambitious and feel exactly right about your abilities, you're probably under-resourced psychologically for the journey ahead. The people who succeed aren't delusional — they're offsetting a known systematic bias in how early work gets evaluated.
