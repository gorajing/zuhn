Hormozi spent six months paralyzed before quitting his consulting job, pacing his condo nightly telling friends he'd quit. The breakthrough came when he played out Plan B specifically: worst case, he'd couch-surf, apply for his old job back, or have an interesting story for business school interviews. The amorphous fear collapsed into a series of manageable steps.

The principle extends beyond career decisions: fear is a product of abstraction. When you name the specific steps — what actually happens next, and then after that — you discover the real downside is usually social (others' opinions of people you don't care about) rather than existential. He also introduces a power metric: the distance between when you make a decision and when you act on it measures personal agency. Shrinking that gap is the practical application of dissolving vague fear into specific action.
