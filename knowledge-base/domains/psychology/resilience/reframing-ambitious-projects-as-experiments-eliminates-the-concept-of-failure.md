Graham and Lisa Randall converge on reframing ambitious projects as experiments rather than performances. This isn't just feel-good advice — it changes the expected value calculation. When a project is framed as performance, failure has negative value (wasted time, embarrassment). When framed as experiment, failure has positive value if the problem was sharply enough defined: a disproven theorem or a structural member that fails under stress both produce genuine knowledge.

The practical technique has two components: first, explicitly frame the project as learning ('I'm curious what happens if...') rather than achievement ('I need this to work'). Graham credits curiosity as his most effective personal motivation, citing both Y Combinator and Bel as projects driven by wanting to see what would happen. Second, define the problem sharply enough that negative results are informative. Vague projects produce vague failures; precise experiments produce precise knowledge regardless of outcome.
