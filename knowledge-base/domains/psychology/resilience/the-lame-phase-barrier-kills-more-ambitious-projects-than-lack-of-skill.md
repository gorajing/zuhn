Great projects almost universally go through a stage where they look unimpressive even to their creators. This is not a signal of failure—it's a structural feature of ambitious work. The problem is that we evaluate early-stage ambitious work using the same standards we'd apply to finished work or less ambitious projects, which systematically undervalues it.

The key insight is that this fear of producing something lame prevents most people from even starting. The ones who do start often quit at the first sign of mediocrity. Recognizing this pattern—that the lame phase is expected and temporary—is the first step to inoculating yourself against premature abandonment. The expected value of an early ambitious project is almost always higher than it appears, because the denominator (potential upside) is large even when the numerator (current quality) is small.
