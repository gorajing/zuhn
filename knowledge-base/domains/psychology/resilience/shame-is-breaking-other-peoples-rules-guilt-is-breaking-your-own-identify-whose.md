Hormozi draws a sharp distinction: guilt arises when you break your own rules; shame arises when you break other people's rules. Someone gay in a religious community feels shame from the community's rules but only feels guilt if they've internalized those rules as their own. Most entrepreneurial paralysis is shame-based — following rules written by people who aren't successful themselves.

The practical technique is specificity: when facing a major decision, ask 'whose voice do I hear?' Usually it's one or two specific people, not 'society.' Hormozi reveals he nearly didn't sell Gym Launch for $46M because one friend might think less of him. Narrowing the blocker to a single person's hypothetical judgment exposed how absurd it was to let that person control his entire trajectory. The same applies to parental dynamics — some people unconsciously sabotage their success to avoid validating difficult parenting, effectively handing permanent control to the person who hurt them.
