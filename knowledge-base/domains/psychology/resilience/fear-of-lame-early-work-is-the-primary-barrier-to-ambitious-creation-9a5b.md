Every ambitious project passes through a stage where it looks unimpressive even to its creator. This is not a sign of failure — it's a structural feature of how great work develops. The problem is that most people judge early work by the standards of finished work, which makes the gap feel insurmountable.

The practical implication is that your relationship with embarrassment is a better predictor of creative output than your raw talent. People who can tolerate producing bad work long enough to iterate past it have a massive structural advantage. This reframes creative blocks: the enemy isn't lack of inspiration but an overactive quality filter applied at the wrong stage of the process.
