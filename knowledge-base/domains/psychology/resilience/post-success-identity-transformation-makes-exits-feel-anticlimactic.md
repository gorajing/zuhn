The article captures a paradox that experienced founders consistently report: by the time you achieve the exit you spent a decade pursuing, you've been so thoroughly transformed by the journey that the outcome no longer maps to the desire that motivated it. The founder refreshing Stripe dashboards in year 1 is a fundamentally different person from the systems thinker allocating capital in year 10. The exit satisfies a ghost.

This connects to hedonic adaptation research but goes further — it's not just that the pleasure fades, it's that the entire motivational structure has been replaced. Founders who understand this in advance can design for the journey rather than the destination, making decisions that optimize for daily sustainability rather than a hypothetical future payoff. It also explains why serial founders exist: the exit doesn't scratch the itch because the itch itself has evolved.
