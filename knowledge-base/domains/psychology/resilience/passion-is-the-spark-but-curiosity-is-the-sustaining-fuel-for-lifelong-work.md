Keating draws a sharp distinction between passion and curiosity as motivational forces. Passion is like the spark that ignites a rocket — exciting and necessary but ephemeral. It's the dopamine hit of imagining a Nobel Prize or a breakthrough discovery. But passion alone cannot sustain the decades of tedious work — soldering equipment, managing logistics, dealing with bureaucracy — that scientific (or entrepreneurial) achievement requires.

Curiosity, by contrast, is self-renewing. Keating observes that Joe Rogan exemplifies this: he won't stop exploring a topic until he viscerally understands it. The practical implication is that when evaluating whether to commit to a long-term project, test whether you have genuine curiosity about the underlying questions, not just excitement about the potential outcomes. If the day-to-day investigation itself doesn't fascinate you, passion alone will not carry you through the inevitable setbacks and tedium.
