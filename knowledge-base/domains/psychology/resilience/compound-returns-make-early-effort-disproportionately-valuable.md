Hormozi frames persistence through the lens of compound returns: outcomes are not linearly proportional to effort but exponentially so. Early effort appears unrewarded because you're in the flat part of the exponential curve. Most people quit here, interpreting slow initial results as evidence the approach doesn't work.

The counterintuitive implication is that the time when quitting feels most rational — when you've invested significant effort with little visible return — is precisely when you're closest to the inflection point. He pairs this with a motivation framework based on perceived deprivation: the gap between where you are and where you believe you should be drives action. This means motivation isn't about positive visualization but about maintaining acute awareness of your current deficiency relative to your potential.
