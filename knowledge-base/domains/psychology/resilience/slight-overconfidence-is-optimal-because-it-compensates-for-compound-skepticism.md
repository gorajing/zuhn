Perfect self-assessment sounds ideal but ignores that ambitious projects face compounding headwinds: your own harsh judgment of early results plus others' dismissiveness. Slight overconfidence acts as armor against both forces simultaneously.

This isn't an argument for delusion—wildly overconfident people crash. The key insight is that the error of slight overconfidence is net-positive because it compensates for systematic errors in the opposite direction. Hardy noticed this in mathematics, and Graham observes it across many fields: the most successful people are consistently slightly overconfident, not perfectly calibrated.
