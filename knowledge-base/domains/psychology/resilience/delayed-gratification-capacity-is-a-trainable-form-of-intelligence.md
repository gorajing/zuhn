Hormozi frames delayed gratification through a biological lens: from primates upward, the sign of intelligence is the ability to abstract a reward back to the original action over increasingly longer delays. A monkey needs immediate feedback; a human can work for years toward an abstract goal.

The counter-intuitive claim is that deliberately practicing longer delay windows — accepting that your reward rate will decrease in the short term — actually increases your learning rate over time. This reframes impatience not as a personality trait but as a cognitive limitation that can be trained. The practical consequence is that people who sprint expecting quick results (seeing only the beginning and end of others' 'marathons') burn out because they misestimated the distance, while those who pace themselves and tolerate uncertainty compound their capabilities.
