Paul Graham identifies a specific psychological bottleneck: ambitious projects inevitably pass through an ugly early phase where results look unimpressive even to the creator. Most people either never start or abandon work at this stage. The fear is not irrational — early work genuinely looks bad — but the mistake is judging it by the standards of finished work rather than recognizing it as a special category.

This reframes creative paralysis as a calibration error rather than a character flaw. The practical implication is that anyone attempting ambitious work needs explicit strategies for surviving the lame phase — overestimating importance, starting with low-commitment media, or surrounding yourself with peers who can distinguish ugly ducklings from baby swans. The people who do great work are not those who skip this phase but those who push through it.
