In helicopter underwater escape training, the single most important lesson is: stay in your seat. When the aircraft flips and sinks, unbuckling feels like the obvious survival move — your body naturally wants to right itself toward the surface. But unbuckling makes you a 'loose sock in the dryer' — you lose all spatial orientation, don't know where exits are, and can't navigate out.

The seat is your reference frame. While sitting, you know the exit is to your right, five seats down. You can feel along the wall. The moment you leave the seat, chairs are 'hanging from the ceiling' and your entire mental map breaks. This principle extends beyond survival scenarios: in any crisis (business, personal, emotional), the instinct to abandon your current position and thrash toward safety often destroys the very orientation you need to escape. The counterintuitive discipline of staying anchored to what you know — your values, your data, your process — while methodically working toward the exit is what separates survivors from casualties.
