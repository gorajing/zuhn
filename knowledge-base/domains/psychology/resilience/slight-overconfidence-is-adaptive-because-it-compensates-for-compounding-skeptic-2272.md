The most successful people in many fields are slightly overconfident — not delusionally so, but enough to offset compounding error sources that all push toward quitting too early. External skeptics dismiss early work, and your own standards punish it. These forces stack. A small positive bias in self-belief doesn't make you a worse judge of reality; it corrects for the asymmetric penalty of abandoning good work prematurely versus persisting with mediocre work slightly too long.

This reframes overconfidence not as a cognitive flaw but as an error-correction mechanism. The optimal calibration for doing ambitious work is not perfect accuracy about your abilities — it's accuracy about expected outcomes, which requires compensating for the systematic downward bias created by the social and psychological environment around early-stage creative work.
