Reeves identifies a persistent double standard: when young men are out of the labor market, the reflexive response is blame -- 'get off the sofa and stop smoking weed.' He notes this blame instinct is conspicuously absent for other struggling groups. The framing matters because it determines whether a problem gets treated as a policy challenge or a moral failing.

The result is that genuine structural problems -- stagnant wages for non-college men since 1979, sixfold increase in male drug deaths since 2001, 80% of suicides being male -- get dismissed as individual failure. Reeves argues the best word for many of these young men is not 'feckless' but 'lost' -- they are genuinely asking what their role in the world is and receiving no constructive answer.
