Naval argues that while genetics may account for half or more of traits like strength and intelligence, the genetic set point for happiness is only about half the story — and the malleable portion is much larger than people assume. Unlike athletic performance where physical limits are hard, mood, temperament, and outlook respond dramatically to deliberate effort.

This reframes happiness from a lottery outcome to a skill acquisition problem. The practical implication is treating happiness like fitness: identifying specific practices, tracking progress, and expecting gradual improvement rather than a single breakthrough. People who believe happiness is fixed devalue it and stop investing in it, creating a self-fulfilling prophecy.
