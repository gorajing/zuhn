When you start an ambitious project, you face skepticism from two directions simultaneously: other people's dismissiveness and your own harsh judgment of incomplete work. If you have perfectly calibrated self-assessment, the net effect is still negative because external skepticism tips the scale toward quitting.

Graham observes that across many fields, the most successful people are slightly overconfident — not delusionally so, but enough to armor themselves against the compound discouragement. This reframes overconfidence not as a cognitive flaw but as a rational error-correction strategy. The key insight is that the optimal belief about your abilities isn't the accurate one — it's the one that produces the best decisions given the asymmetric costs of quitting too early versus persisting too long.
