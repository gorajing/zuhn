The fear of failure is really a fear of wasted effort and damaged reputation. But if you frame a project as an experiment, failure stops being waste — it becomes data. If a theorem turns out to be false, you've learned something. If a structural member fails under stress, you've learned something. The information value exists regardless of whether the outcome matches your hopes.

This isn't just motivational reframing — it changes the actual economics of the decision. Under a performance frame, the expected value of starting equals (probability of success × value of success) minus (probability of failure × cost of failure). Under an experiment frame, the failure cost drops dramatically because you retain the learning. This makes more projects worth starting, which over time compounds into significantly more shots on goal. Lisa Randall adds the important nuance: treat it as an experiment in the full sense — if results definitively rule something out, move on; if they suggest variations, iterate.
