Graham argues that learning is the most fundamental compounding mechanism because knowledge builds on itself — the more you know, the easier it is to learn more. Even when other forms of exponential growth (company growth, audience building) appear distinct, they are almost always intermixed with learning. A failed startup founder who learned deeply is still on an exponential curve.

The practical heuristic is stark: if you're not learning, you're probably not on a path to superlinear returns. But the learning should not be over-optimized toward known-valuable skills. Because you can't predict what knowledge will prove valuable, being too strict about what to learn will cut off the outlier discoveries that generate the steepest returns. Curiosity-driven learning beats career-optimized learning.
