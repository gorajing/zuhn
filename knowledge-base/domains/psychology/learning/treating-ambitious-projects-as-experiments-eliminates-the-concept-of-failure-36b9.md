When you frame a risky project as 'a way to learn' rather than 'a way to make something,' the entire risk calculus shifts. Failure in the traditional sense becomes impossible because any outcome—including the project not working—produces genuine knowledge. This is especially true when the problem is sharply defined: a disproved theorem or a structural member that breaks under stress teaches you something concrete, even if it's not what you hoped to learn. Paul Graham notes that curiosity was his primary motivation for both Y Combinator and his programming language Bel—he wanted to see how things would turn out. This curiosity-driven framing is particularly powerful because it's self-sustaining: each result, positive or negative, generates new questions. The irony Graham identifies is that this reframing isn't actually a mind trick—a lame-looking early version of an ambitious project genuinely is more valuable than it appears. The 'trick' is simply teaching yourself to see the truth that your default judgment obscures.
