The Mongols had never seen a castle before they began raiding settled civilizations, yet they rapidly became experts at siege warfare — not through independent invention, but by systematically interrogating prisoners and adopting their techniques. They similarly absorbed gunpowder technology and even built ships to attack Japan, despite being a landlocked steppe people.

This pattern of 'adaptive absorption' is distinct from organic innovation. Rather than developing capabilities from first principles, the Mongols treated every conquered population as a knowledge resource to be harvested. This is the learning-organization pattern at its most aggressive: the speed of capability acquisition through absorption far exceeds what independent R&D can achieve. Modern parallels include acqui-hires, competitive teardowns, and the deliberate recruitment of domain experts from industries being disrupted.
