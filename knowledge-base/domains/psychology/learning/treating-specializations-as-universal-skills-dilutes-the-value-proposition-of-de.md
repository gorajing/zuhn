The analogy between coding and literacy fails because literacy is a prerequisite for participating in modern society, while programming is one of many valuable specializations. When coding is treated as universally necessary, two harmful effects compound: existing practitioners see their differentiation erode as the market floods, and learners who would thrive in other fields spend time on a skill they'll use superficially at best.

This generalizes beyond coding. Any time a specialized skill gets reframed as universal — data science, design thinking, entrepreneurship — the same dynamic plays out. The skill loses its power as a differentiator, while the opportunity cost of learning it displaces investment in skills where the learner has genuine comparative advantage. The strategic move is to identify which skills are truly universal prerequisites versus which are valuable specializations, and invest accordingly.
