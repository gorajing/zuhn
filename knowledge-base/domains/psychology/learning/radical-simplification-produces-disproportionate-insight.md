Einstein published his field equations in 1915 but couldn't find an exact solution. The equations are coupled, differential, and encode the full complexity of spacetime geometry. Schwarzschild's breakthrough was radical simplification: imagine an eternal universe with nothing in it except a single, non-rotating, electrically neutral point mass. This 'standard physicist thing' of imagining the simplest possible scenario yielded the first exact solution - and that solution already contained black holes, white holes, parallel universes, and wormholes.

The lesson generalizes far beyond physics. When facing a complex system with many interacting variables, the instinct is to model everything at once. But Schwarzschild's approach shows that stripping away every possible complication often reveals the essential structure. The rotating black hole solution (Kerr) took another 48 years to find, but most of the conceptual landscape was already visible in the simple case. In any complex domain - software architecture, business strategy, research - asking 'what does the simplest possible version look like?' is consistently the highest-leverage first move.
