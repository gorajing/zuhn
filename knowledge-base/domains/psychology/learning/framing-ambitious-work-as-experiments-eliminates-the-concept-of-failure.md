Graham catalogs several psychological techniques for pushing through the ugly early phase of ambitious work, and many converge on the same principle: lower the perceived stakes. Calling a painting 'just a sketch,' software 'just a hack,' or a project 'just an experiment' lets you judge initial results by a lower standard. Once the project gains momentum, you can quietly convert it into something more ambitious. This isn't self-deception — it's strategically managing the gap between your standards and your current output.

The experiment framing is particularly powerful because it redefines failure as data. If the theorem you're trying to prove is false, you've learned something. If a structural member fails under stress, that's knowledge. Curiosity as a motivation — trying things just to see how they turn out — achieves the same effect by shifting the reward from the outcome to the process of discovery. These aren't tricks to fool yourself; they're more accurate framings of reality, since early-stage ambitious work genuinely is more valuable than it appears.
