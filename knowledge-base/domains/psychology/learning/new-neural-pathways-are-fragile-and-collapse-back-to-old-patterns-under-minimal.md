After 8 months of practice, Destin could ride the backwards bicycle — but the new skill was extremely fragile. Any minor distraction (a phone ringing, someone calling his name) would instantly revert his brain to the old steering algorithm, causing an immediate crash. The new pathway required continuous conscious attention to maintain.

This reveals a critical principle about skill acquisition and habit change: there is a long intermediate phase where the new behavior works only under ideal conditions. This has direct applications for anyone managing behavior change — whether breaking bad coding habits, adopting new management practices, or changing dietary patterns. The implication is that new habits need environmental protection during the consolidation phase: reduce distractions, simplify context, and don't trust the new pattern under stress until it has been practiced far beyond initial competence. The feeling of 'I've got it' dramatically precedes actual robustness.
