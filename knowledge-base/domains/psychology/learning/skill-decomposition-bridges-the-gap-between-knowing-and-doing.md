Hormozi identifies a critical insight about the knowing-doing gap: the more skilled you are, the more general a command can be and you can still follow it. A CEO can hear 'grow the business' and execute. A novice needs 'click the blue button, then type these exact words.' Most people operate with instructions calibrated for a skill level above their actual ability.

This maps directly to deliberate practice research. Abstract traits like 'be confident' or 'be relational' are useless commands for most people. The fix is decomposition: what does 'confident' look like in observable micro-behaviors? Eye contact duration, speech pace, posture angle, question-to-statement ratio. Each micro-behavior can be practiced independently. The ego resists this decomposition because it implies admitting you're less skilled than you believe, but Hormozi argues our egos grew up while our abilities often didn't.
