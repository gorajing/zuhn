At normal speed, a Prince Rupert's Drop simply appears to explode. At 130,000 frames per second, you can see the failure front propagating as a measurable wave, directly observe which break initiated the cascade, and even calculate the velocity of destruction. The mystery dissolves not because of a new theory but because of better temporal resolution. This principle applies broadly to learning and problem-solving: many 'mysteries' in complex systems — whether debugging software, diagnosing organizational dysfunction, or understanding market behavior — persist because we observe at the wrong timescale. Slowing down (detailed logging, frame-by-frame retrospectives, granular metrics) or speeding up (zooming out to see long-term trends) often reveals causal chains that are invisible at our default observation frequency.
