The proof's core move is brilliant in its simplicity: start with one lighthouse on a circle, use the inverse Pythagorean theorem to split it into two on a larger circle while preserving total brightness, and repeat. At each step you have evenly-spaced lighthouses on a circle with known total brightness π²/4. In the limit, the circle becomes a flat line with infinite evenly-spaced lighthouses.

This 'construct-then-limit' pattern appears across mathematics and engineering: finite element methods approximate continuous systems, neural network universality proofs build from finite width, and even startup strategy uses 'do things that don't scale' (finite) to discover what will (infinite). The key insight is that preserving an invariant through each finite step guarantees the result at infinity.
