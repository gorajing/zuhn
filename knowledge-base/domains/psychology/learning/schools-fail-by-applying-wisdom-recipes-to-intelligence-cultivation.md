Graham argues that most educational traditions are wisdom recipes: submit to the teacher, absorb prescribed material, break down individuality. These methods develop reliable average performance across situations. But intelligence—the capacity for spectacular peaks—requires the opposite: cultivating individual curiosity, tolerating a mistakenly high opinion of one's abilities (which motivates continued effort), and encouraging students to invent rather than recite. The teacher's role flips from lecturer to audience—appreciative but not easily impressed. This explains a persistent puzzle in education: why do institutions that seem well-designed keep producing mediocre results? They may be optimizing for the wrong variable entirely, applying wisdom's remedial, subtractive approach when intelligence demands an additive, nurturing one.
