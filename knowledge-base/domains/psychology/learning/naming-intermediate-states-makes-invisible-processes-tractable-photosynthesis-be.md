The video walks through a process with dozens of chemical transformations, yet makes it tractable by naming every intermediate: Photosystem II, cytochrome complex, ATP synthase, Photosystem I, RuBisCo, RuBP, 3-Phosphoglycerate, G3P. Each name converts an anonymous chemical state into a discrete, memorable entity that can be reasoned about independently. Without these names, the process would be an undifferentiated chain of electron movements impossible to hold in working memory.

This is the same principle that makes programming teachable: variables, functions, and classes are naming strategies that convert anonymous computation into discrete, addressable concepts. When learning or teaching any complex sequential process — a sales pipeline, a deployment workflow, a negotiation strategy — explicitly naming each intermediate state reduces cognitive load from 'track everything simultaneously' to 'understand this one named step and its connections to adjacent named steps.' The names themselves become the compression mechanism.
