Hormozi attributes his stage presence not to natural talent or positive thinking but to years of standing on a box with a microphone leading fitness classes multiple times daily. When he first got on a real stage, he was puzzled by his own lack of nervousness — the reps had habituated his nervous system without him noticing. This mirrors clinical exposure therapy: lock someone afraid of spiders in a room with spiders and after hours of panic attacks, they walk out unafraid.

The key insight is that the same number of reps can span a month or a year depending on dedication. The goal is to 'microwave' the habituation period by maximizing exposure density. This reframes confidence-building from a mindset problem (affirmations, visualization) to a volume problem (compress more reps into less time). It also explains why consistency beats talent — 20 hours of deliberate practice creates basic competence in any skill, but most people take 10 years to log those 20 hours because they avoid the discomfort of early incompetence.
