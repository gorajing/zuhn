DNA was discovered by Friedrich Miescher in 1869 — he isolated it from white blood cells on used hospital bandages and identified it as a new biological substance called 'nuclein.' Yet almost no one outside biology knows his name. Watson and Crick, who synthesized existing data (much of it from Franklin and others) into the double helix model in 1953, became the household names. Miescher had the discovery; Watson and Crick had the narrative.

This pattern repeats across domains. In startups, the company that creates a category rarely dominates it — the one that packages and communicates the concept clearly does. In science, the Higgs boson is named after Peter Higgs though multiple physicists independently proposed it. Credit flows not to who found the raw material, but to who assembled it into a story others could understand and build upon. For anyone doing original work, this means that discovery without synthesis and communication is effectively invisible. The deliverable isn't the finding — it's the frame.
