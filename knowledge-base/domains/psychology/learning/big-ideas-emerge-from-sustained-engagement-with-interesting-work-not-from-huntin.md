Graham argues that big ideas appear as a byproduct of deep familiarity with a domain, not from top-down searching. Einstein, Ford, and Beckenbauer all 'knew their work like a piano player knows the keys,' and this deep familiarity is what allowed them to notice when something seemed amiss. The great question emerges from the anomaly, not from the search.

The practical recipe is iterative: pick a project that seems interesting, make it small enough to finish (under a month), do something hard enough to stretch you, and let each project generate the next one. Over time this becomes self-sustaining, like an internal combustion engine. The critical requirement is genuine interest — not what seems impressive or cool, but what actually engages you. Graham's own mistake was writing 'existentialist' short stories because they seemed intellectually serious, when he should have written what he actually enjoyed. The road to meaningful work runs through authentic engagement, not through performing sophistication.
