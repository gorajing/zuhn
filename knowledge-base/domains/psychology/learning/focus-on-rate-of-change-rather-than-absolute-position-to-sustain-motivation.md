One of the most practical techniques Graham offers for surviving the lame-early-work phase is shifting attention from absolute quality to rate of improvement. If you're producing bad work but it's getting better fast, that trajectory is genuinely encouraging and factually informative — it tells you the project has potential even when current results don't. This reframe explains why dedicating large blocks of time to new work helps: faster iteration means faster visible improvement, which sustains motivation. It also explains a hidden advantage of youth (bigger time blocks) and age (pattern-matching from past cycles of ugly-duckling-to-finished-work).
