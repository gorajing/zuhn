Destin's son, who had been riding a normal bike for over half his life (3 years), learned the backwards bicycle in just 2 weeks. Destin himself required 8 months of daily practice — roughly a 16x difference. This ratio aligns with established neuroscience on critical periods and synaptic pruning: young brains maintain far more flexible neural connections that can be rapidly reorganized. The practical implication is that the 'best time to learn' adage for language acquisition applies broadly to any complex procedural skill. For adults attempting to learn fundamentally new motor or cognitive patterns, the timeline should be calibrated in months, not weeks, and expectations should be set accordingly. The experiment also suggests adults aren't incapable — they're just dramatically slower at neural reorganization.
