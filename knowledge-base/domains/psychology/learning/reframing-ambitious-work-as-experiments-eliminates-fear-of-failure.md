Graham identifies several reframing techniques that reduce the psychological cost of starting ambitious work: calling a painting 'just a sketch,' software 'just a quick hack,' or any project 'just an experiment.' These aren't just comforting lies — they change the standard you judge your early output against, making it easier to continue past the ugly phase.

The deeper principle is that framing determines persistence. If failure is redefined as learning ('if the theorem turns out to be false, you've still learned something'), the downside of attempting ambitious work drops to near zero. Choosing low-commitment media amplifies this effect — it's easier to treat something as a sketch when you're drawing in a notebook than carving stone. The reframe works because early work genuinely is more like an experiment than a finished product.
