Graham and Lisa Randall converge on the same insight: if you treat ambitious projects as experiments rather than performances, failure stops being a threat. An experiment that disproves your hypothesis still taught you something. A theorem that turns out false, a structural member that breaks under stress — these are knowledge, even if not the knowledge you wanted.

This reframe is particularly powerful because it attacks the root cause of creative paralysis. People don't start ambitious projects because they fear the binary outcome: either it works and you're vindicated, or it fails and you're embarrassed. Experiments have no such binary. Every outcome is data. Graham notes that curiosity is the most natural fuel for this mindset — he started Y Combinator partly just to see what would happen. The practical implication is to explicitly label your ambitious projects as experiments, both to yourself and others, because the label changes which neural circuits evaluate the outcome.
