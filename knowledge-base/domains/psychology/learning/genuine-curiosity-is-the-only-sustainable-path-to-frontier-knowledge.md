Naval argues that specific knowledge often lives at the edge of what's known — areas being actively figured out where no established curriculum exists. In these frontier zones, the difference between someone who is genuinely fascinated and someone following market signals becomes decisive. The curious person puts in more hours, thinks about problems in the shower, and develops pattern-matching intuition that the mercenary never will.

This connects to a broader principle about sustainable competitive advantage: the things that feel like play to you but look like work to others are precisely where your specific knowledge lives. This framing redefines career optimization from 'find the highest-paying field' to 'find where your obsessive curiosity intersects with something the market values.' The play-versus-work test is both a discovery mechanism and a sustainability check.
