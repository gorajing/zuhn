Charlie Munger's own example: he read Barron's every week for 50 years, found one actionable idea, made $50M on it, gave that capital to Li Lu who compounded it to $400M. That is the expected value math of serious reading when you have capital or a business to apply ideas to. Senra extends this: a 28-year-old founder listening to Founders who finds a single insight that produces a 10% improvement to his business has just earned a $10M idea. The ratio of reading hours to dollars generated approaches infinity when you have a deployment vehicle.

The deeper mechanism is that a biography represents 50+ years of hard-won wisdom compressed into a week of reading. You won't absorb 90% of Sam Walton's lessons from one book, but you'll reliably absorb 1-5% — and 1-5% of 50 years of retail genius is still an enormous cognitive subsidy. The constraint isn't access (everything is on Kindle), it's discipline: Naval's claim that reading one hour a day puts you in the top 0.0001% of humans is plausible because so few people actually do it. This is the 'unfair advantage' Bezos was searching for — it's durable, scalable, and available to anyone willing to do the work that nobody else will.
