The fear of failure is proportional to how much identity is attached to the outcome. When you frame a project as an experiment, you shift the success criterion from 'did it work?' to 'did I learn something?' Since experiments by definition produce knowledge regardless of outcome — a disproven theorem or a failed structural member still teaches you something — the concept of failure becomes incoherent.

This reframe is especially powerful for sharply-defined problems where failure is itself informative. It also pairs well with curiosity as a motivation: Graham notes that curiosity about 'how will this turn out?' sustained him through years-long projects like Bel. The practical application is to explicitly declare new ventures as experiments at the outset, which lowers the psychological cost of starting and sustains momentum through the inevitable ugly early phase.
