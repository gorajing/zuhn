One of the most powerful psychological reframes for ambitious work is treating projects as experiments rather than performances. Under a performance frame, the project either succeeds or fails, and failure is pure loss. Under an experimental frame, every outcome teaches you something — if the theorem is false, you've learned that; if the structural member breaks, you've learned its limits. The sharper the problem definition, the more informative the failure.

Curiosity amplifies this effect. Paul Graham describes sustaining multi-year projects (Y Combinator, Bel) primarily through curiosity about what would happen rather than attachment to a specific outcome. This motivation is uniquely resilient because it's fed by any outcome, not just success. The experimental frame also reduces the social cost of failure — 'I was testing a hypothesis' carries less stigma than 'I tried and failed' — which further lowers the activation energy for starting ambitious work.
