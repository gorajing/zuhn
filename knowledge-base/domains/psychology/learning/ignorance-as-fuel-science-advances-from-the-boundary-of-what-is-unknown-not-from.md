Columbia biologist Stuart Firestein reframes the relationship between knowledge and ignorance: facts are photographs from a road trip, but ignorance is the fuel driving the vehicle forward. 'It is a mistake to bob around in the circle of facts, instead of riding the wave to the great expanse lying outside the circle.'

This has direct implications for how individuals and organizations should allocate intellectual effort. Optimizing for fact accumulation (studying known answers) has diminishing returns compared to optimizing for question quality (identifying the most productive unknowns). In practice, this means the most valuable intellectual skill isn't retention but the ability to identify which unknowns, if resolved, would unlock the most downstream knowledge. This applies equally to research strategy, startup ideation, and personal learning — the person who asks the best questions at the frontier outperforms the person who memorizes the most facts within it.
