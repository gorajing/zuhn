Graham identifies three reasons people don't know something: it's unimportant (random facts), they're obtuse (can't understand it), or they're inexperienced (haven't encountered it yet). For important ideas aimed at smart audiences, inexperience is the dominant factor. This reframes education and knowledge-sharing — the gap isn't intellectual capacity but exposure and lived experience.

This has practical implications for anyone building educational content, mentorship programs, or knowledge systems. The highest-value knowledge transfer isn't about simplifying complex ideas for slower thinkers — it's about accelerating the timeline on which smart people encounter ideas they would eventually discover anyway. The leverage is in compressing years of experience into accessible form.
