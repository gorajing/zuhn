The experiment provides a controlled comparison: father and son, similar genetics, same task, same daily practice regimen. The child succeeded in 2 weeks; the adult needed 8 months. This roughly 16x difference aligns with neuroscience literature on myelination and synaptic pruning — adult brains trade plasticity for efficiency, making established pathways fast but rigid.

The practical implication is that adults attempting to learn skills requiring procedural rewiring (new languages, instruments, movement patterns, even new mental models) should expect dramatically longer timelines than children and plan accordingly. It also suggests that the common advice to 'learn like a child' is misleading — adults physically cannot learn like children. Instead, adults need strategies that compensate: spaced repetition, deliberate constraint removal, and acceptance of a much longer awkward phase.
