Graham argues that beliefs about the future are so rarely correct that they usually aren't worth the rigidity they impose. The better strategy is to stop trying to point yourself in the right direction and instead become maximally sensitive to the winds of change. This is a fundamentally different epistemic posture: rather than forming strong priors and updating, you maintain weak priors and optimize for signal detection.

Working hypotheses are still useful because they motivate exploration, but the discipline is in preventing hypotheses from hardening into identities. Once a hypothesis becomes associated with your identity, you lose the ability to abandon it when evidence shifts. The practical technique is to hold ideas lightly — pursue them energetically but be ready to drop them the moment they stop fitting the evidence. This mirrors how the best startup investors operate: excited about possibilities but ruthless about updating.
