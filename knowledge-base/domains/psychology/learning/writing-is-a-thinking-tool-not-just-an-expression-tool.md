Paul Graham argues that the act of writing is fundamentally a thinking process, not merely an expression of already-formed thoughts. Half the ideas in any essay emerge during writing itself, because committing to specific words forces you to confront vagueness, incompleteness, and flaws that remain invisible in purely mental reasoning.

This has practical implications: if you want to truly understand something, write about it. The 'stranger test' — reading your own writing as if you're someone who only knows what's on the page — is the mechanism that surfaces gaps. The ideas that survive this process are qualitatively different from the ones you started with. This reframes writing from an output activity to an input activity for your own understanding.
