The popular claim that habits take 21 days (or 30, or 60) to form is not supported by the peer-reviewed literature. Lally et al. (2010) studied a single health behavior — taking a walk after dinner — and found the range was 18 to 254 days across participants. The criterion for 'formed' was performing the behavior ~85% of the time with minimal conscious effort.

Huberman attributes this variance to differences in limbic friction — the activation energy required to override either anxiety (too wired to act) or lethargy (too tired to act). This is governed by the autonomic nervous system's alertness-calm axis. The practical implication is that comparing your habit formation timeline to anyone else's is meaningless. Instead, measure your own limbic friction for each specific habit: how much conscious override does it require? That metric, not days elapsed, is the true indicator of where you are on the path to automaticity.
