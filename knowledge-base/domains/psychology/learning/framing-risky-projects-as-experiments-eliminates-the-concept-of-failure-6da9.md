Graham and Lisa Randall both advocate treating new projects as experiments rather than commitments to succeed. This reframe changes the payoff matrix: in the success-or-failure frame, anything short of the goal is a loss. In the experiment frame, even complete failure produces information ('the theorem is false,' 'this structural member fails under stress'), so the minimum return is always positive.

Curiosity serves the same function. Graham notes that pure curiosity—wanting to see how something turns out—was a primary motivator for both Y Combinator and his programming language Bel. Curiosity-driven work is psychologically easier to sustain because the reward is discovery itself, not a specific outcome. This makes it particularly effective for projects in the ugly early phase, where outcome-oriented motivation would flag. The practical takeaway: before starting something ambitious, explicitly reframe it as 'I want to find out what happens if...' rather than 'I need this to succeed.'
