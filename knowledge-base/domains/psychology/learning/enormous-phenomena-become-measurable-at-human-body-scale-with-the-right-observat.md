By lying on a beach, watching the sun set, then quickly standing to see it set again, you create a measurable time gap between two observations at different heights. Combined with the height of your eyes in each position, this gives you enough data to calculate the Earth's radius through basic geometry. No instruments, no technology — just your body and a timing device.

This exemplifies a powerful learning principle: the barrier to understanding large-scale phenomena is often not equipment or expertise but the right observational frame. The data was always available at human scale; what was missing was knowing which relationship to exploit. This applies broadly — many complex systems have 'body-scale' entry points where a simple, cleverly framed observation reveals deep structure. The skill is learning to ask 'what minimal observation would constrain this variable?' rather than assuming you need sophisticated measurement tools.
