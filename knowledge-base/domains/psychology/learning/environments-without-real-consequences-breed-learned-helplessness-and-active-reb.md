Graham describes how the mediocrity of school bred a rebelliousness that actively drove kids away from learning. Years after high school, he couldn't bring himself to read anything that had been assigned. Worse, words like 'character' and 'integrity' were so debased by their institutional usage that he rejected the concepts entirely.

This has profound implications for anyone designing learning environments, onboarding programs, or training systems. The damage from meaningless work isn't neutral — it's actively negative, creating antibodies against the very material being taught. Corporate training that feels performative doesn't just waste time; it makes employees hostile to the skills being trained. The antidote is ensuring work has real consequences: apprenticeship models where output matters, projects that ship to real users, and feedback loops connected to actual outcomes rather than arbitrary metrics.
