Destin Sandlin understood perfectly that turning the handlebars left would steer the wheel right. He could explain the mechanism. Yet this knowledge provided zero advantage in actually riding the bike — he failed immediately and continued failing for 8 months. This demonstrates that procedural skills (riding, playing an instrument, coding fluently) operate on entirely different neural substrates than declarative knowledge (facts, rules, explanations). The implication is profound for education and training: explaining how something works is fundamentally insufficient for building competence. Drill, repetition, and embodied practice build the actual neural pathways required for performance, and no amount of conceptual understanding can substitute for them.
