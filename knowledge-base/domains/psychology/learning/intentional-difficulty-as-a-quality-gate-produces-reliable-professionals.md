Kaku states bluntly that physics departments flunk most students in elementary physics, and are encouraged to do so by engineering departments. The reasoning is stark: if you can't master friction, levers, pulleys, and gears, you shouldn't be designing bridges or skyscrapers. Before you can work with Einstein's elegant equations, you must master Newton's mechanical fundamentals.

This reveals an underappreciated design pattern in professional training: intentional difficulty at the foundational level serves as a quality gate that protects against catastrophic downstream failures. The high flunk-out rate isn't a bug in physics education — it's a feature. This pattern applies broadly: any domain where errors have severe consequences benefits from deliberately difficult foundational requirements rather than making early stages easier to improve completion rates. The trade-off between accessibility and rigor has real-world consequences when the stakes are high.
