Nielsen distinguishes two types of creative work: routine work (where the goal is speed and outsourcing) and high-variance work (where you must be willing to take weeks, months, or years and spend most of that time stuck). The most important learning happens in the stuck phase. Essays he wrote in a couple days produced little durable knowledge; essays he spent three to six months on he still remembers fifteen years later.

The forcing function matters enormously. 'Going deep' means different things to different people — reading a blog post, reading a book, writing a book — and the stakes you commit to determine what you actually internalize. Dwarkesh's insight about his own podcast is that interviewing experts produces only shallow black-box input-output mappings unless there's a concrete exercise afterward (implement the transformer, work through relativity problems, write 2,000 words connecting the episode to everything else you know).

LLMs are especially seductive here because they make it trivially easy to escape the aversive stuck phase. Every time you'd otherwise sit with confusion, there's a next question to ask — which feels like progress but substitutes entertainment for the hard work that would actually build understanding. The defense is raising the stakes: write the book, not the blog post; teach the class, not attend it; solve the problems, don't read the solutions. Simonton's equal-odds rule applies: productivity dominates quality-per-attempt, so the main thing is to keep shipping artifacts that force real engagement.
