Unlike science, where theories must explain and predict observations, mathematical axioms are simply declared to be true. Their consequences become what we observe within the mathematical universe. If axioms lead to contradictions, we modify or abandon them — but the selection criterion is internal consistency, not empirical fit.

Yet as Wigner noted, this 'invented' system is unreasonably effective in natural sciences. The implication is profound: building internally consistent formal systems — whether in math, software architecture, or organizational design — generates emergent predictive and explanatory power that exceeds the designer's original intent. The rigor of avoiding contradictions creates something that maps onto reality even without trying to. This suggests that investing in internal consistency and formal rigor pays dividends that are hard to predict in advance.
