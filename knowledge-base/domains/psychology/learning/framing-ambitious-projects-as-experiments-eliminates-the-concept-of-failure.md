When you frame a project as a performance, failure means you are inadequate. When you frame it as an experiment, failure means you learned something. Lisa Randall's formulation is precise: if the experiment rules something out, you move on; if there's a way to vary it, you iterate. This reframe is not just motivational — it changes behavior. Experimenters naturally vary their approach, collect data from failures, and pivot without ego damage. Graham notes that curiosity was one of his strongest motivators for both Y Combinator and Bel, because curiosity-driven work has no failure state — you're always learning what happens. The key is that the reframe must be genuine, not performative. You actually have to care about what you'll learn, not just tell yourself you do.
