Graham's prescription for escaping the trap of judging early work too harshly is empirical: study enough cases of great work's origins and you build an internal reference class. When your own early output looks lame, you recognize it as a familiar phase rather than evidence of personal failure.

The challenge is that great people often hide their early work — they too misjudged it and didn't preserve it. The internet has partially solved this: first commits, first videos, first essays are now often discoverable. Michael Nielsen's observation is that this access is historically unusual and underexploited.

This is a learnable epistemic skill, not just a pep talk. It changes the prior you assign to 'lame early work' being worthless. Once that prior shifts, self-skepticism and external dismissal both lose their power to derail momentum.
