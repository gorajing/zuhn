The Kuramoto model demonstrates that when you increase coupling between oscillators (metronomes, fireflies, neurons), synchronization doesn't emerge gradually. Instead, the system remains disordered until coupling crosses a critical threshold, at which point it snaps into coherence — analogous to water freezing at a critical temperature.

This has profound implications for understanding any system where independent agents can influence each other: markets, social movements, adoption curves, team alignment. The key insight is that small increases in connection or influence can appear to do nothing until a tipping point is crossed, after which the entire system reorganizes. Efforts to create alignment should focus on reaching the critical coupling threshold rather than expecting proportional returns from incremental effort.
