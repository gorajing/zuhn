Graham observes that one of the most reliable ways to stay motivated through the early lame phase of ambitious work is to shift attention from absolute quality to rate of change. Lame work that is rapidly improving is far more valuable than mediocre work that is static — but our intuitions about quality are calibrated to current state, not trajectory.

This is especially important for beginners and young people, who tend to improve fastest and have the most time to invest in deep focus. The advantage of rapid improvement is compounding: the faster early work improves, the shorter the lame phase, and the sooner external validation becomes available to reinforce continued effort.

Practically, this means evaluating projects by asking 'is this getting better?' rather than 'is this good?' The former question has a clear and answerable form even when the latter produces discouragement. It also shifts the mental frame from judgment (is this work worthy?) to observation (is this system working?), which reduces the emotional weight of each iteration.
