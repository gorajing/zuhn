Einstein illustrated this with geometry: if knowledge is a circle's area, then ignorance is its circumference. Area grows as πr², but circumference grows as 2πr — so while both expand, the ratio of circumference to area (known-unknowns per unit of knowledge) actually decreases, yet the absolute frontier of darkness always outpaces any single learner's ability to fill it. The field of agnotology formalizes this, studying how ignorance is produced and maintained.

Leeuwenhoek's discovery of microorganisms is the canonical example: solving the mystery of food spoilage simultaneously revealed an entire kingdom of biology nobody knew existed. George Bernard Shaw captured it as 'science never solves a problem without creating ten more.' This reframes the psychology of expertise — feeling increasingly uncertain as you learn more isn't a failure mode, it's the correct epistemic response. Practically, this means progress should be measured not by shrinking uncertainty but by upgrading the quality of questions you can ask.
