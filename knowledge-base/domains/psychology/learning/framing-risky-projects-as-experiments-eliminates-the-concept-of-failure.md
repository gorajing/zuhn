When you frame a project as an experiment, failure becomes information rather than judgment. If a theorem turns out to be false or a structural member fails under stress, you've learned something concrete. Lisa Randall's formulation is precise: treat new things as experiments where ruling something out is a valid outcome, and vary the approach when results suggest a different path might work.

This reframing works because it shifts the success criterion from 'did I produce something impressive' to 'did I learn something.' Under the experimental frame, the only true failure is not starting at all. Combined with curiosity as a motivation — Graham cites his work on Bel and Y Combinator as driven by wanting to see what would happen — this creates a psychological framework where ambitious attempts feel low-risk regardless of outcome.
