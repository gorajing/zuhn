The video highlights the confusing fact that Photosystem II comes before Photosystem I in the electron transport chain. This happened because PSI was discovered and named first, then PSII was found and numbered based on its relationship to PSI rather than its position in the process. The alternative full name — plastoquinone oxidoreductase — is technically precise but practically useless for learners.

This pattern recurs across technical domains: conventions that seem arbitrary or backwards often encode historical discovery order rather than logical order. Git's 'checkout' doing three different things, JavaScript's type coercion rules, musical key signatures — all carry archaeological layers. Explicitly teaching why a convention feels wrong (rather than just insisting students memorize it) reduces cognitive load and builds a more robust mental model. The meta-lesson: when something in a system seems stupidly named, ask whether it preserves discovery history.
