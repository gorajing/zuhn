Gupta distinguishes between mechanical endeavors (learning to drive) where prescriptions work, and anything involving art, creativity, or peak performance where they fail. The mechanism: when you introduce a prescription as an intermediary between you and your goal, your mind shifts focus to satisfying the intermediary instead of reaching the destination. You enter a deal you never signed up for — spending your life trying to live up to the prescription.

Naval reinforces this with concrete examples: business biographies are useless for building great businesses (inspiring but not actionable), people who ask for stock tips aren't serious about investing, and even a world-class performer who retraced their exact steps by mimicking themselves would fail. The details of greatness are not transmissible — and more fundamentally, not even knowable by the person who achieved it. Peak performers operate in a state where they're not consciously thinking about what they're doing.
