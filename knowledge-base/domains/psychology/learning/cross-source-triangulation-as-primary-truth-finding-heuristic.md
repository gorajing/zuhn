Shapiro offers a deceptively simple heuristic for truth-finding: read sources from opposite ends of the ideological spectrum (e.g., New York Times and Breitbart, Daily Wire and Washington Post), and identify the nexus where they agree. That shared core is likely factual; everything else is extrapolated opinion built on different premises. He frames this using the blind-men-and-the-elephant metaphor — each source feels a different part, but the overlap reveals the actual shape.

This is essentially adversarial collaboration applied to information consumption. It works because ideologically opposed sources have orthogonal incentive structures for what they emphasize and what they suppress. Their intersection survives both filters. The technique scales beyond political news to any domain with competing narratives: evaluating competing startup strategies (read both bulls and bears), assessing technical architecture decisions (read advocates and critics of a framework), or understanding market dynamics (compare bullish and bearish analyst reports). The practical cost is reading 2x the content, but the epistemic payoff is dramatically higher signal-to-noise ratio.
