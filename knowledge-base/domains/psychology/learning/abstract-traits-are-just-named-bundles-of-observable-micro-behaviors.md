When we call someone 'charismatic,' we're actually describing a bundle of micro-behaviors: smiling when people walk in, remembering names, changing vocal tonality, making eye contact, addressing everyone in the room. The shorthand label creates a false sense that the trait is innate and unlearnable. This is a linguistic trap — the compression of behaviors into a single word makes the skill seem monolithic and inaccessible.

The fix is operationalization: decompose any abstract trait into observable actions that can be seen with your eyes. If you can't observe it (feelings, intentions, manifestations), it doesn't belong in your instructions. This principle has direct application to team management and system design — when instructions fail, it's usually because one link in the chain contains an abstract term the person can't decompose into actions. The less skilled someone is, the more granular the instructions must be. This is also why 'be more charismatic' fails as feedback but 'smile when greeting, use their name within 10 seconds, vary your vocal pitch' succeeds.
