Paul Graham argues that the way to become a great programmer is to work a lot on hard problems, and the way to sustain that effort is to work on engaging projects you chose yourself. He cites Robert Morris learning networking by connecting Harvard to the Arpanet — a project so consuming he got kicked out of school, yet it built skills no class could match.

This maps to a broader principle about learning: curricula optimize for coverage and assessment, but skill development requires sustained deep engagement that only intrinsic motivation reliably provides. The implication is that structured education's main value may be exposure and credentialing, while the actual skill-building happens in the margins — personal projects, open-source contributions, and obsessive tinkering. For anyone designing their own learning path, the priority should be finding problems hard enough to stretch you and interesting enough to keep you working.
