When Isaac Newton realized that existing mathematics couldn't calculate the motion of a falling moon under an inverse square force field, he didn't search for workarounds — he invented calculus. He created it at the same rate college freshmen learn it today, driven purely by the demands of his specific problem.

This pattern recurs across domains: when existing tools are genuinely insufficient for the problem you're trying to solve, the most productive response isn't to force-fit existing approaches but to build new tools entirely. The key insight is that the problem itself specifies what the tool needs to do — Newton didn't invent calculus in the abstract, he invented it because he needed to track continuous motion under varying force. The practical implication is that hitting a wall with current tools may signal an opportunity to create something fundamentally new, not a signal to give up or simplify the problem.
