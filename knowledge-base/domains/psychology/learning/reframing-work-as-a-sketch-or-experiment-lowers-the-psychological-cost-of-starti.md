Graham identifies a cluster of related techniques for getting through the early lame phase of ambitious work: calling something a sketch instead of a painting, a quick hack instead of software, an experiment instead of a product. The key insight is that these labels aren't lies — they're temporarily accurate, and they lower the internal standard being applied so the work can proceed.

This works best with media that match: fast iteration, low upfront commitment. Drawing in a notebook vs. carving stone. Digital vs. physical. Minecraft vs. AAA game development. When the gap between the early version and the final version is small by medium design, the psychological trick and the reality converge — crude tools make crude acceptable, freeing the creator to focus on ideas over execution.

The strategic move is to pre-commit to a reframe before starting. Decide in advance that this is an experiment where even failure generates knowledge. Then even when the project looks lame, the frame holds. This is more reliable than discipline ('just push through') because it changes how the work is evaluated, not how hard you push against that evaluation.
