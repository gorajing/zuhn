Patel makes an underappreciated point against both LLM maximalists and Sutton-style purists: human learning fits neither paradigm cleanly. We are clearly not predicting next tokens in supervised fashion, but we are also not running around collecting well-defined scalar rewards. Language, legal systems, most technology — none of these were reinvented from scratch by anyone currently alive. That process is much closer to imitation learning than to RL-from-scratch.

The analogy he offers: 'What planes are to birds, supervised learning might end up being to human cultural learning.' Planes don't flap — they exploit the same physics through a different mechanism. Supervised pretraining may be a non-biological but functionally equivalent way to transmit accumulated cultural knowledge into a model.

For anyone reasoning about AI architectures from biological first principles, this is a warning: biological intelligence is not a clean instance of any textbook learning paradigm, so arguments of the form 'real intelligence works like X, therefore AI must work like X' are on shaky ground from the start.
