Research by Justin Berg, Amy Wrzesniewski, and Adam Grant found that teaching growth mindset about personal skills was not enough to boost happiness or performance at work. Only when they added a growth mindset about the job itself — teaching people that their job is a set of flexible building blocks (tasks to accentuate, subtract, or swap with colleagues) — did participants get a sustainable happiness boost lasting at least six months, with no cost to performance.

Grant uses the metaphor: telling someone they can grow while they're stuck in a cage is useless. You need to also show them the walls are movable. This 'dual mindset' approach explains why standalone growth mindset interventions often show weak effects — they address self-belief without addressing environmental constraints. The practical implication is that any growth mindset intervention should include concrete scaffolding: temporary support structures (mentors, peers, tools) that help someone reach a new level, then gradually withdraw so the person learns to sustain it independently.
