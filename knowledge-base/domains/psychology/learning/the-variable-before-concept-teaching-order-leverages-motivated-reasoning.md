The tutorial introduces variables not by defining what a variable is, but by showing a story program where changing a character's name requires editing every occurrence manually. Only after the student feels the pain of this repetition does the instructor introduce variables as the solution. This 'problem-first' ordering creates a gap in the learner's understanding that the concept then fills.

This maps to the psychological principle of 'need for closure' — once a problem is presented, the brain actively seeks resolution, making the subsequent explanation stick more firmly. It's the same pattern used in good product design (show the user their pain before pitching the solution) and in persuasive writing (establish the problem before proposing the thesis). The key is that the pain must be viscerally felt, not abstractly described.
