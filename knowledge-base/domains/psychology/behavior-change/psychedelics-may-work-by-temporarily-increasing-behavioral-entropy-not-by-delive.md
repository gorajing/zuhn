Dr. Johnson's framework suggests that psychedelics don't heal through the specific content of the experience (visions, mystical feelings, emotional catharsis) but through a more fundamental mechanism: temporarily increasing the entropy or randomness of neural activity, which destabilizes the rigid patterns that constitute psychiatric disorders.

This entropy model explains why psychedelics show promise across such diverse conditions — smoking cessation, depression, alcoholism, end-of-life anxiety. These conditions share no surface similarity but all involve entrenched patterns. The psychedelic experience creates a transient state of heightened flexibility — a window where the brain's default patterns are loosened enough that new, healthier patterns can take hold. The implication is that the therapeutic value isn't in what you see during the trip but in the neuroplastic window it opens afterward.
