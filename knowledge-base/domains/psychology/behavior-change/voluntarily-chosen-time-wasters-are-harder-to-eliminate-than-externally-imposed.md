Graham distinguishes two categories of low-value activity: externally imposed (bureaucracy, errands, others' mistakes) and self-chosen (arguing online, addictive pastimes). Counterintuitively, the self-chosen category is harder to eliminate because these activities had to be compelling enough to trick you into choosing them voluntarily — they're optimized to hijack your instincts.

Online arguments exemplify this: when someone attacks you, the instinct to defend yourself feels righteous and necessary. But that instinct evolved for physical confrontations, not comment threads. Recognizing that self-chosen bullshit is the harder adversary reframes the problem — the biggest threat to your time isn't your boss or your commute, it's the things that feel like they matter in the moment but won't matter later. The sharp peak of seeming-to-matter is their signature.
