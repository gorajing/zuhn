In Johnson's Johns Hopkins pilot study, psilocybin-assisted therapy achieved approximately 80% smoking cessation at 6 months — compared to 25-35% for the best available pharmacological treatments (varenicline/Chantix). This is not an incremental improvement; it is a categorically different effect size that suggests a fundamentally different mechanism. Standard smoking cessation treatments work on nicotine receptor pharmacology — they address the chemical hook. Psilocybin does not interact with nicotine receptors at all. Instead, it appears to work by disrupting the broader cognitive and behavioral patterns surrounding addiction — the narrowed repertoire Johnson describes. This result, while from a small pilot study requiring larger replication, suggests that treating addiction as a cognitive flexibility problem rather than a receptor pharmacology problem may be the more productive frame. The shelf life is time-sensitive because larger Phase 2/3 trials are ongoing and will either confirm or revise these dramatic numbers.
