Hormozi distinguishes shame (breaking someone else's rules), guilt (breaking your own rules), and cringe (supposed secondhand embarrassment). He argues cringe is not genuine aesthetic reaction but a defensive move by people whose status is being threatened by your visible effort. The reframe: when someone says 'that's cringe' they're really saying 'I'm beginning to lose status relative to you.' This inverts the signal — cringe accusations become confirmation you're moving up. The practical test is to ask whose rules are being broken, whether you agreed to those rules, and what outcome those rules optimize for. Most cringe rules optimize for keeping everyone inside the critic's peer group.
