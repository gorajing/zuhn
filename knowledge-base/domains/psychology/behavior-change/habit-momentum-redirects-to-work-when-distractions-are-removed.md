Graham describes an elegant inversion: the same habits that enabled procrastination become productive when distractions are removed. You're accustomed to sitting at your computer for hours—that behavioral momentum doesn't disappear when the internet is gone. Instead, faced with a machine that can only do work, you think 'I can't just sit here' and start working.

This reframes the problem of internet addiction as partly a resource to be redirected rather than purely a vice to be eliminated. The hours of sustained computer attention aren't the problem—the content consuming that attention is. By changing the environment rather than trying to change the behavior, you harness the existing habit loop (sit down → do something at the computer) and simply swap the available options. This is a specific instance of the broader principle that it's easier to redirect behavioral energy than to create it from scratch.
