The Environmental Kuznets Curve — where pollution rises with early industrialization then falls as wealth grows — appears to extend beyond air and water quality to biodiversity protection. North America, Europe, and East Asia have all significantly increased forest cover, and even China has undertaken massive reforestation and banned Yangtze River fishing. Steve Pinker's theory in 'Better Angels of Our Nature' that security and comfort breed altruism seems validated by these conservation trends. This matters because it suggests the pessimistic 'race to the bottom' narrative — where economic competition drives ever-worse environmental outcomes — is empirically wrong for local and national environments. The practical implication is that economic development may be the most effective long-term conservation strategy, and that the biggest environmental risks come not from wealthy nations becoming more rapacious but from geopolitical incentives that override the natural wealth-to-conservation progression.
