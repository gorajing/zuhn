The author describes a specific ritual: before bed, write down a few small goals achievable the next day. Completing them and checking them off triggers dopamine release, creating a positive feedback loop that makes you 'addicted to coding.' The inverse—working on problems too large to complete—creates a negative feedback loop where effort produces no reward, leading to learned helplessness and quitting.

This maps to the broader principle that motivation is downstream of reward timing, not willpower. The guitar analogy is precise: learning to press strings without ever playing a song kills motivation. Similarly, building a full working demo quickly—even an ugly one—provides the reward signal needed to sustain the painful early learning phase. The strategic insight is to engineer your reward schedule deliberately rather than relying on intrinsic motivation, which is unreliable during the competence trough.
