The speaker names the three predictable failure modes of paying per story point: engineers inflate point counts ('add a button? 45 story points'), engineers rush and quality drops, and engineers get 'sharp-elbowed' and competitive in a way you don't want in engineering culture. His mitigation is not to monitor harder but to engineer an opposing incentive into a different role. Strategists — former PMs and engineers who scope the work with the client — are compensated on net revenue / customer happiness, not point volume, and they plus the client must approve every ticket through multiple rounds of QA before an engineer is paid for it. This is a concrete application of Goodhart's Law mitigation: rather than trusting a single metric, you create a second party whose pay depends on the metric NOT being gamed. The general principle: whenever you tie pay to an output metric, ask who in the org is paid to defend the quality that metric ignores — if no one, the metric will be exploited.
