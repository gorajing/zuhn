The early Islamic empire is often described as 'spreading by the sword,' but the reality was more nuanced. Non-Muslims weren't forced to convert — they paid a higher tax (jizya). This created a steady economic incentive that, over decades, drove mass voluntary conversion without explicit coercion.

This is a textbook example of incentive architecture outperforming direct compulsion. The empire got the behavioral outcome (widespread conversion) while maintaining the moral high ground ('religion must not be an act of compulsion'). The principle generalizes: persistent asymmetric costs attached to the status quo can drive population-level behavior change more reliably than one-time mandates or persuasion campaigns.
