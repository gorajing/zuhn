Several practical techniques exploit the gap between how we judge different categories of work. By telling yourself a painting is just a sketch or new software is just a quick hack, you invoke a lower standard for evaluation. Once the project gains momentum, you can gradually convert it into something more serious. This works because the psychological barrier isn't really about quality — it's about the mismatch between ambition and current output.

The same principle extends to choosing fast, low-commitment media. Digital tools let you start with almost no upfront commitment compared to carving stone. And framing work as learning or experimentation rather than creation means even genuine failure produces value — if the theorem turns out to be false or the structural member fails under stress, you've gained knowledge. Lisa Randall's suggestion to treat new things as experiments eliminates the concept of failure entirely, leaving only results.
