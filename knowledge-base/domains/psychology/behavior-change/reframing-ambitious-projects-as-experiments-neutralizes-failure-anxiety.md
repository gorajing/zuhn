Graham identifies several psychological reframing techniques that successful creators use to push through the ugly early phase of ambitious work: calling a painting 'just a sketch,' software 'just a hack,' or an entire project 'an experiment.' These aren't self-deception — they're calibration tools that lower the psychological activation energy for starting.

The experiment frame is particularly powerful because it's actually true: if a problem is sharply defined, failure itself is knowledge. A disproven theorem or a structural member that breaks under stress tells you something real. Lisa Randall's version — treat new things as experiments where you either learn to give up or learn to iterate — eliminates the binary success/failure frame entirely. Combined with choosing low-commitment mediums that allow fast iteration, this approach makes starting ambitious work feel much less risky than the default framing suggests.
