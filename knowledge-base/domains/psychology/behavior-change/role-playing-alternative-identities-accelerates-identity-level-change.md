Van der Kolk's 'Shakespeare in the Courts' program assigns juvenile delinquents Shakespearean roles, and the results are striking. Playing a king or warrior gives participants a felt bodily experience of dignity, power, and agency — states their trauma history may have never allowed them to access.

This works because identity change isn't primarily cognitive — you can't think yourself into feeling like a different person. But you can act yourself into it. The body leads and the self-concept follows. This principle has broad applications: founders practicing investor pitches, leaders rehearsing difficult conversations, or anyone trying to expand their behavioral repertoire. The key insight is that 'trying on' a role physically, not just mentally, creates neural pathways for that identity to become accessible in real life.
