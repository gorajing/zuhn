Harlow's experiments showed that infant rhesus monkeys overwhelmingly preferred a soft cloth surrogate mother that provided no food over a wire surrogate that dispensed milk. The monkeys would only briefly visit the wire mother to feed, then immediately return to cling to the cloth mother — especially when frightened.

This demolished the prevailing behaviorist theory that attachment forms through association with food provision (the 'cupboard theory'). The cross-domain implication is powerful: in any relationship — parenting, management, mentorship — emotional safety and comfort are more binding than transactional value delivery. People stay where they feel safe, not where they're best compensated.
