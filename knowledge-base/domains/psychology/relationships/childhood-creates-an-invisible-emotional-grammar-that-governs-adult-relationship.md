De Botton uses a powerful metaphor: just as no one remembers learning to speak yet everyone speaks fluently, no one remembers learning emotional patterns yet everyone has deeply ingrained ones. Between ages zero and ten, children learn whether humans can be trusted, whether communication works, what happens when someone gets angry, and how to express needs. These patterns are as invisible and persistent as a regional accent.

Some people grow up with an emotional grammar where every man is frightening, every dependent person is depressed, or every kind person is slightly sadistic. These are suboptimal languages acquired in childhood that people carry unconsciously into adulthood, where they govern relationship behavior. Understanding this is the first step to changing it.
