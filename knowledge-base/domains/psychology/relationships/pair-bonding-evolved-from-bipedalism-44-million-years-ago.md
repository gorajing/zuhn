Fisher traces the evolution of human romantic love to 4.4 million years ago when our ancestors were forced out of disappearing trees onto dangerous open grasslands. Walking on two legs meant females had to carry babies in their arms instead of on their backs (as chimpanzees do), while also carrying sticks and stones for protection.

A female carrying the equivalent of a 20-pound bowling ball in one arm while defending herself with the other needed a partner. A male could not protect a whole group of females — but he could protect one. This created what Fisher calls the 'monogamy threshold,' selecting for males who would stay and females who preferred males that stayed. Those who did not pair up did not pass their DNA forward, leaving modern humans with a powerful capacity for romantic love and attachment.
