A.E. Fisher's 1955 experiment divided puppies into three groups: consistently treated kindly, consistently punished, or randomly alternating between the two. The third group — raised in uncertainty — became the most attached to and dependent on the researchers. Guy Murchie named this the 'Polarity Principle': stress and uncertainty are ingredients in attachment, not obstacles to it.

This has profound cross-domain implications. In relationships, intermittent reinforcement (hot-cold behavior) creates stronger emotional bonds than steady warmth, which partly explains why people stay in toxic relationships. In product design, variable reward schedules (slot machines, social media feeds) exploit the same mechanism. Recognizing this pattern is the first step to distinguishing healthy attachment from uncertainty-driven dependence — in personal relationships, in consumer behavior, and in organizational loyalty.
