De Botton describes how avoidantly attached people sabotage good relationships because healthy love feels threatening. Using the metaphor of a prison camp survivor who panics at a banquet because they cannot metabolize abundance, he explains that people who grew up without adequate love cannot tolerate it when offered. They retreat to familiar suffering.

This explains the empirically observed pattern: children of alcoholics partner with alcoholics; children of violent parents find volatile partners. Freud called this the repetition compulsion -- humans are not guided by pleasure-seeking but by pattern-repetition, often of pain. The optimistic implication is that once someone understands this mechanism, they can begin to consciously choose differently, tolerating the discomfort of healthy love rather than retreating to familiar dysfunction.
