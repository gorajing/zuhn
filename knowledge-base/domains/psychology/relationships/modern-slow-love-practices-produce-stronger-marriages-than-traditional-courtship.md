Fisher's study of 1,100 married people found that 81% would remarry their current partner. She attributes this to the modern 'slow love' pattern — hookups, friends with benefits, cohabitation — which she reframes not as recklessness but as an extended vetting process. A parallel finding: 67% of cohabiting singles cited fear of divorce (legal, financial, personal fallout) as the reason they hadn't yet married.

The neurological basis: extended courtship allows the initial dopamine-serotonin storm of romantic love to subside, re-engaging prefrontal decision-making. Partners learn how the other handles money, arguments, family, and health before committing. Fisher's framing — 'marriage used to be the beginning of a relationship, now it's the finale' — suggests that slower commitment timelines may be an evolutionary improvement in partner selection, not a cultural decline.
