Fisher's fMRI studies consistently show that the neural signature of romantic love is not scattered across emotional brain regions (which vary person to person) but concentrated in the ventral tegmental area (VTA), a tiny factory near the base of the brain that produces dopamine. This region lies adjacent to circuits for thirst and hunger.

This placement is not coincidental: thirst and hunger keep you alive today, while romantic love drives you to focus mating energy on a single individual and send your DNA into tomorrow. Fisher reclassified romantic love from an emotion to a basic mating drive — a survival mechanism that evolved millions of years ago. The three brain systems (sex drive for seeking partners broadly, romantic love for focusing on one, attachment for staying together through childrearing) evolved in tandem to serve complementary reproductive functions.
