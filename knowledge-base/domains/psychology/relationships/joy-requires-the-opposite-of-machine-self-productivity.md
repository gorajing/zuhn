Bowler identifies the preconditions that consistently produce joy: emotional availability and connection, openness to being surprised, and willingness to put down the phone and be 'experientially surprisable.' These are the direct opposite of the optimized, controlled, efficient 'machine self' that productivity culture demands.

This creates a fundamental tension: the more you optimize for productivity and control, the more you eliminate the conditions for joy. A person who wants to be surprised is much more likely to find joy than someone who has every minute scheduled. Joy ultimately requires love and connection — it's what makes you feel 'it feels good to be alive, to be here, to be put together with whatever you have left.' This is available to anyone, including those in depression, grief, or deep boredom.
