Nagoski's 'pleasure is the measure' thesis directly challenges how both individuals and researchers evaluate sexual health. Standard metrics — frequency per week, orgasm consistency, duration — are proxy variables that can diverge dramatically from actual satisfaction. Couples can have frequent, orgasmic sex and feel deeply disconnected, while others can have infrequent encounters with high pleasure and connection.

This is a textbook case of Goodhart's Law applied to intimate relationships: once frequency or orgasm becomes the target, it ceases to be a good measure. People optimize for the metric rather than the underlying experience. The fix is measuring what actually matters — subjective pleasure and felt connection — which requires honest self-report rather than countable proxies. This principle generalizes to any domain where easily measured proxies crowd out harder-to-measure outcomes that actually matter.
