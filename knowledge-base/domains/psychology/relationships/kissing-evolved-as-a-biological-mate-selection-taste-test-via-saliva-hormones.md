Evolutionary psychologists argue that modern kissing descended from kiss-feeding — the mouth-to-mouth exchange of pre-chewed food between mothers and infants, still common in many primates. This behavior established mouth-to-mouth contact as an intimate, trust-laden act.

Beyond its social bonding function, kissing serves as a biological information exchange. Mucous membranes in the mouth are permeable to hormones like testosterone, effectively making a kiss a subconscious taste-test of a potential mate's health and genetic compatibility. Pre-historic people who enjoyed kissing and did it more often may have made better mate selections, reproduced more successfully, and passed on the preference — explaining why humans today are neurologically wired to find kissing pleasurable.
