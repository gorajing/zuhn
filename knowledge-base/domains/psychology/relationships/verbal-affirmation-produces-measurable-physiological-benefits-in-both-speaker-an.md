Helen Fisher cites research showing that saying nice things to a partner doesn't just improve emotional climate — it produces measurable physiological changes: reduced cholesterol, reduced cortisol (stress hormone), and boosted immune function in the recipient. Critically, the speaker receives the same physiological benefits, making this a rare bidirectional health intervention.

This finding bridges psychology and health: a zero-cost daily practice (Fisher suggests aiming for five positive statements) that functions as both a relationship maintenance tool and a stress-reduction intervention. The bidirectional nature is the surprising element — it suggests that expressing appreciation may be as much a self-care practice as an other-care practice. This maps to broader research on how prosocial behavior improves the health of the actor, not just the recipient.
