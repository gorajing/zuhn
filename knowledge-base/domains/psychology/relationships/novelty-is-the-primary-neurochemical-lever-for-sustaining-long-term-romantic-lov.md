Helen Fisher identifies three brain systems for mating — sex drive (testosterone), romantic love (dopamine), and deep attachment (oxytocin) — and argues each requires deliberate maintenance. The most actionable lever is novelty: new experiences together drive the dopamine system that sustains romantic love. This isn't limited to grand gestures — a different restaurant, reading together, a new discussion topic all qualify.

The practical framework is: regular sex (drives testosterone and oxytocin), novel experiences together (drives dopamine), and physical touch (drives oxytocin). Fisher's brain scanning study of couples married an average of 21 years who were still deeply in love found three active brain regions: empathy, emotional self-regulation, and 'positive illusions' — the ability to overlook what you don't like and focus on what you do. This maps directly to actionable daily behavior: say several nice things daily (Fisher suggests five), which measurably reduces cortisol and boosts immune function in both partners.
