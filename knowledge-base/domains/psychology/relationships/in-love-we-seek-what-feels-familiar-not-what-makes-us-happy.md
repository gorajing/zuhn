De Botton describes Freud's repetition compulsion applied to relationships: human beings are not guided by a search for pleasure but by a search to repeat patterns, often of pain. Children of alcoholics head for partners struggling with alcohol. Offspring of violent parents head for people struggling with temper.

The mechanism is childhood emotional education — between ages 0 and 10, we learn an 'emotional language' as invisibly as we learn grammar. If your emotional dialect includes 'every man is frightening' or 'everyone you depend on is depressed,' you carry that into adulthood unconsciously. When healthy love arrives, it feels unfamiliar and therefore threatening — like a prison camp survivor panicking at a banquet. The avoidantly attached person says 'this is too intense' and withdraws, not because love is bad but because it is too good. The insight is that awareness of these patterns is the first step to breaking them.
