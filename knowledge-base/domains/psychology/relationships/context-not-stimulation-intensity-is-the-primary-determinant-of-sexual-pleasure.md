Nagoski's central empirical claim is that the brain evaluates identical sensory input differently depending on context. The same touch that produces pleasure in a safe, connected moment produces irritation or discomfort under stress. This means optimizing for sexual satisfaction is fundamentally an environmental and relational design problem, not a technique problem.

This has a direct parallel to flow states in performance psychology: the same task difficulty produces flow or anxiety depending on whether the performer feels psychologically safe. It also connects to embodied cognition research showing that emotional context physically alters sensory processing. The actionable takeaway is that investing in reducing stress, building emotional safety, and curating the surrounding context yields far higher returns than learning new techniques.
