Johnson reflects that cybercrime was an addiction, and like all addictions, it made real love impossible. He lied to his first wife every day for nine years. He couldn't stop committing crime even when it was destroying his relationships. He argues the mechanism is universal: addiction demands that everything else — love, friendship, honesty — be subordinated to feeding the addiction. You become 'truly a despicable human being' not through malice but through the progressive erosion of every value that competes with the addiction.

His eventual redemption came through three stages: his sister's unconditional love (driving seven hours just to say 'I love you' for 10 minutes), two and a half years of prison reflection to accept that 'I committed crime because I wanted to, chose to,' and cognitive behavioral therapy. But the turning point was his current wife Michelle, who 'didn't need me for what I could give her — she just wanted me for me.' This broke the pattern of transactional relationships driven by addiction. The insight applies broadly: recovery requires both internal accountability and at least one relationship where you're valued for who you are rather than what you produce.
