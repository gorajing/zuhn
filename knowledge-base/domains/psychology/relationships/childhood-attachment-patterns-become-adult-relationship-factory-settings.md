Kennedy explains that attachment is an evolutionary survival mechanism — children literally cannot survive alone, so they are constantly calibrating: 'What parts of me keep me connected to my parents? What parts of me exist in aloneness?' A child whose distress is met with 'you are being ridiculous' learns two things: it is not safe to be upset in close relationships, and other people know how they feel better than they do. These lessons become 'factory settings' — unconscious defaults that generalize from parent to all relationships. The adult who cannot ask for help, who minimizes their own feelings, who people-pleases compulsively is running childhood settings. Kennedy notes 'we have the same body today as we had in childhood — it is a collection of all the memories.' The settings can be changed, but only through deliberate work, not through aging.
