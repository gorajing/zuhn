De Botton introduces attachment theory (Bowlby and Ainsworth, 1950s UK) through its origin: during WWII, children evacuated from London to escape bombing began exhibiting cataclysmic distress — bed-wetting, rage, inability to eat. Researchers realized the emotional damage of separation exceeded the physical danger of bombs. Children were sent home, averting a generational catastrophe.

This research spawned attachment theory, which categorizes how people relate to intimacy based on childhood caregiver experiences. Avoidant attachment (pulling away when things get intimate), anxious attachment (clinging desperately), and secure attachment are the main styles. The critical statistic: caregivers are estimated to let down about 50% of children. Half the population carries attachment wounds. These are not the end of the story — they can be worked with — but they must first be recognized. The therapeutic age de Botton advocates starts with this recognition.
