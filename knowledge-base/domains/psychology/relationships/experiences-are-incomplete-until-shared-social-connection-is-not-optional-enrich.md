Michael's most surprising post-isolation insight wasn't about boredom or time distortion but about the nature of experience itself. He reported: 'If you only have your own experiences, you're not fully having them. You have to have someone else to listen to them and react to them and then you've fully experienced them.' He also noted that what he missed most wasn't entertainment or food — it was having a meal WITH people, hearing words other than his own.

This maps to the proverb he reconsidered: 'He who travels fastest travels alone, but he who travels furthest travels with others.' The distinction between speed and distance is key — isolation may enable short-term efficiency, but sustained growth and meaning require social processing. The brain literally generates imaginary companions in true isolation (talking to cameras, anthropomorphizing objects like volleyballs or shock buttons), suggesting social cognition isn't optional overhead but core architecture.
