Alain de Botton argues that the romantic revolution of the late 18th century — the idea that individuals should choose partners by the movements of their own heart — has been a beautiful but catastrophic experiment. We are not appreciably happier than when marriages were arranged for dynastic reasons.

The romantic worldview venerated instinct over reason, feeling over analysis, and music over philosophy. It created prejudices: discussing money is unromantic, analyzing childhood patterns is unromantic, criticizing your partner is offensive ('you're trying to change me'). Ancient Greeks would be baffled — they saw love as a process of education. De Botton proposes moving from the romantic age to a 'therapeutic age' that applies psychotherapy's 120 years of insights to how we form and maintain relationships. The key shift: love is not a feeling you discover but a skill you develop.
