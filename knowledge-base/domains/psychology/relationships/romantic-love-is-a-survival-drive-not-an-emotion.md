Helen Fisher's brain scanning research surprised even her: she expected to find romantic love linked to emotion circuits and cognitive processes. Instead, all subjects showed activity in the ventral tegmental area (VTA), a tiny factory near the base of the brain that produces dopamine. This region sits right next to the circuits that orchestrate thirst and hunger.

This reclassifies romantic love from an emotion to a survival mechanism. Thirst and hunger keep you alive today; romantic love focuses your mating energy on a single individual to drive your DNA into tomorrow. Fisher identifies three evolved brain systems operating in tandem: sex drive (to seek a range of partners), romantic love (to focus on one), and attachment (to stay together long enough to raise a child through infancy).
