Harry Harlow's famous 1950s experiments demonstrated that infant monkeys preferred a soft, comforting cloth mother over a wire mother that provided food — proving that warmth and comfort outweigh nourishment in forming attachment. Even more striking, when Harlow built a rejecting mother that blasted pressurized air at approaching monkeys, the infants clung tighter than those with non-rejecting mothers.

A.E. Fisher's 1955 puppy experiment extended this finding: puppies randomly treated with kindness or punishment became more attached to researchers than puppies treated with consistent kindness or consistent punishment. Guy Murchie called this the Polarity Principle — stress, including the mental stress of uncertainty, is an ingredient in attachment. This has profound implications for understanding trauma bonds, toxic relationships, and why intermittent reinforcement (slot machines, unpredictable social media rewards) creates the strongest behavioral hooks.
