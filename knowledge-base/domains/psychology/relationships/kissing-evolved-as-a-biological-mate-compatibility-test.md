Evolutionary psychologists argue that kissing descended from kiss-feeding — the mouth-to-mouth transfer of pre-chewed food between mothers and infants, still common in primates. This established mouth-to-mouth contact as an intimacy signal.

Beyond the cultural ritual, kissing serves a biological function: mucous membranes in the mouth are permeable to hormones like testosterone, making a kiss a literal taste-test of a potential mate's health and genetic compatibility. Pre-historic humans who enjoyed kissing may have made better mate selections, reproduced more successfully, and passed on the preference — explaining why kissing feels rewarding today. The implication is that romantic rituals we consider purely cultural often have deep biological selection pressures behind them.
