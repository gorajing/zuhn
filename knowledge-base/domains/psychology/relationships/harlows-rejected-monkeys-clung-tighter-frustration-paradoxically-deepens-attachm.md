Harry Harlow's monkey experiments at the University of Wisconsin revealed that infant monkeys chose a soft cloth surrogate mother over a wire mother that provided food — demonstrating that comfort trumps nourishment in forming attachment. Even more striking, when Harlow built a 'rejecting mother' that blasted pressurized air at approaching infants, the monkeys clung tighter than control groups.

Eckhard Hess replicated this with ducklings: electric shocks meant to discourage following their imprinted object only strengthened the following behavior. The instinct for warmth and security in newborns is so fundamental that it not only resists frustration but is amplified by it. This has direct applications in understanding why abusive relationships create intense bonds, why harsh organizational cultures can paradoxically increase loyalty, and why 'tough love' approaches to attachment disorders often backfire.
