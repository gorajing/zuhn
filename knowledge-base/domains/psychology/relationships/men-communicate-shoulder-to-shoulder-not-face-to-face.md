Reeves presents evidence that men communicate more comfortably shoulder-to-shoulder than face-to-face. Face-to-face positioning is experienced as threatening. This explains why fishing trips, hikes, drives, and bench-sitting produce more honest male conversation than sitting across from each other in a room.

A volunteer who works with troubled boys simply takes them for walks rather than sitting them down in a classroom -- and finds they are much more likely to open up. Psychologists are increasingly adopting walk-and-talk therapy for this reason. The practical implication is that any system designed to reach men -- therapy, mentorship, support groups -- should be structured around shared activity rather than face-to-face conversation.
