The Harvard Study of Adult Development, the longest study of adult life ever conducted, has tracked 724 men and their families since 1938 across dramatically different socioeconomic backgrounds — Harvard sophomores and boys from Boston's poorest families. The central finding is unambiguous: the quality of relationships is the strongest predictor of both physical health and life satisfaction.

People with warm, supportive connections stayed healthier longer and lived longer than those who were isolated or in conflict-ridden relationships. This held true regardless of wealth, social class, or achievement. When asked how they survived the Great Depression and World War II, participants universally pointed to their relationships — neighbors sharing resources, fellow soldiers providing support, letters from home sustaining morale. The research suggests that most people overestimate the importance of wealth and fame while underestimating the health impact of social connection.
