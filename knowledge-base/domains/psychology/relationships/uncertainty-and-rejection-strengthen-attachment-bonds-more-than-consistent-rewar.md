Fisher's puppy studies and Harlow's wire mother experiments converge on a counterintuitive finding: organisms that experience intermittent reinforcement — alternating reward and rejection — form stronger attachment bonds than those receiving consistent positive feedback. This is the Polarity Principle.

This maps directly to variable ratio reinforcement schedules in behavioral psychology. The uncertainty creates a compulsive seeking behavior that feels like intense attachment. The mechanism explains why 'playing hard to get' works, why slot machines are addictive, and why abusive relationships can feel more intense than stable ones. The insight is that attachment intensity and attachment health are orthogonal dimensions — strong bonds aren't necessarily good bonds.
