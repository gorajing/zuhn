When the architects of the Federal Reserve Act needed public support, they didn't run a PR campaign — they publicly denounced their own bill in newspapers. The public, reasoning that anything bankers opposed must serve ordinary people, rallied behind legislation designed by those very bankers. This is a masterclass in adversarial persuasion: by positioning themselves as opponents, the manipulators hijacked the public's distrust of institutions and redirected it.

This pattern recurs throughout history and modern politics. The technique works because it exploits a genuine and often useful heuristic (cui bono — who benefits?) while inverting its signal. Recognizing manufactured opposition requires asking not just 'who opposes this?' but 'does this opposition cost the opponent anything real?' Costless opposition — newspaper quotes with no material sacrifice — should trigger skepticism rather than trust.
