Investor audiences at early-stage pitches face a double handicap: limited time and limited technical background. In this evaluation vacuum, the founder's own confidence in what they've built becomes — almost by default — the single biggest piece of evidence. This isn't about faking enthusiasm; Graham explicitly warns against saying 'we're passionate' or 'our product is great,' which audiences read as hollow or dishonest.

The effective form is implicit confidence: presenting as though you're doing investors a favor by letting them know about your work. This applies far beyond pitches — any time you're presenting complex work to a non-expert audience (board meetings, cross-functional reviews, client presentations), your visible relationship to your own work heavily shapes their perception of it. The asymmetry matters: nervousness and apology are interpreted as evidence against the product, not just against the presenter.
