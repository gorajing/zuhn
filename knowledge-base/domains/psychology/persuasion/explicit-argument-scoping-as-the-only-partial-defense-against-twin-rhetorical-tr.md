After being accused of committing a malicious streetlight effect in his crime statistics posts, Alexander's practical takeaway was to explicitly scope his arguments — stating upfront that he's addressing crime rates specifically, and will address disorder separately. This is the communication equivalent of defining your terms before a debate.

The limitation is that this defense is imperfect. Motivated critics can still accuse you of choosing to address the less important half of the issue, or of using the promise of 'I'll address that later' as a way to bury inconvenient concerns. But it forces the accusation to be about editorial priorities rather than intellectual dishonesty, which is a more defensible position. For anyone writing about contested empirical topics, the lesson is: scope early, scope explicitly, and acknowledge the existence of related concerns you're not addressing in the current piece.
