There's a non-random pattern in the phonetic shape of profanity: swear words tend to be monosyllabic, feature plosive consonants (p, t, k, b, d, g), and can be shouted quickly. This isn't coincidence — it's cultural selection at work. Words that function as emotional weapons need to be deployable in the heat of the moment (short), carry percussive acoustic energy (plosives), and be unmistakable even in noisy environments.

This connects to a broader principle about memetic fitness: the 'design' of culturally transmitted units isn't random but shaped by selection pressures just as biological traits are. The most durable swear words are the ones optimally shaped for their function — a form of cultural engineering without an engineer. This has implications for anyone designing language for impact: brevity, hard sounds, and phonetic distinctiveness increase memorability and emotional punch.
