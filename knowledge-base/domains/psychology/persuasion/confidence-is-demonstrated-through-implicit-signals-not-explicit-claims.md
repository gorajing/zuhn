There's a fundamental asymmetry in persuasion: explicit self-promotion ('we're passionate,' 'our product is great') gets filtered out or actively triggers negative judgment, while the same message conveyed implicitly through behavior lands with full force. This is because audiences have well-calibrated bullshit detectors for direct claims but are much more susceptible to indirect signals.

The practical implication is that confidence must be shown, not told. Speaking with conviction about the problem you've solved, showing genuine excitement through your demeanor rather than your words, and treating the pitch as doing investors a favor by telling them about your work — these implicit signals carry far more weight than any direct assertion. This principle extends beyond pitching to any persuasion context: the more important the claim, the more it must be demonstrated rather than stated.
