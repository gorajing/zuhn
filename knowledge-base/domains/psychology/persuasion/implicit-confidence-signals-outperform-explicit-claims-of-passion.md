Graham identifies a critical asymmetry in investor presentations: between brief time and limited technical background, many investors can't properly evaluate the product. The single biggest piece of evidence becomes the founder's own confidence. But this must be shown, not told.

Explicitly saying 'we're passionate' or 'our product is great' triggers a credibility penalty—people ignore it or actively mark you as inauthentic. The effective approach is to reframe your mental model: if you've truly made something good, you're doing investors a favor by telling them about it. This isn't arrogance—it's the logical conclusion of genuine belief in your work. If you don't believe investors would benefit from investing, why are you investing your own time?
