Graham identifies a fundamental asymmetry in persuasion: the same message ('we believe in this') works powerfully when shown through behavior but backfires when stated directly. Audiences have finely tuned detectors for hollow self-promotion and will actively downgrade speakers who trigger them. The mechanism is that stated claims ('we're passionate') are cheap signals anyone can produce, so they carry no information. But behavioral signals—speaking with genuine authority about the problem, showing real excitement during a demo, treating investors as lucky to hear about this—are harder to fake and therefore credible. This maps to a broader principle in signaling theory: costly or hard-to-fake signals are believed, while cheap verbal claims are discounted or penalized.
