Graham draws a sharp line between showing and telling confidence. Explicitly stating passion or product quality is not just ineffective—it's actively counterproductive, causing audiences to write you off. The confidence signal must be structural: embedded in how you present, what you emphasize, and the frame you put around your work.

The deeper principle is about information theory in persuasion. When someone tells you they're great, that's zero-information because everyone says it. When someone's behavior radiates genuine conviction—through the specificity of their knowledge, their comfort with hard questions, their evident excitement about technical details—that's high-information because it's costly to fake. Graham frames this as doing investors a favor by telling them about your work, which inverts the supplicant dynamic most founders default to.
