Walter describes maintaining a personal notes document where he records the specific questions executives ask and the feedback they give across various meetings and forums. Over time, this document becomes a predictive model of each executive's decision-making patterns, concerns, and priorities.

This practice is powerful because it converts scattered observations into a structured knowledge asset. Most people treat each executive interaction as isolated; Walter treats them as data points in a pattern. When he later needs executive support, he can anticipate objections and tailor his approach based on accumulated evidence rather than guesswork. The compound returns are significant — each additional data point improves the model, making influence attempts progressively more effective.
