Graham identifies that between brief presentation time and non-technical audiences, many investors simply cannot evaluate the actual product. In this information vacuum, the founder's own confidence becomes the dominant signal. But there's a critical distinction: you must show confidence implicitly, never state it explicitly.

Saying 'we're passionate' or 'our product is great' gets ignored or marks you as a bullshitter. The confidence must be structural — embedded in how you present, not what you claim. The reframe Graham offers is powerful: if you've truly made something good, you're doing investors a favor by telling them about it. If you don't believe that, maybe change what you're building.
