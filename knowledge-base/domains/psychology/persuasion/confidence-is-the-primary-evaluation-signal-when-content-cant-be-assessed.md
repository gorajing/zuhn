Graham identifies a fundamental asymmetry in investor presentations: founders who are bad at explaining talk to investors who are bad at understanding technical matters. In this low-information environment, confidence becomes the primary proxy for quality.

Critically, this must be shown not told. Saying 'we're passionate' or 'our product is great' gets ignored or marks you as a bullshitter. The confidence must be implicit — conveyed through how you present, not what you claim. The mental frame Graham suggests: if you've truly made something good, you're doing investors a favor by telling them about it. If you don't believe that, maybe change what you're building.
