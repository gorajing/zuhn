When Coca-Cola dropped 'sovereign remedy for headache and nervousness' as a slogan, the explicit reason was reach (advertise to the masses, not the few). But there was a second, quieter reason that Acquired highlights: you don't want a Pavlovian association between your brand and a negative state. Every time a customer sees an ad that mentions headaches, your brand gets paired in their head with the discomfort of having a headache. Over thousands of impressions, the brand starts to feel slightly bad to think about — even though you're nominally offering the cure.

The inverse strategy is what built Coke into a global emotional infrastructure: only ever associate the product with feelings the customer wants to have. Happiness. Family. Beautiful people. Sports heroes. Christmas. The polar bears. None of it has anything to do with the drink itself; all of it conditions a positive feeling that gets transferred to the can. By the time the customer is in a store, the can already feels good before they pick it up.

This applies far beyond beverages. Insurance companies that lead with imagery of accidents and disasters are anchoring their brand to fear; insurance companies that lead with peace of mind are anchoring their brand to relief. Pharma ads in the US do the negative version constantly (depression, pain, anxiety) because regulatory constraints force them to — and you can feel the difference in how the brands sit emotionally. Whenever the regulatory environment lets you choose, choose positive associations. The conditioning compounds, and once it's in place it's very hard for a competitor to dislodge.
