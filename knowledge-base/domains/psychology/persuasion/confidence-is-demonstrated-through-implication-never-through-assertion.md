Graham identifies a fundamental asymmetry in persuasion: explicit claims of quality or passion are automatically discounted or worse, mark you as a bullshitter. But implicit confidence—speaking with authority about your problem space, showing rather than telling—bypasses the audience's skepticism filter.

This maps to a broader principle in communication theory: meta-statements about your own credibility are paradoxically incredible. The frame 'you're doing investors a favor by telling them about this' is powerful precisely because it's a private attitude that shapes your demeanor, not a public claim. This show-don't-tell principle applies far beyond pitching—to writing, leadership, sales, and any context where you need to establish authority.
