Graham identifies a critical asymmetry in investor evaluation: because audiences lack the technical background to fully evaluate the product in a few minutes, the founder's own confidence becomes the single biggest piece of evidence. But this confidence must be shown, not stated. Explicit claims ('we're passionate,' 'our product is great') are worse than useless — they're actively discounted as cheap signals that anyone can produce.

This maps to a broader persuasion principle: sophisticated evaluators (investors, hiring managers, domain experts) have learned to discount verbal self-assessment entirely. What they can't easily fake-detect is the structural confidence that comes through in how someone talks about their work — the specificity of examples, the comfort with hard questions, the implicit assumption that the audience should be grateful for the opportunity. The medium is the message.
