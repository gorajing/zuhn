When evaluating early startups, investors lack the technical background and time to deeply assess the product. The single biggest initial evidence they rely on is the founders' own confidence. But this must be demonstrated implicitly — through energy, certainty in responses, and depth of understanding — never stated explicitly.

Explicit self-praise ('we're passionate,' 'our product is great') triggers a credibility penalty. Audiences either ignore these claims or actively downgrade the speaker as a bullshitter. The asymmetry is stark: implicit confidence is processed as authentic evidence, while explicit claims of the same quality are processed as noise or deception. This applies far beyond pitching — in any persuasion context, show-don't-tell is not just stylistic advice but a cognitive processing reality.
