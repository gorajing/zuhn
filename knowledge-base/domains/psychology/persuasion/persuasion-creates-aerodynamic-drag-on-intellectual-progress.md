Graham models the relationship between persuasion and discovery as a drag equation: the more surprising your conclusions, the more effort goes to overcoming reader resistance instead of developing the ideas further. At the limit, 100% of energy goes to persuasion and zero to discovery. This has a practical implication for anyone doing original thinking — optimizing for audience acceptance creates a ceiling on how novel your ideas can be. The people producing the most surprising insights are necessarily the ones who have stopped trying to persuade, because persuasion load scales with novelty. This is why discovery-oriented writers often appear arrogant — they're not posturing, they're just refusing to pay the persuasion tax.
