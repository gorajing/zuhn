Graham identifies a fundamental asymmetry in persuasion: explicit confidence claims ('we're passionate,' 'our product is great') are either ignored or actively count against you, while implicit confidence signals — speaking as if you're doing the audience a favor, demonstrating rather than claiming — build genuine conviction.

This maps to a broader principle about signaling theory: cheap signals (words anyone can say) carry no information, while costly signals (behavior that's hard to fake) carry strong information. A founder who frames their pitch as doing investors a favor by letting them invest is exhibiting costly confidence — it would be socially risky if unfounded. This same dynamic applies far beyond pitching: in job interviews, sales, and any persuasive context, show-don't-tell consistently outperforms explicit claims.
