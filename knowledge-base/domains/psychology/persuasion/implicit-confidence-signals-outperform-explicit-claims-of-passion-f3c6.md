Graham identifies a communication paradox: when audiences lack technical background to evaluate substance, your own confidence becomes the primary evidence they use. But explicit claims ('we're passionate,' 'our product is great') get filtered as noise or worse, mark you as a bullshitter.

The implication is that persuasion under information asymmetry works through show-not-tell. The right frame is 'you're doing investors a favor by telling them about this opportunity.' If you don't genuinely believe that, the fix isn't better presentation — it's changing what your company does. This maps to a broader principle: audiences with limited evaluation capacity rely on meta-signals (confidence, specificity, candor) more than content.
