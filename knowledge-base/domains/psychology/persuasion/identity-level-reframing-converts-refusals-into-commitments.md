When Sculley refused to leave PepsiCo for Apple — a rational decision given Pepsi's stability versus a 4-year-old garage startup — Jobs didn't counter with better compensation or logical arguments. Instead, he deployed what became one of the most famous recruiting lines in business history: 'Do you want to sell sugar water for the rest of your life, or do you want to come with me and change the world?'

This works because it operates at the identity level rather than the analytical level. Sculley's rational objections (risk, stability, career trajectory) were all valid at the job-comparison level. But Jobs reframed the decision to be about what kind of person Sculley wanted to be — someone who optimized for comfort or someone who pursued meaning. Once the frame shifts to identity, the rational objections feel petty by comparison. Sculley himself said he knew he 'would wonder for the rest of my life what I would have missed.' The technique is powerful precisely because it makes saying no feel like a permanent self-betrayal rather than a prudent career choice.
