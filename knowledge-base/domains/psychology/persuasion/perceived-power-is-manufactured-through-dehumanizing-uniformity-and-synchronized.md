Riot police are almost always outnumbered, so they must artificially create an advantage. Dehumanizing head-to-toe gear makes individual officers indistinguishable, creating a psychological barrier that makes both protesters and the officers themselves perceive the police as more powerful. Synchronized marching and collective action reinforce this — when individuals act as a visible unit, they feel they wield the power of the collective, and observers perceive them that way too.

This is a general principle of perceived power: coordination signals competence and strength beyond what raw numbers would suggest. It applies to business negotiations, brand presentation, and any context where a smaller entity needs to project authority against a larger one. The key insight is that the perception is bidirectional — the performers themselves internalize the projected power, creating a self-reinforcing confidence loop.
