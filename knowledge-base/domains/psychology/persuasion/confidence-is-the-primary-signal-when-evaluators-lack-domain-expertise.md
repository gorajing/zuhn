Graham identifies a structural communication problem: investors often lack the technical background to evaluate what founders have built, and the brief pitch format compounds this. In this low-information environment, the founder's own confidence becomes a dominant signal—not stated confidence ('we're passionate,' 'our product is great,' which gets discarded as bullshit), but demonstrated confidence through demeanor and framing.

This has a deeper implication: if you frame your pitch as doing investors a favor by telling them about your work, that belief restructures your entire delivery. If you don't genuinely believe investors would benefit from your startup, Graham suggests you should change what your company is doing rather than fake the confidence. The insight generalizes beyond pitching—whenever evaluators lack domain expertise, meta-signals about the presenter's conviction carry disproportionate weight.
