When audiences lack the technical background to evaluate what you've built, they fall back on proxy signals — and founder confidence is the single biggest one. But confidence must be implicit. Explicit claims like 'we're passionate' or 'our product is great' get ignored or worse, trigger bullshit detectors.

This creates a specific communication discipline: you must genuinely believe you're doing investors a favor by letting them invest, and that belief must come through in your demeanor rather than your words. If you can't project that authentic conviction, the problem may not be your presentation skills but your company's direction. The principle extends beyond pitching — in any persuasion context, stated confidence undermines itself while demonstrated confidence compounds.
