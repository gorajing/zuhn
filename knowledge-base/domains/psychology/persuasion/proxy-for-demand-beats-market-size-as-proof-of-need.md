Graham cites Sequoia's Greg McAdoo on looking for 'proxy for demand' — evidence that people are already doing something with inadequate tools that shows they need what you're building. This is fundamentally a persuasion principle about revealed preference: watching someone struggle with a spreadsheet to do what your product handles natively is more convincing than any market size estimate.

The hierarchy of evidence is clear: best is your own need (Apple, Microsoft, Yahoo, Google all grew from founders' personal needs), next is needs of people you know personally, and then broader user stories. This maps to a general persuasion principle — specific, concrete, personal stories are more memorable and convincing than abstract statistics. The actor analogy crystallizes it: telling investors about market size is like a new actor telling his parents how much Tom Hanks makes. The important part isn't the ceiling, it's how you get there.
