The video documents a textbook escalation pattern: invoke emergency powers → use popular mandate to override legislative opposition → replace the judiciary → reinterpret the constitution. Each step was individually defensible — the emergency session had constitutional backing, the new legislators were democratically elected, the judges were arguably obstructionist, and the term limit reinterpretation served 'the will of the people.' No single step looked like a coup.

This pattern is important to recognize because it applies beyond politics — to corporate governance, organizational power dynamics, and even personal relationships. The mechanism works because humans evaluate actions in isolation rather than as cumulative trajectories. Each decision has a locally-valid justification, but the trajectory is toward total consolidation. The antidote is pattern recognition: not asking 'is this individual action justified?' but 'what trajectory does this series of actions describe?' Bukele's 90% approval rating at each stage shows that popular support is not a reliable check against this pattern — it can actually be the fuel.
