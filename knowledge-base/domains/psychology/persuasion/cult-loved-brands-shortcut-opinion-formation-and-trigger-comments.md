When content invokes a category ('shoes,' 'operating systems,' 'pasta'), viewers have to construct an opinion from scratch before responding — a high-friction step most skip. When content invokes a specific cult-loved entity ('Nike,' 'Linux,' 'Carbone'), viewers already have a cached opinion. The cognitive work is done; all they need to do is hit the comment box.

This is why naming specific brands, founders, ideological movements, or polarizing figures as the subject of a stance consistently outperforms generic framing. The viewer skips the opinion-formation step and goes straight to defense or attack. The tactical move is to rewrite topic angles to swap out generic nouns for specific cult-loved proper nouns wherever possible.
