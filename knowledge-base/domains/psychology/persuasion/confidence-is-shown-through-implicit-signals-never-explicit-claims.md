When audiences lack technical background to evaluate your work directly, they rely heavily on your own confidence as a proxy signal. But this signal must be implicit: demonstrated through how you present, not declared in words. Saying 'we're passionate' triggers a credibility penalty — people either ignore it as noise or actively mark you as a bullshitter.

The underlying principle is that high-value signals must be costly to fake. Anyone can say 'our product is great,' so the statement carries zero information. But genuine deep knowledge of the problem, calm composure under questioning, and the framing that you're doing investors a favor by telling them about this — these are harder to manufacture and therefore more credible. If you don't genuinely believe investors would benefit from hearing about your work, the real fix isn't better presentation — it's changing what your company does.
