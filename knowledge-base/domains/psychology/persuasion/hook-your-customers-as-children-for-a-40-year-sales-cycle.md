Ben Gilbert and Doug DeMuro observe that Porsche effectively markets to people who will not become customers for three to five decades. The 911's shape, dashboard layout, and model-numbering lore have been preserved with only incremental refinement since 1963 specifically so that a child who posters a 911 on their bedroom wall in 1985 can walk into a dealership in 2025 and buy 'the same car' they dreamed about. Everything about the brand experience — the Heritage paint colors, the continuing parts support for vintage cars, the Stuttgart factory tours — is designed to reinforce that the emotional promise made to you as a child will still be honored when you can finally afford to act on it.

This generates an entirely different competitive dynamic than conventional marketing. You are not competing for today's buyer against today's alternatives; you are competing against decades-old memories and the emotional identity formation of a child. That's a vastly more defensible position, but it requires extreme continuity discipline — every time the 911 was nearly killed (the 928 era, the Wiedeking cost-cutting) the long-cycle customer promise was at risk.

Reference for any brand that serves aspirational purchases: your marketing budget today should include something aimed at people who cannot yet buy from you.
