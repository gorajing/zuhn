Ulbricht didn't just run a drug marketplace — he constructed a philosophical framework around it, positioning Silk Road as reducing violence by replacing cartels and street dealers with anonymous online transactions. His rules prohibiting products that 'cause harm to innocent people' created a moral architecture that made participants feel like freedom fighters rather than criminals. This ideological scaffolding serves multiple functions: it recruits more committed participants who see themselves as part of a cause, it sustains the founder's motivation through legal risk, and it generates community defenders even after prosecution. The pattern extends beyond dark web markets — any operation that successfully frames rule-breaking as moral duty (whistleblowing, civil disobedience, regulatory arbitrage) gains resilience that purely profit-driven operations lack.
