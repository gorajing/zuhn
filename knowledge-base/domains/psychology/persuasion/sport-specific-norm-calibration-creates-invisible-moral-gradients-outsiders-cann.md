Harris maps a spectrum from soccer (where fake fouls are accepted gamesmanship) to hockey (where punching is tolerated) to golf (where failing to sign a scorecard disqualifies a Masters winner). People outside a domain often can't perceive these moral gradients — they see 'cheating in sports' as a single category. But insiders understand that the identical act of deception carries radically different moral weight depending on where the domain's norms have settled.

This insight applies broadly to evaluating behavior across professional and social contexts. Every industry, community, and organization develops its own invisible norm calibration. What's sharp practice in one field is standard operating procedure in another. Effective judgment requires understanding the specific norm landscape of each domain rather than applying a universal moral framework. The person who doesn't understand golf's integrity culture literally cannot understand why Trump's behavior is diagnostic.
