Paul Graham observes that both major US parties calibrate their positions so precisely to voter preferences that policy differences effectively cancel out. The election then hinges on the one variable parties can't engineer: personal charisma. This pattern generalizes beyond politics — in any competitive arena where rivals converge on the same optimized playbook (startup pitches, job interviews, product demos), the human perception of energy, confidence, and likability becomes the decisive edge. The implication is that investing in substance alone is necessary but insufficient; the marginal return shifts to presentation once substance reaches parity.
