When investors can't fully evaluate technical work due to time constraints and knowledge gaps, the founder's own confidence becomes the single biggest evidence signal. But this must be shown, not told. Saying 'we're passionate' gets you written off as a bullshitter; demonstrating mastery of the problem and genuine excitement about the solution communicates the same thing credibly.

This maps to a deeper principle in persuasion: the most powerful messages are the ones the audience feels they discovered themselves rather than were told. An explicit claim ('we're great') invites scrutiny and skepticism. An implicit signal (deep command of the problem, obvious excitement) lets the audience draw their own conclusion — which they then hold more strongly because it feels like their own judgment, not your marketing.
