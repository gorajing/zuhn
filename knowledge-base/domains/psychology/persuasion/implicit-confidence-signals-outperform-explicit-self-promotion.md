Graham identifies a fundamental asymmetry in persuasion: explicit claims about your own qualities ('we're passionate,' 'our product is great') are automatically discounted or mark you as a bullshitter, while the same qualities demonstrated implicitly through deep knowledge, genuine enthusiasm about the problem, and command of details create authentic conviction.

This connects to a broader principle about communication credibility. The audience's evaluation of your confidence is the single biggest piece of evidence they use when they can't fully evaluate your technology. But that evaluation must come from behavioral signals — speaking with authority about the problem, showing genuine excitement during the demo, treating the investor meeting as doing them a favor rather than begging. The frame you bring to the interaction communicates more than any slide about your passion ever could.
