Arthur Sackler pioneered pharmaceutical demand generation by inventing the diagnostic category 'psychic tension' to market Valium beyond its original anxiety indication. This reframing converted a narrow psychiatric drug into a mass-market product, becoming the first drug to pass $100M in sales. The technique works because it doesn't require proving a drug treats a new condition — it requires convincing doctors and patients that a normal human experience is actually a medical problem.

This pattern repeats across industries: create vocabulary that pathologizes the status quo, then sell the cure. The Sackler playbook later applied the same logic to OxyContin by rebranding pain relief as a 'sacred right' and 'universal narcotic entitlement,' expanding the addressable market from terminal cancer patients to anyone rating their pain above a certain number. The lesson is that category-defining language is often more powerful than the product itself.
