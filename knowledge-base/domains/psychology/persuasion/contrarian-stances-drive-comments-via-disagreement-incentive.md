Comments require higher activation energy than likes, and that energy is most readily supplied by the urge to disagree. If a video plays the middle or hedges, viewers have nothing to push back against and stay passive. If a video takes a position the majority disagrees with, the majority feels compelled to respond — and the algorithm interprets that comment volume as signal of relevance.

This creates a design pattern: intentionally pick the contrarian side of a live debate, state it with confidence rather than qualifiers, and let disagreement drive the engagement-rate metric that pushes the video to more people. The risk is reputational — contrarianism-for-its-own-sake burns credibility over time — so the stance must be one you can defend, not just one engineered for outrage.
