When Mars launched M&M's in America, the product's innovation (a hard candy shell over chocolate) was literally invisible to consumers. The brilliant move was the slogan: 'the milk chocolate that melts in your mouth, not in your hand.' This didn't sell the candy's taste or the kids' happiness — both were already assumed by the buyer. It targeted the hidden anxiety of the actual purchase decision-maker: parents who didn't want chocolate smeared across their furniture, walls, and clothing.

The lesson is counter-intuitive but repeatable. In any purchase where the buyer and user diverge (parents/kids, IT/developers, managers/employees, procurement/end-users), the stated preference comes from the user but the decision gets made based on the buyer's hidden pain. Most marketing fails because it sells to the user — 'kids will love it!' Mars sold to the parent — 'your home will survive it.'

Within five years (1951-1956), M&M's became the best-selling candy in America at $40M in revenue, beating Snickers, Milky Way, and Hershey bars. The product hadn't changed; the positioning had found the true buyer. When designing marketing for any two-audience product, ask: what is the buyer secretly afraid of, and does anyone's current message speak to that fear?
