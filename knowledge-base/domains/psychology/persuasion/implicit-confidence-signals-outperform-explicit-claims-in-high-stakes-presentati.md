Graham identifies a fundamental asymmetry in persuasion: for non-technical audiences who can't deeply evaluate your product, your own confidence becomes the single biggest piece of evidence. But this confidence must be shown, never told. Explicit claims ('we're passionate,' 'our product is great') trigger automatic skepticism — audiences discard these as meaningless or worse, mark you as someone who substitutes hype for substance.

The same dynamic explains why seeming nervous and apologetic is so damaging. If you don't appear to believe your own product is worth investors' attention, you've provided strong negative evidence via the channel they trust most. Graham's reframe is powerful: if you've truly built something good, you're doing investors a favor by telling them about it. This isn't arrogance — it's the logical consequence of genuine conviction, and audiences can detect the difference.
