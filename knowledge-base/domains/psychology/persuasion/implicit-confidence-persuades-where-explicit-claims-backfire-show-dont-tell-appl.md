Graham identifies a paradox: for early-stage startups where investors can't fully evaluate the technical merits, the founder's own confidence becomes 'probably the single biggest piece of evidence' for investment decisions. Yet explicitly stating that confidence ('we're passionate,' 'our product is great') causes the opposite effect — investors write you off as bullshitters.

This is a general principle of persuasion: stated confidence is cheap talk that gets discounted, while revealed confidence (through demeanor, depth of knowledge, and how you frame the problem) is costly to fake and therefore credible. The same dynamic appears in job interviews, sales, and leadership — the people who say 'trust me' are the ones you trust least. The frame Graham suggests is genuinely believing you're doing investors a favor by letting them invest, which naturally produces the right implicit signals.
