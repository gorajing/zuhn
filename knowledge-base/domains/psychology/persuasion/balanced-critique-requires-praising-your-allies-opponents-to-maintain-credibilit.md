Murray shares an anecdote about a UK politician who asked him to 'please don't forget to say when we've got something right' alongside inevitable criticism. Murray argues this dual posture — castigating failures AND acknowledging successes — is essential but extremely rare. Most commentators remember to do one or the other, almost never both.

This maps to a broader principle about credibility maintenance. In any domain where you evaluate others — code reviews, management, political commentary — your criticism only carries weight if you've demonstrated willingness to praise. One-directional critique gets discounted as tribal loyalty or personal animus. The actionable insight is that strategic praise of opponents is not moral weakness but credibility infrastructure. It's the deposit that makes future criticism withdrawals possible.
