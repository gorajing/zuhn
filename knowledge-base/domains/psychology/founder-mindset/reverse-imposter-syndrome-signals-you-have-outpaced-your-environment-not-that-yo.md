The article's most counter-intuitive reframe is treating reverse imposter syndrome as a positive leading indicator. The dissonance of feeling unseen typically appears just before others catch up — it signals that skills, traction, or insight have outpaced external perception. This mirrors early product-market fit: traction is real but quiet, users rave but metrics haven't caught up, growth lives in spreadsheets not headlines.

The psychological mechanism is self-perception alignment theory: humans need the world to reflect what they know about themselves, and when this feedback loop breaks, cognitive friction builds. But the article argues this friction is transitional, not terminal. The founders who navigate it successfully hold their ground while intentionally closing the gap between truth and visibility — treating the lag as a solvable translation problem rather than an existential crisis.
