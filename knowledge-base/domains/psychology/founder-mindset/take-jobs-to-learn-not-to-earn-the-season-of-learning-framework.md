Hormozi illustrates this with a candidate who negotiated for an extra $3,000/year instead of recognizing the learning opportunity. The premium employers pay for experienced hires reflects accumulated knowledge — if a new role adds nothing to your skillset, you're selling your current abilities rather than building new ones.

The framework distinguishes between 'seasons of learning' and 'seasons of earning.' The mistake is conflating the two. A $25,000/year salary difference in your 20s is inconsequential on a long time horizon compared to the skills that could 10x your earning ceiling. The people willing to pay a premium for your current skills often have nothing to teach you — the highest-growth environments may pay less but compound your capabilities.
