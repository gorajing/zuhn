Perfect calibration of one's abilities sounds optimal in theory but fails in practice because it doesn't account for the asymmetric drag of external and internal skepticism. Other people's dismissiveness and your own harsh self-judgment create a systematic downward bias on your perceived chances of success. Slight overconfidence acts as armor against both forces.

This explains why the most successful people in many fields tend to be slightly overconfident rather than perfectly calibrated. The error isn't irrational — it's a compensation mechanism. Similarly, overestimating the importance of what you're working on corrects for undervaluing early results, producing a more accurate expected-value calculation even though both individual estimates are wrong.
