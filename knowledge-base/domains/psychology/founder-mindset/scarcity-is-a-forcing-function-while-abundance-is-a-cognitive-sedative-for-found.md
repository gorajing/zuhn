The psychological dimension of overfunding is distinct from the financial one. With millions in the bank, founders are tempted to spend big, hire quickly, launch early, and outsource problems — the capital starts to shape the company more than the customer does. As both Paul Graham and Fred Wilson warn, abundance makes companies rigid, less adaptable, and more vulnerable to internal bloat.

This maps to a broader cognitive pattern: when resources are constrained, every decision carries weight, forcing founders to learn from customers, validate assumptions, and make hard tradeoffs. When those constraints disappear, founders delegate learning itself — hiring VPs before personally closing deals, building customer success teams before handling support themselves. The Startup Genome study found that this cultural shift from scrappy to corporate is one of the most toxic effects of overcapitalization, and once lost, startup DNA is nearly impossible to recover.
