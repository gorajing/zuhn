Sakichi Toyoda's founding impulse wasn't market analysis — he watched his mother work painfully hard at manual weaving and decided to build a machine to ease her burden. That empathy-rooted origin story became Toyota's cultural DNA: making things that reliably improve people's lives. His son Kiichiro inherited the same ethos, insisting on independent R&D rather than copying Western designs, stating he wanted to 'get rid of this attitude of taking the easiest path by taking someone else's hard work.' This founding motivation proved remarkably durable — over a century later, Toyota's reliability-first philosophy still traces back to Sakichi's original impulse. The pattern suggests that deeply personal founding motivations create stronger cultural anchors than abstract market opportunities, because they're harder to abandon when times get tough.
