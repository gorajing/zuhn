The essay identifies three failing postures during disruption: the craftsman defending depreciated skills, the tastemaker protecting validated approaches, and the finite-game optimizer declaring categories dead ('SaaS is dead,' 'programming is over'). All three share a common error — treating a fundamentally expansionary moment as a zero-sum contest.

The infinite-game posture, borrowed from art history, treats every disruption as an expansion of the creative surface. When photography threatened painting, painters didn't optimize for competing with cameras — they invented Impressionism, Expressionism, and Cubism. The game got bigger, not smaller.

David Bowie's principle crystallizes the operational version: 'if you feel safe in the area you're working in, you're not working in the right area.' For founders, this means deliberately moving toward discomfort and unfamiliar territory rather than optimizing within known bounds.
