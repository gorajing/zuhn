Levine observes that while crises often have genuine external causes (market shifts, regulatory changes, investor pullbacks), founders who attribute the crisis externally tend to enter a passive waiting posture — hoping conditions will revert — rather than actively adapting. Taking ownership, even of externally caused problems, keeps the founder's cognitive frame in agency mode.

This connects to locus-of-control research in psychology: an internal locus of control correlates with more adaptive responses to adversity. For founders specifically, the ownership stance also has downstream effects on team morale and investor confidence. A founder who says 'the market changed and we need to adapt' signals competence differently than one who says 'the market screwed us.' The former invites solution-finding; the latter invites sympathy but not action.
