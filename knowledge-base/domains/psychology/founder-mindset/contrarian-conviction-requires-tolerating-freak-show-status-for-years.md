Palantir was called a failure for 15 years. They went public, got crushed to $6/share by analysts, then ran to $80+. Karp openly calls Palantir a 'freak show' and embraces it. His challenge to founders: do you have the conviction to keep building when everyone tells you to stop? Are you willing to be a freak show for 20 years to find out if you're right?

This maps to a deeper pattern: the most valuable positions are the ones that take the longest to validate. If expert consensus could identify them, they wouldn't be contrarian. The people who bought Palantir at $6 weren't Sand Hill Road — they were retail investors who did their own work and ignored analysts. Karp's dyslexia metaphor reinforces this: in a non-playbook world, the inability to follow the playbook becomes an advantage because it forces you to invent new approaches.
