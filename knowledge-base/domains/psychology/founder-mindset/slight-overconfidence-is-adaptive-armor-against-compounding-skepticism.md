Graham observes that across many fields, the most successful people are slightly overconfident — which seems paradoxical since accurate self-assessment should be optimal. The explanation is that overconfidence compensates for multiple error sources in the opposite direction: other people's skepticism and your own harsh self-judgment. Similarly, overestimating the importance of your work counterbalances underestimating your early results. If something is 20% toward a goal worth 100 but you believe it's 10% toward a goal worth 200, your expected-value estimate is accidentally correct. This isn't delusion — it's a calibration offset that corrects for systematic pessimism bias in how early work is evaluated.
