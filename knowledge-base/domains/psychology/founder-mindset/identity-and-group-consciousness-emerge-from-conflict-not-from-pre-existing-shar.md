Marx's theory of class struggle contains a psychological insight that extends far beyond economics: groups do not develop coherent identity from shared characteristics alone. It is the act of struggling against an opposing force that crystallizes group consciousness. Without conflict, shared traits remain latent and unorganized — workers don't think of themselves as 'the working class' until they collectively push back against capital.

This principle applies to startup communities, open-source movements, and brand tribes. Early-stage communities often struggle to coalesce until they identify a shared antagonist — whether it's a dominant incumbent, a broken status quo, or a cultural norm to rebel against. Linux developed its identity through opposition to proprietary software. Tesla's early community cohered around opposition to fossil fuel dependency. For founders building communities, the implication is counterintuitive: don't just define what your community is for — define what it's against. Shared opposition catalyzes the identity formation that shared enthusiasm alone cannot.
