Naval makes the counter-intuitive case that becoming happier actually increases effectiveness even if it reduces raw drive. The mechanism is decision quality: a peaceful mind has fewer extraneous threads, sleeps better, avoids emotionally impetuous choices, and can prioritize clearly. In a leveraged world — where code, capital, media, and community multiply each decision — the quality of judgment matters far more than hours worked.

He cites Warren Buffett as the exemplar: making perhaps one major decision per year, spending most time reading and thinking, yet outperforming competitors because his hit rate is 85% versus their 65-70%. The trade-off is real (a fully enlightened person won't build rockets), but for most people the gain in decision quality far exceeds the loss in drive. This directly challenges the startup culture belief that anxiety and urgency are prerequisites for success.
