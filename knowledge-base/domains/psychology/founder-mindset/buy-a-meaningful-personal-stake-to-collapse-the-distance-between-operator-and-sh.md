Before starting at Bank One, Dimon bought roughly $60 million of stock — about half his net worth — with his own money. He described the reasoning plainly: shareholders needed to know he would 'go down with the ship or up with the ship,' and that long-term decisions wouldn't be second-guessed as a hired gun's short-term plays. Granted options vest on a schedule; cash buys commit capital that is gone if you're wrong.

The signaling is asymmetric. Compensation packages can be structured to look like alignment while preserving downside protection for the executive. A large open-market purchase cannot. Employees, directors, and investors all update their priors immediately. It also changes the executive's own decision-making — loss aversion on personal capital is a stronger discipline than any governance document.

For founders taking outside CEOs, for operators stepping into turnarounds, or for executives joining a struggling firm: ask whether the incoming leader is willing to buy in with their own money at market price. The answer is a near-costless test of their actual conviction.
