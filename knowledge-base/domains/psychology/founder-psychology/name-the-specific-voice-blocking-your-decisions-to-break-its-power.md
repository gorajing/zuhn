Hormozi nearly didn't sell Gym Launch for $46 million because he thought a specific colleague would consider the amount unimpressive. When he surfaced this — 'Am I really going to let this one person's imagined opinion control a $46M decision?' — the paralysis broke instantly. The fear wasn't abstract; it was a specific person's voice playing in his subconscious.

This is a practical debugging technique for decision paralysis. Most people experience the resistance as a vague feeling of 'I shouldn't' or 'it's not enough.' But when you force yourself to name the actual person whose judgment you're afraid of, two things happen: (1) you realize it's usually someone whose opinion shouldn't carry that weight, and (2) you recognize the judgment is imagined, not real — you're projecting what they might think. The technique works because it converts an amorphous emotional blocker into a concrete, evaluable claim that rarely survives scrutiny.
