Paul Graham identifies a specific cognitive trap: we apply the same evaluative standards to embryonic ambitious projects that we apply to finished, polished work or to less ambitious projects. This comparison is structurally unfair — a 20% sketch of something that will be 10x more valuable than what it's being compared to is already worth more than its surface appearance suggests.

This matters practically because the fear of looking lame is not random — it is specifically calibrated to discourage the early phases of ambitious work. Safe, conventional projects don't trigger this fear because their early versions resemble their finished versions closely. Ambitious projects suffer the inverse: the gap between early and final form is largest precisely when the final form is most valuable.

The corrective is epistemic: study the early histories of people who did great work. First commits, early sketches, first recordings — they're often feeble. Internalizing this pattern recalibrates your judgment and makes you immune to both your own self-skepticism and others' dismissal.
