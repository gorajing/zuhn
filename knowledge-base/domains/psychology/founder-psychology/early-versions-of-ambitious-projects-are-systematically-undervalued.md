When we judge early versions of ambitious projects, we apply the wrong standard: we compare them to finished work or to less ambitious projects, rather than recognizing them as a special case. A project 20% of the way to a goal worth 100 looks worse than a project 80% of the way to a goal worth 10, even if the former has far more expected value.

This miscalibration is not purely psychological weakness — it's a genuine lack of social customs around early-stage work. As a species, we've only been creating radically new things for a few centuries, not long enough to evolve reliable intuitions. The 'mind games' creators play to stay motivated aren't delusions — they're corrections toward a truer assessment.

The practical implication: when evaluating your own or others' early work on ambitious projects, the question isn't 'is this good?' but 'does this have the structure of something that could become great?' Those are different questions requiring different faculties.
