Current quality of early work is a poor signal for whether to continue, because early work on ambitious projects is structurally bad relative to finished work — that's not a flaw, it's a phase. The more relevant question is whether the work is improving and at what rate.

This reframe has a practical motivational benefit: it's much easier to sustain effort when you can see clear improvement, even if you're still far from the goal. Spending concentrated time on a new project (a natural advantage of youth, when time blocks are larger) accelerates the improvement rate, making the early phase less discouraging.

For evaluators as well as creators, this suggests a different due diligence question: not 'is this impressive now?' but 'what is the learning velocity here?' A team that shipped something mediocre but has gotten meaningfully better each iteration is a stronger signal than a polished demo from a team whose improvement curve is flat.
