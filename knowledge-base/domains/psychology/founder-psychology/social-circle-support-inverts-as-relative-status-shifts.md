Hormozi identifies a counterintuitive pattern in social support for entrepreneurs: the initial encouragement you receive when starting out isn't pure goodwill — it's partly because quitting a stable job to pursue something risky actually lowers your status relative to peers. You've stepped down in income and prestige, which makes them feel better about their own position. They're genuinely happy because your risk-taking effectively removes you from competition.

As you succeed, this dynamic inverts. Your growth becomes a mirror reflecting the dreams your social circle abandoned. It's not that they become bad people — it's that your existence as someone who started from the same resources and achieved more creates cognitive dissonance they resolve through withdrawal or hostility. This explains why 'going home' becomes harder for successful entrepreneurs: childhood peers represent the strongest possible same-starting-point comparison. Understanding this pattern prevents entrepreneurs from being blindsided by shifting social dynamics and from making the mistake of seeking validation from people whose support was never unconditional.
