Graham (citing G.H. Hardy) argues that the most successful people across many fields tend to be slightly overconfident. This seems paradoxical — surely accurate self-knowledge is optimal — but it makes sense once you account for the systematic errors that bias in the opposite direction.

Early-stage creative and intellectual work is routinely underestimated by its creator. If you judge a 20%-complete project as worth 10% of a 50-point goal, you'll quit. But if you overestimate both the progress (25%) and the goal (150 points), your expected value estimate stays correct even though both components are wrong. The errors cancel.

This has a practical corollary: advice like 'be more humble' or 'keep your expectations realistic' is actively harmful for people doing early ambitious work. It removes the psychological buffer that allows creators to push through the phase where their work genuinely looks bad before it gets good. The appropriate intervention is not humility but rather better calibration of what early-stage work should look like.
