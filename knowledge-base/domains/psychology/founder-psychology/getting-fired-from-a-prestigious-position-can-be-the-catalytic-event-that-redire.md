Keating was fired from his postdoc at Stanford because he was sketching plans for his own ambitious experiment instead of doing the work his advisor was paying him for. Rather than being a setback, this forced exit led directly to his working at Caltech with Andrew Lange, which led to the BICEP experiment — the defining work of his career. He describes it as 'possibly the best thing that could ever happen to me.'

This pattern appears repeatedly in founder stories: Steve Jobs being ousted from Apple, scientists whose rejected grant applications forced them into more creative directions. The mechanism is that prestigious positions create comfortable constraints — you have status, salary, and defined work, which suppresses the risk-taking needed for breakthrough work. Getting fired removes these golden handcuffs. The practical lesson is not to seek termination, but to recognize that when forced exits happen, they often represent the removal of constraints that were preventing you from pursuing your most ambitious ideas.
