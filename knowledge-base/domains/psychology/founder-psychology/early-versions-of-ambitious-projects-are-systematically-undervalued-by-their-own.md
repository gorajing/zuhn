Paul Graham observes that most great projects pass through a phase where they seem unimpressive — even to their creators. This isn't evidence of actual weakness but a structural artifact: we lack calibrated instincts for evaluating early-stage ambitious work because, evolutionarily, making genuinely new things is too recent a human activity to have generated good cultural heuristics.

The insight has a practical edge: if early badness is a near-universal feature of great work, then disgust with one's own early output is almost meaningless as signal. The correct response is not to raise confidence artificially but to recalibrate the prior — recognize that bad-looking early work and eventual greatness are compatible, even correlated.

For founders and creators, this reframes the 'should I keep going?' question. The relevant evidence is not current quality but rate of improvement and whether the underlying idea has real substance.
