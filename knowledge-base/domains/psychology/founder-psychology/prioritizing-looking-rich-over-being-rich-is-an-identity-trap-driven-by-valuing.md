Lifestyle inflation is typically framed as a financial problem, but Hormozi identifies it as an identity problem. The person who upgrades their car, wardrobe, and lifestyle with each raise is making a psychological trade: they're purchasing external validation at the cost of actual wealth accumulation. The root cause is valuing others' approval over your own self-assessment.

This connects to several of Hormozi's other anti-patterns: taking advice from poor people (who themselves confuse signaling with substance), believing what others think about you more than what you think about yourself, and the 'when-then' fallacy of moving goalposts. The practical reversal is being willing to 'look poor' — to tolerate others' judgment about your appearance while quietly building real equity. Hormozi himself demonstrates this by filming from a basement in a plain shirt despite running a $100M+ portfolio, proving that the discomfort of looking unsuccessful is far cheaper than the cost of performing success.
