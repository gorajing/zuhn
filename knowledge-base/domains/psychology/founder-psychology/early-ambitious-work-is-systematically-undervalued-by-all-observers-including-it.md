Paul Graham observes that humans are evolutionarily and culturally unprepared to evaluate early-stage ambitious work. We've only had a few centuries of rapid innovation, so we've never developed customs for treating early versions of big ideas differently from finished products — we judge them the same way we'd judge less ambitious work that's nearly complete.

The core problem is a calibration error: a project 20% of the way to a 100-point goal looks worse than a project 80% of the way to a 10-point goal, even if its expected value is higher. This applies to inventors judging their own sketches, investors evaluating early startups, and peers assessing colleagues' strange-sounding ideas.

The practical implication is that the feeling of embarrassment about early work is not a reliable signal. The thing that looks lame often is the great work — just not yet finished. Treating that feeling as diagnostic (rather than as calibration noise) causes both individuals and cultures to kill ambitious projects prematurely.
