The 'shower principle' describes two cognitive modes: focus mode (in the IDE, clear blueprint, good for execution but prone to blind spots and inhibition) and diffuse mode (walking, showering, playing with the dog), where the subconscious surfaces full-form creative solutions. Historically, entering diffuse mode meant stopping work. Proser's claim is that remote control breaks this tradeoff: a Claude Code session keeps running on your dev machine with full filesystem access while you poke it from your phone on LTE miles away — he has reviewed PRs and fired design ideas back from a trail. The recommended day shape: load up in focus mode at the start, get agents turning on identified work tracks, then leave the desk — reducing RSI and physical strain — while remaining in the loop. The deeper insight crosses domains: a known psychology principle (diffuse-mode creativity) becomes far more valuable once tooling lets the insight be applied instantly rather than remembered for later, and it also lets you direct work via natural-language PR comments from mobile.
