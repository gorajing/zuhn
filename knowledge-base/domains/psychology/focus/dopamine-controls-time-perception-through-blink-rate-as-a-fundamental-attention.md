Research published in Current Biology (Terhune et al., 'Time dilates after spontaneous blinking') demonstrates that blinks function as temporal resets—like scene breaks in a film. After each blink, the brain recalibrates its perception of time. Higher dopamine leads to less blinking, finer time-slicing (slow-motion perception), and overestimation of durations. Lower dopamine leads to more blinking, coarser time perception, and underestimation of durations.

This elegantly connects several ADHD symptoms: low dopamine → more blinking → coarser time perception → chronic lateness and inability to estimate task duration. It also explains why stimulant medications (Adderall, Ritalin, caffeine) reduce blink rate. Practical application: deliberately reducing blink rate during focused work (visual fixation training) can enhance attention. Studies on elementary school students showed that brief visual focus training—staring at a target while consciously limiting blinks—significantly improved subsequent cognitive focus.
