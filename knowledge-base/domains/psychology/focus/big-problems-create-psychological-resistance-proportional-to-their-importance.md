Graham identifies that beyond delayed gratification and fear of wasting time, big problems produce something closer to physical pain—'like having a vacuum cleaner hooked up to your imagination.' Your initial ideas get sucked out immediately, and the void is terrifying. This is why even people who enjoy their work find it easier to tackle small problems over big ones.

The practical remedy is oblique approach: face the big problem enough to catch the excitement radiating from it, but not so directly that it paralyzes you. Work on small things that could grow into big things, work on successively larger things, or split the moral load with collaborators. This isn't weakness—'the very best work has been done this way.' The sailboat metaphor is apt: you can tighten the angle toward the wind once you get underway.
