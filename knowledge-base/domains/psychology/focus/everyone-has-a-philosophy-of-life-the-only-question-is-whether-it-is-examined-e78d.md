Pigliucci confronts people who say they do not need a philosophy of life. He points out that if he observed their behavior — their choices, priorities, values — he could reconstruct their implicit philosophy. Everyone has one; the question is whether it is conscious and deliberate.

But he also warns against the opposite extreme: blindly adopting a philosophy off the shelf without critical examination, or creating an eclectic mishmash of incompatible ideas. The healthiest approach is committing to a coherent framework while maintaining enough skepticism to question whether it actually fits your life. His own approach: mostly Stoic, tempered by Skepticism about human fallibility.
