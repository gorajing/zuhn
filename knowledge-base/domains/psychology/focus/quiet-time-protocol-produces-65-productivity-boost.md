Leslie Perlow's quiet time experiment with engineers established a simple protocol: no meetings, no interruptions, no Slack, no emails before noon on specific days (e.g., Thursday and Friday). The result was 65% above average productivity during those protected blocks. The average person checks email 72 times per day (per Gloria Mark's pre-COVID data), creating what Brigid Schulte calls 'time confetti' — meaningful blocks sliced into tiny, unusable dots that erode both accomplishment and joy.

Grant connects this to chronotype research: morning people should protect morning blocks for deep analytical and creative work, while night owls should protect late afternoon blocks. New evidence suggests early birds actually do their most creative work in the morning (contradicting the conventional wisdom that creativity peaks in off-peak hours), likely because higher energy levels fuel more divergent thinking. The practical protocol: protect your peak-energy hours from all interruptions, batch meetings into post-lunch slots (where people are 30% less likely to multitask), and reserve the bookends of your day for deep work.
