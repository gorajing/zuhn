Attentional blinks are moments when the brain shuts off perception after detecting a target—like finding Waldo and then being unable to spot a second target right next to it. People with ADHD experience far more attentional blinks than average, and they increase with age as part of cognitive decline.

Research cited in Goleman and Davidson's 'Altered Traits' shows that a single 17-minute session of interoceptive focus—sitting with eyes closed, attending to breathing and body sensations, redirecting attention when the mind wanders—significantly reduced attentional blinks in a near-permanent way without additional training. This suggests something fundamental shifts in how the brain allocates attention after even brief periods of deliberate internal awareness practice. The mechanism likely involves rebalancing the default mode and task networks rather than building interoceptive skill per se.
