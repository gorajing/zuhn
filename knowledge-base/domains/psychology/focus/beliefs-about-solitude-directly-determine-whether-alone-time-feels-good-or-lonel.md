Longitudinal research tracking people over time finds that beliefs about solitude are the mediating variable between being alone and feeling lonely. If you believe alone time is beneficial, you actually experience positive emotions during solitude. If you believe it is harmful, the same objective state produces loneliness.

This finding is practically significant because beliefs are modifiable. The intervention is reframing: shifting from 'something is wrong with me' to 'this is an opportunity for creativity and pleasant reflection.' Media overwhelmingly reinforces negative framing — being alone is described as negative approximately ten times more often than positive — which shapes cultural beliefs and, through them, actual emotional experience.
