Frenkel describes how Einstein would think intensely, then go for walks and whistle, and the breakthroughs came during the whistling — not the thinking. This isn't coincidence: 'the moment of discovery is the moment when thinking stops... you kind of almost become that truth that you're seeking.' When you're fully absorbed in creation, time stands still — two hours feel like seconds. Frenkel argues this timeless state may be closer to fundamental truth than our normal time-bound consciousness.

The practical application for any knowledge worker: intense preparation and deliberation are necessary preconditions, but the breakthrough itself requires releasing the deliberate thinking process. This maps to well-documented research on incubation effects in problem-solving. The action item is to build systematic alternation between intense focused work and genuine mental release (walks, music, activities that occupy the conscious mind) into your creative process. The preparation loads the problem; the release lets the solution emerge.
