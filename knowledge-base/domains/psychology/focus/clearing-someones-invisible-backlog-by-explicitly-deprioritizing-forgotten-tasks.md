Hormozi describes a practice of pulling up a team member's full task list before assigning a new priority, then explicitly crossing off old items: 'Oh, I forgot I even asked you to do that' or 'We solved that in another department already.' Even tasks people aren't actively working on consume attention because they know they should be working on them, creating a background anxiety tax on their cognitive capacity.

This is a cross-domain application of cognitive load theory to management. The leader's job isn't just to add the right priority — it's to subtract the accumulated backlog of implicit commitments. Without this explicit clearing, the team member hears 'this is now the most important thing' while still carrying the weight of three other 'most important things' from previous weeks. The deprioritization conversation is as valuable as the prioritization one.
