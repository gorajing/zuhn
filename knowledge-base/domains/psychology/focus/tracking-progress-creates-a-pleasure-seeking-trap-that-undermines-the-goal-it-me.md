Naval describes noticing that feeling 'more free than before' and recording progress becomes its own pleasure cycle. The excitement of progress is pleasure derived from a self-image ('I'm in a better place now'). This creates fragility: any setback — a moment of anger, anxiety, or perceived regression — withdraws that pleasure and produces suffering proportional to the investment in the progress narrative.

Gupta identifies this as 'the game of the mind' — whether it's a spiritual game or materialistic game, as long as the mind has you captured playing a certain game (including the self-improvement game), you remain unfree. The implication is counterintuitive for the quantified-self movement: the act of measurement itself can become the primary obstacle. This doesn't mean progress doesn't happen — it means attachment to tracking progress replaces one trap (the original problem) with another (the progress-monitoring identity).
