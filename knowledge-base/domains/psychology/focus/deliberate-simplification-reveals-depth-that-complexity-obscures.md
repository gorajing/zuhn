Modern culture equates a rich life with a complex one: busy schedules, active social lives, frequent travels, elaborate goals. Thoreau's Walden experiment challenged this by stripping life to essentials. What he found was that complexity obscures rather than reveals meaning.

The practical application isn't literal cabin-in-the-woods living but the principle of deliberate subtraction: each commitment, possession, and relationship has both value and attention cost. When the attention cost exceeds the value, simplification increases rather than decreases life quality.
