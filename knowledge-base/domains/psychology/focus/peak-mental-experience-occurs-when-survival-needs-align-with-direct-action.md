Jonas describes killing the moose as one of the happiest moments of his life — not because of the competition, but because weeks of genuine starvation pressure were resolved through his own direct action. He notes that all our dopamine receptors are tuned for exactly this experience: needing something to survive and then making it happen.

Modern life abstracts us from this direct connection between effort and survival. The insight is that flow states are deepest when stakes feel genuinely consequential and the feedback loop is immediate. This suggests that artificially creating conditions of direct consequence — clear metrics, tight deadlines, personal accountability — can unlock deeper engagement and satisfaction, even in knowledge work.
