Adams admits that at the 1980 Olympics, he had world-class judo training, exceptional conditioning, and had beaten everyone in the world that year — but his nutrition was 'appalling.' He had excellent coaching for fitness and technical judo but hadn't sorted out the basic science of fueling his body during a weight cut.

This pattern repeats across disciplines: at the highest levels, the differentiator is often not the sophisticated skill but the neglected fundamental. Adams had the talent, the technique, the conditioning, and the mental game — but lost the Olympic final partly because he didn't eat properly. The broader lesson applies to any competitive domain including business: the most likely point of failure at the elite level is the basic thing you assumed was 'good enough' while focusing on more sophisticated improvements.
