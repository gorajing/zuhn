The fundamental insight is that makers and managers operate on incompatible time currencies. A manager divides their day into 20+ small chunks (15-60 minutes each), so one meeting costs roughly 1/100th of their weekly capacity. A maker operates on 2-3 large blocks per day (~10 per week), meaning one meeting destroys 10% of their total weekly output — not just the meeting duration, but the entire block it sits in.

This asymmetry creates a toxic dynamic: managers view empty maker calendars as unproductive (projecting their own productivity framework), then schedule meetings that feel costless to them but devastating to makers. The Zeigarnik effect compounds this — an upcoming meeting prevents flow state entry even hours before it occurs, because the maker's brain keeps the meeting as an 'open loop' that consumes cognitive resources. This explains why entrepreneurs feel busy all day but accomplish nothing — their maker blocks are being fragmented by manager-style interruptions.
