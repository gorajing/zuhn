Lucas discovered through experimentation that focus isn't just about willpower — it's about managing a finite resource he calls mental bandwidth. Each thing you focus on takes up space, and critically, those things don't leave when you stop actively thinking about them. When he checked his email mid-challenge, the business deal he read about stayed in the back of his mind all day, competing with his main work for bandwidth.

He visualized this as a brain diagram where inputs pile up and permanently reduce available space. The implication: it's not that you can't multitask well — it's that every context you introduce shrinks your capacity for everything else, including presence with people and richness of experience. More preserved bandwidth means better focus on work AND more availability for life's small moments. The bandwidth roughly resets each day, making the order and quantity of daily inputs a design decision.
