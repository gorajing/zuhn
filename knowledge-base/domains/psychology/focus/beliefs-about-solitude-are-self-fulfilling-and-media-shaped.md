Research shows that media descriptions of being alone are approximately 10 times more likely to frame the experience as negative than positive. This matters because exposure to positive vs negative framing of solitude directly shifts how people experience time alone. People who believe solitude is beneficial actually feel good during alone time. People who believe it is harmful feel lonelier.

Critically, these beliefs are malleable. Interventions that shift people's framing of solitude from threat to opportunity — time for creativity, pleasant reflection, self-directed thought — produce measurable improvements in wellbeing during alone time. The distinction between loneliness (perceived social isolation that causes distress) and solitude (chosen time alone that can be restorative) is not inherent in the situation but in the meaning we assign to it.
