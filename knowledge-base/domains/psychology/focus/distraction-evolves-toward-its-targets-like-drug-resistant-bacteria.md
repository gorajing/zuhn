Paul Graham observes that distraction is not a rock you avoid once—it actively seeks you out and evolves. Television refined itself over 50 years into 'visual crack,' and as people learned to avoid TV, the internet emerged as an even more potent distractor delivered directly to work computers.

This framing matters because it shifts the mental model from willpower ('just resist it') to an arms race. Each generation of distraction technology is more addictive and harder to distinguish from productive work. The implication is that any fixed rule or habit for managing distraction has a shelf life—you must continuously audit your environment for new forms of time sinks that didn't exist when you last set up your defenses.
