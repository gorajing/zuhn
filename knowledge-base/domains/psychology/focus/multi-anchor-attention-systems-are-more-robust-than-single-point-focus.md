Harris explicitly instructs practitioners to return not just to breath but to 'the sensations of sitting,' 'the feeling of your back against the chair,' and 'sounds in the room.' This is not redundancy — it is a designed fallback hierarchy. When the breath sensation is too subtle to track, body pressure remains. When body sensation habituates, sounds arise continuously and involuntarily.

This maps onto the engineering principle of graceful degradation: systems that distribute load across multiple components fail more gracefully than those with a single critical path. In attention terms, single-point focus (staring at a candle, counting breaths only) creates brittleness — one disruption breaks the whole chain. Multi-anchor systems distribute attentional load and provide automatic recovery paths.

The practical takeaway extends to deep work contexts: knowledge workers who anchor focus only to a task file or document are more disrupted by a single notification than those who layer anchors (physical environment, time-boxing, body posture cues). Multi-sensory environmental design for focus is an underexplored application of this principle.
