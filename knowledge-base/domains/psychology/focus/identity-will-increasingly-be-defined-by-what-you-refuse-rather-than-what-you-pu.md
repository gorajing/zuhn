Graham's essay ends with a powerful reframing: in a world of accelerating addictiveness, the scarce resource isn't opportunity or information — it's the ability to say no. The people who get things done will be those who develop personal customs for refusal before society develops collective ones.

This connects to a broader pattern in productivity and focus. When stimuli were scarce, success came from seeking out more. Now that stimuli are abundant and engineered for maximum engagement, success comes from filtering ruthlessly. Graham's own example — refusing to own an iPhone, taking long hikes specifically for uninterrupted thinking time — illustrates the kind of deliberate, personally-engineered constraints that replace missing social customs. The 'lonely squirming' he describes is the interim cost of self-regulation before norms catch up.
