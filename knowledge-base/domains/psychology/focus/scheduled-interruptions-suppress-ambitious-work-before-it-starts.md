The common assumption is that scheduled interruptions are less disruptive because they're predictable. Graham argues the opposite: when you know a meeting is coming, you unconsciously avoid starting anything that requires deep loading of context into working memory. You never even attempt the hard problem.

Unscheduled interruptions can wipe your mental state, but at least you had the chance to enter the deep state and make progress before the interruption hit. The practical implication is that meeting-free blocks need to be long enough that programmers believe they can complete a deep-work cycle — not just free of meetings, but perceived as free of meetings. Calendar fragmentation damages output even when the fragments are individually long enough.
