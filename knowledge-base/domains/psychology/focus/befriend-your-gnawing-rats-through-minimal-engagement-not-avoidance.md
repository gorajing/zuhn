Dutch Zen monk Paul Loomans describes chronically avoided tasks as 'gnawing rats' — they consume energy in the background even when we're not engaging with them. The solution isn't willpower-driven action but befriending: consciously acknowledging the task exists and building a psychological relationship with it.

This might mean simply entering the cluttered room without cleaning it, or visualizing the first two steps of a feared project, or writing down what would be involved. Any form of conscious acknowledgment transforms 'gnawing rats' into 'fluffy white sheep.' Once you stop spending energy pretending a task doesn't exist, the right moment to take action naturally arises. The key is ending the avoidance, not forcing the completion.
