The brain has two key circuit sets governing attention: the default mode network (active at rest) and task-directed networks (active during goal-oriented behavior). In a normally functioning brain, these are anti-correlated—when one is active, the other is suppressed, like a seesaw. In ADHD, these networks become abnormally correlated, firing together rather than alternating.

Dopamine serves as the conductor orchestrating this alternation. The low dopamine hypothesis (formalized by Spencer 2015 in Biological Psychiatry) explains that insufficient dopamine causes neurons to fire when they shouldn't, breaking the anti-correlation. When ADHD is successfully treated or people age out of it, brain imaging shows these networks return to their proper anti-correlated state. This reframes ADHD not as a broken brain but as a mis-conducted orchestra.
