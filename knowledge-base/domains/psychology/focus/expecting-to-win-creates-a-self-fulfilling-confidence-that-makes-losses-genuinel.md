Adams reveals a particular quality of elite mental preparation: he didn't just hope to win or plan to win — he genuinely could not conceive of losing. This isn't bravado or self-deception. His record supported it: he never lost a European title across seven golds, and his only losses were Olympic and World Championship finals decided by split decisions.

The distinction is important: this isn't blind confidence but calibrated confidence backed by evidence. He won every preliminary match by submission before time, often by big throws. When he finally did lose, he was genuinely surprised rather than prepared for it. This suggests that at the highest levels, the expectation of winning creates a performance state where the athlete operates without the friction of doubt — a self-reinforcing loop where confidence enables the performance that justifies the confidence.
