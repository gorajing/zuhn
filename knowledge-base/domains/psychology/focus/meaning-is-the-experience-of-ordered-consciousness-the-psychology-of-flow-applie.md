The conventional framing of meaning treats it as something external to find (a calling, a cause, a purpose). Dan Koe reframes it via Csikszentmihalyi: meaning is ordered consciousness, produced when attention is invested in a challenge slightly above current capacity with clear feedback. At capacity level 10, a level 1 challenge is boring (disordering), a level 20 challenge is daunting (disordering), and a level 11 challenge produces flow (ordering). Meaning is not found; it is generated through this ordering process. The practical implication: a life designed for ease or distraction produces entropy and emptiness regardless of material success; a life designed around slightly-above-capacity challenges produces meaning even in otherwise difficult circumstances. For anyone choosing between an easier path (AI-optimized productivity) and a harder path (essay-writing, original thought), the meaning-generation differential alone justifies the harder path. The emerging meaning economy exists because most people have abundant material resources and insufficient ordered-consciousness opportunities; creators who provide such opportunities (through challenging content, teachable difficult skills, frameworks for ordering one's own consciousness) serve a real and growing demand.
