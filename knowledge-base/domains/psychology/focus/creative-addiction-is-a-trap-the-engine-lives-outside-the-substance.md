Callaghan describes how his early life with HPPD (hallucinogen-induced visual disorder) led to alcohol self-medication, which he then convinced himself was essential to his creative process — just as Hunter S. Thompson did. The addictive brain constructs a narrative that substance use has a 'symbiotic inspirational thing going on' with creativity.

But after cutting out most substances, Callaghan found he was 'just as creative.' Hunter S. Thompson's trajectory proves the counter-case: in his final years, his daily routine was extreme substance consumption with zero creative output. The creative engine exists independently of substances; addiction just convinces you otherwise. This applies beyond drugs — any crutch (overwork, chaos, drama) that a creative person believes is 'part of the process' should be tested by removing it.
