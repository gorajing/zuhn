Bonanno coined the term 'fallacy of uniform efficacy' to describe the widespread belief that certain coping strategies are always good (mindfulness, social support, expression) while others are always bad (suppression, avoidance, impulsive behavior). His research shows this does not hold up.

Emotional suppression, widely labeled as maladaptive, was essential for parents with young children during 9/11 — they had to bottle their emotions to avoid frightening their children. In combat, suppression enables focus when survival demands it. Even showing too much positive emotion after winning can be socially maladaptive. Conversely, mindfulness and social support do not solve every problem. Bonanno calls contextually appropriate use of 'bad' strategies 'coping ugly' — referencing John Lennon's 'Whatever Gets You Through the Night.' The key is matching strategy to context, not following universal prescriptions.
