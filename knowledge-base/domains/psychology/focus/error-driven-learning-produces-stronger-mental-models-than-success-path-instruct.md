Malan's teaching style is distinctive in how much time he spends on errors. He deliberately writes buggy code, shows the error messages, and walks through the debugging process live. This isn't pedagogical sloppiness — it's a calculated strategy. Students who only see correct code develop fragile understanding that collapses at the first unexpected output. Students who've seen and resolved errors develop mental models that include failure modes.

This maps directly to how expertise develops in any domain. Novices know what works; experts know what fails and why. The error-driven approach builds the 'failure library' that distinguishes expert performance. In practice, this means when learning any new skill, you should actively seek out and study failure cases — not just best practices. Read post-mortems, try to break things intentionally, and spend time in the debugger. The discomfort of encountering errors is the signal that deeper learning is happening.
