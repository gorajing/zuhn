Harris draws a direct line from American lynch mobs in the early 20th century — where thousands gathered in festive celebration to watch torture and murder — to modern Western norms that treat such behavior as monstrous. This isn't about permanent differences between peoples; it's about the power of ideas and ethical frameworks that emerge in specific times and places.

The implication is profound: if we accept that our own ancestors were morally worse by our current standards, we must accept that different contemporary cultures can be at different points on this moral trajectory. Denying this — insisting all cultures are morally equivalent at all times — actually denies the possibility of moral progress itself. The same logic that lets us say 'Americans today are better than their slaveholding ancestors' requires us to acknowledge that some contemporary ethical frameworks produce systematically different behaviors around violence and civilian protection.
