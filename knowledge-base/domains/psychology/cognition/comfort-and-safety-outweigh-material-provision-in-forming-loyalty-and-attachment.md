Before Harlow's experiments, the dominant 'cupboard theory' held that infants bond with caregivers primarily because they provide food. Harlow's wire-versus-cloth mother experiment directly refuted this: baby monkeys consistently chose the soft cloth mother that provided zero nourishment over the wire mother that dispensed milk. When frightened, they ran to comfort, not calories.

This finding generalizes beyond parenting. In organizational psychology, employee loyalty correlates more strongly with psychological safety and belonging than with compensation. In product design, users develop stronger attachment to products that feel comfortable and trustworthy than to those that are merely functional. The principle suggests that anyone seeking to build loyalty — whether as a leader, brand, or partner — should prioritize creating emotional safety over delivering material benefits.
