The AI Index 2026 Chapter 9 documents a durable self-other split in AI sentiment. Globally, 56% of respondents believe AI will reduce the amount of time it takes them to get things done (higher in China at 78% and Southeast Asia above 60%). 40% say AI will make their own job better (33% in the US). But 64% of US adults expect AI to produce fewer jobs over the next 20 years, with only 5% expecting more. The pattern is that people are consistently AI-optimistic for themselves (personal productivity, entertainment, time savings, health) and AI-pessimistic for the aggregate (jobs, economy, disinformation). This is not a contradiction to be resolved by better communication — it is the same self-other bias documented in media literacy research ('I don't fall for fake news, but others do'), applied to AI. Policy implications: (1) consent to aggressive AI deployment in individual tools will be easier to secure than consent to labor-market displacement at scale; (2) political opposition to AI will form around aggregate framings (jobs, economy, disinformation) rather than individual use; (3) any business model that requires public legitimacy for aggregate-level deployment (autonomous trucking, healthcare systems, election infrastructure) faces a harder sell than individual-level products.
