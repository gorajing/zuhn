The nervous system's entire function reduces to three stages: sensory input (detecting stimuli), integration (processing and deciding), and motor output (executing a response). This architecture is universal across all nervous systems from simple invertebrates to humans. When a spider lands on your knee, sensory neurons detect pressure, interneurons integrate that signal and decide on a response, and motor neurons execute the action.

This framework is immediately actionable as a debugging tool for any intelligent behavior failure. If you're making bad decisions, the breakdown is in one of exactly three places: you're not sensing the right inputs (information deficit), you're not integrating correctly (faulty reasoning or emotional hijacking), or your execution is failing (poor motor output, procrastination, skill gap). Diagnosing which stage is broken changes the intervention completely. Most self-improvement advice conflates all three, but they require fundamentally different fixes.
