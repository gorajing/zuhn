The Holocaust is uniquely horrifying not because of its scale alone, but because it deployed the signature achievements of the Enlightenment and Scientific Revolution — meticulous bureaucratic record-keeping, industrial mass production, advanced transportation technology — in service of systematic murder. The death camps were, in a grotesque sense, optimized factories.

This forces a fundamental reassessment of the progress narrative: capability amplification is morally neutral. The same organizational sophistication that builds hospitals builds death camps. The same logistics that deliver consumer goods deliver people to extermination. This insight is directly relevant to AI development — the tools being built are capability amplifiers, and their moral valence depends entirely on deployment context. Anyone building powerful systems must internalize that efficiency and sophistication are not inherently good.
