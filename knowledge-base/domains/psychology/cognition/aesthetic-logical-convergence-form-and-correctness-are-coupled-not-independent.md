Graham's central claim is that writing quality and idea quality appear independent (like a car's speed and color) but are actually deeply coupled (like a plane's shape and its flight characteristics). The coupling works through multiple mechanisms: rhythm must match idea complexity, revision for sound forces logical improvement, and readable prose exposes remaining errors to the writer.

This has a provocative corollary: clumsily written work usually has gotten the ideas wrong too. The coupling isn't rigid — Graham calls it a rope, not a rod — but it's strong enough that form reliably signals substance in exploratory writing. The important caveat is that this only applies to writing that develops ideas, not writing that reports pre-existing results (textbooks, lab reports), where the ideas live more in the work than in the prose.
