Graham identifies a specific cognitive technique used across disciplines: starting a project under a label that implies low standards, then escalating after momentum exists. A painter begins 'just a sketch.' A programmer writes 'just a quick hack.' This is not self-deception about quality — it's a deliberate calibration of the internal critic.

The technique works because the fear of making something lame is triggered by the gap between current state and desired final state. By redefining the artifact type, you shrink the apparent gap. Once the project has enough momentum and early results, the psychological cost of continuing is much lower than the cost of starting from scratch.

The best media and tools for this strategy are those with fast iteration cycles and low upfront commitment — digital tools over physical ones, rapid prototyping environments over formal build processes. This is also why Minecraft and Roblox mods (which Graham's footnote via Carmack highlights) produced more careers than AAA game mods: the crude aesthetic removed the gap between early work and 'finished' work, making it possible to ship.
