Dutton's trolley problem analysis reveals empathy as a dual-system phenomenon rather than a single capacity. Cold empathy — the ability to understand another's situation rationally — operates through the prefrontal cortex, posterior parietal cortex, anterior cingulate cortex, temporal pole, and superior temporal sulcus. Hot empathy — the visceral feeling of another's experience — operates through the amygdala and medial orbital frontal cortex.

Psychopaths demonstrate that these systems are dissociable: they retain full cold empathy (they understand the dilemma perfectly) while lacking hot empathy (they don't feel the personal horror of pushing someone to their death). This dual-system model has implications for understanding conditions beyond psychopathy — autism, for example, often involves intact hot empathy with impaired cold empathy, the inverse pattern. Recognizing empathy as two independent systems rather than one unified trait fundamentally changes how we think about emotional intelligence, moral development, and interpersonal dysfunction.
