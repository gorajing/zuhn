Graham identifies a strange inversion: Darwin's essay on natural selection was arguably the best essay of 1844, but it would be unimpressive today precisely because it succeeded — everyone already knows about natural selection. The most impactful ideas destroy their own novelty through cultural assimilation. Bertrand Russell's 'trial marriage' proposals were revolutionary but now read as boring because they won; trial marriage became 'dating.'

This has deep implications for knowledge work. Ideas that stick become invisible infrastructure — you can't surprise someone with something they already absorbed. The only way to write timelessly surprising essays is to write about things people never learn except through experience: the overengineering tendency of juniors, the hollowness of status prizes discovered too late, or the gap between school incentives and real-world ones. These topics have a perpetual conveyor belt of naive readers.
