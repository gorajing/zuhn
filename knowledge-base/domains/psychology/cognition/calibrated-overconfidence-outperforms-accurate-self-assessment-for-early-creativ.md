Graham observes that across many fields, the most successful people are measurably slightly overconfident. This seems paradoxical — shouldn't accurate self-assessment be optimal? But the insight is that accurate assessment of unfinished work is nearly impossible; the baseline human error is systematic undervaluation. Slight overconfidence is therefore a corrective bias, not a distortion.

Hardy's formulation makes this mathematically precise: if you estimate that your project is 10% toward a goal worth 200 when it's actually 20% toward a goal worth 100, your expected value estimate is still correct even though both component estimates are wrong. The errors cancel.

This reframes the virtue of humility in creative contexts. Humility about finished work is appropriate — humble confidence in what you've actually shipped. But humility about early-stage work is actively harmful, because it compounds an already-strong negative bias rather than counteracting it.
