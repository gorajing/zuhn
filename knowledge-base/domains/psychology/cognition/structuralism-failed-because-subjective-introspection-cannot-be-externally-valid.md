Wundt and Titchener's structuralism attempted to decompose consciousness into basic elements, mirroring how chemists broke matter into elements. But their primary tool — asking patients to introspect about how they felt during experiences — was fatally flawed. Two people experiencing the same stimulus could report entirely different internal states, with no way to verify or reconcile the differences.

This failure carries a broader lesson about methodology: any measurement system that lacks external validation is vulnerable to subjective drift. The same problem appears in modern contexts — self-reported productivity metrics, subjective code quality assessments, and user satisfaction surveys all suffer from the same fundamental limitation that killed structuralism. The behaviorists who followed solved this by focusing exclusively on observable, measurable behavior.
