Derek's experience in the anechoic chamber followed a predictable arc: initial disorientation, followed by curiosity, then comfort, then even boredom. He lasted well beyond the supposed 45-minute limit with no distress. This pattern — alarm followed by rapid normalization — is the brain's default response to novelty, not its exception.

This has broad implications for how we think about human psychological limits. Popular culture tends to emphasize fragility ('people can't handle X'), but the evidence consistently shows that healthy humans adapt to extreme environments remarkably quickly. Astronauts adapt to weightlessness, prisoners adapt to solitary conditions, and meditators actively seek sensory deprivation. The myth of the anechoic chamber serves as a case study in how we systematically underestimate human adaptability while overestimating psychological fragility.
