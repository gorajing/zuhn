The story layers reality recursively: the banker may be an alcoholic dreaming he's a banker; the alcoholic visiting the Foundation is told he's 'too many levels deep'; the final diner scene exists outside all layers. This isn't just literary cleverness — it dramatizes a genuine decision-theoretic problem. When you cannot verify which layer of reality you inhabit, strategies that depend on knowing your true position (like 'I'm actually rich so I don't need to be generous') become unreliable.

The only decision-theoretically robust strategy in a nested-simulation scenario is to behave consistently across all possible positions — which is precisely Rawls' veil of ignorance. If you might be anyone, the rational policy is one you'd endorse from any position. This connects to simulation arguments, multiverse decision theory, and even practical contexts like designing policies when you don't know which stakeholder you'll end up being. Uncertainty about your position in the system is an argument for fairness, not paralysis.
