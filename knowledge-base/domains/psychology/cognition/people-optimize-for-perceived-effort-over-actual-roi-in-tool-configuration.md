The article's observation — 'most people would rather spend 50 hours getting mediocre outputs than 10 minutes setting up properly' — captures a broader cognitive bias around tool configuration. Setup time feels like overhead rather than investment because the benefit is diffuse (spread across all future interactions) while the cost is concentrated (right now, before you get to do anything).

This is a variant of hyperbolic discounting applied to tool usage. The compounding nature of good configuration means even a 5% improvement per interaction multiplied across hundreds of conversations dwarfs the initial setup cost. Yet it's psychologically invisible because each individual interaction feels 'good enough.' This same pattern explains why most developers don't configure their IDEs, most writers don't set up text expansion, and most teams don't invest in CI/CD until pain becomes acute.
