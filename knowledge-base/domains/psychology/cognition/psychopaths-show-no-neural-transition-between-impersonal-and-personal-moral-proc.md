When normal people encounter the shift from impersonal to personal moral dilemmas, their amygdala and related emotional circuits activate dramatically — what Dutton describes as 'lighting up like a pinball machine.' In psychopaths, this transition produces no observable change in brain activation. The emotion neighborhood of their brain has what Dutton calls a 'neural curfew.'

This isn't a matter of psychopaths suppressing emotion through willpower — it's a structural difference in neural activation patterns. They process both scenarios through the same cold-empathy prefrontal circuits. This finding reframes psychopathy from a behavioral description to a measurable neurological difference, and suggests that the capacity for personal moral anguish is not a universal human feature but a specific neural capability that varies across the population.
