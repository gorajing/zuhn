The hadith tradition includes a sophisticated source-criticism methodology (isnad analysis) that grades individual sayings by the reliability of their transmission chain. Well-sourced hadiths — where a close companion directly heard the Prophet — carry near-scriptural weight. Poorly-sourced ones are treated with skepticism or outright rejected by mainstream scholars.

This internal quality hierarchy is an underappreciated feature of knowledge systems. The 72 virgins example is instructive: outsiders treat it as core Islamic doctrine, but within the tradition it's a poorly-sourced hadith that most Muslims don't accept. The broader lesson is that mature epistemological systems build in their own credibility gradients — and understanding those gradients is essential before making claims about what a tradition 'believes.'
