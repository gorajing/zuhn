Research shows that humans have disproportionately white scleras compared to other primates and especially predators. This isn't incidental — it's a vital piece of human eye morphology that makes gaze direction immediately readable at a glance. Predators benefit from hiding where they're looking, so their eyes tend to have less contrast between iris and sclera.

Humans also possess dedicated gaze-direction neural networks with individual neurons that fire specifically when someone stares directly at us and stop firing when gaze shifts by just one or two degrees. This extreme sensitivity to being watched — combined with eyes designed to broadcast gaze — reflects humanity's deep evolutionary dependence on cooperation, social coordination, and trust. The cooperative payoff of transparent attention outweighed any predatory advantage of concealment.
