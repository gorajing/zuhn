Before the internet, most things people said and did would be forgotten and misremembered within a fairly short time. This ephemerality was a feature, not a bug — it allowed people to evolve, craft new personas, and move beyond past baggage. The internet's permanent memory has eliminated this social affordance.

The consequences cut both ways. Being 'canceled' at 35 for something said as a teenager forces lifelong reputational vigilance, even from childhood. Prospective partners and business associates can Google your entire history, and erasing it creates its own suspicion signal. On the positive side, maintaining friendships and recalling past arguments is easier. But the net effect is a historically unprecedented constraint on identity fluidity — humans evolved in environments where reputation was local and memory was unreliable, and we're now living in an environment where reputation is global and memory is perfect.
