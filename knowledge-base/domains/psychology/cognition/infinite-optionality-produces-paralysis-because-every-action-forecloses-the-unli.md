The essay describes a specific mechanism of paralysis: social media shows every possible version of who you could be, all at once. The promise 'you can be anything' combined with real-time exposure to millions of lives creates not inspiration but frozen inaction. Every action limits the future, and the internet convinced Gen Z their future should be unlimited. One in five Gen Zers are not in employment, education, or training — not because they lack ability but because they've internalized that commitment equals loss.

This extends the paradox of choice beyond consumer decisions into identity formation. Barry Schwartz showed that too many jam options reduce purchase likelihood; the essay argues the same mechanism operates on life choices. Gen Z doesn't rebel because 'anything transgressive can already be done from the screen in our pockets' — the simulation of experience substitutes for the real thing. The antidote the essay proposes is deliberate constraint: choosing scaffolding (religious, philosophical, communal) that narrows options enough to enable action, the way an artist must learn rules before breaking them.
