Across multiple Johns Hopkins studies, the degree to which participants report mystical-type experiences — unity, transcendence of time and space, sacredness, noetic quality — is the strongest predictor of positive therapeutic outcomes, even after controlling for dose. Two people receiving identical doses can have vastly different therapeutic results depending on the depth of their subjective experience.

This finding is remarkable because it suggests that a subjective, consciousness-level phenomenon has causal therapeutic power — not just the pharmacological action of the drug on serotonin receptors. It challenges purely mechanistic models of psychiatry and implies that the quality of conscious experience itself is a therapeutic variable that can be optimized through set, setting, and preparation rather than just neurochemistry.
