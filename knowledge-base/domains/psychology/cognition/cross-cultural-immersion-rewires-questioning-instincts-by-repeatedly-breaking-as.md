Gregersen's 30-year research journey began with a personal observation: living and working in Finland, England, France, the UAE, and the US taught him that assuming knowledge in unfamiliar contexts leads to grave misunderstandings. Each cultural context broke his existing mental models, forcing him into a questioning posture.

This isn't just anecdotal — it reveals a cognitive mechanism. When your assumptions consistently fail (as they do in foreign cultures), your brain gradually shifts from a default mode of assertion ('I know how this works') to a default mode of inquiry ('I need to find out how this works'). The leaders Gregersen studied who were most skilled at catalytic questioning shared this trait of extensive cross-context exposure. The practical implication is that deliberately placing yourself in unfamiliar environments — not just traveling but living and working in them — is one of the most reliable ways to develop the questioning instinct that drives innovation.
