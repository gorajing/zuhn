RuBisCo was perfectly adapted to an early Earth atmosphere rich in CO2 and nearly devoid of oxygen. But plants' own success — flooding the atmosphere with oxygen as a photosynthesis byproduct — changed the environment that RuBisCo was optimized for. Now the enzyme confuses oxygen for carbon dioxide roughly half the time, producing toxic phosphoglycolate that plants must spend energy to neutralize.

This is a profound model for cognitive and organizational rigidity. Expertise optimized for one environment becomes a liability when conditions shift. The very success that changed the environment was driven by the same system now suffering from the change. In human cognition, this manifests as expert blind spots: deep pattern-matching trained in one paradigm becomes actively misleading in a new one. The hardest part isn't building new capabilities — it's that your existing optimization actively fights against adaptation.
