Bo Seo describes a counter-intuitive practice among elite debaters: in the final moments before going on stage — after weeks of research reinforcing their position — they take out a fresh sheet of paper and write the four best arguments for the opposing side. They then re-read their own case through the eyes of someone who fervently disagrees, identifying every flaw.

This practice works because certainty is the enemy of adaptability. Weeks of one-sided preparation create cognitive rigidity — you become unable to anticipate or respond to arguments you haven't considered. The side-switch exercise restores intellectual flexibility at the moment you need it most.

The broader principle applies to any high-stakes presentation or decision: founders pitching to skeptical investors, product managers defending roadmap choices, engineers proposing architectural changes. Spending 10 minutes stress-testing your own position from the opposition's perspective yields better results than spending those same 10 minutes polishing your existing argument.
