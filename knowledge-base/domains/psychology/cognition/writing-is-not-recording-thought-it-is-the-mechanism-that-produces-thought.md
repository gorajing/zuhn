Graham credits Montaigne with discovering that expressing ideas helps form them, then strengthens the claim: 'helps is far too weak a word.' He reports that 90% of what ends up in his essays was stuff he only thought of when he sat down to write. Writing isn't transcription of pre-formed thoughts — it's the engine that produces them.

This reframes how to approach any knowledge work requiring original thinking. Waiting until you 'know what you want to say' before writing is backwards — the writing itself is how you discover what you think. This applies to product strategy documents, technical architecture decisions, and research synthesis. Starting to write before you feel ready isn't premature; it's the only way the real thinking begins.
