At Fatima, Catholics saw the Virgin Mary. At Dhammakaya, Buddhists saw monk Luang Pu Sodh with a crystal sphere in his belly — an image directly from their meditation practice of visualizing a Buddha with a crystal ball at the heart center. Both groups saw the same structural phenomenon: sun spinning, colors shifting, light becoming non-blinding.

This clean separation between structure and content is a powerful framework for understanding anomalous experiences generally. The mechanism (likely related to prolonged bright-light gazing in an emotionally heightened, meditative state) produces a predictable perceptual template. But the mind fills in the template with culturally available content. The Dhammakaya practitioners had been meditating on crystal-ball-in-belly imagery immediately before the event, and that's exactly what they projected onto the visual phenomenon.

This structure/content distinction applies broadly: from pareidolia (mechanism = pattern-matching on noise; content = faces, because that's what brains are primed to find) to dreams, psychedelic experiences, and even conspiracy thinking. The generative mechanism is universal; the narrative overlay is local.
