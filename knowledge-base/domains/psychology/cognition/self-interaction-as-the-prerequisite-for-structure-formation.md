Tyson explains that dark matter is diffusely spread across the universe rather than concentrated because its particles don't interact with one another. Normal matter forms planets, stars, and galaxies because atomic and molecular bonds allow particles to accumulate and create concentrated structures. Without self-interaction, you just get an undifferentiated zone of mass.

This principle generalizes powerfully: in any system, the capacity for components to interact with EACH OTHER (not just respond to external forces) is what enables emergent structure. A marketplace where buyers and sellers only transact but never communicate stays flat. A team where members only report to the manager but never collaborate stays unstructured. A community where members consume content but never interact stays diffuse. The design implication is clear — if you want complex structure to emerge, you must enable and encourage self-interaction among components, not just top-down coordination.
