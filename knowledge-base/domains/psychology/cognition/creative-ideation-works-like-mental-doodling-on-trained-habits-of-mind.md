Graham proposes that creative ideation works like doodling: just as trained artists unconsciously make characteristic gestures when their hand wanders, trained minds unconsciously apply domain-specific 'functions' to random 'arguments.' A metaphor is literally a function applied to an argument of the wrong type. This explains why you must work in a field for a while before having ideas in it—you need to develop habits of mind to invoke.

Critically, the most productive idea generation happens when habits from one field are applied to problems in a distant field. Computer science and electrical engineering are too obviously related for cross-pollination to yield surprises. But hacking and painting—both 'maker' disciplines—represent practically virgin territory for idea transfer. This suggests that the deliberate cultivation of diverse expertise, especially in fields with non-obvious connections, is a more reliable creativity strategy than brainstorming techniques.
