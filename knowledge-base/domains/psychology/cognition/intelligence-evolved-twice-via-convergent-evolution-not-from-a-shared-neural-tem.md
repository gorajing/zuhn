Three 2025 Science papers compared cell-type atlases of bird and mammal pallium and found that while functional circuitry is strikingly similar, the underlying neurons arise from different developmental zones (dorsal vs ventral) in the last common ancestor (~320Mya). This makes intelligence a case of convergent evolution similar to bird vs bat flight or octopus vs mammal camera eyes.

The mechanism matters beyond biology: it demonstrates that complex cognition is not tied to a specific neural architecture. Evolution found two distinct physical implementations of the same computational function. This is a strong biological precedent for substrate independence — the principle that intelligence depends on information processing patterns rather than a specific physical realization.

For AI researchers and cognitive scientists, this reframes the question 'what is necessary for intelligence?' toward circuit-level computational properties rather than specific cell types or brain regions. It also suggests the design space for intelligence is wider than human neuroanatomy implies.
