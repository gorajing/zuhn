Graham notes that he couldn't remember which books or experiences had originally convinced him that 'a perfect formulation of a problem is already half its solution.' He had the belief, he had conviction in the belief, but the causal chain was opaque. This is not unusual — it is the normal epistemic condition. Mental models are compiled from thousands of inputs and the compilation discards the source map.

This has significant implications for intellectual honesty and debate. When someone holds a confident belief, demanding they cite sources may be asking for something that no longer exists — not because they're being evasive, but because the evidence was absorbed into the model long ago. The belief's justification is distributed across forgotten experiences and half-remembered readings.

For knowledge work, this suggests that epistemic hygiene requires building external records at the point of learning (notes, highlights, journals) rather than relying on later recall — because the internal record of provenance will degrade while the belief itself persists and may even strengthen.
