The Fermi Paradox's power comes from a simple Bayesian insight: given the number of habitable planets (billions), the time available (billions of years), and the relatively short time needed to colonize a galaxy (~2 million years), even one spacefaring civilization should have left detectable traces everywhere. The continued absence of any signal isn't just 'we haven't looked hard enough' — it's statistically meaningful.

This reasoning pattern applies well beyond astronomy. In business, if a seemingly obvious product idea has never been successfully built despite many well-funded attempts, that absence is informative — there's likely a hidden barrier. In debugging, if a bug 'should' be obvious but extensive testing reveals nothing, the absence suggests your mental model of the system is wrong. The key threshold is: would the thing be hard to miss if it existed? If yes, not finding it is real data.
