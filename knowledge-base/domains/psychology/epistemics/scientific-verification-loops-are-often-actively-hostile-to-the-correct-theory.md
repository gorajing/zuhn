Prout hypothesized in 1815 that all atomic nuclei are made of hydrogen and should have whole-number weights. Chlorine measured at 35.5, and over the next decades, better measurements pushed it to 35.46 — the evidence was getting further from the 'correct' answer, not closer. Only in 1900+ did isotopes resolve the puzzle. For 85 years, the verification loop was actively hostile to the truth.

Nielsen pairs this with Uranus (where an anomaly correctly pointed to an unseen planet, Neptune) and Mercury (where the same move pointed to a non-existent planet, Vulcan, and actually required general relativity). Ex ante you cannot distinguish 'anomaly means hidden object' from 'anomaly means new theory.' 99.9% of the time, Pioneer-spacecraft-style anomalies are mundane (thermal asymmetry), but occasionally they're Nobel Prizes. The falsificationist picture where one experiment kills one theory is mostly fiction.

The practical upshot: a healthy research community needs protected diversity — parallel programs pursuing mutually inconsistent hypotheses, funded even when current evidence looks bad, because the verification loop is too noisy and too slow to pick winners in real time. This is also why 'AI will solve science by running experiments faster' underestimates the problem: faster experiments don't resolve the many-theories-compatible-with-any-data problem.
