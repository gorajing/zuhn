Cancer cells form cooperative tumors to commandeer blood supply and resources, but their inherent genetic instability means some mutant cells will eventually defect from this cooperation, creating hypertumors that starve the original cancer. This is a profound epistemic pattern: systems built on selfish cooperation are inherently unstable because the same selfish logic that created the collective will eventually turn inward. The principle extends beyond biology — monopolistic cartels fracture when individual members defect, authoritarian regimes destabilize when lieutenants compete for power, and even malware ecosystems see competing strains cannibalizing each other. Recognizing this pattern suggests that sometimes the most effective strategy against a harmful system is not direct opposition but allowing its internal contradictions to play out.
