The standard trade-off framing: you can have honest beliefs (painful) OR comforting beliefs (nice). This is a false dichotomy. In most situations there is a non-empty intersection — beliefs that are both honest AND comforting, just differently comforting than the self-deceptive version. If a relationship ends, the self-deceptive comfort is 'they never really cared about me anyway.' The honest-comforting alternative might be 'we genuinely loved each other but were incompatible, and I'm capable of finding another relationship.' Both are comforting; only one is honest. Galef's claim is that people reflexively jump to self-deception because it's the first comfort that comes to mind, not because honest comforts don't exist. The practical advice: when you catch yourself reaching for a self-deceptive belief, pause and ask 'what would feel almost as comforting while being true?' The answer is usually available with five minutes of searching. The general insight: the honest-comforting intersection is almost always non-empty, and most self-deception happens from laziness rather than necessity.
