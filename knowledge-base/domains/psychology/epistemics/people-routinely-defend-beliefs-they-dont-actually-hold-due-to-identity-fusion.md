When someone argues with you, your instinct is to defend your position. But Galef points out a surprising failure mode: often the 'position' you're defending is not one you actually hold. It's a position someone assumed you held — because of your tribe, your profession, your appearance, your previous statements — and you started defending it without checking whether you actually believed it. Once you've defended it publicly, it becomes part of your identity, and now you have a real stake in not backing down. The social momentum does the work. Paul Graham's 'Keep Your Identity Small' essay argues this is one of the key moves in clear thinking: keep your self-concept narrow enough that most arguments don't feel like attacks on YOU. Galef's practical technique: when you notice yourself defending a position, pause and ask 'do I actually believe this, or am I defending it because someone assumed I did?' The answer is often clarifying — many defended positions evaporate under this test. This generalizes to any context where social pressure shapes which beliefs you appear to hold vs which you actually hold.
