Organizations default to measuring what is easiest to measure (stock price, daily active users, followers, clicks, email opens). These metrics are legible to managers, comparable across quarters, and chart-friendly. But they are typically false proxies for the actual mission. Yahoo's stock ticker in the office caused thousands of employees to optimize every decision for one-day stock movement, which is not the same as long-term company health. The behavioral response is consistent: once a number is made visible and associated with judgment, humans will move that number by whatever means available, including means that damage the underlying goal. The fix is not better incentives on the false proxy (this makes it worse) but replacing the proxy with a measurement closer to the real goal. Seth's example for email marketing: subscriber count plus open rate on the wall. Both numbers are hard to game in ways that damage the mission; both correlate with the mission. This selection discipline is a core leadership function.
