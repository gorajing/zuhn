The Weismann Barrier held as dogma for nearly a century. Even as phenotypic inheritance evidence accumulated over the last 20 years, the mechanism remained speculative. What broke the dogma was not more phenotype studies but the ability to identify specific microRNAs in sperm and the fertilized egg that regulate relevant downstream genes — a direct mechanistic link revealed by sequencing technology that didn't exist during Weismann's lifetime.

This pattern recurs in science: (1) dogma forms around what current instruments can detect, (2) anomalies accumulate but can't be explained mechanistically, (3) a new measurement technique finally reveals the mechanism, (4) the dogma collapses. It's why punctuated equilibrium had to wait for Bayesian phylogenetic computation, why the bird-intelligence question had to wait for single-cell atlases, and why so many scientific revolutions are, at their core, instrumentation revolutions.

Practically: when a field has a long-standing unresolved debate, watch the measurement frontier. The resolution rarely comes from more arguments — it comes from someone building a new tool that lets you see what no one could see before.
