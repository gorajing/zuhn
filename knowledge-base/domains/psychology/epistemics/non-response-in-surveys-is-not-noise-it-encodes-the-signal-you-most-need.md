Standard polling practice imputes non-respondents using demographic proxies (age, sex, race). This is defensible only when the reason for non-response is unrelated to the question being asked. Graham's analysis shows this assumption breaks down precisely when stakes are highest: contested elections where one candidate's supporters face social costs for disclosure.

This is a general epistemological trap. In any domain where self-reporting is used to estimate population beliefs or behaviors, the analyst must ask: is the act of reporting independent of the content being reported? If not, the sample is systematically biased in the direction of socially acceptable answers. The practical fix is to use indirect elicitation methods (list experiments, randomized response techniques) or to treat non-response rate itself as a variable—high refusal rates in a demographic group may be the finding, not an obstacle to the finding.
