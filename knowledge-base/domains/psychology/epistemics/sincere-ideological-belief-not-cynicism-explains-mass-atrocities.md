Kotkin pushes hard against the modern temptation to explain mass atrocity through cynicism — the comforting assumption that 'no one really believed that stuff, they must have been smart like us.' The declassified archives from the Soviet period reveal the opposite: true belief was pervasive and durable, even among educated people, even among people whose own families were destroyed by the system, even in the face of overwhelming empirical falsification.

Goebbels was physically disabled and helped murder the disabled. Xi Jinping's family was persecuted in the Cultural Revolution and he doubled down on the party monopoly. Khrushchev denounced Stalin's crimes and then 'got a second wind of belief' in the system rather than questioning state ownership or the dictatorship. The pattern isn't weakness or stupidity — humans can hold contradictory thoughts simultaneously and the ideological framework 'empowers them across the board' to be the decision-makers without having to win elections or earn legitimacy through results.

The practical lesson: when analyzing authoritarian or ideological movements today, assume the believers mean it. Treat stated ideology as a load-bearing predictor of behavior rather than a cover story for cynical self-interest. This applies equally to studying contemporary political movements, evaluating mission-driven organizations, and assessing the risk profile of ideologically-committed adversaries.
