Graham notes that people can easily recognize the dot-com consulting boom as a temporary aberration because it lacks ideological weight — nobody built a political movement around web consulting in 1999. But the union wage premium of the 1950s accumulated decades of ideological meaning, making it nearly impossible for people to see it as the same kind of temporary market distortion.

This is a general epistemic trap: the more politically or morally charged a historical event becomes, the harder it is to analyze it with the same cold eye you'd apply to a market correction. The 'golden age' narrative about mid-century labor economics is a case study in how ideology can substitute for structural analysis.

For knowledge workers and investors, the implication is to actively seek stripped-down structural explanations before reaching for narratives about heroism, moral failure, or institutional capture. Ask: what were the underlying market conditions? What growth phase was this industry in? The simplest structural explanation is often more accurate than the most emotionally satisfying narrative.
