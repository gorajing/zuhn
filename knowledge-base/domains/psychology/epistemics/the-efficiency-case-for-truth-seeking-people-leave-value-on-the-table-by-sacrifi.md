Most arguments for intellectual honesty are moral: you OUGHT to seek truth because it's virtuous or because self-deception is dishonest. Galef's distinctive angle is different: scout thinking is efficient. Across thousands of life decisions — what to eat, whom to marry, which job to take, whether to move, which medical treatment to choose, what to invest in — more accurate maps of reality produce better outcomes by a compounding margin. The person who sees clearly makes slightly better choices in each individual decision, and those slight improvements compound into dramatically better life outcomes over decades. Meanwhile the emotional benefit of each individual self-deception is usually small and fleeting. The trade is bad even on pure self-interest grounds. 'I hate waste' is Galef's framing — she finds it viscerally frustrating that people leave value on the table by refusing to see clearly. This reframes truth-seeking from a moral burden to a rational self-interest calculation, which may be more motivating for many people than moral arguments.
