The Hubble constant has been contested since the 1920s, but by the early 2000s, the field converged on ~70 and most people thought the war was over. What reopened it was not a new value but tighter error bars on both sides: Planck's CMB-based 67 with tiny uncertainty, and SH0ES's 73 with tiny uncertainty. Neither side could accommodate the other within its stated error budget. This is the generalizable pattern: two independent measurements of the same quantity can tolerate disagreement indefinitely as long as their uncertainties overlap, but when both shrink their error bars below the gap, the disagreement becomes load-bearing and one method (or the theory connecting them) has to be wrong. The precision itself is what forces the question.
