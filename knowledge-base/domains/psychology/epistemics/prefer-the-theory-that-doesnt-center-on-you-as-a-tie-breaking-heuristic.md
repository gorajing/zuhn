Graham offers a practical decision rule derived from intellectual history: when choosing between competing explanations, prefer the one that doesn't revolve around you. This is the everyday version of Copernicus — a default correction for the systematic anthropocentric bias wired into human cognition.

This applies directly to interpersonal situations. When a housemate eats your food, 'they were hungry' outcompetes 'they wanted to upset me' — not because you can rule out malice, but because hunger is more common and requires less intentionality. The broader principle: randomness, circumstance, and mundane motives explain more of the world than deliberate targeting of us.

As a practical habit, running this check before settling on an explanation can interrupt the default pattern of over-attributing agency and intention to events that are better explained by chance or unrelated motivations.
