Midha argues this is the best era in history for first-principles thinkers because the things they reasoned would happen are now coming true, while the venture community defaults to clinging to axioms that were true in the previous era and proclaiming them with false confidence. The core distinction: an axiom can be proven from internal consistency, whereas a heuristic is merely a shortcut. The error he repeatedly encounters is people using heuristics as if they were axioms to judge which people and companies will succeed (e.g., dismissing Anthropic as 'just training models, they'll be the next box SaaS'). A related, deeper failure he names is reasoning by analogy plus invisibly-held assumptions — he illustrates with Jensen Huang and Dwarkesh Patel 'passing each other' on a podcast because they held different abstractions (a five-layer industry cake vs. a pre/mid/post-training loop) for the same underlying reality. The practical discipline is to make your assumptions visible, distinguish heuristic from axiom, and notice when someone is usefully precise (asking about 'real-time action prediction models' rather than the looser abstraction 'world models') as a signal they've actually done the work.
