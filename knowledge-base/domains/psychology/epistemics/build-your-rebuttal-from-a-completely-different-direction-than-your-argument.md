Paine learned this framework teaching at the Naval War College, where students are required to construct a counterargument to their own thesis in every paper. The key insight: a rebuttal that restates the original argument is worthless and annoying. The most effective rebuttals come at the problem from a completely different direction than either the argument or counterargument, creating an orthogonal line of support that the counterargument cannot reach.

Paine's own lecture is the example. Argument: Japan's smart decisions overturned the Asian balance of power. Counterargument: No, China's perfect storm of civil wars, imperialism, and Manchu collapse did the work — Japan got lucky. Rebuttal: Coming from the Russia angle — Russia was the catalyst, and it was Japan leveraging Russia's pressure on China that produced the outcome. Notice that 'Russia' isn't mentioned in either the argument or counterargument; it's a third axis.

Beyond debate: this practice forces you to steelman opposing views, surface legitimate points you'd otherwise dismiss, escape monocausal explanations, and anticipate your boss's objections before the meeting. Most people never change their minds because they never build a real counterargument to themselves, and therefore double down on bad decisions. The framework is cheap discipline with huge compounding epistemics returns.
