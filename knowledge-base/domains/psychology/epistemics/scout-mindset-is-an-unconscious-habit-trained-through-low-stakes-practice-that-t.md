The common mistake in trying to think more clearly is to start with the decisions where it matters most — your marriage, your career, your deepest political beliefs. But those are precisely the domains where the soldier mode is loudest and scout training is hardest. Galef's recommendation is counterintuitive: practice scout thinking where the stakes are low. Notice yourself looking for exceptions to judgments, emotionally consider being wrong about small things, steelman opposing views on topics you don't care about. These become unconscious mental habits — patterns you fall into automatically — rather than conscious techniques you have to remember to deploy. Once the habits are trained, they transfer: the same pattern that made you scout-think about a trivia argument now kicks in on a major career decision. The implication is that 'rationality training' should look more like practicing a skill on drills than studying logic textbooks — habit formation through repetition, not information acquisition.
