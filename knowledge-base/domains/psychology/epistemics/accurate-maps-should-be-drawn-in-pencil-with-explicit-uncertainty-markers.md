People like certainty. A confident answer feels better than a tentative one, so we reach for confident framings even when we don't actually know. This is a problem: once you've committed to a confident framing, any new evidence that contradicts it threatens your prior commitment, and the soldier mode fires to defend it. The alternative is to draw your maps in pencil — hold beliefs as provisional with explicit uncertainty markers. 'I'm about 70% confident X is true given my current evidence; if Y turns up, I'll revise downward.' This sounds pedantic but it serves a specific purpose: it pre-commits you to updating when new evidence arrives, because you've already framed your belief as conditional on the evidence. When the evidence changes, updating is a natural continuation of your original framing rather than a humiliating reversal. Practical consequence: get comfortable with phrases like 'I currently think,' 'my best guess is,' 'I'm moderately confident' — they are not weasel words, they are the correct shape of a belief under uncertainty, and they make you much more willing to update later.
