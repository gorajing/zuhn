The polarization problem is commonly framed as people having different values or opinions. But algorithmic clustering runs deeper: when two people's feeds are sufficiently diverged, they are exposed to different sets of claimed facts, different framings of events, and different figures of authority. The result is not disagreement about conclusions but incompatible starting premises — which makes persuasion or common ground nearly impossible.

This has a practical epistemics implication for anyone who wants accurate models of the world: your feed is not a window onto reality, it's a curated projection optimized for your past engagement. Deliberately seeking sources from outside your cluster — sources that feel uncomfortable or foreign — is not just intellectually virtuous, it's the only way to correct for algorithmic distortion. The people most confident they have 'the real picture' are often the most deeply clustered.
