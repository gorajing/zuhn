Henrich's field research shows that when anthropologists ask Hadza bow-makers or Inuit hunters why they do specific steps in a process, the typical answer is 'it's our custom' or 'it's important to people around here.' They don't know that bitter cassava's 10-step processing prevents cyanide accumulation, or that food taboos around pregnancy protect against marine toxins. When bitter cassava was transplanted to Africa, people started eating it improperly and developing goitre — the practice was decoupled from its cultural context and its protective effect was lost.

This creates what Henrich calls an 'epistemic hell': you don't know why something works, but you know that breaking with tradition might kill you or your family. Religious taboos and mystical frames become the mechanism by which causally-opaque knowledge gets enforced across generations. The WEIRD instinct to demand explanations before following a practice is actually a relatively recent and risky move.

The operating principle is: before dismantling a cultural practice, institution, or convention you don't understand, figure out what invariant it's maintaining. 'It's just tradition' might mean 'someone already solved this problem and you're about to unsolve it.' This applies equally to team rituals, codebases, organizational conventions, and anything with path-dependent logic.
