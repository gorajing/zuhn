A common misconception is that supermassive black holes function like the Sun in our solar system — a central anchor holding everything in orbit. In reality, SMBHs contain only about 0.001% of their galaxy's mass (compared to the Sun's 99.86% of solar system mass). It's the invisible dark matter that gravitationally binds galaxies together.

This pattern recurs across domains: the loudest voice in a room isn't necessarily the leader, the most visible founder isn't always the one holding the company together, and the most obvious metric isn't always the one driving outcomes. Correctly identifying the actual structural force — rather than the most prominent one — is a critical epistemic skill.
