The proof maps the abstract sum 1 + 1/4 + 1/9 + 1/16 + ... onto lighthouses at integer positions, where the inverse square law naturally produces the 1/n² terms as apparent brightness. This physical framing transforms the question from 'what does this series converge to?' into 'can we rearrange lighthouses without changing total brightness?' — a question with geometric tools available.

This illustrates a broader principle: when an abstract problem resists direct attack, encoding it into a physical system can reveal hidden structure. The invariant (total brightness) becomes something you can reason about spatially rather than algebraically. The technique generalizes beyond math — any time you're stuck on an abstract optimization, ask whether there's a physical or spatial analogy that makes the constraints tangible.
