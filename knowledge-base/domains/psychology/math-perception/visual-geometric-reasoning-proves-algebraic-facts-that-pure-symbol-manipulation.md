The proof compares three areas: a triangle inscribed in a unit circle (area = cos θ sin θ / 2), a circular sector (area = θ / 2), and a circumscribing triangle (area = tan θ / 2). The geometric containment relationship forces an inequality chain that, after algebraic manipulation, traps sin θ / θ between cos θ and 1/cos θ. The key insight — that these three regions are nested — is fundamentally visual. No amount of symbol manipulation would suggest comparing these specific areas.

This illustrates a broader principle: different representational formats unlock different insights. A spreadsheet reveals patterns that narrative obscures. A graph reveals trends that tables hide. A diagram reveals relationships that equations mask. The most powerful problem-solvers aren't those who master one representational format but those who can translate fluently between visual, symbolic, and verbal modes — using whichever representation makes the crucial pattern most visible.
