The course dedicates an entire section to interpreting derivatives in context: when f(x) is distance, f'(x) is velocity; when f(x) is cost, f'(x) is marginal cost; when f(x) is temperature, f'(x) is the rate of cooling. The mathematical operation is identical in every case — take the limit of the difference quotient — but the meaning changes completely based on what f(x) represents. A bike ride, a cup of coffee cooling, and a production cost function all use the same calculus but require different interpretive frames.

This reveals something profound about mathematical abstraction: the power of calculus isn't in the computation but in the translation layer between formal symbols and real-world meaning. Students who can compute derivatives but can't interpret f'(200) = $7.50 as 'each additional iPad costs roughly $7.50 to produce' have learned the syntax but not the semantics. This same pattern — identical formal operations with radically different interpretations — appears throughout quantitative thinking and is what separates mechanical calculation from genuine understanding.
