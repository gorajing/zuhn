The 4πr² formula for a sphere's surface area is actually a special case of Cauchy's formula: for any convex body, the average projected area (shadow) over all orientations in 3D space equals exactly one-fourth the total surface area. A sphere, being symmetric, casts the same shadow (πr²) from every angle, making the average trivially πr² = (1/4)(4πr²).

This generalization is a masterclass in mathematical taste — moving from a specific beautiful result to the general principle it instantiates. The broader lesson is that when you prove something for a highly symmetric case, asking 'does this hold without the symmetry?' often reveals the real theorem. In applied contexts, this principle underpins stereological methods used in materials science, biology, and medical imaging, where surface areas of irregular objects are estimated from 2D cross-sections and projections.
