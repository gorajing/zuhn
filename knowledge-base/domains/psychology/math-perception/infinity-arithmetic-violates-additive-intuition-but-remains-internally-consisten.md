Hilbert's Hotel demonstrates that infinite sets behave nothing like finite collections. A fully booked hotel with infinite rooms can always accommodate more guests by shifting everyone down. Infinity minus one is still infinity. Infinity divided by two is still infinity. The set of even numbers has the same cardinality as the set of all whole numbers, despite seeming like it should be half the size.

This matters beyond pure mathematics because it reveals a systematic failure mode in human reasoning: we unconsciously apply finite-world heuristics to domains where they don't hold. The same cognitive trap appears in probability (the Monty Hall problem), economics (exponential growth intuitions), and AI scaling (linear extrapolation of capability curves). Recognizing that our intuition has a domain boundary — it works for countable, manipulable quantities and breaks outside that range — is the first step toward compensating for it.
