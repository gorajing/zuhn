In calculus, the limit as x approaches a of f(x) equals L even if f(a) is undefined or equals something completely different from L. The sushi bar example illustrates this perfectly: the price approaches $10 as weight approaches 1 pound, even though the actual price at exactly 1 pound is $0 (free lunch). This mathematical insight maps onto real-world judgment: evaluating a person's trajectory matters more than their current position, and a company's trend line matters more than today's revenue number. The formal epsilon-delta definition of limits makes this precise — for any desired closeness to L, there exists a neighborhood around a where all function values are that close. This neighborhood-based thinking is a powerful mental model for assessing anything that changes continuously.
