The standard Fourier Transform formula — an integral of a function multiplied by a complex exponential — looks impenetrable in symbolic form. But 3Blue1Brown maps each component to a physical operation: the exponential becomes rotation around a circle, the function scales the rotating vector's length, and the integral computes the center of mass. Suddenly the formula isn't a black box; it's a machine you can watch operate.

This reveals a general principle: abstract mathematical operations often have spatial or physical analogues that make them feel natural. The center-of-mass metaphor works because humans have strong spatial intuition but weak symbolic intuition. When you can see why a spike appears at frequency 3 (everything lines up on one side of the circle), the math stops being magic and becomes mechanical. Finding these physical-to-abstract bridges is one of the highest-leverage moves in mathematical education.
