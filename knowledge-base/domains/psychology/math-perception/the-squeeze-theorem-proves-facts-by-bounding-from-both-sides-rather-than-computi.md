The geometric proof that lim(sin θ/θ) = 1 is a masterclass in indirect reasoning. Instead of computing the limit directly, the proof bounds sin θ/θ between cos θ and 1/cos θ using area comparisons of triangles and circular sectors. Since both bounds approach 1 as θ→0, the trapped quantity must also approach 1. No direct calculation of sin θ/θ was ever needed.

This bounding strategy is enormously useful outside mathematics. When you can't compute an exact answer — estimating market size, predicting project timelines, assessing risk — establishing credible upper and lower bounds is often more valuable than a single point estimate. If your optimistic and pessimistic scenarios both lead to the same decision, you don't need the exact number. Fermi estimation, confidence intervals, and even negotiation tactics all use this same squeeze principle: constrain from both sides until the uncertainty band is narrow enough to act on.
