Sanderson's key move is showing that the sum of ring areas (2πr·dr for r from 0 to 3) can be visualized as thin rectangles under the graph of 2πr — a straight line that forms a triangle. The area of that triangle (½ · 3 · 6π = 9π = π·3²) immediately gives the circle's area.

This exemplifies the power of representational shifts. The algebraic expression '∑ 2πr·dr' is opaque. The geometric picture 'area under a line' is transparent. The information content is identical, but the visual representation makes the answer literally visible. This generalizes: when stuck on any accumulation problem, try plotting the summands as a function and looking at the shape they form.
