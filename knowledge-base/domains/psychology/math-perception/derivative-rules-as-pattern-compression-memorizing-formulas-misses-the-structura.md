Students who memorize the product rule (fg)' = f'g + fg' without understanding why it works — that it comes from expanding f(x+h)g(x+h) and adding/subtracting f(x+h)g(x) — hit a wall when encountering unfamiliar function compositions or when they need to derive rules for new operations. The chain rule is particularly illustrative: it encodes the idea that rates of change compose multiplicatively, which is why dy/dx = (dy/du)(du/dx) works.

This mirrors a broader learning principle: compressed rules (principles) are only as useful as one's understanding of the decompressed reasoning behind them. In any domain, someone who understands why a heuristic works can adapt it to edge cases, while someone who only memorized the heuristic is stuck when conditions change. Calculus pedagogy accidentally teaches this meta-lesson about the relationship between principles and their foundations.
