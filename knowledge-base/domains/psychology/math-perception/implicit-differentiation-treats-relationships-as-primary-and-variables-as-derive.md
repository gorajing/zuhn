Most calculus courses start with y = f(x) — explicit functions where y is expressed in terms of x. But many important relationships, like the ellipse 9x² + 4y² = 25, cannot be cleanly solved for y. Implicit differentiation sidesteps this by differentiating both sides of the equation with respect to x, using the chain rule on y terms (since y is implicitly a function of x). The result is dy/dx expressed in terms of both x and y. This technique reflects a deeper truth: in complex systems (economics, biology, engineering), variables are typically constrained by relationships rather than cleanly expressed as functions of each other. Pressure, volume, and temperature in a gas are linked by PV = nRT — none is 'the' dependent variable. Implicit differentiation teaches the mental habit of reasoning about how variables change relative to each other within a constraint, rather than insisting on isolating cause and effect into a y = f(x) frame.
