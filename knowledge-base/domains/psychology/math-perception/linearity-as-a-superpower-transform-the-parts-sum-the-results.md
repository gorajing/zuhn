The video highlights a property that seems technical but is actually the entire reason the Fourier Transform is useful: if you transform signal A and signal B separately, then add the results, you get the same thing as transforming (A + B) together. This linearity means each frequency component produces an independent spike regardless of what other frequencies are present.

Linearity is arguably the most powerful property in all of applied mathematics. It's why superposition works in physics, why you can analyze circuits one source at a time, why convolutions are tractable, and why linear algebra dominates engineering. When a system is linear, complexity becomes manageable because the whole is exactly the sum of its parts — no interaction effects, no emergent complexity from combination. Recognizing when a problem has linear structure is one of the most valuable pattern-recognition skills in technical problem-solving.
