Henrich argues that almost every major first-invention in human history occurred in Eurasia because it was the largest, most interconnected continent — oriented along an east-west axis where ideas and people flowed freely across the 'Lucky Latitudes.' Neanderthals had larger brains and more individual computational power, yet the expanding modern-human population (~1-10,000 people from the Near East ~70,000 years ago) out-competed them because they had denser cultural networks and institutions that preserved and accumulated knowledge.

The practical corollary is that when a population gets isolated or fragmented, it loses technology. The Tasmanian case is the textbook example, but Henrich also points to the Dorset (displaced by Inuit despite having better individual technology) and the fact that human brain size has actually declined over the last 10,000 years as we offloaded cognition into specialists and institutions — a 'superorganism' transition similar to what happens to ant brains when colonies develop occupational castes.

For anyone building communities, teams, or institutions: the lesson is that connectivity and population scale beat individual brilliance. Isolating the smartest people actually hurts — they need to be embedded in a thick network of other minds with different vantage points and enough churn to generate novelty.
