Several techniques exploit the same psychological lever: temporarily lowering the standard against which you judge your output. Calling a painting 'just a sketch,' software 'just a quick hack,' or any project 'an experiment' creates psychological permission to produce imperfect work. Once the project gains momentum, you can sneakily convert it into something serious. The key enabler is choosing a medium that lets you work fast with low upfront commitment — digital over physical, notebooks over carved stone.

The experimental framing has an additional benefit: it redefines failure as knowledge. If the theorem is false or the structural member breaks, you've learned something. This converts every outcome into a win, which sustains motivation through the ugly phase. Lisa Randall's advice crystallizes this: treat new things as experiments — there's no failing, only ruling things out or finding variations that work better.
