Paul Graham argues that experienced hackers develop a 'nose' for good and bad technology — a form of expert intuition built from years of pattern matching. Rather than evaluating every new technology through exhaustive technical analysis, they learn to read contextual signals: who promotes it, who loves it, how it's governed, and what motivations drive its creation.

This maps to research on expert intuition in other domains (chess, medicine, firefighting) where practitioners develop rapid, reliable pattern recognition. The key insight is that technology quality correlates strongly with sociological signals — organic adoption, creator passion, small-team origins — making surface-level evaluation a legitimate and efficient filtering strategy. With hundreds of technologies competing for attention, this heuristic is not laziness but a necessary survival skill.
