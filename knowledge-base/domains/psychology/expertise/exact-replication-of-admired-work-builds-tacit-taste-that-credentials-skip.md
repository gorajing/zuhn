Asked how someone without a PhD develops research taste, Chen calls credentials overrated and points to replication as the best mechanism he has found. As a beginner he took canonical papers (ResNet, PixelCNN) and tried to reproduce their training curves precisely — hitting the exact training loss or perplexity the papers hinted at. The value isn't the result; it's that exact reproduction forces you 'a couple layers deeper,' where you absorb the techniques practitioners use but never write down.

The key design choice is the precision target. Loosely 'building something similar' lets you skip the hard parts; demanding the exact same metric removes the escape hatch and surfaces the tacit knowledge gap. The target metric is what converts a vague exercise into a forcing function.

This generalizes across any expertise with tacit components — music production, writing, trading, design. Reproducing a master's output exactly, rather than admiring it or reading about it, is what transfers the unstated craft. It pairs with Chen's broader claim that taste, not formal training, is what differentiates top researchers, and that the ability to 'creatively solve problems and think outside the box' can be trained up in people without conventional backgrounds.
