When you frame a project as 'making something,' failure means you made something bad. When you frame it as 'running an experiment,' failure means you learned something. This single reframe changes the entire emotional calculus of ambitious work. The fear of lameness loses its grip because experiments are supposed to produce messy, incomplete results.

This connects to a deeper principle: sharply defined failures are themselves knowledge. If you prove a theorem false or a structural approach inadequate, you've eliminated a possibility — that's genuine progress even if it doesn't feel like it. The experimental frame also creates natural stopping points. Instead of the binary succeed/fail, you can vary parameters, pivot approaches, or cleanly abandon dead ends without ego damage. Curiosity becomes a sustainable fuel source because each iteration reveals something new regardless of outcome quality.
