Graham claims this pattern is bidirectional after years of observation: successful people don't become haters because achieving fame reveals how random it is, and haters don't produce great work because the psychological energy directed at obsessive criticism crowds out creation. He suspects frustrated talent — 'it's unfair that so-and-so is famous, and not me' — is a key driver.

This has implications beyond fame management. It suggests that obsessive comparison with specific individuals is a reliable signal of stagnation. The mental orientation required for sustained creative output — internal focus, tolerance for randomness, acceptance of others' success — is fundamentally incompatible with the hater's fixation on someone else's undeserved status. If you find yourself developing hater-like patterns toward anyone, it may be a diagnostic signal about your own creative trajectory.
