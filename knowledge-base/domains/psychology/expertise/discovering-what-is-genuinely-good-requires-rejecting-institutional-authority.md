Graham's epiphany came from the art world, where the corruption was so blatant it 'snapped the leash of credulity.' He realized it was a choice between doing good work and being an insider — and that this tension exists to some degree in almost every field. Philosophy professors published beautifully typeset papers that said nothing definite enough to refute. English teachers assigned fiction optimized for classroom use, not quality.

The implication is that developing genuine taste requires a period of distrust toward authorities. This doesn't mean authorities are always wrong, but their incentives (publishing careers, institutional prestige, insider status) create systematic biases in what they endorse. The independent practitioner must ultimately trust their own quality judgments, informed by the 'garage sale test' and 'guilty pleasure' heuristics rather than institutional approval.
