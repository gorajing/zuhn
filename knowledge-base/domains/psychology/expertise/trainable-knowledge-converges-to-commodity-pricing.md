Naval frames a sharp economic test for career value: if you can be trained for it, you can be replaced. When knowledge is codifiable enough to put into a curriculum, the supply of people with that knowledge scales rapidly — through schools, bootcamps, and eventually automation. This drives compensation toward the marginal cost of producing another trained worker.

The implication is that the highest-value knowledge lives outside formal training systems. It accumulates through pattern-matching in complex environments, innate talent cultivated over years, and judgment built through direct experience. This is why two people with identical credentials can have wildly different market value — the credential measures the trainable part, but the market pays for the untrained part.
