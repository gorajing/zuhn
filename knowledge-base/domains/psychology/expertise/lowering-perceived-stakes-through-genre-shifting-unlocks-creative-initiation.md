Quality standards are essential for good work but toxic for initiating work. Graham identifies a specific psychological maneuver used by successful creators: deliberately miscategorizing their project's genre to lower the applicable quality bar. A painter calls their canvas 'just a sketch'; a programmer calls their system 'just a quick hack.' This isn't denial — it's a deliberate cognitive reframe that makes the gap between current state and acceptable state small enough to be non-paralyzing.

The technique works because genre-shifting is reversible. Once momentum exists, you can 'sneakily convert' the project to its true ambition level. The key enabler is choosing media that support this pattern — low-commitment, fast-iteration tools where early work genuinely can look like a sketch. This explains why digital media has been so productive: the distance between prototype and product is small enough that the genre-shift trick remains plausible throughout development.
