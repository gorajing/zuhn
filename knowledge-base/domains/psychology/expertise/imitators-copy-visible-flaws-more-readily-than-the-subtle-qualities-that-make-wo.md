Graham's example is devastating: 18th and 19th century painters imitated Renaissance masters by using brownish colors — not realizing the brown was centuries of accumulated dirt, not an artistic choice. When the originals were cleaned, they revealed brilliant colors, while the imitators remained permanently brown.

This pattern recurs across domains. Junior developers copy the verbose error handling of senior engineers' legacy code rather than the clean abstractions in their newer work. Writers imitate a famous author's quirky punctuation rather than their narrative structure. The flaw is more salient and easier to reproduce than the quality. The corrective is to explicitly ask 'what makes this good?' before copying, rather than defaulting to 'what stands out?'
