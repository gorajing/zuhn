Most people judge early-stage ambitious work using the same criteria they'd apply to finished products or less ambitious projects. This is a category error — early work is a special case that requires a fundamentally different evaluation lens. The rational expected value of a project that's 20% complete toward a high-value goal can far exceed a polished but low-ambition finished product.

Graham argues this misjudgment isn't deeply rooted in human nature but is simply a gap in our cultural toolkit. We haven't had enough historical experience with early-stage ambitious projects to develop appropriate evaluation norms. Silicon Valley is one of the few places that has begun developing these norms, and the economic payoff proves the approach works. The practical implication: when evaluating new work (your own or others'), consciously switch from 'list reasons it won't work' to 'imagine ways it could work.'
