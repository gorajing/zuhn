Humans default to skepticism when encountering new ideas, which Graham argues is a cultural artifact rather than a deep-rooted instinct. The correct technique is to invert polarity entirely: instead of listing reasons an idea will fail, actively try to imagine ways it could succeed. This is not about lowering standards but about exercising a different cognitive muscle. Graham developed this skill through years at Y Combinator, where the power-law distribution of startup outcomes makes it painfully costly to dismiss the rare world-changing idea hidden among thousands of strange-sounding pitches. The insight generalizes beyond startups: in any domain where outcomes follow a power law, the cost of a false negative (dismissing the breakthrough) vastly exceeds the cost of a false positive (wasting time on a dud).
