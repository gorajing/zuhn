The internal critic applies different standards to different categories of work. A sketch doesn't need to be good — it just needs to exist. By framing ambitious work as a lower-stakes genre (sketch, hack, experiment), you route it past the quality filter that would otherwise prevent you from producing anything at all. Once momentum builds, you can 'sneakily convert' the work into its true ambitious form.

This technique works because the critic's power is front-loaded — it's strongest at the start when the gap between vision and reality is widest. The key enabler is choosing media that support fast iteration and low upfront commitment: digital over physical, notebooks over stone, code over architecture diagrams. The medium itself becomes part of the psychological trick — it's easier to believe something is 'just a hack' when the tool you're using actually supports hacking. Patrick Collison extends this further: making the tenuousness a feature rather than hiding it.
