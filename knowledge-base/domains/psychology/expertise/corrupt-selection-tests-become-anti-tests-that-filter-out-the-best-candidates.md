Graham introduces the concept of 'anti-tests' — selection mechanisms so corrupt they actively filter out the best candidates. High school popularity contests reward political maneuvering that thoughtful people can't stomach. Corporate hierarchies demand attention to politics that someone like Bill Gates could never sustain. The test doesn't just fail to identify talent; it specifically repels it.

The diagnostic for whether a test has become an anti-test is to check the overlap between leading practitioners and the people who teach or gatekeep the field. In math and physics, nearly all teachers are top practitioners — the test is honest. In business and visual arts, there's almost no overlap — the gatekeepers and the doers have diverged, suggesting the selection mechanism has decoupled from actual ability.

This framework has broad application: when evaluating any domain, check whether the path to recognized success requires behaviors that would repel the genuinely talented. If climbing the ladder requires skills orthogonal to the actual work, the system is selecting for the wrong traits.
