Most people assume optimal performance comes from perfectly calibrated self-assessment, but Paul Graham observes that the most successful people across many fields are slightly overconfident. The mechanism is error compensation: early-stage ambitious work is systematically undervalued by both the creator and observers. Slight overconfidence acts as a counterweight to this compounding negative bias, producing a net estimate closer to the true expected value.

This is not about delusion — it's about correcting for a known systematic error. If you look at something 20% complete toward a goal worth 100 and perceive it as 10% toward a goal worth 200, your expected-value estimate is actually correct despite both components being wrong. The practical implication is that cultivating mild overconfidence about importance and ability is a rational strategy, not a character flaw.
