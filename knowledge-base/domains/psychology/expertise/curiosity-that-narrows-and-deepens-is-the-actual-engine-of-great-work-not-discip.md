Graham observes that people who do great work are universally terrible procrastinators who cannot make themselves do anything they're not interested in — yet they produce extraordinary output. The resolution is that curiosity transforms work into play. Einstein didn't experience relativity as drudgery; it was a mystery he was compelled to solve.

School creates a false model where discipline appears essential because subjects are taught so boringly that only willpower gets you through. This illusion persists into adulthood, causing people to pursue goals they think they 'should' want rather than questions that genuinely fascinate them. The practical implication: if you need enormous discipline to do something, that's diagnostic evidence you're working on the wrong problem. The right amount of discipline is just enough to sit down and start — after that, interest should take over.
