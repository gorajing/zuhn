Graham makes an elegant logical argument against taste relativism: as you design more, your taste changes. Since you know you're getting better at your job, your old tastes were not merely different but worse. This destroys the axiom that taste can't be wrong. The cultural norm of 'taste is subjective' originates not from philosophical truth but from parents trying to stop siblings from bickering.

This has practical implications for anyone building creative skills. It means taste can be studied, developed, and refined through exposure to great work and deliberate practice. It also means experts genuinely do have better taste than novices — not just different preferences. The recipe Graham distills is: very exacting taste plus the ability to gratify it. Both components are trainable, but developing taste requires admitting that better and worse exist.
