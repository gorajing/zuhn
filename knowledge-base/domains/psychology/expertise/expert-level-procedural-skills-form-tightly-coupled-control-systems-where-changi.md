Bicycle riding involves the simultaneous coordination of pedal force, body lean, handlebar input, gyroscopic effects, and balance corrections. Destin's key observation is that reversing just one variable (steering direction) doesn't create a proportionally small challenge — it breaks the entire system. No one at any of his demonstrations could ride even 10 feet.

This reveals that expert skills are not assembled from independent, swappable modules. They are tightly coupled control systems where each variable is co-adapted to every other. This principle extends beyond motor skills: an expert programmer's workflow, a surgeon's technique, or a trader's decision process are all similarly integrated. Changing 'just one thing' about an expert's process — a new tool, a different framework, a reorganized team structure — can produce catastrophic performance drops far out of proportion to the apparent size of the change. This argues for extreme caution when modifying established expert workflows and explains why experts often resist seemingly minor process changes that outsiders view as trivial.
