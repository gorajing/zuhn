Graham notes a specific failure mode in private writing: when he hits difficulties, he tends to 'conclude with a few vague questions and then drift off to get a cup of tea.' He compares the audience effect to how inviting people over forces you to clean your apartment. The audience doesn't tell you what to think — it forces you to finish thinking.

This explains why blogging, teaching, and public writing produce deeper understanding than private note-taking. The same principle applies to code reviews, design critiques, and presenting technical decisions to teammates. The audience creates just enough social pressure to push you past the comfortable stopping point where most thinking terminates. The implication for knowledge workers is that making your thinking visible to others — even a small audience — is a forcing function for rigor, not merely a communication exercise.
