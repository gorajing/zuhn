Nielsen walks through several examples: Michelson (who did the key experiment) believed in the ether until he died. Lorentz had the mathematics of special relativity but interpreted it as distortions caused by moving through the ether. Poincaré understood that the speed of light is the same in all inertial frames but still clung to a dynamical picture where particles were being 'pushed' by an external force. Einstein, 25 years younger and less invested, made the clean kinematic leap that space and time themselves are different.

The hypothesis is that these brilliant scientists 'knew too much' — their expertise gave them strong priors that made the counterintuitive move aversive. Einstein as a teenager in the 1890s also believed in the ether, but he wasn't yet staked to it. Kuhn's famous observation that 'science advances one funeral at a time' gets mechanistic grounding here: generational turnover matters because younger researchers haven't yet compiled the sunk costs of a worldview.

For anyone operating in a rapidly shifting field (AI right now), the takeaway is uncomfortable: the more you've invested in mastering the current paradigm, the harder the next paradigm will be to see. Deliberate practices — reading adjacent fields, taking contrarian theories seriously as working hypotheses, befriending smart newcomers — are defenses against expertise-induced blindness.
