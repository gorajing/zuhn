Graham spent years puzzling over why founders don't listen to YC partners, initially attributing it to stubbornness. The deeper insight is that startups are so counterintuitive that correct advice literally sounds wrong to someone without direct experience. This creates a paradox: the domains where advice is most valuable (highly counterintuitive ones) are exactly where advice is least likely to be followed.

This has broad implications beyond startups. In any counterintuitive domain — investing, health, management — experts face the same challenge. The advice-resistance isn't a character flaw; it's a rational response to hearing something that contradicts your mental model. The only reliable teacher is direct experience, which is why founders come back a year later saying 'we wish we'd listened.' This suggests advisors should focus on creating conditions for rapid experiential learning rather than just delivering correct answers.
