Graham makes a subtle but important distinction: expression is not just a verification step for understanding but an active component of the understanding process itself. If you can't make a model of something or write about it, you don't fully understand it — and the act of trying to express it produces deeper understanding than you had before the attempt.

This has practical implications for how to allocate learning time. Rather than consuming more input (reading, listening, watching) when trying to understand something deeply, the higher-leverage move is to attempt output — write about it, build a model, explain it to someone. The struggle of articulation is where the deepening happens. This principle also explains why prolific writers and builders often understand their domains more deeply than pure consumers of the same material.
