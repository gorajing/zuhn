People misjudge early work because they lack reference points for what ambitious projects look like at the beginning. By studying historical cases — first commits, first videos, first drafts — you build an internal library of what 'normal early' looks like for work that eventually became great. This recalibrates your judgment so you can distinguish between genuinely bad ideas and ideas that merely look bad because they're incomplete.

The internet has made this easier than ever: you can now see programmers' first commits, musicians' first recordings, and writers' first published pieces. Graham suggests that enough exposure to these cases can make you 'immune both to other people's skepticism and your own fear of making something lame.' The practical application is deliberate study of origin stories with emphasis on the earliest, most embarrassing stages — not the polished retrospective narratives creators prefer to tell.
