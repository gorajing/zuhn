It seems paradoxical that being mistaken about your abilities could be advantageous — surely perfectly calibrated self-assessment would be optimal. But Graham points out that overconfidence compensates for multiple error sources in the opposite direction: your own harsh judgment of early work, and other people's skepticism. Similarly, overestimating the importance of what you're working on compensates for undervaluing early results. If you see 20% progress toward a goal worth 100 but perceive it as 10% toward a goal worth 200, your expected value estimate is accidentally correct.

This has practical implications for how to calibrate self-belief during creative work. The optimal mindset isn't maximum accuracy — it's slight positive bias. Hardy's advice that good work is not done by humble men reflects the same principle: a touch of grandiosity about your subject and your role in it functions as psychological armor against the inevitable discouragement of early-stage work.
