Terra was marketed as a safe, stable alternative to crypto volatility — essentially a high-yield savings account. This attracted people who were explicitly trying to avoid speculation: conservative savers frustrated by low bank rates who wanted to preserve purchasing power against inflation. When the system collapsed, these were the people posting about losing life savings and homes, because they had allocated capital they couldn't afford to lose based on the safety framing.

This creates a perverse selection effect: the more aggressively a product is marketed as safe, the more it attracts people with low risk tolerance and concentrated positions. Sophisticated investors diversify and size positions based on actual risk assessment. But safety branding short-circuits risk assessment entirely — why diversify something that's safe? The result is that the most devastating failures disproportionately harm people who were trying to be responsible with their money. This same dynamic played out with AAA-rated mortgage-backed securities in 2008 and will recur whenever financial engineering creates products that appear safe while carrying hidden catastrophic risk.
