Apophenia — the tendency to perceive meaningful connections in random information — exists because evolution favors false positives over false negatives in threat detection. If a shadow might be a predator, the organism that runs from shadows survives to reproduce, while the organism that waits for certainty occasionally gets eaten. This asymmetric payoff matrix means pattern-over-detection became the evolutionary default.

This has profound cross-domain implications. In investing, it explains why we see trends in random price movements and why technical analysis feels compelling despite weak evidence. In startups, it explains why founders see market signals that aren't there. In security, it explains why we over-index on dramatic but rare threats. The key insight is that apophenia isn't irrationality — it's a rational response to asymmetric costs that gets misapplied when the cost structure changes. In environments where false positives are cheap (ancestral savanna), it's optimal. In environments where false positives are expensive (trading, medical diagnosis), it becomes a liability.
