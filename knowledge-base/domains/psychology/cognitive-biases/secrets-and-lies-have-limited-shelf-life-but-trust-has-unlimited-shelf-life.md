CIA training teaches that secrets and lies behave like perishable goods—they have a limited shelf life and their value degrades over time. If you cash in on a secret while it's fresh, you capture short-term value before it spoils (someone else reveals it, circumstances change, etc.). However, the trust earned from NOT being the one who cashed in has no expiration date.

This creates a clear decision framework: sharing a secret is always trading unlimited-shelf-life trust for a limited-shelf-life gain. The secret will eventually come to light from someone else—it already has a built-in expiration. But what you win by not being the one who broke it is immense, compounding trust. This principle applies directly to business relationships, partnerships, and personal relationships. In an age where social media incentivizes being the person who 'breaks' information for likes and engagement, the calculus has shifted culturally toward short-term cashing in—but the underlying economics haven't changed.
