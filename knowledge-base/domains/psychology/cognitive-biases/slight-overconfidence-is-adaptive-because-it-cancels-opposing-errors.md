Graham notes that the most successful people across many fields tend to be slightly overconfident—which seems paradoxical since accurate self-assessment should be optimal. The resolution is that overconfidence doesn't exist in a vacuum; it compensates for two opposing forces pulling you toward quitting: other people's skepticism and your own harsh internal critic.

This reframes overconfidence from a cognitive bias to an adaptive calibration error. If the world systematically undervalues early work (which it does), then slightly overvaluing your own contribution brings your net assessment closer to truth. The key word is 'slightly'—massive overconfidence leads to Theranos, but a modest surplus of self-belief provides the activation energy to keep going when feedback is discouraging.
