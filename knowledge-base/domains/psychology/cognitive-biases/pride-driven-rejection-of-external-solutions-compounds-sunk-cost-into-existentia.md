Nokia held 51% mobile market share in 2007 but faced the iPhone threat. Rather than adopting Android — an open platform that could have combined with Nokia's superior hardware — CEO Anssi Vanjoki rejected it with a contemptuous metaphor. Meanwhile, Nokia's internal software efforts split into two competing teams (Symbian revamp vs. MeeGo) that battled each other for resources, creating decision paralysis that took 'the better part of a year' on basic choices like chip selection.

The key pattern is that the rejection wasn't strategic — it was identity-based. Nokia saw itself as a company that builds its own technology stack. This pride transformed what could have been a quick, low-cost recovery (adopt Android, leverage hardware strengths) into a $5B/year R&D sinkhole that produced nothing competitive. The identity-based frame made the sunk cost invisible: each year of failed internal development made the external solution feel even more like an admission of failure.

This bias is especially dangerous because it presents as conviction and self-reliance — virtues in most contexts — but becomes lethal when the competitive window is closing and the internal alternative isn't converging on a solution.
