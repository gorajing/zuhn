The TSA failed to detect 95% of prohibited items in 2015 tests and still missed 70% after improvements in 2017. Yet no terrorist attack has been attempted on a US-departing flight since 9/11. This creates a paradox: the system is objectively terrible at its stated function but may succeed through its unstated one—deterrence. The mere existence of screening, regardless of effectiveness, raises the perceived risk of failure for attackers enough to redirect them elsewhere. This pattern generalizes beyond airports: many security and compliance systems function primarily as deterrents rather than actual barriers, and their value should be measured by attacks prevented (hard to observe) rather than threats detected (easy to measure but misleading).
