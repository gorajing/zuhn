Before measles vaccination (1963), 135 million children caught measles annually — virtually every child on Earth. After widespread vaccination reduced cases to ~83,000 in Europe (2018), the disease became invisible while vaccine side effects remained visible. This creates a systematic perceptual distortion: hundreds of millions vaccinated vs. thousands infected means even mild side effects generate more absolute anecdotes than severe disease outcomes. This prevention paradox applies far beyond vaccines — any successful risk-mitigation system (cybersecurity, financial regulation, infrastructure maintenance) faces the same dynamic where success erodes the perceived need for the intervention.
