The McArthur Wheeler bank robbery case — where a man used lemon juice as a disguise because he vaguely knew it made invisible ink — exemplifies the extreme end of the Dunning-Kruger effect. Wheeler didn't just lack knowledge about chemistry and cameras; he lacked the knowledge needed to recognize that he lacked knowledge.

The effect operates symmetrically: experts tend to underestimate their abilities or assume others share their knowledge level. This has direct practical implications for self-assessment, hiring, team formation, and any situation where people must evaluate their own competence. The actionable takeaway is to actively seek external calibration — peer review, testing against objective standards, or deliberately engaging with people at different skill levels — because internal self-assessment is structurally unreliable at the extremes.
