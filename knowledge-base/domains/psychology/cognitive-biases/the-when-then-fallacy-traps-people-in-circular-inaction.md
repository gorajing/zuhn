Hormozi identifies a logical fallacy he calls 'when-then': the belief that conditions must improve before you can take action, when the action itself is what creates the improved conditions. 'I'll invest in the program that makes me money once I have money.' 'I'll start the fitness plan once I'm in shape.' 'I'll save money once I'm rich.'

This isn't just a sales reframe — it's a fundamental cognitive distortion that affects all decision-making. The fallacy inverts the causal sequence: the program creates the money, the fitness plan creates the fitness, saving creates the wealth. Waiting for the outcome before starting the process that produces the outcome is circular reasoning.

The power of naming this fallacy explicitly is that it's self-evidently absurd once stated clearly. You don't need to argue — you just help someone see their own reasoning restated in a domain where the absurdity is obvious ('I'll go to the hospital once I'm better'). This cross-domain restatement technique works because the logical structure is identical even when the emotional investment is different.
