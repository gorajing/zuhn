Kaku points out that every single physics textbook on Earth contains a fundamental error: they state the universe is mainly made of atoms. In reality, ordinary matter (hydrogen, helium stars) makes up only 4% of the universe, with higher elements like those composing humans at just 0.03%. The remaining 96% is dark energy (73%) and dark matter (23%) — substances we can detect but cannot explain.

This isn't a minor correction at the margins. The single most basic question about the universe — 'what is it made of?' — has been answered incorrectly in every standard reference. The implication extends beyond physics: if the most empirically rigorous field in science can be fundamentally wrong about its most basic fact, then confidence in any established consensus should be held with epistemic humility. The lesson is not that expertise is worthless, but that 'everyone agrees' is not the same as 'this is true.' The biggest discoveries often come from questioning the most taken-for-granted assumptions.
