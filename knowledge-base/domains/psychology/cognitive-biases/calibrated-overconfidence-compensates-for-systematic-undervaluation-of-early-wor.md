Conventional wisdom says optimal performance comes from perfectly accurate self-assessment. Graham argues this is wrong because it ignores the interaction between multiple sources of error. If you're slightly overconfident about your abilities, this compensates for the separate bias of undervaluing early-stage work and for the discouragement caused by others' skepticism.

The math is instructive: if you look at something 20% complete toward a goal worth 100 but you believe it's 10% complete toward a goal worth 200, your expected value estimate ends up correct despite both components being wrong. This means error correction doesn't always require fixing the error itself — sometimes introducing a counterbalancing error is more practical. This has implications for how we think about cognitive biases generally: some biases may be adaptive precisely because they offset other biases.
