Hamkins tells the historical story of the axiom of choice controversy: the principle that for any collection of non-empty sets, you can select one element from each. It seems completely obvious -- Russell illustrates it with shoes (always pick the left one) vs socks (indistinguishable, so you can't describe a rule). Yet when Zermelo used this axiom to prove the well-ordering theorem, mathematicians erupted because the consequence seemed absurd -- nobody could imagine a well-ordering of the real numbers. But when Zermelo examined the papers of his most vocal critics, he found they were implicitly using the axiom of choice in their own arguments. They didn't realize it because the principle is so natural you use it 'by accident.' This pattern extends far beyond math: people routinely reject explicit statements of principles they unconsciously rely on. The axiom of choice teaches that the most dangerous assumptions aren't controversial ones but the ones so natural we don't notice we're making them.
