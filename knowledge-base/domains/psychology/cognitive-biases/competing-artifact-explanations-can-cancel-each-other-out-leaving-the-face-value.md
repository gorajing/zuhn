A common pattern in motivated reasoning is stacking artifact explanations: 'the murder rate looks low because of medical care improvements, AND other crimes look low because of reporting bias.' But this compound explanation requires two independent factors to change at precisely the right rates to produce the observed uniform decline across all crime categories.

This is a general principle for evaluating skeptical claims about data. Single-artifact explanations are healthy skepticism. But when you need multiple independent artifacts all conspiring to produce a clean, consistent signal, you've likely crossed from skepticism into denialism. The face-value reading — that crime actually declined — requires fewer assumptions. This pattern appears frequently in debates about climate data, vaccine efficacy, and economic statistics where motivated parties stack measurement criticisms.
