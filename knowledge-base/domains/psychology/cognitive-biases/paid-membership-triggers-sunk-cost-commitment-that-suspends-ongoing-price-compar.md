Both Costco and Amazon Prime exploit the sunk-cost fallacy, but with an important structural difference. Amazon Prime is optional: customers self-select based on whether they calculate the membership will pay off. This creates loyalty but doesn't change the underlying business model — Amazon still needs to compete on individual transaction prices. Costco's membership is mandatory for entry, which removes the calculation entirely. Once the $60 fee is paid, the psychological default becomes 'use Costco to get value from what I already spent,' not 'compare prices across retailers.' Customers stop shopping around not because prices are always lowest, but because not using the membership feels like a loss. This insight matters for product and pricing design: optional premium tiers create loyalty among those who opt in, but mandatory access fees (or strong switching costs embedded at onboarding) can shift the entire competitive frame. The customer's reference point moves from 'is this the best price?' to 'am I extracting value from my commitment?'
