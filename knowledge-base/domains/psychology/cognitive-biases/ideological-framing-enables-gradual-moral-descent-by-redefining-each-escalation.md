Ross Ulbricht's psychological arc is a case study in how ideology lubricates moral descent. He started with a coherent libertarian philosophy: people should choose what they put in their bodies, the government shouldn't control markets. This framing let him rationalize each escalation — guns were 'constitutional rights,' body parts were 'consensual transactions,' hit orders were 'protecting the community.' The Breaking Bad parallel the documentary draws is apt: both Walter White and Ross started with arguably noble motivations (medical bills / personal freedom) but gradually became the thing they initially would have condemned. The mechanism is identity-protective cognition: once you've defined yourself as fighting for freedom, admitting that your platform enables harm means admitting your identity framework is wrong. Each escalation gets absorbed into the existing narrative rather than triggering a reassessment. This pattern appears in many domains — founders who compromise on ethics 'for the mission,' investors who overlook red flags 'for the returns,' leaders who centralize power 'for efficiency.'
