Americus Reed at UPenn frames brand attachment not as shallow consumerism but as a response to institutional decline. Humans have a fundamental need for identity support systems — historically provided by religion, community organizations, and civic institutions. As these have weakened, brands have stepped into the vacuum, offering ready-made identity narratives people can adopt.

This reframes the 'anti-brand' stance as itself a brand identity, not an escape from the dynamic. It also suggests that the power of brands isn't primarily about product quality or even marketing spend — it's about how well they serve as identity infrastructure. Founders and marketers who understand this build brands that function as communities and belief systems, not just product categories.
