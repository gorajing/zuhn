Harris makes the subtle but critical point that 'your capacity to be offended isn't something that anyone needs to respect — it's something you should be on your guard for.' He argues that moral outrage functions as a cognitive shortcut that bypasses analytical reasoning: when you feel viscerally offended, that feeling presents itself as proof that injustice has occurred, even when the actual evidence is ambiguous.

This has practical implications for any domain involving judgment under emotional pressure. Harris demonstrates this with police videos that 'seem very easy to interpret' but are actually quite ambiguous when examined carefully. The broader principle is that emotional certainty and evidential certainty are not just different — they often move in opposite directions. The more outraged you feel, the less likely you are to notice the complexity in a situation. This applies equally to business decisions, interpersonal conflicts, and public policy debates.
