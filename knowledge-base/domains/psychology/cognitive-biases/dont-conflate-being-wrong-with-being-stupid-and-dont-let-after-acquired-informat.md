Blankfein separates two failure modes that look identical in outcome: losing money because someone was stupid versus losing money because a smart person was simply wrong. 'Smart people tend not to do stupid things, but they tend to be wrong'—he cites that the best hitters in baseball make out two-thirds of the time. Treating the merely-wrong as stupid is corrosive because in a business that's paid to take risk, you must keep people willing to 'lean in' rather than shrivel up after getting singed.

The deeper bias he names is hindsight contamination: 'the big fault of risk management or bosses or managers is they let after-acquired information seep into their judgment of what they would have done at the time.' Because 'none of us know the future—and most of us don't even know the present,' decisions must be judged on the fog that actually existed when they were made. He extends this directly to AI investing: future pundits 'won't be able to put themselves in the shoes without the after-acquired information' and will call today's bets stupid, but 'you weren't stupid with the information available today.' The operational rule is to evaluate decision process given contemporaneous information, separate it from outcome, and extend forgiveness for good bets that didn't pay off—otherwise you suppress the very risk-taking the enterprise depends on.
