The Ethyl Corporation's defense strategy created a template still used today: fund scientists who conclude your product is safe, then cite those conclusions as 'the science.' Their researchers claimed environmental lead was natural and therefore harmless. This framing was demolished by Clair Patterson's independent measurements showing lead concentrations were 10x higher near ocean surfaces (indicating recent contamination, not natural baseline) and that modern Americans had 1,000x more bone lead than ancient mummies.

The structural problem is that industry-funded science doesn't need to fabricate data — it merely needs to frame questions favorably. 'Is a little lead harmful?' (implying a safe threshold exists) vs. 'How much lead is natural?' (Patterson's reframe that exposed the entire premise). Midgley himself demonstrated this performative science by pouring tetraethyl lead on his hands at a press conference while privately recovering from lead poisoning in Florida.

This pattern — fund the science, control the framing, attack independent researchers — appears across tobacco, sugar, opioids, and climate. The antidote is methodological: always ask who funded the research, what question they chose to ask, and whether independent replication exists.
