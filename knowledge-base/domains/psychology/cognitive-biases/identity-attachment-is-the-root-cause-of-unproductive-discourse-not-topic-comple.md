Paul Graham observes that the common factor in unproductive discussions — whether about religion, politics, or programming languages — is not the inherent difficulty of the question but whether participants have made their position part of their identity. A discussion about Bronze Age battles stays rational because no one alive identifies with either side, while the same analytical framework applied to modern geopolitics triggers identity defense mechanisms.

This reframes the problem of polarized discourse entirely. The standard explanation — that some questions simply have no right answers — is wrong. Many political questions have definite answers, yet still provoke religious wars. The actual variable is identity attachment: the same topic can be discussed rationally by people who haven't incorporated it into their self-concept, and irrationally by those who have. This means the quality of a discussion is a function of who's in the room, not what's on the agenda.
