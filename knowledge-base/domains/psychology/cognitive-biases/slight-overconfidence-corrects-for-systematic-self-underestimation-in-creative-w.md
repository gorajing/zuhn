Naive optimization would suggest that accurate self-knowledge maximizes performance. Graham argues this is wrong in practice because the error landscape is asymmetric: both your own inner critic and the social environment tend to penalize ambitious early work too heavily. A slight overconfidence bias counteracts these forces.

The mechanism is double-sided: overestimating the importance of your work inflates the expected-value estimate of the early result (20% of a goal worth 200 = 40, even if you thought the goal was worth 100). And overestimating your own abilities makes you harder to discourage when others dismiss the work. Two wrongs making a right.

The implication is not to abandon calibration broadly, but to recognize that in domains with high ambiguity and high asymmetric downside (social ridicule, self-abandonment), a slight positive tilt is a rational correction, not a cognitive flaw.
