Carlson articulates a purely outcome-based leadership evaluation framework: Is life expectancy going up? Is the suicide rate declining? Are people having children? Is the standard of living improving? Will the next generation be more prosperous and free? He explicitly rejects personality-driven and moral-narrative evaluation as 'theater' that enables deception.

This framework is interesting because it cuts through partisan framing entirely. It doesn't matter what system a leader claims to represent or what ideology they espouse — the same metrics apply to every leader. The practical application extends beyond politics: any organization's leadership can be evaluated by measurable outcomes for the people they lead rather than the stories they tell about themselves. The weakness is that outcome metrics lag — a leader can inherit good metrics from predecessors or damage metrics that won't manifest for years. But as a baseline heuristic, 'what are the fruits of their behavior?' is more reliable than narrative evaluation.
