Alexander draws a parallel between student test-taking and AI behavior. Most students will guess on multiple choice (low shame risk) but won't fabricate an essay answer (high shame risk). The one student who tried it in a presentation became a permanent cautionary memory. Humans are not epistemically pure — we simply have a shame-based cost function that makes confabulation negative-EV in social contexts.

This has interesting implications for understanding human cognition. Our truthfulness isn't driven by some deep commitment to truth but by social incentive structures. Remove the social cost (anonymous settings, low-stakes contexts, in-group audiences) and humans confabulate freely. AIs lack this shame mechanism entirely, which is why their 'guessing' is unconstrained. This suggests that adding social-cost-like penalties to AI training (which is essentially what RLHF does) is the right architectural approach.
