Harari identifies a fundamental asymmetry in the information marketplace that explains why more information doesn't automatically lead to better outcomes. Truth requires massive investment — historians study for 10+ years, evidence must be cross-referenced and fact-checked, and reality is irreducibly complex. Fiction, by contrast, requires only imagination, can be simplified to any desired level, and can be made as pleasant as the audience wants.

This framing rejects the naive 'sunlight is the best disinfectant' view of information. Just as more food isn't always healthier (especially junk food), more information isn't always more truthful. The implication is that any system relying on free information flow without institutional truth-infrastructure will systematically degrade toward fiction dominance. This applies to social media, content creation, and any information marketplace where production costs and audience preferences determine what spreads.
