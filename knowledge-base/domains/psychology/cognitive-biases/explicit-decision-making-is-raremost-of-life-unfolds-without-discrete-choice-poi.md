Scott Alexander noted he 'never once actually made a decision in his life,' later clarifying he meant that life mostly doesn't present as a series of explicit decisions, though they occasionally come up. This offhand remark captures a profound psychological truth: the decision-framing of life is largely a retrospective narrative. Most trajectories emerge from default paths, environmental nudges, and gradual drift.

The practical implication is significant: if explicit decisions are rare, then designing good defaults and environments matters far more than improving decision-making skill at the margin. Rather than optimizing the occasional fork-in-the-road moment, the higher-leverage intervention is auditing the defaults you're currently drifting along—your routines, relationships, information diet, and physical environment. The few genuine decision points that do arise deserve disproportionate attention precisely because they're so rare.
