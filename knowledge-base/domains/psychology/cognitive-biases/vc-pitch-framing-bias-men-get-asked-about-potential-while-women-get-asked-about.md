A Harvard study revealed a consistent framing asymmetry in VC pitch meetings: male founders receive promotion-oriented questions ('How big can this get?') while female founders receive prevention-oriented questions ('What if it doesn't work?'). Same business, same metrics, but fundamentally different conversational frames that shape funding outcomes over time.

This is a concrete manifestation of how pattern-matching in venture capital morphs into exclusion. With 85-90% of VC partners being men, the default mental model of a billion-dollar founder remains narrow. The bias isn't malicious — it's habitual. A woman pitching a high-growth platform 'doesn't look like what the last unicorn looked like.' The practical fix is trainable: teams can audit pitch conversations for framing bias in real time and deliberately steer all pitches toward upside, market opportunity, and long-term vision regardless of who's presenting.
