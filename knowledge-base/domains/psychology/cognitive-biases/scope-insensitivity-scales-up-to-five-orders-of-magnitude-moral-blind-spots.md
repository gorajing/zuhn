Dogfighting involves thousands of animals per year and has been criminalized at both federal and state levels. Cockfighting — which is literally chicken fighting — is a felony in every state. Yet the factory farming of chickens and pigs, which inflicts arguably worse suffering on ten billion animals per year, is considered normal commerce. The ratio is roughly 100,000:1 on scale and the gap exists only because we flinch at local vivid cruelty while failing to scale that flinch to an industrial population.

This is a textbook case of scope insensitivity — the well-documented bias where subjective moral weight plateaus or even decreases as the number of victims grows. Kahneman's work showed people would pay roughly the same amount to save 2,000 or 200,000 birds from oil spills. The factory farming case shows the same bias operating at five orders of magnitude and in official policy, not just individual intuition.

The practical implication: any serious moral analysis needs explicit scope calibration. If a policy outlaws practice X at scale Y, the presence of practice X at scale 100,000Y should automatically trigger a review — not as a gotcha, but because our intuitions simply cannot feel the difference between thousands and billions without deliberate counterweight.
