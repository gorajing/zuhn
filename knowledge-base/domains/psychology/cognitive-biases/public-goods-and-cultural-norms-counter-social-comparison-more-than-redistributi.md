Smith argues that neither redistribution nor economic growth can give everyone an influencer lifestyle — the math doesn't work. Instead, he proposes flattening lifestyle visibility through public goods (parks, transit, beaches) that everyone can access regardless of income, and through cultural norms that discourage conspicuous wealth display (citing Japan as an example). This is a counter-intuitive policy insight: the problem isn't material inequality itself but perceived lifestyle inequality. Public goods work because they create shared reference points — if everyone goes to the same beautiful beach, the beach stops being a marker of status. This reframes inequality as partly a perception problem with perception-level solutions.
