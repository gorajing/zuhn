The ground rule to 'adopt a beginner's mindset' is really a debiasing technique for confirmation bias. Once a founder has a hypothesis about their customer's problem, every interview becomes an unconscious search for confirming evidence. Leading questions get asked, disconfirming signals get rationalized away, and ambiguous statements get interpreted favorably.

The beginner's mindset instruction works by creating a deliberate cognitive frame shift: pretend you know nothing. This is psychologically difficult — expertise and conviction are usually assets — but in discovery contexts they become liabilities. Paired with the rule to 'listen more than you talk' and 'get facts not opinions,' these three practices together form a lightweight protocol for reducing interviewer bias. The interviewer who talks less, assumes less, and demands concrete examples over abstract opinions will collect far more reliable data than one running on intuition and pattern-matching.
