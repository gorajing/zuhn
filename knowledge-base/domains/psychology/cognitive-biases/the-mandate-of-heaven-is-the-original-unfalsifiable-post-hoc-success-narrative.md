The Zhou dynasty invented the Mandate of Heaven to legitimize overthrowing the Shang, then retroactively applied it to the fictional Xia dynasty. The framework is brilliantly self-sealing: if a dynasty falls, the emperor must have been immoral; if it persists, the emperor must be virtuous. Natural disasters, floods, and rebellions all become evidence of moral failure rather than material causes.

This pattern recurs in modern contexts — startup post-mortems that attribute failure to founder character rather than market conditions, political narratives that frame election losses as moral judgments, and investor storytelling that retrofits virtue onto successful bets. Recognizing unfalsifiable moral narratives is critical for avoiding survivorship bias in any domain where outcomes are used to retroactively judge the character of decision-makers.
