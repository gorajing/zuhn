Historically, military actions create a 'rally round the flag' effect where public support surges at the outset and only gradually declines. The Iran War defied this pattern entirely: polls showed 53% opposition from day one, with only 40% support. Even Fox News found opinion evenly split among its typically hawkish audience.

Smith attributes this to a critical factor: the US started the war without being attacked first. Independents — now the bulk of the American electorate — have no partisan loyalty forcing them to support the action, and they carry deep wariness of Middle Eastern wars. This suggests a durable psychological principle: the rally effect depends on perceived defensive necessity. Wars of choice, especially in theaters where voters have negative priors, cannot manufacture the patriotic reflex that defensive wars trigger automatically.
