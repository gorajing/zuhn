Harris identifies preference falsification — saying what's politically acceptable while privately believing something different — as a critical distortion mechanism in polarized environments. He notes that polls showed Trump trailing badly, yet people's behavior in private (like voting booths) diverged significantly from their public statements.

This concept, originally formalized by economist Timur Kuran, has profound implications for any system that relies on expressed preferences as input: market research, employee surveys, political polling, and social media discourse. The gap between stated and actual beliefs widens as social punishment for dissent increases, creating a brittle illusion of consensus that can shatter suddenly — as seen in surprise election outcomes and rapid institutional collapses.
