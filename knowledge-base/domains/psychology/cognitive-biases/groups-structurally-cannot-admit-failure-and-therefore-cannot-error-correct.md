When group A holds position X and group B holds position Y, the 'compromise' Z is a position nobody actually believed was correct. When Z fails, nobody learns anything — A says 'told you X was right' and B says 'told you Y was right.' This is the poverty of compromise. It extends further: groups that try to change their minds fragment (Catholic → Protestant, Bitcoin → forks). Non-profits are a concrete example — every project is declared a success because there's no objective feedback mechanism (profit/loss). Foundations misdirect resources endlessly because social consensus replaces reality feedback. The practical takeaway: for-profit entities are paradoxically better vehicles for changing the world because they must take feedback from reality. If you want truth-seeking, protect individual dissent within organizations and create objective feedback mechanisms. 'Strong opinions loosely held' is the ideal — but groups tend toward either 'strong opinions strongly held' (intolerant minority) or 'weak opinions loosely held' (compromise model).
