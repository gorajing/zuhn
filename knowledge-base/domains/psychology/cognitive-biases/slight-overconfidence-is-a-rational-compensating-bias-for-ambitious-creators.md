Naively, exact calibration of one's abilities seems optimal. But this ignores that the error landscape is asymmetric: external skepticism, social pressure to quit, and self-doubt all push in the pessimistic direction. A slight upward bias in self-assessment balances these forces and produces better expected outcomes. Hardy noted this pattern in mathematicians; Graham generalizes it. The practical implication: consciously overestimating both the importance of your project and your own ability to execute is not self-deception—it's error correction.
