The essay identifies a specific cognitive pattern: founders and investors applying finite framing ('SaaS is dead,' 'it's the end of programmers') to what is fundamentally an infinite, expansionary process. This framing error has a consistent structure — it treats the disrupted category as fixed in scope, assumes displacement is the primary dynamic, and ignores the expansion mechanism.

The finite framing is seductive because windows of opportunity do open and close (the finite game is real at the tactical level). But at the strategic level, the windows always multiply. The essay notes that 'many will succumb to that fear' — suggesting this cognitive error is not just common but the default human response to disruption.

This maps to a broader principle: zero-sum bias is particularly dangerous during inflection points because it causes people to fight over shrinking old territory while ignoring expanding new territory. The art world's history shows that those who recognized the infinite game earliest — the Impressionists, the street photographers, the Polaroid inventors — captured the most value.
