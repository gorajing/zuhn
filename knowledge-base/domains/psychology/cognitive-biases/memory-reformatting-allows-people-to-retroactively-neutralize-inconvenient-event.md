Harris highlights how figures who saw January 6th clearly in its immediate aftermath have since 'reformatted their hard drives' to treat it as a non-issue — crucially, 'with no new discoveries about January 6th.' This is not a case of new evidence changing minds; it's pure motivated reasoning overwriting genuine perception.

The 'hard drive reformatting' metaphor captures something specific about how motivated reasoning works over time. It's not that people forget — it's that they actively reconstruct their memory of their own past assessments to align with their current tribal commitments. This has implications for how we evaluate intellectual honesty: the strongest test isn't whether someone reaches the right conclusion in the heat of the moment (many did after January 6th), but whether they maintain that conclusion when tribal incentives shift against it. Consistency under social pressure is the real signal of epistemic integrity.
