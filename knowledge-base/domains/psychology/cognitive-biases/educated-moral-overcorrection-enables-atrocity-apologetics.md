Harris identifies a specific failure mode among educated, ethically-minded people: their trained instinct to distrust black-and-white moral framing and seek nuance becomes a vulnerability when confronted with genuinely asymmetric moral situations. Students who 'lose sleep over cultural appropriation and wrong pronouns' — whose moral sensitivity is real — can nonetheless fail to make straightforward moral distinctions about violence because their framework treats all moral confidence as suspect.

This is a form of cognitive overcorrection: having learned that wartime propaganda often dehumanizes enemies with morally charged language, educated observers develop a blanket skepticism toward any moral distinction in conflict. But skepticism about moral language is not the same as skepticism about moral reality. The concrete behaviors Harris describes — human shields, celebration of massacre, hostage parade — are observable facts, not propaganda framings. The insight generalizes: expertise in detecting bias can itself become a bias when it prevents acknowledging clear evidence.
