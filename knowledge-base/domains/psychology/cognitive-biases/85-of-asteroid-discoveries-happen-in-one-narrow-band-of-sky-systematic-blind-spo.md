The opposition effect in asteroid detection reveals that 85%+ of discoveries occur in just 45 degrees of sky directly opposite the sun. Asteroids are dark objects (reflecting only ~15% of light) that are only visible when fully illuminated — meaning anything approaching from the sun's direction is structurally invisible. This is exactly how Chelyabinsk arrived undetected. Critically, this isn't a sensitivity problem that better telescopes can solve — it's a geometric constraint of ground-based observation. Only space-based telescopes positioned differently can close this gap. This structural blind spot pattern appears across decision-making domains: organizations often try to improve existing detection methods (hire more analysts, add more dashboards, increase survey frequency) when the actual problem is that their observation position has inherent geometric limitations. Customer feedback surveys miss non-customers. Internal audits miss systemic issues. Market research in your existing channels misses adjacent markets. The fix isn't better instruments from the same vantage point — it's fundamentally different observation angles.
