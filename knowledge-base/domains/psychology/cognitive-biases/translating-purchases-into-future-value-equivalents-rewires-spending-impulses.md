Hormozi presents a simple reframe: a $200 pair of Jordans invested in the S&P 500 for 40 years at 9% becomes over $6,000. His test is: 'If you wouldn't buy those shoes for $6,000, don't buy them for $200 today.' This converts an abstract opportunity cost into a concrete, emotionally resonant comparison.

This works because humans are poor at intuiting exponential growth (a well-documented cognitive bias). By doing the compound math upfront and presenting it as the 'real price,' you bypass the system-1 impulse that says '$200 is affordable.' The reframe also scales: 130 such $200 purchases equal $1 million in foregone wealth. This technique leverages loss aversion — the $6,000 loss feels more painful than the $200 gain of wearing nice shoes.
