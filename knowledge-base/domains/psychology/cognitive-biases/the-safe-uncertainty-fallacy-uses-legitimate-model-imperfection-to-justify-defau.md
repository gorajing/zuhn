When Bio Anchors critics identified that the model was imperfect and could fail, many defaulted to what the author calls the Safe Uncertainty Fallacy: since we can never be sure a model is exactly right, things are uncertain, which means we can continue to believe everything is fine and normal. But as Yudkowsky pointed out, uncertainty is symmetric — an imperfect forecast could be wrong in either direction, including being too conservative.

This fallacy is particularly insidious because it weaponizes genuine epistemics (all models are imperfect) to reach a motivated conclusion (therefore I don't need to update my beliefs). The antidote is recognizing that desperation for real information — even fallible, error-laden probabilistic forecasts — is the correct response to uncertainty, not dismissal. Treat forecasts as small updates useful for resolving near-total uncertainty into merely partial uncertainty, rather than as claims that must be perfectly right or completely worthless.
