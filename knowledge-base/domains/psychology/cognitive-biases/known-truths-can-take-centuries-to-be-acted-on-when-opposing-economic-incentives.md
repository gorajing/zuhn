Benjamin Franklin wrote in 1786 about lead's known dangers: 'You will observe with concern how long a useful truth may be known and exist before it is generally received and practiced on.' Despite this, Thomas Midgley Jr. chose to add tetraethyl lead to gasoline in 1921, and it took until 2021 for Algeria — the last country — to ban leaded fuel.

The pattern reveals that truth adoption follows economic incentives, not evidence quality. Ethanol was a known effective anti-knock agent, but Midgley rejected it because 'you needed a lot of it' and it was 'expensive and hard to turn a profit on.' The Ethyl Corporation — backed by GM, DuPont, and Standard Oil — dismissed warnings from MIT, Harvard, Yale, and the US Health Service. When economic incentives align against a known truth, institutional power actively suppresses adoption rather than merely ignoring it.

This has broad application: any domain where a known harmful practice generates significant profit will exhibit this same multi-decade (or multi-century) lag between evidence and action. The implication is that identifying a known truth is necessary but wildly insufficient — you must also account for the incentive structures opposing it.
