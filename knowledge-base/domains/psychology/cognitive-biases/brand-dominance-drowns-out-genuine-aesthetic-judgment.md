Graham uses the Mona Lisa as a case study: shown anonymously among other paintings, labeled as a portrait by an unknown fifteenth-century artist, most people would walk by without a second look. The painting's fame — not its visual qualities — drives the crowd. This is a compass-next-to-a-magnet problem: the error signal (brand, social proof, cultural authority) is so strong that any popular vote on art quality measures only the error, not genuine aesthetic response.

This has practical implications beyond art. Any domain where brand or prestige dominates evaluation — restaurants, universities, software tools, expert opinions — suffers the same corruption. The antidote is repeated, close-up, de-branded exposure: come back to the painting over and over until the fame wears off, stand close enough to see details absent from reproductions. The general principle is that genuine quality assessment requires stripping away status signals first.
