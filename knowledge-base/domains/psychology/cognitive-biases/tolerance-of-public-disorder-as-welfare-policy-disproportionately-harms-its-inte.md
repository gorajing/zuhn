San Francisco's hardline progressives framed tolerance of property crime, open drug markets, tent cities, and street harassment as compassion — viewing enforcement as unacceptably harmful to perpetrators. But this created a predictable second-order effect: poor people bore the heaviest burden of the resulting disorder. This is a recurring pattern in policy design where first-order compassion produces second-order cruelty. The people who can least afford private security, alternative transit, or relocation are trapped in the conditions that permissive policies create. When Lurie reversed course with enforcement, crime dropped 25%, shootings fell 16%, and car break-ins declined 43% — benefits that accrued disproportionately to the same vulnerable populations that disorder had harmed most.
