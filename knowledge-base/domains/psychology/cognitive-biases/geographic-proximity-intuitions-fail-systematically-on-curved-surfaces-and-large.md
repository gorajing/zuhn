The video repeatedly surfaces distance facts that feel impossible: Canada's easternmost point is closer to Croatia than to Vancouver. Russia's easternmost point is closer to Mexico than to Moscow. Finland's north is closer to Greenland than to Poland. Recife, Brazil is closer to Dakar, Senegal than to Porto Alegre, Brazil.

These aren't trivia — they reveal a systematic cognitive bias. Humans reason about geography using flat-map mental models (typically Mercator projections), which distort distances at high latitudes and across continental boundaries. This bias has practical consequences: supply chain planners underestimate trans-oceanic route efficiency, diplomats misjudge regional proximity dynamics, and investors mismodel trade corridor economics. The bias is particularly insidious because it feels like spatial common sense rather than a distortion.
