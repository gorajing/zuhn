Isaac Asimov's 1988 prediction was technically precise: home computers connected to vast libraries where anyone could ask questions and follow their curiosity at their own pace. This is essentially the internet plus Wikipedia plus Khan Academy. Yet his conclusion—that traditional schooling would become outdated—was wrong because he predicted the technology without predicting human behavior. Kids use the technology to watch videos and play games, not to self-educate.

This reveals a critical forecasting error: conflating technological capability with behavioral adoption. A prediction is only useful for decision-making if it correctly forecasts both what technology enables AND how humans actually use it. For investors and product builders, this means always stress-testing technology predictions against realistic human behavior models rather than assuming rational adoption of superior tools.
