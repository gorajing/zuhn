Harari, self-identifying as conservative 'in the Burkean sense,' observes that conservative parties globally have abandoned their foundational insight: that institutions are flawed but take generations to build, humans lack the capacity to design perfect societies from scratch, and previous corrections from real historical events are baked into existing systems. The traditional conservative position held that institutions need amendment and improvement, not demolition.

The structural parallel to Leninism is precise — the Bolsheviks argued that the existing order was so corrupted that only total destruction and rebuilding could work. This is now the position of parties that call themselves conservative. The irony is that this revolutionary stance validates the very cynicism it claims to oppose: by declaring all institutions irredeemable, it removes the possibility of reform and creates the conditions for authoritarian replacement. The genuine conservative response to institutional failure is to strengthen self-correcting mechanisms, not to destroy the institutions that contain them.
