In an inverted sinking helicopter, every survival instinct works against you. Unbuckling your seatbelt (instinct: free yourself) makes you float to what is now the floor, losing all orientation. Holding your breath (instinct: don't inhale water) prevents you from using the emergency air supply. Rising toward air (instinct: surface) takes you deeper into the wreckage. The training exists specifically because the correct actions — stay buckled, breathe through flooded sinuses, remain inverted — violate every evolved response.

This maps directly to paradigm shifts in any domain. When market conditions invert, business instincts calibrated to the old environment become liabilities. The entrepreneur's instinct to 'move fast' becomes dangerous in a downturn requiring capital preservation. The investor's instinct to 'buy the dip' fails in a structural regime change. The key insight: the more extreme the environmental shift, the more likely your trained responses are exactly wrong. Recognizing that your instincts are now adversaries — not allies — is the first step to surviving any inversion.
