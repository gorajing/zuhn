Creating new things is itself new for our species — it happened too slowly for most of human history for us to develop evaluation customs for early-stage work. As a result, we apply the same standards to a 20%-complete ambitious project as we would to a finished piece or a less ambitious project. This is a systematic bias, not an individual failing.

Graham proposes a polarity switch: instead of listing reasons an idea won't work, try to imagine ways it could work. This isn't lower standards — it's a fundamentally different evaluation mode appropriate for early-stage work. The bias has concrete consequences: the people who dismiss early work are statistically more likely to be right on any individual prediction, but catastrophically wrong when predictions are weighted by outcome in a power-law distributed world.
