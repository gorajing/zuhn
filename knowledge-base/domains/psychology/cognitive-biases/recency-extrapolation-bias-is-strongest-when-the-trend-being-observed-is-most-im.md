The Japan-to-China arc in geopolitical forecasting illustrates a deep cognitive bias: when recent evidence is vivid, legible, and impressive — high-speed rail, EV dominance, gleaming cities, soaring trade surpluses — observers default to projecting that trajectory forward indefinitely. This is recency extrapolation, and it intensifies the more impressive the evidence is.

The perverse result is that the bias is worst at exactly the wrong moment: peak bull consensus on Japan formed in 1989, just as the asset bubble was cresting. Peak bull consensus on China appears to be forming in the mid-2020s, just as Noah Smith is updating toward skepticism based on structural weaknesses. The visibility of surface-level achievements makes it psychologically easier to dismiss or ignore slower-moving fundamentals (demographics, political fragility, productivity gaps) that will ultimately determine long-run trajectories.

Practically, the antidote isn't reflexive contrarianism — it's distinguishing between surface-legible trends (easy to see, easy to extrapolate) and structural fundamentals (harder to observe, slower-moving, but more durable predictors). Forecasts built primarily on the former should be held with lower confidence regardless of how convincing the recent evidence looks.
