David J. Hand's 'probability lever' explains why seemingly impossible coincidences are actually expected. While the average person has a tiny chance of being struck by lightning, an avid outdoor sportsman like Walter Summerford has a much higher conditional probability. The base rate for 'any person' masks the dramatically different rates for specific subpopulations.

This principle has immediate practical value. In investing, a stock crash that's a 1-in-100 event for the market might be a near-certainty for a specific overleveraged sector. In hiring, a candidate who seems improbably qualified might simply occupy a probability niche where that skill combination is common. In risk management, insuring against the average probability can leave you fatally exposed to your actual conditional probability. The takeaway: always ask 'rare for whom?' before treating an event as extraordinary or dismissing it as impossible.
