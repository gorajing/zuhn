Reeves identifies a structural blind spot: men and women at the top of the economic ladder—four-year degrees, decent incomes—don't see male disadvantage because it doesn't affect their social circle. He borrows Sandberg's phrase to frame it as 'so busy leaning in that we don't look down.' The reality for working-class men, Black men, and men lower on the economic ladder is starkly different.

This is a cognitive bias with policy consequences. If the people who write policy, run media organizations, and shape cultural narratives are in the demographic least affected by male disadvantage, the problem stays invisible. It also explains why framing gender as zero-sum persists—those at the top experience gender dynamics as competitive, while those at the bottom experience them as compounding with class disadvantage. The insight applies broadly: any inequality that doesn't affect the decision-making class will be systematically underweighted.
