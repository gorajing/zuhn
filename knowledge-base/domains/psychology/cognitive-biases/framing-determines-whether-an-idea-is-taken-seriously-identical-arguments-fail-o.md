Einstein's core argument was remarkably consistent across decades: quantum mechanics requires non-local influences, which contradicts relativity. He presented this at the 1927 Solvay Conference (where Bohr 'didn't understand what precisely is the point'), through the photon box thought experiment (where Bohr 'just misunderstood it' and drew a diagram missing the key mirror), and finally in the 1935 EPR paper. Despite being the same argument, each formulation was received differently based on context.

The photon box version was recorded in history as 'Bohr's great victory over Einstein' even though Bohr had misunderstood the setup entirely. 'History is written by the victors.' This reveals a critical pattern: the logical content of an argument matters far less than the social context in which it's received. For anyone trying to communicate important but counterintuitive ideas, the implication is that you may need to present the same core insight multiple times in different frames before it lands — and that a single misunderstanding by an influential person can bury a correct idea for decades.
