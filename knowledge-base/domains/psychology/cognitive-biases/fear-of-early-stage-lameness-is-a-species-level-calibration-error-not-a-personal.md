Making new things is itself a recent phenomenon in human history. For most of our existence, change happened too slowly for any individual to notice, so we never developed cultural norms for how to treat early versions of ambitious projects. We default to evaluating them as either finished work (too harsh) or unambitious work (missing the point).

This reframing matters because it shifts the problem from personal weakness to collective inexperience. Silicon Valley's optimistic evaluation culture didn't emerge from moral superiority—it evolved because the financial incentives of power-law outcomes made it urgent. The fact that this culture developed quickly and is spreading proves that harsh dismissal of new ideas is a shallow habit, not a deep feature of human nature. Our attitudes toward early work are themselves early work—crude version 1.0 of a set of customs still being developed.
