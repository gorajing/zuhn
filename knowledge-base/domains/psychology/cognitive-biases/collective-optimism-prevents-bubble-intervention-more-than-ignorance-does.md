The documentary explicitly identifies optimism as 'the real killer' in Japan's bubble. Authorities had the tools and data to see aggressive lending and asset price inflation, but the national mood of prosperity created a collective action problem: nobody wanted to stop the party when everyone was making money.

This insight challenges the common narrative that bubbles persist due to ignorance or information asymmetry. In Japan's case, the mechanisms were visible — companies like Nissan were making more money from speculation than manufacturing cars, golf club memberships were trading at $3.5M, and the total golf membership market ($200B) exceeded Taiwan's GDP. These weren't hidden signals. The barrier to intervention was psychological, not informational. This maps to the broader principle that in group settings, shared optimism becomes self-reinforcing and suppresses dissent more effectively than censorship.
