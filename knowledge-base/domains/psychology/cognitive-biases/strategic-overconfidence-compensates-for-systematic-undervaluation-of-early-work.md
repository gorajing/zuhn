Hardy's observation that good work is not done by humble men points to a real mathematical advantage in calibrated overconfidence. If you overestimate both the importance of your work and your ability to execute it, these errors compensate for the systematic bias of judging early-stage work too harshly. Someone who sees 20% progress toward a goal worth 100 but believes it's 10% progress toward a goal worth 200 arrives at the correct expected value despite being wrong on both inputs.

This explains the empirical observation that the most successful people across many fields tend to be slightly overconfident — not delusionally so, but enough to push through the discouraging early phases where accurate self-assessment would cause them to quit. The overconfidence is not a character flaw but a functional adaptation to the asymmetric costs of starting vs. not starting.
