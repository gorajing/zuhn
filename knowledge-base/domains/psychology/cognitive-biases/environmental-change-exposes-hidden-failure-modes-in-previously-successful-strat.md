RuBisCo evolved in an atmosphere with abundant CO2 and minimal O2. Under those conditions, its tendency to bind oxygen instead of carbon dioxide was irrelevant — there was almost no oxygen to confuse it. The failure mode existed from the beginning but was completely invisible because the environment didn't trigger it. When plants' own success flooded the atmosphere with oxygen, the latent bug became a massive liability.

This pattern is critical for decision-making: strategies that have 'always worked' may contain failure modes that current conditions simply don't activate. A startup's hiring process might work brilliantly at 20 people but contain scaling failures invisible until 200. A trading strategy might perform well in low-volatility environments while harboring catastrophic risk that only manifests in a regime change. The dangerous part is that long track records of success create false confidence — the longer something works, the more convinced we become it's robust, when it may just be untested.

The meta-lesson from RuBisCo is even darker: sometimes your own success changes the environment in ways that expose your hidden weaknesses. Growth itself can create the conditions that break your core strategy.
