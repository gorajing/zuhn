The idea that Columbus bravely sailed against flat Earth consensus is fiction, largely traceable to Washington Irving's 1828 biography. In reality, ancient Greeks calculated Earth's circumference with remarkable accuracy, and medieval scholars universally accepted a spherical Earth. The actual debate was about the size of the ocean — Columbus's critics correctly argued the distance was too far for his ships.

This is a case study in how false narratives persist. The Columbus myth survived because it served a compelling narrative structure (brave explorer vs. ignorant establishment) and was repeated in textbooks for generations. The mechanism — narrative appeal overriding factual accuracy — applies broadly. When evaluating any widely-believed historical claim, check whether its persistence comes from evidence or from how satisfying the story feels to tell.
