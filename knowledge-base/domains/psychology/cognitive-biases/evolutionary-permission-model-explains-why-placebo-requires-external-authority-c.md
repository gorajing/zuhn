The video proposes an evolutionary model for the placebo effect: when a child is hurt, symptoms like pain and crying serve dual purposes — keeping the child still (protective) and signaling adults for help. When help arrives, even without active treatment, the brain receives a signal that it's safe to redirect resources from distress-signaling to actual healing. The placebo essentially mimics the 'help has arrived' signal.

This model explains several placebo puzzles. It explains why authority matters (a doctor's placebo works better than a friend's — the brain recognizes hierarchical competence signals). It explains the potency hierarchy (more 'serious' interventions signal more serious help has arrived). And it explains why placebos work better in institutional settings — the brain's resource-allocation system evolved to respond to environmental safety cues, not to evaluate pharmacological mechanisms. The implication for product and experience design is that creating perceived authority and safety may be more important than the actual intervention content.
