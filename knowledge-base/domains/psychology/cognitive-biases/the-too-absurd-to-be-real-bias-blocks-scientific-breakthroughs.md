When 19-year-old Chandrasekhar proved that electron degeneracy pressure has a mass limit (the Chandrasekhar limit), Eddington - one of the most respected scientists alive - publicly dismissed it, saying nature must have a law preventing such absurd collapse. This wasn't a technical objection but an aesthetic one: the conclusion felt wrong. Even after Oppenheimer and Volkoff proved neutron stars also have a mass limit, and Oppenheimer and Snyder showed indefinite collapse is inevitable for massive stars, Einstein himself couldn't accept it.

The pattern is striking: each time scientists found a mechanism to prevent complete collapse (electron degeneracy, then neutron degeneracy), they assumed the universe must always provide such a mechanism. This 'surely something will save us' reasoning is a form of motivated cognition where the desired conclusion (no black holes) drives the search for supporting evidence rather than the reverse. The broader lesson applies beyond physics: when rigorous analysis produces uncomfortable conclusions, the instinct to reject them based on 'that can't be right' feelings is one of the most persistent obstacles to progress.
