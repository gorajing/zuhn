Kurzgesagt's core rhetorical strategy — walking through second-by-second human experience rather than showing explosion simulations — reveals how abstraction functions as a psychological defense mechanism. When nuclear weapons are discussed as deterrence theory, warhead counts, or geopolitical leverage, the framing activates analytical processing that treats mass death as a variable in an equation. Reframing the same reality as lived experience (you're walking to work, then you're on fire) activates visceral threat assessment that evolution designed for survival. This is a specific instance of psychic numbing: the gap between how we process 'one person suffers' versus 'millions suffer' isn't linear but collapses toward indifference. The insight generalizes — any existential risk discussed primarily through abstract frameworks (AI alignment math, climate models, pandemic statistics) will systematically under-trigger the action response that concrete, personal framing would produce.
