Archaeological evidence consistently shows that foragers had healthier bones and teeth, worked fewer hours, and had more leisure time than early agriculturalists. Yet agriculture arose independently across Africa, China, the Americas, and the Fertile Crescent. Every civilization that encountered the trade-off chose the same way: worse individual health for greater collective food security and population capacity.

This reveals a deep cognitive pattern: humans systematically underweight measurable individual costs when presented with abstract collective benefits like 'food security' or 'growth potential.' The same pattern appears in modern contexts — workers accepting worse health outcomes for career advancement, founders sacrificing wellbeing for company growth, societies choosing GDP growth over quality-of-life metrics. Recognizing this bias doesn't prevent it, but it does suggest that any decision framed as 'sacrifice now for scale later' deserves extra scrutiny on the individual cost side.
