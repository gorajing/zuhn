RuBisCo was well-adapted to Earth's original low-oxygen atmosphere. But photosynthesis itself produced oxygen as a waste byproduct, gradually transforming the atmosphere. As O2 levels rose, RuBisCo increasingly mistook oxygen for CO2, creating toxic phosphoglycolate. Plants had to evolve expensive compensatory machinery to manage this, yet they couldn't abandon RuBisCo because it was too deeply entrenched.

This is a profound systems trap: the organism's own success changed the environment in ways that degraded its core mechanism, but the depth of commitment to that mechanism made switching impossible. The solution — make even more RuBisCo and add detox pathways — layered complexity on top of the original flaw.

Humans and organizations fall into the same trap: strategies that produced early success alter the competitive or social environment (pricing, culture, regulations, norms), and those changes make the original strategy progressively less effective. The more committed the actor, the harder the pivot. Recognizing this pattern is the first step to planning exits before the lock-in becomes irreversible.
