The naive view is that perfectly calibrated self-assessment would be optimal. Graham argues this ignores that creators face two systematic downward biases: other people's skepticism and their own harsh judgment of early work. Slight overconfidence is not a cognitive flaw but a compensating mechanism — the error in one direction offsets larger errors in the other.

This is structurally similar to how engineers intentionally over-provision systems to handle unexpected load. The implication is that the people who appear optimally calibrated may actually be slightly under-performing, while the slightly overconfident are the ones with the net-correct estimate of expected value. Hardy's observation that good work is not done by humble men reflects this same asymmetry.
