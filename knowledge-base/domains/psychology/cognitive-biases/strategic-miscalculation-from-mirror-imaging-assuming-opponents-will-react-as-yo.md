Japan's decision to bomb Pearl Harbor was predicated on the theory that a dramatic show of force would convince the United States to remain neutral. This was a textbook mirror-imaging error — projecting your own decision-making framework onto an opponent with a fundamentally different one. Japan's military leadership, operating within a culture that respected decisive shows of force, assumed America would calculate that the cost of war exceeded the benefit and back down.

Instead, the attack unified a previously divided American public and triggered total mobilization. The error wasn't stupidity — Japan's strategists were sophisticated — but rather an inability to model an opponent whose institutional and cultural response patterns differed from their own. This cognitive bias appears constantly in business: incumbents assume startups will behave 'rationally' (i.e., as the incumbent would), and startups assume customers will respond to superior products the way engineers would. The antidote is adversarial red-teaming that explicitly models opponents as having different utility functions.
