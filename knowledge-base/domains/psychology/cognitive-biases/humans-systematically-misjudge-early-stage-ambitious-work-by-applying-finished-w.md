Graham identifies a specific cognitive failure mode: humans evaluate early-stage ambitious work using the same criteria they'd apply to finished work or less ambitious completed projects. This isn't stupidity — it's the absence of learned customs. Making new things is evolutionarily recent, and we simply haven't developed the judgment frameworks needed to assess incomplete ambitious work separately from complete modest work. The bias is compounded by social dynamics: dismissing ambitious early work makes the critic appear shrewd (they're statistically more likely to be right in any individual case), and some people actively hope ambitious peers will fail to avoid status displacement. Graham's key reframe is that the correct evaluation mode isn't lower standards but reversed polarity — instead of listing reasons something won't work, actively imagining ways it could. This is a trainable skill, not an innate trait, as Silicon Valley's evolution demonstrates.
