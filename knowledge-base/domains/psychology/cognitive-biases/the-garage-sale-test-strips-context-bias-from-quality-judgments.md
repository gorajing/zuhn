Graham's 'garage sale test' — imagining a museum painting found frameless, dirty, and unsigned — is a practical debiasing technique for separating intrinsic quality from contextual prestige. When you walk through a museum applying this filter, the results are startling: works you thought you admired turn out to be carried entirely by their framing.

This generalizes beyond art. Code architectures, business strategies, and research papers all benefit from stripping away the credentials and presentation that surround them. The technique works because prestige cues (famous author, beautiful typesetting, impressive venue) activate a different evaluation circuit than genuine quality assessment. By mentally removing those cues, you force yourself to evaluate the thing itself.
