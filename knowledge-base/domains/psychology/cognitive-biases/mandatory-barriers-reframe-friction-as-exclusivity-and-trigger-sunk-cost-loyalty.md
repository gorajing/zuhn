The $60 membership fee seems like it should be a fatal obstacle—why would customers pay just to enter a store? But the fee works as a commitment device. Once paid, customers rationalize continued use: 'I've already invested, so I should shop here to get my money's worth.' This stops them from price-comparing at competitors, creating loyalty that voluntary programs cannot match.

The fee also acts as a demographic filter, selecting for households earning ~$100,000 who spend more per visit. Amazon Prime triggers similar sunk-cost psychology, but because Prime is optional, users self-sort based on whether it pencils out. Costco's mandatory fee means everyone who enters is already psychologically committed. The broader principle: sometimes making access harder—not easier—builds a more loyal, higher-value customer base. This applies to communities, products, and platforms where selective friction outperforms frictionless onboarding.
