The neurological trap of chronic loneliness is remarkably specific: the brain becomes more receptive and alert to social signals while simultaneously getting worse at interpreting them correctly. The face-recognition system recalibrates to classify neutral expressions as hostile. This creates a vicious cycle — isolation leads to sadness, which triggers selective attention to negative interactions, which generates negative self/other narratives, which drives avoidance behavior, which deepens isolation.

This is a textbook self-fulfilling prophecy operating at the neurological level. The lonely person's defensive posture (appearing cold, unfriendly, socially awkward) isn't their actual personality — it's a self-preservation response. Breaking the cycle requires recognizing the distortion first: actively questioning whether interactions were truly negative, whether you're adding hostile meaning to neutral words, and whether you've pre-decided how social situations will go before entering them.
