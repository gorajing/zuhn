Innovation at the individual level is historically new for our species. For most of human history, change happened too slowly to be visible within a lifetime, so we developed no cultural customs for evaluating embryonic ambitious work. The result is a systematic bias: we apply the same evaluative framework to a two-week prototype as to a finished product.

This isn't just an individual cognitive error—it's a missing cultural institution. Silicon Valley partly solved it by developing customs where dismissing strange-sounding ideas from unknown people is recognized as dangerous. The fact that these customs evolved quickly and proved extremely lucrative suggests the bias is shallow and correctable, not deeply rooted in human nature.
