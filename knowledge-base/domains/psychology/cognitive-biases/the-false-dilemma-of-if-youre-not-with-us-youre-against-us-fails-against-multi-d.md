Graham points out that accidental moderates may not even care about the questions that the left and right consider paramount. Their political identity can only be measured from the intersection of their concerns with mainstream political concerns — and this intersection can be vanishingly small.

This insight generalizes beyond politics: any time someone frames a complex, multi-dimensional space as a binary choice, they're implicitly assuming everyone's opinions are correlated along a single axis. This assumption holds for ideologues and intentional moderates, whose opinions do cluster predictably. But it completely breaks down for independent thinkers, whose opinions are distributed across many dimensions. Recognizing this pattern helps identify when binary framing is being used not as a simplification but as a misrepresentation.
