The 2008 crisis exposed a fundamental incentive misalignment: credit rating agencies were paid by the banks whose products they rated. If Moody's gave a CDO its true junk rating, the bank would simply go to S&P or Fitch for the desired AAA stamp. The agencies rated toxic debt as the safest possible investment because their business model required it.

This pattern generalizes far beyond finance. Any evaluator whose revenue comes from the evaluated — app store reviewers, audit firms, certification bodies, even peer reviewers whose careers depend on reciprocal goodwill — faces the same structural capture. The takeaway for decision-makers: never trust a rating or certification without understanding who pays the rater. The more consequential the rating, the stronger the incentive to corrupt it. Build independent verification into any system where you rely on third-party quality signals.
