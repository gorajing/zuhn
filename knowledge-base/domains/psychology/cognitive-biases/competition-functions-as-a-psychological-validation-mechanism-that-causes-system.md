Thiel argues that competition-as-validation is not merely an intellectual blind spot but a deep psychological one rooted in mimetic (imitative) behavior — Shakespeare's era already recognized that 'ape' meant both primate and imitate. The practical damage is severe: 20,000 people move to LA yearly to become movie stars, ~20 succeed. Harvard Business School studies show the largest graduating cohort systematically piles into whatever's hot (Michael Milken's junk bonds in 1989, dotcom in 1999-2000, housing/PE in 2005-2007) — catching the peak of each bubble perfectly. Thiel's own experience at a top NYC law firm illustrates the trap: everyone on the outside wanted to get in, everyone on the inside wanted to leave, and a colleague described leaving as 'escaping from Alcatraz' when all you had to do was walk out the front door. The antidote isn't a simple formula — Thiel admits he's only 10% of the way there — but starts with never underestimating how deeply competition-as-validation affects you personally, not just other people. His closing metaphor: don't rush through the tiny door everyone's fighting over; go around the corner through the vast gate no one's taking.
