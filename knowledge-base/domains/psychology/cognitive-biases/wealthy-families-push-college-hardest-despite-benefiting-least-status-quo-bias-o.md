Hormozi identifies a counterintuitive pattern: families with the highest socioeconomic status are the most insistent that their children attend college, yet research controlling for SAT scores and socioeconomic factors shows these students receive zero marginal value-add from attending. The box is 'already checked at birth' — they already possess the networks, reference groups, and behavioral models that college provides to first-generation students.

This is a textbook status quo bias compounded by social signaling: parents who succeeded partly through college credentials assume the same path is necessary for their children, and the social cost of a child not attending college ('what will our friends think?') creates strong conformity pressure. The practical lesson extends beyond education: any time the most invested stakeholders are the strongest advocates for the default option, investigate whether the default still delivers marginal value or has become pure signaling. The decision framework should be: does this investment change my environment meaningfully, or am I already in the target environment?
