RuBisCo, the enzyme responsible for carbon fixation in plants, is widely regarded as inefficient — it misfires roughly half the time in oxygen-rich environments, latching onto O2 instead of CO2 and producing toxic byproducts plants must then expend energy to neutralize. Yet it is estimated there are 40 billion tons of RuBisCo on Earth at any given time, making it the single most abundant protein in the biosphere.

This is a textbook case of path dependency: RuBisCo evolved in an early atmosphere with almost no oxygen, where it was perfectly adequate. By the time the oxygen-rich environment it helped create made it maladaptive, the entire photosynthetic machinery — hundreds of enzymes, structural proteins, thylakoid architecture — had been optimized around it. Replacing it would have required simultaneous coordinated changes across the entire system, which natural selection cannot easily execute.

The strategic implication is that incumbency is often more powerful than quality. In technology, business, and cognitive habits alike, the first good-enough solution that attracts sufficient dependent infrastructure tends to persist indefinitely. This is not a failure of rational updating — it is the rational response to ecosystem lock-in. Challengers must offer not just marginal improvement but enough value to justify replacing the entire surrounding stack.
