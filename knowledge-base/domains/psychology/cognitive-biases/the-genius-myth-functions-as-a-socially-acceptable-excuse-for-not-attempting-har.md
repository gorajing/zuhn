Graham identifies a specific cognitive bias: when choosing between two theories — one that implies you should work harder and one that gives you an excuse for being lazy — the lazy-excuse theory is almost certainly wrong. The genius narrative (Shakespeare and Einstein were simply born different) is psychologically comforting because it means our own mediocrity isn't our fault.

This connects to how biographies distort reality. Knowing how the story ends, biographers streamline the plot into a destiny narrative, making the subject's success seem inevitable rather than hard-won. The 16-year-old Shakespeare or Einstein, sitting in your classroom, would seem impressive but not alien. The uncomfortable implication is that greatness requires sustained, difficult work — and the genius myth is society's collective mechanism for avoiding that truth.
