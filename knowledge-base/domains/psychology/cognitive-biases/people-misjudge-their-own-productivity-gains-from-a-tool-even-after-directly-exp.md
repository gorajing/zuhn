The most striking psychological result is the gap between perception and measurement. Even after completing the study, participants believed AI had sped them up by ~20%, while the data showed a 19% slowdown—a roughly 40-point error in self-assessment about their own recent, directly-experienced work. This is not naive forecasting (predicting an uncertain future) but a failure of retrospective metacognition about events they personally lived through.

The mechanism Becker emphasizes is 'overoptimism about AI usefulness': a prior belief that the tool helps causes both overuse and a biased read of the experience. The cross-domain principle is that subjective sense of effort or flow is a poor proxy for actual output—the engagement of using a sophisticated tool can feel productive while consuming net time. The practical implication: any decision about adopting a productivity tool (AI or otherwise) should rest on measured outcomes, not on how productive it feels, because the feeling can be systematically wrong even for experts evaluating themselves.
