Graham identifies obstinacy not as mere stubbornness but as a reflexive resistance to changing one's ideas that becomes a 'sort of induced stupidity as contrary evidence mounts.' This is a dynamic, not a static trait — the obstinate person becomes effectively stupider over time because their model of reality grows increasingly stale while the world keeps updating.

This has immediate practical implications for self-diagnosis. The question isn't 'am I smart enough?' but 'am I updating fast enough?' Someone of average intelligence who updates readily will outperform a brilliant person who doesn't, given enough time and enough contrary evidence. The compounding nature of this effect means early obstinacy on important questions is especially costly — you're not just wrong now, you're building on a foundation that gets worse.
