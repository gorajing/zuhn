The Valhalla scandal of 1987 was Enron's original sin. Two oil traders were caught moving money to fake accounts with names like 'M.Y. Ass,' gambling beyond their authority, and stealing. Auditors flagged it. Lay's response: keep them because they're making money, and send a letter saying 'please keep making us millions.' This single decision established the foundational norm — profit justifies any behavior. When Skilling later introduced mark-to-market accounting to book imaginary future profits, when Fastow created shell companies to hide debt, when traders manipulated California's energy grid — each escalation was possible because the precedent was already set. This is the normalization of deviance pattern from organizational psychology: each tolerated violation makes the next one seem merely incremental. The lesson for leaders is that the response to the first ethical breach — however small — sets the ceiling for all future behavior.
