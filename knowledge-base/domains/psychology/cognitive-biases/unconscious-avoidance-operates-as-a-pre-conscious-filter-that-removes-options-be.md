Schlep blindness illustrates a broader cognitive pattern: the unconscious mind acts as a pre-filter, removing options from consideration before they ever reach conscious deliberation. Graham notes this extends far beyond startups — most people don't consciously decide against Olympic-level fitness; their unconscious simply never presents it as a real option.

This is more insidious than conscious risk aversion because you can't counteract a bias you don't know is operating. The person who 'chose' to build a recipe site over fixing payments never experienced a moment of choice. The payments idea was invisible to them. Recognizing this pattern suggests that when you notice yourself gravitating toward comfortable, familiar problem spaces, you should actively interrogate what difficult alternatives your unconscious might be hiding from you.
