Gasoline is a uniquely visible price signal — consumers see it posted on large signs multiple times per day, purchase it frequently, and pay for it as a standalone transaction rather than bundled into other goods. This creates a salience effect where gas price changes dominate consumer sentiment about the economy even when they represent a small fraction of total spending.

This disproportionate sensitivity has real consequences: it means that energy policy failures (or self-inflicted supply shocks like the Iran war) carry political costs far exceeding their actual macroeconomic weight. For anyone analyzing political risk or public sentiment, gasoline prices function as a leading indicator of mood shifts that may seem irrational from a purely economic perspective but are entirely predictable from a behavioral one.
