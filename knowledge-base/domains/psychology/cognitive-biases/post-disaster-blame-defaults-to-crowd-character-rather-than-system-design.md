After nearly every crowd disaster, the narrative focuses on the crowd's character: hooliganism at sporting events, youth culture at concerts, fanaticism at religious gatherings. After the 1979 Who concert, media focused on the substances fans had taken and 'rock-and-roll culture.' This framing consistently misses that individuals in dense crowds (above 5 people per square meter) lose the ability to control their own movement entirely — crowd physics at that density follows fluid dynamics, not individual choice. Security guards at the front dismiss pleas from crushing victims as greed, while people at the back pushing forward have no awareness of the pressure they're creating.

This is the fundamental attribution error applied at scale: attributing systemic outcomes to individual character. It's directly relevant to incident post-mortems in any domain — blaming operators rather than examining system design prevents learning. The corrective is to ask 'what system design made this outcome predictable?' rather than 'who behaved badly?'
