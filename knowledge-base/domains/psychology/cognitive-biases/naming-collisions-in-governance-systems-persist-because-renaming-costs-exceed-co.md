Washington Territory was deliberately renamed from Columbia to avoid confusion with the District of Columbia. The rename failed completely because DC's city also became known as Washington. Kansas City sits in Missouri, not Kansas. North, South Carolina exists. These aren't random accidents — they reveal a deep pattern about naming in complex systems.

The cost of living with a naming collision is distributed across millions of small moments of confusion, each individually trivial. The cost of renaming is concentrated, visible, and politically charged. This asymmetry means naming collisions are effectively permanent once established. The same pattern appears in software (deprecated APIs that never die), corporate structures (redundant division names post-merger), and brand architecture. For anyone designing systems — technical or institutional — the implication is that naming decisions are nearly irreversible and deserve disproportionate upfront investment.
