Both Costco and Amazon Prime use upfront fees to trigger sunk-cost thinking — once you've paid, you feel compelled to shop there to 'get your money's worth.' But there's a critical difference in how each deploys this mechanism. Amazon Prime is optional, meaning users self-select based on rational calculation of whether the fee pays for itself. This creates loyalty but doesn't fundamentally alter behavior for non-subscribers.

Costco's membership is mandatory — you literally cannot enter the store without it. This means 100% of Costco's customer base is experiencing sunk-cost pressure, not just a self-selected subset. The psychological effect is uniform and total: every shopper has already paid and therefore stops comparing prices with competitors. The broader principle is that mandatory participation in a cost structure creates more consistent behavioral change than optional participation, even when the optional version might attract more total users.
