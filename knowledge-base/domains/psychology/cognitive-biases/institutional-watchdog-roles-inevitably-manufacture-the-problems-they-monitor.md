Graham identifies a clean incentive trap: dedicated watchdog roles (compliance officers, inclusion administrators, security theater staff) pursue their designated problems as if their jobs depended on it — because they do. If no problems are found, the role has no justification. This creates a ratchet effect where the monitored problem appears to grow over time regardless of its actual trajectory.

The insight generalizes far beyond its political context. It applies to any organization that creates dedicated monitoring roles: security teams that inflate threat assessments, QA teams that block releases over marginal issues, risk committees that discover ever-more-exotic risks. The structural fix is to embed monitoring responsibilities within operational roles rather than creating dedicated watchdog positions, or to measure watchdog effectiveness by outcomes (actual harm prevented) rather than inputs (problems detected). Organizations should be deeply suspicious whenever a monitoring function reports that its monitored problem is growing — that's exactly what you'd expect whether the problem is growing or not.
