Paine's diagnosis of Japanese delusion is precise: they observed America letting Britain bleed against the Nazis, inferred 'Americans won't fight,' and extrapolated that a costly attack would push isolationists to disengage further. The error was treating American isolationism as a trait rather than a conditional response. The attack itself was the stimulus that flipped the condition.

This is a general failure mode in adversary modeling across domains. Competitors, regulators, incumbents, and nation-states all have latent response functions that look dormant under peace but activate sharply under attack. Modeling them by their steady-state behavior systematically understates the cost of provocation.

The practical fix is to ask: 'What is my adversary's behavior when provoked, not when observed?' Most strategic actors, most of the time, only know the second. The gap between the two is where delusional optimism lives — and where empires die.
