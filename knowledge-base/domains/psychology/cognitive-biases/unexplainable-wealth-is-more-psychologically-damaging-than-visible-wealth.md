David Marx's key insight is that traditional social comparison included an explanatory component: you knew the rich neighbor ran a car dealership, so you could attribute his wealth to hard work, risk tolerance, and some luck. This narrative let you maintain self-esteem ('I could do that if I chose to'). Influencer wealth strips away this attribution — you see the lifestyle but not the mechanism. Without a story to explain the gap, comparison becomes pure status pain with no cognitive escape hatch. This suggests that transparency about wealth sources could partially defuse comparison-driven unhappiness.
