The idea that Columbus set out to prove the Earth was round — and that people before him thought it was flat — is a fabrication popularized by Washington Irving in 1828. In reality, ancient Greeks had measured Earth's circumference with reasonable accuracy, and medieval scholars universally accepted a spherical Earth. The myth persists because it creates a satisfying narrative: brave explorer challenges ignorant masses.

This pattern — where false beliefs survive because they serve a narrative need — appears everywhere. In startups, founding myths get embellished because 'we pivoted after a crisis' is more compelling than 'we gradually iterated.' In organizations, origin stories calcify around dramatic moments that may not have happened as remembered. The actionable takeaway: when a 'fact' makes for a great story, that's precisely when you should verify it most carefully. Narrative appeal and truth are often inversely correlated.
