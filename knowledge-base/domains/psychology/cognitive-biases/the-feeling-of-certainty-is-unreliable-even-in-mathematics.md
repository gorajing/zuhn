Deutsch and Hall use a vivid demonstration: draw two dots on paper and you feel absolutely certain only one straight line passes through them. But wrap the paper around a sphere and you can push a pen through both dots — a different straight line entirely. This overturns a 'certainty' held for centuries in Euclidean geometry, and the resolution (non-Euclidean geometry) directly enabled Einstein's general relativity.

The deeper point is what Deutsch calls the 'mathematician's misconception': the belief that mathematical proof yields absolute certainty. Since mathematicians are physical beings subject to error, and axioms themselves can be wrong (as Gödel's incompleteness theorem implies), all knowledge — including mathematical knowledge — is conjectural. This matters practically: when you feel most certain about something, that's precisely when to probe your assumptions hardest. The history of progress shows breakthroughs come from questioning the 'obviously true' foundations.
