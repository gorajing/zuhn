The Pioneer plaque, designed by Drake and Sagan, was criticized by human scientists for being difficult to decipher — let alone aliens. The arrow indicating the probe's trajectory assumes familiarity with projectile-based hunting cultures. The use of a man and woman for scale assumes bipedal body plans. Even the pulsar map assumes the recipient shares our concept of spatial diagrams.

This is a powerful case study in the curse of knowledge: the smarter and more immersed you are in your own framework, the harder it is to see what's culturally contingent vs. truly universal. The only elements that survived cross-cultural scrutiny were rooted in physics (hydrogen hyperfine transitions) rather than human convention. This suggests that when designing for radically different audiences — whether alien civilizations or unfamiliar user segments — grounding communication in shared physical or mathematical reality is more robust than relying on symbols that feel intuitive.
