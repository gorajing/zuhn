Graham observes that founders' attachment to their company name doesn't present itself as simple emotional attachment (which would be easy to discount) but instead as a collection of specious beliefs about the name's intrinsic qualities. This is a specific manifestation of the endowment effect operating through identity — when something becomes 'who we are,' we unconsciously construct rationalizations for why it's objectively superior. Graham notes this same mechanism operates in nationality and religion, suggesting it's a deep cognitive pattern. The antidote is a thought experiment: imagine you'd chosen differently, and notice you'd be equally attached to that alternative.
