The original metric unit of mass was called the 'grave' (from Latin gravitas). It was renamed during the French Revolution because it sounded like the aristocratic title 'graf' — incompatible with the revolutionary cry of equality. Scientists first shrank to the 'gramme' (1/1000th), then realized it was too small and invented the 'kilogram' — making it the only SI base unit with a prefix in its name.

This historical accident reveals a pattern: standards that present themselves as purely rational and objective are frequently shaped by social, political, and cultural forces. The bias isn't just in who sets the standards but in the very structure and naming of the system. This applies broadly — coding conventions, industry benchmarks, regulatory frameworks, and scientific norms all carry hidden political fingerprints.

Lavoisier himself, who helped devise the metric system, was executed during the Revolution — not for his science but for his aristocratic tax-collecting role. The messenger's social identity trumped the quality of his measurement work.
