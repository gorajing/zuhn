Both Costco and Amazon Prime use upfront fees to create switching costs, but there's a critical structural difference. Amazon Prime is optional—customers self-sort based on whether they calculate enough value to justify the fee. This creates loyalty among subscribers but doesn't fundamentally change Amazon's business model for non-subscribers.

Costco's membership is mandatory—you literally cannot enter the store without it. This means every Costco member has an active sunk-cost calculation running: 'I've already paid $60/$120, so I should maximize value by shopping here.' The mandatory nature eliminates the comparison-shopping phase entirely. Customers stop evaluating per-item prices against competitors because the psychological frame shifts from 'is this item cheaper?' to 'am I getting my membership's worth?' This subtle distinction in payment architecture produces measurably different behavioral outcomes: Costco's 90% renewal rate versus the constant churn management required by optional subscription models.
