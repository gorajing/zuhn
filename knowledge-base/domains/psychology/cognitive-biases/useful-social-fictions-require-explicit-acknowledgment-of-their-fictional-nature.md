Harari draws a sharp contrast between the Ten Commandments and the US Constitution — both are foundational 'fictions' that organize society, but one claims divine authorship (precluding amendment) while the other starts with 'We the People' and includes explicit amendment procedures. The Ten Commandments still endorse slavery in the 10th Commandment with no mechanism for correction; the Constitution has been amended precisely because it acknowledges its human fallibility.

This principle extends far beyond governance. Any system — organizational culture, business strategy, personal belief — that claims to be objectively true rather than a useful construct becomes brittle and uncorrectable. The paradox is that acknowledging a fiction's constructed nature doesn't weaken it; it strengthens it by enabling error correction. Money is a fiction everyone agrees on, and it works precisely because we can adjust monetary policy. The key test for any organizing principle: does it include a mechanism for identifying and correcting its own mistakes?
