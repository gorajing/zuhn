After running post-mortems on their biggest investment misses, Sequoia kept arriving at the same conclusion: the error wasn't analytical — it was psychological. Competitive excitement influenced judgment. Similarity bias drew them toward familiar-feeling founders. Rather than treating these as vague warnings, they cataloged 40 specific cognitive biases and gave each one a name.

The naming is the mechanism. When a pattern has a shared label, team members can call it out in real-time without it feeling like a personal attack. 'I think we're seeing competitive excitement here' is actionable feedback; 'I think you're being emotional' is not. This transforms individual psychological vulnerability into organizational immune response. The practice works because it makes the invisible visible through language.
