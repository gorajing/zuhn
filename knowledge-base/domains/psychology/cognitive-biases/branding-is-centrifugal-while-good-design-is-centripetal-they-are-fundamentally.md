Graham identifies a deep structural tension: good design seeks optimal solutions, and optimal solutions converge (like math). Branding requires distinctiveness by definition — if you choose the best option, others will too, eliminating your brand differentiation. This means branding can only be achieved at the expense of good design in mature, well-explored design spaces.

The only exceptions are when the design space is enormous (like painting, where Leonardo could be both optimal and distinctive among ~10 peers) or when the space is unexplored (being first to find the right answer lets you claim it as yours temporarily). In mature product categories like watches, the conflict is inescapable. Graham extends this insight beyond product design to religion itself: distinctive customs must be inconvenient and unreasonable, or other groups would adopt them too. This framework explains why brand-age products in any industry trend toward the bizarre.
