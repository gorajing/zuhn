Both Costco and Amazon Prime exploit sunk-cost fallacy: after paying $60–$120 upfront, members feel psychologically invested in using the service to 'get their money's worth,' which short-circuits comparative shopping. The rational calculation — 'should I check if Target is cheaper?' — gets replaced by 'I already have Prime, why bother?'

This is a well-documented cognitive bias where the irrecoverability of a past cost distorts future decisions. The annual fee effectively creates a mental anchor that makes the default behavior loyalty rather than optimization. Members stop being price-sensitive consumers and become brand-loyal ones.

For product designers: annual subscriptions paid upfront suppress churn and comparison shopping more effectively than monthly plans, because the sunk cost is larger and sits further in the past. The behavioral moat isn't the product — it's the payment structure.
