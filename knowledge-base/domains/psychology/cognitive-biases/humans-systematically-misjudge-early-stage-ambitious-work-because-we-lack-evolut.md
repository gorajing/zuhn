Graham's core thesis is that harsh judgment of early work is not an immutable human trait but a cultural absence. For most of human history, genuinely new things appeared so rarely that no customs developed for how to respond to them. We default to two inappropriate benchmarks: comparing early work to finished work (too harsh) or comparing ambitious early work to less ambitious finished work (category error).

Silicon Valley serves as existence proof that these defaults can be overwritten quickly. Within decades, the Valley developed customs that flip the evaluation polarity—from 'list reasons this won't work' to 'imagine ways this could work.' Graham argues this shift happened because the economic incentives were strong enough (power-law returns reward optimism), and that similar customs will inevitably spread to other fields as the evidence accumulates. The implication is that dismissiveness toward new ideas is a solvable cultural problem, not a fixed feature of human psychology.
