Early Chinese historians like Sima Qian didn't just record events — they shaped narratives to demonstrate that virtuous rulers prospered and corrupt ones fell, reinforcing Confucian moral philosophy. Each new dynasty commissioned histories of the previous one that conveniently proved the old regime deserved to fall.

This is a foundational cognitive bias that extends far beyond Chinese history. Every retrospective narrative — startup post-mortems, case studies, corporate histories — is shaped by the framework of whoever writes it. Success stories emphasize decisions that fit the narrator's worldview while omitting contradictory evidence. Recognizing this 'historiography bias' means treating all after-the-fact narratives as arguments rather than evidence, and actively seeking accounts from multiple perspectives before drawing conclusions.
