Environmental organizations that historically sounded alarms over Chinese overfishing have largely fallen silent in recent years, even as the problem has worsened dramatically. This pattern — where advocacy groups selectively apply their stated universal principles based on the identity of the offender — illustrates a broader cognitive bias in institutional behavior. When an organization's ideological commitments (e.g., critiquing Western capitalism) conflict with their stated mission (environmental protection), the ideology often wins. This same dynamic appears in climate groups' refusal to criticize China despite it being the world's largest emitter. The insight generalizes: any mission-driven organization risks irrelevance when it allows political identity to override the universal application of its core principles. The corrective is to audit advocacy organizations for consistency of criticism across all actors, not just ideologically convenient targets.
