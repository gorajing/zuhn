JCal's framing is pointed: 'Media is all about habits.' Commuters formed routines around buying a newspaper or two magazines before boarding the train. This habit was the engine — not the quality of the articles. When smartphones arrived, the commute ritual changed, and the habit-slot that newspapers had occupied was filled by the phone. Revenue from newsstand sales collapsed not because journalism got worse, but because the behavioral trigger (commute → grab something to read) was replaced.

For any product that depends on repeated engagement, this is the correct threat model: you're not competing on features, you're competing for habit slots. The danger isn't a better product in the same ritual — it's a device or context change that rewires the ritual entirely. Builders of daily-use products should map their product to the specific behavioral trigger that activates use, then ask: what context change would eliminate that trigger? For media businesses today, that question still applies — AI-generated summaries may not kill quality journalism, but they might kill the habit of opening a publication.
