Graham notes that people sometimes do change what they want — but the mechanism is typically a second-order desire first: 'I want to like classical music,' then gradually coming to actually like it. The same pattern appears in successful long-term behavior change: people who sustain new habits usually first want to be the kind of person who has those habits. The first-order desire follows.

This has an immediate application: when helping someone change behavior, the first diagnostic question is not 'what do they do?' but 'do they want to want to change?' If they only want the outcome without wanting to want the process (e.g., they want to be fit but don't want to want to exercise), interventions that rely on raw willpower will fail. Interventions that cultivate genuine second-order motivation — identity shifts, community membership, values clarification — are more likely to produce lasting change.
