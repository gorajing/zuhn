Geoff reframes the tired 'should you go to college' debate around signaling economics: 'getting a badge is useful if you don't have any other badges.' Someone who has already become world-class at a verifiable thing — a top content creator, athlete, or founder — doesn't need the institutional badge, because their achievement is itself the external validator. But someone without such proof should deliberately collect badges (win a science fair, a math competition, get into Harvard) because they supply the confidence and third-party validation that you are 'at least good at something.'

He separates two things that usually get conflated: educational content versus the badging system. He expects the content of education to be reformed, but predicts the badging system will not change — so the rational move is to acquire a badge while it still carries signaling value, treating it as a confidence-and-credibility instrument rather than as the source of the underlying skill. The actionable rule: audit what proof of excellence you already hold; if you have a genuine world-class achievement, stop optimizing for credentials; if you don't, go collect one. (Note the self-aware caveat that credential-skeptics like Peter Thiel often already hold every elite badge themselves.)
