To measure whether antimatter falls up or down, physicists can't use antiprotons: even tiny stray electric fields influence them more than gravity does. They need antihydrogen (neutral), which in turn is nearly impossible to trap because the magnetic confinement used for charged particles no longer applies. GBAR's workaround is to make an antihydrogen ion (charged → easy to cool), laser-cool it to 10 micro-Kelvin, then strip a positron to neutralize it for the final 20cm fall. The counterintuitive lesson: the weakest force in physics (gravity) is the hardest to measure on small scales precisely because the stronger forces overwhelm its signal. Signal isolation, not signal amplification, is the deeper engineering problem.
