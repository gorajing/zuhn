The light-dependent reactions begin when a photon — created by hydrogen fusion 93 million miles away — strikes chlorophyll and excites an electron. The entire elaborate machinery of Photosystem II, the Cytochrome Complex, and Photosystem I exists to systematically extract that photon's energy through a series of electron transfers, converting it into ATP and NADPH. These energy carriers then power the Calvin Cycle to fix carbon into organic molecules.

This means virtually all life on Earth — every plant, animal, fungus, and most bacteria — runs on energy that originated in nuclear fusion. The food chain is just a series of energy transfers that ultimately trace back to excited electrons in chlorophyll. This connects to the insight that the sun provides low entropy rather than just energy: what photosynthesis captures is not raw heat but the organized, low-entropy energy packets of individual photons, which it converts into the organized chemical structures of organic molecules.
