David Kipping describes research showing that on super-Earth planets (significantly more massive than Earth), the surface gravity is so high that the chemical potential energy of any conceivable rocket fuel is insufficient to reach escape velocity. The rocket equation demands that nearly all mass be fuel, requiring pyramid-sized rockets to launch tiny payloads — and even that may not work.

This has profound implications for the Fermi paradox: super-Earths are the most common rocky planet type in the galaxy, so if intelligent life arises on them, those civilizations would never become space-faring. They'd be invisible to us — not because they don't exist, but because physics permanently confines them. It also highlights a selection bias in our thinking: we assume space travel is achievable because we happen to live on a planet where the gravity-to-fuel-energy ratio narrowly permits it.
