The intuitive assumption — black balls absorb more heat, therefore they heat the water — is wrong. The balls get hot on top but the air inside acts as thermal insulation (like a double-paned window), preventing heat transfer to the water below. Measurements showed water is actually cooler under black shade balls than under direct sunlight.

This reveals a common physics-intuition failure: people focus on the most salient variable (color/absorption) while ignoring the dominant mechanism (insulation/conduction path). The same error appears in many domains — we fixate on the obvious factor while the less visible structural factor drives the outcome. Shade balls reduce evaporation by 80-90% despite being black, because blocking airflow and reducing exposed surface area matters far more than surface color.
