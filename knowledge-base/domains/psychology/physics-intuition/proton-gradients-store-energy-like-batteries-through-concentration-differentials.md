In photosynthesis, the thylakoid membrane accumulates protons on one side through water-splitting and active pumping via the Cytochrome Complex. This concentration gradient — not any single chemical bond — is what stores the energy. Protons then flow through ATP Synthase driven by their desire to equalize, and that flow powers the attachment of inorganic phosphate to ADP, creating ATP.

This pattern — storing energy as a spatial imbalance rather than in a molecule — is counterintuitive but fundamental. It appears across biology (mitochondria use the same trick in respiration) and maps to engineering principles: capacitors store energy as charge separation, dams store energy as water height differentials. The insight is that energy storage often means creating ordered asymmetry, not accumulating stuff.
