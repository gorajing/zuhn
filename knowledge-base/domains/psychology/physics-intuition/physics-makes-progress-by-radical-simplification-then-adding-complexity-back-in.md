Carroll explains that physics is hard precisely because its subject matter permits radical simplification. Frictionless surfaces, point particles, and spherical cows are absurd in the real world but enormously productive in physics because the underlying systems actually are simple enough that the idealizations converge on truth.

This strategy would completely fail in psychology, biology, or political science because the complexity in those fields is not decorative — it is load-bearing. The insight for knowledge work: before adopting any methodology, ask whether the domain's complexity is incidental (removable) or essential (constitutive). Applying physics-style reduction to inherently complex systems produces elegant but wrong models.
