Tyson demonstrates that the Coriolis effect — Earth's rotation — deflects a football's path by a noticeable amount over the distance of a field goal. Most people dismiss rotational effects as only relevant at weather-system scales, but the math shows accumulation happens at every scale; we just lack the precision to notice it in daily life.

This is a powerful principle for any domain: forces you dismiss as 'too small to matter' are always accumulating. In compound interest, daily rounding errors compound into significant sums. In team dynamics, micro-aggressions that seem individually negligible accumulate into toxic culture. In engineering, thermal expansion that's invisible at room temperature warps precision instruments. The lesson: if a force exists, it's acting — the only question is whether you've measured long enough or precisely enough to see the drift.
