When liquid evaporates from a gel, surface tension forces pull on the solid skeleton and collapse it. Samuel Kistler solved this in 1931 by heating the liquid past its critical point, where the distinction between liquid and gas ceases to exist. No interface means no surface tension, so the solid structure survives intact.

This is a powerful problem-solving pattern: when a transition between two states causes damage, don't try to make the transition gentler — eliminate the distinction between the states entirely. The solution isn't incremental improvement of the evaporation process; it's reframing the problem by finding a regime where the problematic boundary doesn't exist. This pattern appears in organizational change (gradual role transitions vs. restructuring that eliminates the old/new distinction) and in software migrations (parallel running vs. feature flags that blur the boundary between old and new systems).
