The ANU team explicitly frames their momentum-entanglement result as unsurprising in theory but essential to measure: 'if we can't measure it, maybe the universe doesn't work the way we think it does.' This captures a core epistemic discipline — extrapolating a model from the regime where it was validated (photons) into a new regime (massive atoms, momentum observables) is an untested claim, not a derived fact. The same logic applies outside physics: a codebase pattern that worked at 100 users isn't proven at 10M; a drug that worked in mice isn't proven in humans; a trading strategy that worked in a bull market isn't proven in a drawdown. The failure mode is treating validation-in-regime-A as validation-in-general. Good epistemics requires re-running the measurement every time the regime shifts, especially when the new regime is one where your theory would most plausibly break.
