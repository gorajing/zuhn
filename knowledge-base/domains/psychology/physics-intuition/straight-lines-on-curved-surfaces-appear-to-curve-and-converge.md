The ribbon test demonstrates a powerful geometric principle: on a curved surface like a sphere, two paths that start parallel and never turn will eventually converge and meet. To an observer, this looks exactly like an attractive force pulling the objects together, but no force exists — it's a pure consequence of the surface's curvature.

This is the core analogy for understanding general relativity. Mass curves spacetime the way a sphere curves a flat sheet. Objects following their natural straight-line paths through this curved geometry appear to 'fall' toward each other. The convergence is real, but the force is an illusion. This mental model — geometry producing apparent force — is one of the most powerful conceptual tools in modern physics and applies anywhere you encounter emergent behavior from structural constraints.
