Kipping argues that if a typical civilization's technological lifetime is a few hundred to ten thousand years (from agriculture or AI-driven collapse), that's essentially nothing on cosmic timescales. The galaxy is 13 billion years old. Even if many civilizations have existed, the probability of two coexisting at the same time is extraordinarily low.

This reframes the Fermi paradox from 'where is everybody?' to 'why would we expect temporal overlap?' The practical implication is that the most promising form of interstellar communication may be time capsules rather than real-time signals — messages left for future civilizations to discover. Kipping specifically suggests the Moon as an ideal repository: no weathering, stable for billions of years, and objects buried meters below the surface would survive indefinitely. This shifts the search strategy from listening for signals to archaeology of nearby celestial bodies.
