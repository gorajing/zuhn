When a phenomenon is fundamentally inaccessible — like the interior of a black hole — direct observation is impossible. Scientists solved this by building analog systems: acoustic black holes (dumbholes) that trap sound the way black holes trap light. These use special fluids traveling at the speed of sound to create regions from which sound cannot escape.

This approach generalizes beyond physics. When you can't study a system directly, building a structurally similar analog in an accessible domain lets you extract real insights. The key requirement is that the analog preserves the mathematical relationships of the original system, even if the physical substrate is completely different. This is why dumbholes work — the equations governing sound trapping in fluid mirror those governing light trapping in curved spacetime.
