A shadow cast on the Moon can sweep thousands of kilometers in a fraction of a second, and scissor-blade intersection points on light-year-long blades could cross a light year in one second. Neither violates relativity because the speed-of-light limit applies to information and causal influence, not to geometric patterns or the absence of light.

This distinction matters beyond physics. In any system, apparent motion (a metric moving, a trend spreading) can outpace the actual causal mechanism driving it. Recognizing the difference between something that looks like it's moving fast and something that is actually transmitting influence prevents misattribution of cause and effect — whether in markets, organizational change, or technology adoption curves.
