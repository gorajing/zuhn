Schwarzschild laid coordinates over part of the solution to Einstein's equations, like mapping only the northern hemisphere. When other physicists asked 'what about negative coordinates?' - the mathematical equivalent of asking about the southern hemisphere - they discovered white holes, parallel universes, and Einstein-Rosen bridges (wormholes). The simplest possible solution to Einstein's equations, a single non-rotating point mass, already contained all of these structures. They were always there in the math; it just required extending the coordinate system to its maximum extent to see them.

This 'maximal extension' strategy is remarkably powerful: instead of asking 'what exists?', you ask 'what does the math allow?' and then check whether nature realizes it. Black holes went from mathematical curiosity to observed reality. White holes and traversable wormholes remain theoretically allowed geometries but likely require exotic matter that doesn't exist. The key insight is that mathematical completeness is a better guide to physical possibility than human intuition about what 'should' exist.
