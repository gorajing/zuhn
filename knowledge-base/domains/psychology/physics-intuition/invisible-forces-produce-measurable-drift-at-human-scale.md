Tyson calculated that a Cincinnati Bengals overtime field goal that bounced off the left upright and in was aided by approximately a third of an inch of rightward drift from the Coriolis force — Earth's rotation deflecting northward-moving objects to the right in the northern hemisphere. The same force that creates hurricane circulation patterns operated at football scale.

The broader insight is about threshold effects and the danger of dismissing small forces. In most contexts, a third of an inch is truly negligible. But at the margin — when a ball is already heading toward the edge of an upright — that negligible force becomes decisive. This applies to any domain with threshold effects: in A/B testing, tiny conversion rate differences compound over millions of users; in negotiations, small framing shifts change outcomes when parties are near their walk-away point; in engineering, tolerances that seem generous fail at edge cases. The practical rule: audit what you're calling 'negligible' and ask whether your system has any thresholds where that force could tip the outcome.
