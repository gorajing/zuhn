When a massive star collapses from Earth-size to city-size, conservation of angular momentum forces it to spin enormously faster — from a gentle rotation to many revolutions per second. The ballerina analogy is precise: pulling arms in speeds up the spin. This is not energy being added; it's existing rotational energy being concentrated into a smaller radius.

This conservation principle applies broadly: when any system contracts or focuses, its existing properties intensify rather than diminish. A company that shrinks to its core competency often executes faster. A person who narrows their focus area concentrates their accumulated knowledge into fewer problems. The counterintuitive result is that contraction can produce more intensity than expansion — the same total energy in a smaller space creates more extreme local conditions.
