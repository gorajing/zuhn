Hermès under Jean-Louis Dumas was 70%+ family-owned, had 187 years of continuity, and was arguably the most unassailable brand in luxury. Bernard Arnault still identified it as vulnerable and built a covert 10-year position via equity-swap derivatives that would eventually reach over 20% ownership. What he saw was that generational handoff was in progress: Pierre-Alexis Dumas would take the artistic director role, but the CEO role was being separated for the first time in company history and handed to a non-family member on an interim basis while the next family CEO (Axel Dumas) paid his dues. This structural ambiguity is the attack surface. The same pattern recurs across Hermès's own history — every generational transfer coincided with an existential threat (consultants telling them to outsource in the 1970s; the near-takeover by LVMH in the 2000s). The lesson for leaders of long-lived institutions: the strategic risk profile spikes during succession even when financial performance is strong, and external actors with long time horizons will specifically watch for these moments. Succession planning is not just talent management, it's defense planning.
