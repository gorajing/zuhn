Jake Paul's risk heuristic is a via-negativa rule: 'Everything you do has risk. Just don't take fatal risk... don't go to jail, don't die, and as long as you take risks and calculated bets and keep compounding, you'll eventually end up somewhere good.' The asymmetry is that survivable downside plus repeated compounding bets trends upward, while the truly ruinous outcomes are a short, avoidable list.

The sharper, less obvious half is about the cost of inaction: 'most people are just totally irrelevant... you need to do something a little bit risky to even get to a place to be cancelable. Most people can't even be cancelled because you didn't exist.' Excessive caution is itself the failure mode — it guarantees irrelevance, which is a worse expected outcome than a recoverable misstep. The principle inverts the usual framing of risk as something to minimize: minimize only the irreversible tail, and otherwise bias toward action, because optionality and reputation are built by people willing to warrant attention at all. This generalizes well beyond a public career to founding companies, career bets, and creative work.
