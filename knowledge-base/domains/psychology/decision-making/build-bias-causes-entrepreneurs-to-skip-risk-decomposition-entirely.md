There's a well-documented action bias in entrepreneurship — the feeling that progress means building, shipping, and doing visible work. Hypothesis identification and prioritization feels abstract and unproductive by comparison, even though it's often the highest-leverage activity available.

This bias is reinforced by startup culture's celebration of speed and shipping. 'Move fast and break things' implicitly values building over thinking. But the article's core insight is that the entrepreneurs who fail most expensively are those who never explicitly identified what they were assuming — they built elaborate solutions to problems nobody had, or built for markets that didn't exist, because they never stopped to ask which assumptions carried the most existential risk.
