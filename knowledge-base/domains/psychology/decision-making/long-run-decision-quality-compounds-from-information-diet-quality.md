Most people treat decision-making as a discrete cognitive act: gather information, weigh options, choose. But the quality of that in-the-moment reasoning depends heavily on the background model of the world built up over years of information consumption. Someone who has consistently consumed high-signal, accurate, diverse sources has a richer prior to reason from than someone with a narrower or lower-quality diet — even if they're equally intelligent in the moment.

The actionable implication is that information curation deserves deliberate investment, treated more like exercise than entertainment. Periodically auditing your primary information sources — asking whether they're expanding your model of the world or confirming existing beliefs, whether their predictions have tracked, whether they expose you to uncomfortable data — is a high-leverage habit that pays off over years. The people with the best judgment in any field tend to be those who quietly optimized their information diet long before they needed to make the decisions that mattered.
