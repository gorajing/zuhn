When collecting evidence to validate a problem statement, Rachitsky recommends explicitly playing devil's advocate: try to convince yourself the problem isn't real or important enough. This surfaces gaps in your evidence and tests whether data points actually support the conclusion you've drawn. The recommendation for 3-5 strong data points over a dozen weak ones reflects the same principle — padding your case with tangential evidence actually weakens it by creating an illusion of rigor.

This maps to a broader cognitive bias pattern: once we've committed to a problem framing, we selectively gather confirming evidence. The devil's advocate step is a structured de-biasing technique. It's particularly valuable because sometimes this step reveals the problem genuinely isn't worth prioritizing right now, or that the framing needs adjustment — outcomes the framework explicitly welcomes rather than treats as failures. The goal is confidence in your bet, not confirmation of your assumption.
