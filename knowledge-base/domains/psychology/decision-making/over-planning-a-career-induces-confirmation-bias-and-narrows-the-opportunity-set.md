Dara's advice to young people is explicitly anti-planning: the most common mistake he sees is people deciding they want to be VP by X age or earn Y by Z age, because once you commit to a plan, your brain actively searches for evidence that agrees with it and discards everything else. This is a mechanistic claim about cognitive bias, not vague advice to 'stay open.'

His own trajectory validates the model to an unusual degree. He became an investment banker because he was chasing a girlfriend in New York (they broke up six months later). He got assigned to the Paramount hostile bid as an analyst only because his VP got sick. He met Barry Diller because Diller wanted to see the raw model and Dara happened to be the person running the numbers. He took the Uber job because Daniel Ek cornered him at Sun Valley. Every major career inflection was emergent, not planned.

The implication for high-agency people is counterintuitive: it's not 'no plan at all,' it's 'plan for your current situation and go all-in, but don't commit to the destination.' The going-all-in is what creates the next opportunity; the refusal to pre-commit to a specific outcome is what lets you recognize it when it arrives.
