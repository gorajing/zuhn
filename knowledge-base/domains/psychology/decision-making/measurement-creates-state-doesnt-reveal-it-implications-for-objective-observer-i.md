From the quantum entanglement essay: 'The act of measurement doesn't reveal a pre-existing state. It brings a state into existence. And once you accept this, you start to see its implications way beyond physics — it suggests that what we call reality is not a fixed structure, but a process, a continuous unfolding of interactions that define properties in the moment that they're observed.' The crucial clarification: this is NOT 'consciousness creates reality' (a frequent misinterpretation in pop-physics). Measurement doesn't require a human observer; it can be any physical interaction that exchanges energy or information. But the framework of 'there's an objective external state, and good experiments reveal it' breaks down. There is no observer-independent description of an unobserved particle that is both complete and meaningful. Reality at the deepest level is constituted through interaction. The practical extrapolation for decision-making: be careful about claiming to know what's true about a system you haven't interacted with. Market predictions, organizational dynamics, AI model behaviors, customer preferences — all are partly constituted by your measurement of them. The implication for AI research: the entire 'we will eventually have a true objective model of intelligence' framing inherits assumptions that quantum mechanics has empirically eroded. Models don't reveal what intelligence IS; they define what intelligence DOES when measured a particular way. Different benchmark = different 'intelligence.' This isn't relativism — the measurements remain reliable and reproducible — but it's a humility check on claims about observer-independent truth.
