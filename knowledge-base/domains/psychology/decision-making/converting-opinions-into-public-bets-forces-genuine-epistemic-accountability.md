Graham identifies an asymmetry in how people hold beliefs: casual opinions carry no cost of being wrong, so they drift toward whatever is comfortable or socially rewarded. But when you bet on ideas — whether financially as an investor or reputationally through published writing — you create a feedback loop that penalizes obsolete thinking.

The mechanism matters: it's not just accountability to others, but accountability to your future self. Startup investors who passed on Google remember it forever. Writers whose predictions age poorly have a permanent record. This suggests a practical technique for improving your own thinking: convert your strongest opinions into some durable, public form. The act of committing forces you to examine whether you actually believe what you think you believe, and whether your evidence is current.
