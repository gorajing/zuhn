    geopolitical, and personal-life arguments. The reasoning test: if you reject
    a conclusion, can you point to the premise you disagree with? If not, you're
    rejecting on aesthetic grounds, not epistemic ones.

Tom Bilyeu cites Bostrom's 2003 paper: computing power has roughly doubled every 2 years for decades. If that trend continues (or even slows dramatically), future civilizations will eventually be capable of running ancestor simulations indistinguishable from reality. Bostrom's structure is a trilemma — at least one of these must be true: (1) Civilizations like ours go extinct before reaching simulation-capable compute (the Great Filter is ahead). (2) Civilizations that reach simulation-capable compute choose NOT to run ancestor simulations (a uniform preference across all advanced civilizations, which is itself implausible). (3) We are almost certainly in a simulation, because there will be vastly more simulated ancestors than original ones, and by the principle of indifference our existence is more probably among the simulated. Each branch is uncomfortable: (1) implies near-term existential risk, (2) requires a uniform anti-simulation preference across all civilizations, (3) implies we're not 'base reality.' The practical lesson isn't 'we should believe we're simulated' — that's a low-confidence claim regardless. The lesson is methodological: a rigorously-argued conclusion that conflicts with intuition should be engaged with on its merits, not dismissed because it feels weird. Most decision-making errors come from prematurely dismissing arguments because their conclusions are uncomfortable, rather than because the arguments are flawed. Bostrom's argument is contested, but the right reason to disagree is to identify a flaw in the argument's premises — not 'this can't be right because then we'd be in a simulation.' This discipline extends to any field: AI safety arguments that imply uncomfortable conclusions (loss of human control, value misalignment) deserve engagement with the argument structure, not dismissal because the conclusion is uncomfortable. Same for economic, geopolitical, and personal-life arguments. The reasoning test: if you reject a conclusion, can you point to the premise you disagree with? If not, you're rejecting on aesthetic grounds, not epistemic ones.
