Dwarkesh Patel's articulation of why he resists confident specific-scenario predictions: 'Your conclusions about where the world ends up as a result of changing many of these parameters is almost like a hash function. You change it slightly and you just get a very different world on the other end. And it's important to acknowledge that, because you sort of want to know how robust this whole end conclusion is to any part of the story changing. And then it also informs if you do believe that things could just go one way or another, you don't want to do big radical moves that only make sense under one specific story and are really counterproductive in other stories.' Specific example he cites as a fragile-scenario policy: nationalization of AI labs. Only makes sense under one story (where US-China arms race dominates and centralized control wins). Counter-productive under others (where decentralized progress and checks-and-balances matter more). The prescription Dwarkesh advocates: 'Classical liberalism just has been a helpful way to navigate the world when we're under this kind of epistemic hell of one thing changing.' Both Scott and Daniel agree: 'I think that's kind of why all of our policy prescriptions are things like more transparency, get more people involved, try to have lots of people working on this.' Why this matters: most policy debates assume the future will be one specific way ('AI will go well, so push for adoption' vs 'AI will go badly, so slow it down'). The robust framing instead: 'AI will go many possible ways depending on parameters we don't know — design policies that improve outcomes across many scenarios.' Transparency improves outcomes whether AI develops fast or slow. Checks-and-balances improve outcomes whether AI is misaligned or aligned. Pluralism improves outcomes whether power concentrates in CEOs or governments. Concentration-shaped policies (nationalize labs, ban research, etc.) work in only one scenario and fail badly in others. The general principle for any deep-uncertainty domain: ask not 'which scenario do I think is most likely?' but 'what policy improves outcomes across the realistic range of scenarios?' This is the meta-principle behind classical liberalism's longevity — it's a meta-policy that produces decent outcomes across many possible worlds rather than excellent outcomes in one possible world. The corollary for personal decision-making under uncertainty: career paths, investment strategies, relationship choices all benefit from being scenario-robust. The strategy of 'bet everything on this one specific future being correct' has high variance and low EV. The strategy of 'preserve optionality, maintain decentralized checks on outcomes' has lower variance and higher EV in expectation.
