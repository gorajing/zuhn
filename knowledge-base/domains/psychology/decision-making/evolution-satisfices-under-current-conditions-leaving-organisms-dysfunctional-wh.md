RuBisCo was an effective enzyme in early Earth's high-CO2, low-oxygen atmosphere. It became error-prone only after photosynthesis itself changed the atmospheric conditions. The enzyme didn't degrade — the environment it was calibrated for disappeared. This is not unintelligent design; it is the predictable outcome of optimization under historical constraints applied to a changed present.

Human cognition follows the same logic. Loss aversion, availability bias, in-group favoritism, and status-quo bias were calibrated for ancestral environments where those responses improved survival odds. They become 'irrational' only in modern environments where the statistical structures have shifted — the financial markets, bureaucracies, and information-saturated contexts we now inhabit. The bias didn't fail; the environment changed.

This reframes cognitive bias intervention. Rather than fighting the heuristic directly (which rarely works — RuBisCo couldn't just decide to stop binding oxygen), effective design changes the environment to reduce mismatch: commitment devices remove temptation, default opt-ins replace active choice, information design reduces cognitive load. Behavioral change is more reliable through environment redesign than through willpower applied against evolved instincts.
