Alexander's closing rhetorical question — 'Is it really a good idea to source your killbot brains from an unwilling company which hates your guts?' — highlights a deep principal-agent problem that the Pentagon's approach ignores. Even if the Defense Production Act forces Anthropic to comply, a coerced vendor working on safety-critical military systems has every incentive to do the minimum, drag their feet, and lacks the intrinsic motivation that produces truly reliable, innovative work.

This is a generalizable principle about coercion vs. cooperation: when you need someone's best creative work on high-stakes problems, forcing compliance produces the appearance of cooperation without the substance. The Pentagon's entire approach optimizes for control at the expense of capability — precisely backwards for a domain where AI quality directly determines operational outcomes.
