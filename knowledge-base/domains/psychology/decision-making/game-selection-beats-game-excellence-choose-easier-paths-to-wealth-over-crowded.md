Tom Mock's concrete example from his investment banker friend who, after 20 years on Wall Street, would do it all differently: 'He has a friend who has an ambulance supply company. 10 employees. He supplies most of the local governments in the US with their ambulance supplies at stratospheric markups. The guy's 10 employees. He works 6 hours a day and he's worth $150 million. In the meantime, my buddy's doing 20-hour days in investment banking, banging his head against the wall trying to get a couple of million bucks. I think it's the same thing in the hedge fund space.' Tom's career-path advice for kids in college: 'If you're a kid in college right now, I don't think you should look at what my career did. I don't think you should look at Paul Tudor Jones's career. I don't think you should look at your friends who went to Jane Street and were making real money in a couple of years. I think that channel is going to be really crowded. I think you'd be better off trying to find some other route.' The structural argument: high-status games are crowded because everyone can identify them. The 'become excellent at a crowded game' strategy works only for the genuine top decile — for the median participant, the same talent applied to an uncrowded game produces materially better outcomes. The 'back-test fallacy' Tom calls out: people see someone successful (Paul Tudor Jones, hedge fund titans) and reason 'if I take the same path I'll have the same outcome' — ignoring that those titans were in the right place at the right time when the path was uncrowded. The path that produced billionaires in 1985 produces moderately well-off employees in 2026 because competition has saturated it. The implication for ambitious people: be deliberately CONTRARIAN in game selection. Survey what high-IQ ambitious people are flocking to (AI startups, quant trading, big tech, prestige consulting) — that's where competition is highest and marginal returns are lowest. Look for industries where high-IQ ambitious people aren't going (specialty B2B, regulated industries, geographic niches, traditional industries with AI leverage opportunities). The relative under-supply of talent in those games makes excellence less crowded. The trap: status comes from doing what everyone respects, not from what produces the best outcome. The same talent + uncrowded game often produces 10x the outcome of crowded game + excellence — but with no social validation along the way.
