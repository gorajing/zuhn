The talk's mini-history of compensation is really a catalog of incentive misalignments. Hourly pay disincentivizes working faster — there's literally no upside to finishing early, so it rewards slowness. Fixed-bid (per-project) pay collides with a known fact: engineers are bad at estimating, so to eliminate their downside they pad a five-hour job to fifteen or twenty hours — which is exactly why buyers refuse to pay per project and retreat to time-and-materials. Salary-plus-bonus produces 'punch in at nine, leave at five' because effort and pay are decoupled. Equity works — it's the foundation of the startup ecosystem — but only at the rare company that exits big, and risk-averse engineers treat cash as non-negotiable while taking equity as a nice-to-have, so it fails to motivate the majority. The reference value here is the analytical template: for any pay structure, trace the dominant strategy it rewards and you'll find the behavior you'll actually get, which is rarely the behavior you wanted.
