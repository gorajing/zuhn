Robin Carhart-Harris's REBUS model ('Relaxed Beliefs Under Psychedelics') from his 2019 paper formalizes psychedelics' mechanism: under psychedelics, the brain's 'precision weighting' (confidence in its high-level priors) decreases. This means top-down predictions become less rigid, and bottom-up sensory information has more power to update beliefs. A depressed person's prior 'I am useless and everything goes wrong' is held with high precision — incoming positive data gets dismissed as noise rather than updating the prior. Under psychedelics, that prior's precision drops, and the same positive data now reshapes the model. The depression sometimes lifts because the underlying belief structure has been updated. The same precision-weighting mechanism explains many domains: (1) Expert blindness: experts hold their priors with high precision (justified by years of accurate predictions), but this same precision blocks them from updating on unusual cases. The expert who's 'always seen this before' misses the case that's different. (2) Political polarization: people with extreme political priors weight contradictory evidence as low-precision (biased media, lying experts), preventing update. (3) Organizational ossification: senior leaders with high-precision priors about 'how this market works' dismiss signals about market shifts as noise until the signals become catastrophic. (4) Founder pivoting: high confidence in your original thesis (the sunk-cost cousin) blocks pivot signals. The practical implication: when you notice you're not updating in response to evidence, the diagnostic question is 'how high is my precision on this prior, and is it justified?' The therapy isn't more evidence (high-precision priors filter it out); it's mechanisms that temporarily relax precision — sleep, meditation, conversations with people who hold different priors, or for clinical depression, psilocybin or LSD therapy. This is the most actionable cognitive-science insight in the documentary.
