Every human instinct screams to unbuckle and swim upward when a helicopter inverts in water. But unbuckling causes your buoyant body to 'right itself' — which in an inverted aircraft means floating to what is now the ceiling, away from exits, completely disoriented. The trained response (stay in seat, grab reference point, breathe, then methodically exit) contradicts every survival instinct.

This is a powerful model for high-stakes decision-making: the instinctive response to a crisis (sell everything in a crash, ship a quick fix under pressure, flee a difficult conversation) often makes the situation worse. The correct response requires pre-trained mental models that override instinct. You cannot reason your way to the counter-intuitive answer in the moment — you need the 'rodeo grip' equivalent practiced before the crisis hits. Vision drops to near zero in crisis conditions (literally underwater, metaphorically in fog of war), making instinct even more dangerous and training even more critical.
