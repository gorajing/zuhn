Paine's examples of Japanese inter-service dysfunction are almost comic if they weren't catastrophic. After 1906, the army and navy maintained completely separate, secret war plans — each assuming the other would do critical things for them. The Navy started the southern advance in 1942 without telling the Army the outer perimeter of airfields wasn't complete; the Army only learned on August 17, 1942 when a US landing at Guadalcanal exposed an incomplete Japanese airfield. The Navy hid the loss of four aircraft carriers at Midway from the Army for three to four months — in the middle of the war.

These aren't the failures of stupid or disloyal people. They are the predictable output of a culture where in-group/out-group lines are drawn so finely that other units, let alone sister services, are genuine outsiders. You sign up together, train together, fight together, die together — and never share hard-won lessons with the next cohort. Japanese pilots didn't rotate between combat and training the way US pilots did, so tactical learning didn't propagate. 'Hot washes' (post-operation blameless reviews) that are standard in Western militaries are culturally difficult because face-loss is intolerable.

The principle ports directly to any organization. When teams optimize for in-group loyalty over mission, the most expensive information — failures, surprises, near-misses — stops flowing at team boundaries. The fix is not 'try harder to communicate.' It is structurally rewarding cross-team information sharing, normalizing failure disclosure, and making mission identity stronger than sub-team identity. Without that, you can hire brilliant people and still field an army that can't coordinate an airfield with its own navy.
