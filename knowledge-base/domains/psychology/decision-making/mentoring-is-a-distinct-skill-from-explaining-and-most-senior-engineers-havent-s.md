Self-reflection from an 8-year senior engineer at Atlassian: 'I find it easy to help people to point out areas where they need understanding and to deliver that understanding to them, to break down complex things into simple terms so that they can build a mental model of the system. I have that ability. I'm quite good at that. But mentoring is distinct from that.' The specific gap: when mentoring an intern, the speaker found it hard to calibrate 'how much time to give the mentee, and what that time consists of — I didn't want to give them answers to problems, but I didn't want them to get so stuck that they become frustrated.' Explaining is a one-way transfer (the explainer's job is to make the concept clear); mentoring is a longitudinal optimization (the mentor's job is to maximize learning over weeks, which often means withholding answers in moments where giving them would feel kinder). Many strong technical communicators are weak mentors precisely because their reflex is to make things clear — but in a mentoring context, premature clarity can short-circuit the struggle that produces deep learning. The implication for any senior engineer being asked to mentor: don't assume your explanation skill transfers. Mentoring requires deliberate practice on a different axis (when to NOT explain) that explanation skill doesn't develop. The corollary for AI: agents that always answer perfectly are excellent explainers but terrible mentors — and if we eventually want AI to teach, we need to design in deliberate withholding.
