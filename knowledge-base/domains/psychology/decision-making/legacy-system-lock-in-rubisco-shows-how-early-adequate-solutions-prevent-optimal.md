RuBisCo evolved when atmospheric CO2 was abundant and O2 was scarce, making its poor specificity acceptable. As plants changed the atmosphere by producing oxygen, RuBisCo began grabbing O2 instead of CO2 roughly half the time — creating toxic byproducts. Yet plants doubled down, producing ~40 billion tons of the enzyme rather than evolving a better one.

This maps directly to technology and organizational decisions: early architectural choices made under different conditions become load-bearing infrastructure. The cost of compensating for the original choice (plants evolved entire metabolic pathways to handle phosphoglycolate) compounds over time but remains cheaper than replacement. The lesson is that your earliest 'good enough' decisions deserve disproportionate scrutiny, because you'll likely be patching around them forever rather than replacing them.
