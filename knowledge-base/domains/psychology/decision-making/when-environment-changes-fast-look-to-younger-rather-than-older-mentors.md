Henrich's cultural-evolutionary models show that the ratio of cultural learning vs. individual learning vs. genetic encoding is determined by the rate at which the environment changes. Too slow: encode it genetically. Too fast: individual learning wins because the previous generation knows nothing applicable. In between: copy from prestigious elders.

The key insight Henrich raises in the interview: as cultural change speeds up, the value of older members of the previous generation declines, because the world they adapted to is increasingly distant from the world you need to adapt to. 'Optimally, you would look less far back or you would look to relatively younger individuals.'

This is Henrich directly contradicting our instinct to automatically credential older mentors. In hunter-gatherer societies, the best hunters are 36-40 because the environment is stable and their cumulative knowledge outweighs their physical decline. But in a fast-moving field (say, LLMs or crypto or any tech still being defined), a 25-year-old who entered post-paradigm-shift may have more relevant models than a 55-year-old whose intuitions were shaped by an obsolete regime. Use this as an explicit test: ask how fast your domain is changing, and recalibrate who you're treating as authoritative.
