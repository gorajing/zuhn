Chris Anokute's framing: 'The only person who isn't delusional is the customer. So if I like something, then I'm right. If I'm consuming it, then I have the right to. And if I'm buying it, that is the result.' The Disa data point: 9,000 respondents across 9 countries; 87% reported they would be happy streaming AI music as long as the music is good. The motivation isn't 'AI music is amazing' — it's 'we're sick and tired of the DSP, Spotify, Apple programming and telling us what to listen to.' Consumers want quality and they want curation independence. AI music delivers both at lower cost than human-curated DSP playlists. The structural argument: in any market, the consumer's choice is the ground truth. Industries that try to overrule consumer preference (Hollywood vs streaming in 2010, taxi industry vs Uber, recording industry vs MP3, recording industry vs Spotify, now recording industry vs AI music) consistently lose. The pattern: established industry argues 'consumers can't be trusted to decide, our industry knows what's good,' consumers pick the new option anyway, established industry collapses or adapts. The corollary for the music industry: stop debating AI music ethics in industry forums. The debate is over — consumers have already voted. The relevant questions are: (a) how does the industry adapt its compensation models, (b) how does the industry create AI music professionally rather than ceding to outsider platforms, (c) how does the industry use AI to improve quality of human-created music. The corollary for any industry watching AI disruption: when consumers vote with their wallets/attention, the debate is over. Industry sentiment lags consumer preference by years; the lag IS the disruption window. Companies that recognize and accept consumer preference faster outperform those who try to litigate / lobby / shame consumers out of their preference. The corollary for product builders generally: if you're confused about what consumers want, run a Disa-style direct survey across diverse markets. The answers are usually clearer than the industry-discourse-distorted views. The consumer's relationship to your product is the only truth that ultimately matters.
