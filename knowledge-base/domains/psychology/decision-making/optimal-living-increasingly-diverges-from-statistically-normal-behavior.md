Graham draws a sharp distinction between two meanings of 'normal': the statistical average of what everyone does, and the optimal operating range for human wellbeing. These two definitions are pulling apart as addictive technologies become more prevalent and more people succumb.

This has practical implications for decision-making. Using social proof ('everyone does it') as a heuristic becomes actively dangerous when the population baseline includes widespread addictive behavior. The person trying to live well will increasingly seem abstemious or eccentric relative to peers. This reframes contrarianism not as rebellion but as a survival strategy — you'll increasingly be defined by what you say no to, not what you say yes to.
