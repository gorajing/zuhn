Smith connects widespread 90s/2000s nostalgia to the collapse of forward-looking confidence. Those decades weren't free of problems, but they had a quality the present lacks: legibility. People felt they could extrapolate their trajectory. The future was imaginable, even if imperfect.

This reframes nostalgia from a harmless cultural quirk to a diagnostic signal. When a society becomes intensely nostalgic, it may indicate not that conditions have worsened but that the future has become unreadable. For decision-makers, this means nostalgia-driven preferences (in politics, product design, culture) are symptoms of uncertainty-avoidance rather than genuine preference for past conditions. Addressing the underlying uncertainty is more productive than either indulging or dismissing the nostalgia.
