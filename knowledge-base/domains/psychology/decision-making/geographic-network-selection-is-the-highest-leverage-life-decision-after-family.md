The article presents converging evidence that geographic location is the single most actionable network variable. Cities exhibit nonlinear scaling — as population grows, citizens get 15% more income, ideas, and stimulation while infrastructure costs 15% less per capita. Physical proximity drives network formation frequency, and most people end up working and living near their college geography.

The Hurricane Katrina natural experiment is particularly compelling: displaced New Orleans residents who moved to growing cities like Houston saw their standard of living rise significantly — a move they never would have made voluntarily due to network gravity. The VC investor Saar Gur's reflection that 'staying in SF was the most impactful decision — everything else was noise' crystallizes the principle. Each city has a distinct aspiration signature (NYC = earn, SF = create, DC = connect) that shapes your network's expectations of you. The key insight: every time you move, you reset your network 'bank account,' so commit to a geography and compound.
