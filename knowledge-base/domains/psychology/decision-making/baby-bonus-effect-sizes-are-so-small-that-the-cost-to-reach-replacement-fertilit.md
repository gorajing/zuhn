South Korea's substantial baby bonus (~$700/month) reduced fertility decline by only 4.7% — nowhere near enough to offset the ongoing collapse. Lyman Stone's analysis suggests restoring US fertility to replacement would require $5,300-$23,000 per child per year on top of existing benefits, sums that are political non-starters. Hungary tried generous payments with no lasting effect. China has been trying economic support for years with no visible result.

This is a powerful case study in the limits of financial incentives for deeply social behaviors. The decision to have children is embedded in identity, lifestyle, social norms, career expectations, and relationship dynamics — all factors that money can nudge but not override. The insight generalizes: when a behavior is driven primarily by social and psychological factors, direct financial incentives will show positive but insufficient effect sizes, creating a trap where policymakers can point to 'evidence-based' programs that technically work while being orders of magnitude too small to solve the actual problem.
