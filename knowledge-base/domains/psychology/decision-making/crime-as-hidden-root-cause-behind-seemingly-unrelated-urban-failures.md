The article builds a causal chain: high crime → people flee cities for suburbs → resistance to transit stations (criminals ride trains) → NIMBYism blocks dense housing (crime fears) → car-dependent sprawl → lack of walkable urbanism. Each of these is typically analyzed as a separate policy failure, but they share a common root cause. When BART installed fare gates, crime dropped 54% — demonstrating that the transit-crime link is real, not imagined.

This is a powerful example of root-cause analysis revealing that multiple seemingly independent problems share a single upstream driver. The implication is that solving the crime problem would produce cascading improvements across housing, transit, and urban quality of life — far more efficiently than addressing each downstream symptom separately. It also explains why European and Asian cities feel so different: what Americans perceive as 'better urbanism' is largely just 'less crime'.
