China's fertility rate dropped from ~6 to ~2.7 between the 1960s and 1979, before the one-child policy was implemented. The policy then 'accelerated' the decline to below replacement. Yet the dominant narrative credits the policy itself with China's demographic transformation. This is a textbook case of confusing correlation with causation — the policy rode an existing wave and claimed credit for the tide.

This pattern recurs across policy domains: governments implement interventions during existing trends and then attribute the trend's continuation to their intervention. The cognitive mechanism is powerful because the timeline appears to confirm causation (policy enacted → outcome observed). Recognizing this bias is critical for evaluating any claimed policy success — the key question is always 'what was the trajectory before the intervention?' This applies equally to economic policy, public health interventions, and corporate strategy claims.
