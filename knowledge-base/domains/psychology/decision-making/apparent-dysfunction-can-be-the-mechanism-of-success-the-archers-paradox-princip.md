The archer's paradox reveals that an arrow must bend and oscillate around the bow's riser to hit the target — if the arrow were perfectly rigid, it would deflect off the bow and miss. This maps directly to decision-making: processes that look chaotic or wrong in their intermediate states (a startup pivoting repeatedly, a negotiation conceding early ground, a learner making 'wrong' attempts) may be functioning correctly. The instinct to intervene and 'straighten out' apparent dysfunction can be destructive when the flex IS the mechanism. The key diagnostic is whether the apparent dysfunction converges on the intended outcome over time — the arrow's oscillations dampen and it flies true, just as productive chaos resolves into results.
