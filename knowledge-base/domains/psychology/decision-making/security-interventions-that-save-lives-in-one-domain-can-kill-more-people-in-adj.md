Flying is empirically hundreds to thousands of times safer than driving per mile traveled. When post-9/11 security made flying slower and more invasive, economists measured a 6% decline in air travel as passengers substituted driving for short-haul flights. In 2002 alone, this modal shift resulted in over 500 additional highway fatalities—people who died because security friction pushed them toward a statistically far more dangerous transportation mode. Meanwhile, the TSA's estimated cost per life saved is $667 million. This is a textbook case of intervention bias: the visible threat (terrorism) triggers a costly response, while the invisible cost (driving deaths) goes unmeasured and unattributed. The pattern applies broadly—any friction added to a safe system can push users toward less safe alternatives, and the net effect on safety can be negative even when the intervention 'works' on its own terms.
