Graham identifies a 'conspicuous pattern' across his life: still life painting (least prestigious painting form), Viaweb (seemed lame), Y Combinator (seemed lame), online essays (seemed like nutjob rants), and Lisp (intellectually respected but unhip) all turned out to be deeply valuable pursuits. The pattern works as a double filter.

First, it filters for genuine motivation: 'Impure motives are a big danger for the ambitious. If anything is going to lead you astray, it will be the desire to impress people.' Working on unprestigious things guarantees you're not chasing status. Second, it signals untapped opportunity: things that are genuinely valuable but currently unprestigious are underexplored precisely because status-seekers avoid them. This creates a systematic market inefficiency that authentically motivated people can exploit. The key insight is that prestige is a lagging indicator — by the time something is prestigious, the opportunity to do foundational work in it has passed.
