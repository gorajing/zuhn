The classic line-cutting experiment (Langer et al.) showed that asking 'Can I cut because I'm late for class?' and 'Can I cut because I have a dog?' produced nearly identical compliance rates, both far above 'Can I cut?' with no reason. The cognitive shortcut is that humans parse the structure 'request + because + X' as justified, without evaluating X. The practical implication for marketing and CTAs: never ask without a reason, even a weak one. Anniversary sales, holiday promotions, personal milestones, random events — any framing that provides a 'because' outperforms bare CTAs. This is additive to stronger reasons (scarcity, urgency, genuine capacity constraints), not a substitute — but when stronger reasons aren't available, use weaker ones rather than none.
