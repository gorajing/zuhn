RuBisCo evolved in a low-oxygen atmosphere where its inability to distinguish CO2 from O2 didn't matter. When oxygen levels rose, RuBisCo began grabbing oxygen half the time, creating toxic byproducts. Rather than evolving a better enzyme, plants doubled down — producing staggering quantities of RuBisCo (estimated 40 billion tons on Earth at any time) and developing secondary pathways to detoxify the waste.

This mirrors how organizations handle legacy technical debt or early strategic commitments. Once a system is deeply integrated, the perceived cost of replacement always exceeds the cost of adding another compensating layer. Teams hire more people to work around bad tooling, companies build middleware to bridge incompatible systems, and startups add features to mask architectural flaws. The compounding cost of compensation is invisible because each individual patch seems cheap — just as each additional ton of RuBisCo costs less than redesigning carbon fixation from scratch.

The lesson is that path dependence doesn't feel like a trap. It feels like pragmatism. Recognizing when you're producing your own 'RuBisCo' — compensating for a fundamental flaw with scale — requires stepping outside the incremental logic that makes each patch seem reasonable.
