RuBisCo evolved in an atmosphere with abundant CO2 and almost no oxygen. In that environment, it worked fine — it didn't need to distinguish between the two gases because only one was present. But as photosynthetic organisms succeeded and flooded the atmosphere with oxygen, RuBisCo's inability to discriminate became a critical flaw, causing it to grab oxygen half the time and produce toxic phosphoglycolate.

The mechanism never changed; the environment did. This is a powerful mental model for decision-making: strategies that work brilliantly in one context can become actively destructive in another without any internal modification. Founders, investors, and leaders should regularly audit whether their core assumptions still match current conditions, because success itself can shift the environment that made a strategy viable.
