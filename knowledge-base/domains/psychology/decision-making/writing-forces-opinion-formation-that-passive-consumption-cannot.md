Chamath explicitly notes he writes quick 1-2 page essays on topics that seem important specifically to 'develop a quick point of view and understand the nuances a little bit better.' This is writing as a thinking tool, not as a publishing exercise. The essay is for him, not for the audience.

This aligns with research on the generation effect in cognitive science—actively producing information creates stronger memory traces and deeper understanding than passively consuming it. But the insight goes beyond memory: writing forces you to confront what you actually believe versus what you've merely been exposed to. You cannot write a coherent essay on a topic without taking a position, and taking a position exposes the gaps in your reasoning in a way that nodding along to someone else's argument never does. The specific format (1-2 pages, quick) matters too—it's short enough to be sustainable but long enough to require real synthesis.
