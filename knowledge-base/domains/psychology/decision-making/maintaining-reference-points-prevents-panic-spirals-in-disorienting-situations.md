In the helicopter egress training, Marines who unbuckled and left their seats immediately lost all spatial orientation — described as 'a loose sock in the dryer.' The seat, despite being attached to a sinking inverted helicopter, served as the critical reference frame. From the seat, you know your position relative to exits. Without it, chairs hang from ceilings and bubbles go the wrong direction.

This maps directly to decision-making under pressure in any domain: when everything inverts (market crash, project crisis, organizational upheaval), the instinct is to abandon your position and scramble. But maintaining your anchor — your known framework, your seat — lets you think sequentially toward the exit. The instructor's key insight: 'If you're in a seat you kind of know where you're at.' Abandoning your reference frame for perceived safety creates a worse problem than the original crisis.
