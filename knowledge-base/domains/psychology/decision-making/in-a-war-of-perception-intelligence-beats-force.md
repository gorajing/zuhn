When the U-2 first flew over the Soviet Union on July 4, 1956, the Soviets tracked it on radar from takeoff — contrary to US assumptions. But they said nothing for four years, because admitting they could see it would require also admitting they could not shoot it down, which would be a bigger loss of face than quietly letting the US photograph their installations. Meanwhile the US got near-perfect intelligence on Soviet nuclear capability, which let both sides avoid miscalculation and never actually fight.

The pattern generalizes to any competitive game where the players prefer stability over a decisive outcome: boardroom negotiations, cartel dynamics, diplomatic posturing, even enterprise sales cycles with multiple vendors. The question to ask is not 'who has the bigger weapon?' but 'who has better information about the other side's actual state, and what is the cost of revealing what you know?' Sometimes you want the other side to know you can see them (deterrence — the Polaris nuclear submarines); sometimes you want them to not know (gathering intelligence — the U-2). The strategic art is knowing which regime you are in.

The practical translation: in any high-stakes competitive situation, invest disproportionately in information gathering and in controlling what the other side knows about what you know. Most actors over-invest in capability and under-invest in intelligence and signaling, because capability is visible and intelligence is not.
