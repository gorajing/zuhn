Graham identifies a structural paradox in evaluating novel ideas: for an idea to be both good and novel, it almost certainly has to seem bad to most people. If it seemed obviously good, someone would already be doing it. This means the base rate of 'this seems like a bad idea' feedback is extremely high for both actually-bad ideas and potentially-great ones, making rejection signal nearly useless for distinguishing the two.

This has implications far beyond fundraising. Any evaluation system — investors, hiring committees, grant reviewers, publishers — that relies on consensus approval will systematically filter out the most innovative options. The best evaluators must be 'intellectual contrarians' who can distinguish 'seems wrong and is wrong' from 'seems wrong because it's too early for most people to see it.' Graham notes that angels (often former founders) are better at this than VCs (mostly finance people), suggesting domain expertise in building things is more valuable than pattern-matching on business metrics.
