Graham invokes Silicon Valley's principle that 'you make what you measure' to argue that economic inequality is a dangerously misleading metric. The mechanism is identical to Goodhart's Law: once a proxy becomes the target, it ceases to be a good proxy. A university president who targets graduation rates will find ways to graduate more students — easier classes, lower standards — without improving actual education. Similarly, targeting economic inequality as a number will produce policies that reduce the Gini coefficient without addressing the specific problems (poverty, lack of social mobility, rent-seeking) that make inequality harmful. The actionable insight is to always decompose aggregate metrics into their causal components before optimizing. In any domain — business, policy, personal goals — ask whether the metric you're targeting is the actual outcome you want, or a convenient aggregate that bundles good and bad causes together.
