Chen attributes much of OpenAI's 'alpha' to taking a lot of high-risk bets, which is what has kept them at the frontier consistently. The corollary is that many bets won't pan out, and the discipline is twofold: first, don't delude yourself into clinging to a bet that isn't working — make the call to disconnect when a once-promising idea proves less important than thought; second, judge a risk-taking researcher on expectation, not on consecutive misses. He explicitly invokes a trading lens: a person on the 'riskier frontier' only has to justify their bets 'once in a while' for the math to work — they just need to add value in expectation.

The guardrail that separates this from reckless gambling is soundness: ideas can be ambitious but must still be sound. Expected-value tolerance is not a license for arbitrary bets; it's tolerance for high-variance sound bets. Chen has seen researchers go bet-after-bet without a hit and then land a 'mega hit' right at the brink of frustration — which only works because the underlying ideas were sound, not merely bold.

Failed bets are also not pure loss: even when a technique doesn't prove out, the writeup is valuable because the failed idea is usually a 'natural' one others would independently try, so documenting it saves the next person from repeating it. This is a cross-domain principle linking trading, venture investing, startups, and research — manage a portfolio of sound high-variance bets on expectation, and treat documented failures as compounding institutional knowledge.
