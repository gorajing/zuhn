Paine closes the lecture with a compact decision heuristic that generalizes far beyond geopolitics: before you commit — 'before you leave the parking lot' — map every player's primary objective, primary adversary, and primary theater of interest, and check whether the alignments actually support the outcome you want. Then form your hypotheses about how events will play out. Then, critically, reassess early and often to see if your assumptions still hold. 'Some people get really hung up about being wrong. Don't worry. Reassessing is a sign of strength. It's like, I got more information, I've changed my mind. Good thing. Don't double down on bad information.'

The second half of the heuristic is about prioritization under finite resources: 'Part of good strategy is recognizing some problems it's not feasible to solve. And then we all have scarce resources. You can't do everything. Focus on those things where you think you can solve.' The US spent decades trying to reconcile India and Pakistan on the false assumption that they could be cajoled into settling their differences — an unsolvable problem given the realities of Partition and primary-enemy alignment. That effort burned American credibility with both sides and produced nothing.

The generalizable insight: strategic failure usually comes from two places — refusing to update when the facts change, and spending resources on problems that were never solvable in the first place. Both are ego-driven, and both are avoidable if you treat reassessment as a sign of clear thinking rather than an admission of failure.
