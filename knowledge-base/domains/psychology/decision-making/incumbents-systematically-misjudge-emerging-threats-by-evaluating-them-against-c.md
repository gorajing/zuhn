Intel's CEO made his decision using a static snapshot: mobile chip margins in 2005 couldn't justify the R&D investment, and the iPhone seemed unlikely to succeed. This is the classic incumbent error Christensen identified — evaluating a disruptive technology against the current market structure rather than its performance trajectory. The same bias caused the broader industry to dismiss Apple's 2013 claim of 'desktop-class architecture' in a phone chip as mere marketing. But the performance trajectory chart told the real story: Apple's chips were improving at 300% per five years versus Intel's 28%. Anyone who plotted the trajectory could see the crossover coming. The cognitive trap is that incumbents anchor to the present competitive landscape (where they're dominant) rather than extrapolating the rate of change (where the disruptor is faster). This pattern appears in every disruption cycle — the technology that looks 'too weak today' is the one improving fastest.
