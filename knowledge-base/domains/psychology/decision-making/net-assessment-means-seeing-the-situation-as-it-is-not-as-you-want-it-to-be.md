Paine highlights a group of American Foreign Service officers in 1940s China — many of them the children of missionaries who spoke fluent Chinese and understood the countryside — who told Washington that Chiang Kai-shek could not win. Their assessment rested on concrete observations: Chiang was hated by the peasantry because he was drafting them into his armies, while the Communists were giving them land and education. The peasantry, predictably, supported the side giving them something. This was 'a net assessment of not what you want it to be but an accurate one,' and it was correct.

The price of being right was the McCarthy purges: these officers lost their jobs, their careers, and years of their lives before being exonerated decades later. The pattern — domain experts with ground truth being punished for delivering unwelcome accurate forecasts — is a recurring failure mode in institutions facing ideologically loaded decisions. The practical implication is twofold: organizations should actively protect the people who deliver inconvenient net assessments, and individual decision-makers should explicitly separate the question 'what do I want to be true?' from 'what is actually the case?' before committing resources. Wishful framing at the assessment stage dooms every downstream decision.
