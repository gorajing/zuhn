The core tension is between consequentialist ethics (does collaboration produce better outcomes?) and deontological concerns (does participation in a bad system corrupt you?). Alexander argues that voter behavior is so disconnected from policy quality that 'letting things get worse to create voter backlash' rarely works — voters are uninformed, polarized, and forgetful about specific policy decisions.

This means the real-world cost of non-engagement is high (bad policy happens) while the political benefit (voter anger leading to change) is negligible. The exception might be highly visible, personally-felt issues like gas prices near election time. For most policy domains — vaccines, regulatory decisions, scientific funding — the consequentialist math strongly favors insider engagement over principled abstention.
