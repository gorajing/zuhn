The recursive desire analysis shows that ultimate self-causation is impossible — you always bottom out at desires you didn't choose. Yet we don't typically think this eliminates moral responsibility. Graham's framework implies that responsibility is grounded in the layers of agency you do have: whether the action flowed from your deliberation, values, and second-order endorsements, not whether those were constructed ex nihilo.

For practical decision-making, this clarifies when self-blame is appropriate and when it is not. You are accountable for choices within your accessible agency — acting on what you value, deliberating carefully — but not for the brute desires that sit below the accessible levels. Excessive self-blame often involves holding yourself accountable for things at the inaccessible levels. Recognizing this boundary is a form of epistemic hygiene about one's own psychology.
