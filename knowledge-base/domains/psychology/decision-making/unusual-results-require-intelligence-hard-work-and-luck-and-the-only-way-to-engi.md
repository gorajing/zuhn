Asked whether he and Buffett could rebuild Berkshire today starting at 30, Munger says no — not because they aren't capable, but because luck is load-bearing and cannot be reproduced on demand. 'Everybody that has an unusually good result, almost everybody has three things. They're very intelligent, they worked very hard, and they were very lucky. It takes all three to get on this list of the super successful.'

The interesting part is his framing of how to 'arrange' for luck: 'You can start early and keep trying a long time and maybe you'll get one or two.' Luck is not controllable, but duration of exposure to luck is. The longer you remain in the game trying sensible things, the more dice rolls you get. This reframes longevity and staying power as the only legitimate strategies for engineering exceptional outcomes.

The practical consequence is that short-horizon thinking is doubly punishing — not only does it prevent compounding, it also denies you the luck dice rolls that exceptional outcomes require. The asymmetry is that the downside of staying in the game is modest (slow compounding) and the upside is the only realistic path to the tail. This is also why Munger emphasizes 'you only have to get rich once' — the lucky break doesn't have to come often, but you have to still be in the game when it arrives.
