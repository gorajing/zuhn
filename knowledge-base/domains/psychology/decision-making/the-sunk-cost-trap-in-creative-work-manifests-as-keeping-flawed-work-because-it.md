Graham reports cutting a 17-paragraph subtree from this very essay, plus countless shorter ones, and advocates being quick to cut in general. The sunk cost fallacy takes a specific form in creative work: you keep a flawed section not because of pure cost-already-invested reasoning, but because it contains a few genuinely good fragments embedded in a broken structure. This makes it feel wasteful to discard.

The same dynamic plays out in software (keeping overcomplicated code because some parts are clever) and painting (preserving a section because one element works). The antidote is recognizing that the good fragments can usually be recovered — reattached elsewhere, boiled down to a footnote, or spun off independently — but only if you're willing to kill the flawed whole first. Speed of cutting correlates with output quality because every minute spent polishing a wrong direction is a minute not spent exploring better branches.
