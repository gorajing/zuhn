Leeson's initial losses were modest — his team made bad bets during market volatility. At that stage, disclosure would have cost him his job but nothing more. Instead, he chose concealment, creating the Five Eights account. This first concealment created a new problem: margin payments on hidden losing trades required external funding, which required fabricating fake clients, which required falsifying records.

The trajectory was exponential: £4M → £94M → £160M → £330M → £830M. At each stage, the cost of disclosure grew faster than the losses themselves because disclosure now meant not just admitting losses but admitting fraud. The psychological trap is that the concealment itself becomes the thing being protected — not the original position. Leeson wasn't trying to save his trades; he was trying to save his lie.

This pattern appears in corporate scandals, personal finance, and relationship failures alike. The actionable insight is that the cost of transparency is always lowest at the moment of the original error. Every day of concealment is a compounding interest rate on eventual damage.
