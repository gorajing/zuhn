Graham's key realization was that the freedom he lost by having kids — spontaneous international travel, unstructured time — was freedom he rarely actually used. He paid for that optionality in loneliness but almost never converted it into action. This is a profound insight about how humans systematically overvalue uncommitted flexibility.

This applies broadly to commitment decisions: choosing a company over freelancing, picking a technology stack, committing to a relationship. The theoretical freedom of keeping options open feels valuable but carries real costs (loneliness, indecision, lack of depth), while the options themselves often go unexercised. Counting actual happy moments rather than potential happiness reveals that commitment frequently wins.
