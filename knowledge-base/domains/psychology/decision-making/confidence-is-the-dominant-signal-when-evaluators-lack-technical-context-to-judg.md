Paul Graham observes that between brief time slots and non-technical backgrounds, many investors at Demo Day simply cannot evaluate the technical merits of what founders have built. In this information vacuum, the founder's own confidence becomes probably the single biggest piece of evidence. This isn't about faking enthusiasm—saying 'we're passionate' or 'our product is great' gets you written off as a bullshitter.

The insight generalizes beyond pitching: in any domain where evaluators face information asymmetry (job interviews, grant reviews, editorial pitches), demonstrated conviction through behavior—not stated conviction through words—becomes the proxy for quality. The corollary is that nervousness and apology are disproportionately costly, not because they're bad manners, but because they're actively interpreted as evidence that even you don't believe in your work.
