Berg's maintenance strategy: people relapse when stressed, fatigued, or hungry — exactly the conditions where willpower fails. The intervention isn't more discipline; it's removing the option to fail. Purge junk food from the fridge and pantry. Replace with healthy alternatives. Optionally create keto-version analogues of comfort foods (instead of depriving, substitute). Now relapse requires actively going to a store, which raises the activation energy enough that the default behavior is the healthy one. This is the standard 'stack the odds' framing from behavior design.

Application: this pattern generalizes to any habit — exercise (lay out clothes the night before), focus work (block sites at the router level, not just browser), savings (auto-transfer on payday), AI deployment ('AI by default' in workflows that benefit, blocked in workflows that don't). For Memric specifically: same logic for customer onboarding — make the right behavior (uploading new IC memos, reviewing principle retirement events) the default zero-friction action, not something that requires deliberate decision.
