John Archibald Wheeler proposed in the 1970s a variant of the double-slit experiment: delay the decision of whether to install the which-slit detector until AFTER the particle has already passed through. The intuition: by then the particle has 'already' chosen wave or particle behavior, so the late decision shouldn't matter. The 2007 French experiment (Institute d'Optique) ran this with the required precision. Result: when researchers turned on the detector AFTER the particle passed, it retroactively behaved as a particle during passage. When they chose not to observe, it retroactively behaved as a wave. The present detection reached back in time. There's no locally-real mechanism that accounts for this. The implication: the past is not a fixed record waiting to be discovered — at the quantum level, it's partially constructed by present measurement. The simulation-hypothesis interpretation: a game engine doesn't need to maintain history; it computes whatever past is consistent with the current observation when needed. The practical extrapolation for human cognition: even at macroscopic scales, memory and historical record are partly constructed at the moment of recall — both individually (memory is reconstructive, not retrieval) and socially (what 'really happened' in a contested event depends partly on present framing). This isn't to claim macroscopic time isn't real, but to suggest the model 'fixed past, evolving present, unknown future' is wrong at the deepest level, and probably wrong in subtler ways at human scales too. The decision-making implication: don't treat your interpretation of past events as ground truth — your present measurement (current emotional state, current narrative needs, current information) is partly constructing what you 'remember.'
