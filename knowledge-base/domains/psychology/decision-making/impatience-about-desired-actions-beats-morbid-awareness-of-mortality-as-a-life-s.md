The traditional response to life's shortness is memento mori — constant awareness of death. Graham argues this is the wrong end of the problem. Morbid death-awareness creates anxiety without clear direction, and most people find it unsustainable as a daily practice.

The alternative is to cultivate impatience specifically about the things you most want to do. Don't wait to write the book, climb the mountain, or visit your mother. This reframes mortality from a background dread into a forward-driving force. The mechanism is different: instead of being pushed by fear of death, you're pulled by urgency toward what matters. The practical test is simple — you don't need to constantly remind yourself why you shouldn't wait. Just don't wait. This is particularly important for relationships, where the 'windows' close permanently when people die.
