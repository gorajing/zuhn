    Jean-Baptiste Kempf (VLC/FFmpeg president): 'The important is — is your code
    good? Is your code great? We care about excellent code. We don't care who
    you are. We have no idea to check, we cannot check. Maybe you're a dog, I
    don't care. I don't care where you come from. I need to look at your code.
    People come to the community, send patches, and they get rejected, and they
    don't like it: oh yeah, but I'm an engineer at this very large company in
    Italy, in Germany, in the US. We don't care. We care about the quality of
    your code because this is what defines our community.' The empirical result:
    'Teenagers have written more assembly in FFmpeg than Google engineers.'
    Felix started contributing to VLC at 16 and now does everything on Mac/iOS.
    Edward Wong was 14 in Google Summer of Code. Ruikai Peng was 16 finding and
    fixing security issues that senior researchers missed. The structural
    argument for why anti-credentialism beats credentialism: (1) Credentials are
    correlated with skill but full of noise — many credentialed people aren't
    actually good. (2) Lack-of-credentials is much weaker negative signal — many
    uncredentialed people are exceptional. (3) The actual signal (code quality)
    is directly observable and trustworthy. So the credential filter introduces
    both false positives (admitting mediocre credentialed engineers) and false
    negatives (rejecting brilliant teenagers). Pure code-quality filter avoids
    both. The corollary for hiring in any technical field: the most informative
    signal is a work sample. Resumes, LinkedIn, and credentials are higher-noise
    proxies. Companies that prioritize work samples (live coding, take-home
    projects, contribution review) systematically outperform those that
    prioritize credentials. The corollary for individuals building careers:
    focus on producing demonstrably excellent work in PUBLIC venues where
    credentials don't gate access. Open source contributions, public writing,
    public artifacts — these are credential-free meritocracies. Anyone with
    skill and dedication can win. The corollary for educational systems:
    degree-conferring institutions provide credentials, not skills. The skill
    development can happen anywhere with disciplined practice and feedback. The
    institutions that genuinely develop skill (vs filter for it) are rare; the
    institutions that genuinely teach (vs grade) are rarer still. The corollary
    for the broader software industry: the FFmpeg/VLC model — small team of
    exceptional contributors maintaining world-class software — is the
    structural template for AI-amplified small teams. The pattern of 'ten
    exceptional people serving billions of users' becomes increasingly viable as
    AI tools amplify individual productivity. The credentialed-large-team model
    that dominated 2000-2024 will lose share to the anti-credentialed-small-team
    model in 2025-2030.
stance: >-
  The reason FFmpeg and VLC produce world-class software while running on ~5-15
  core maintainers and volunteer contributors is a single ruthless filter: 'we
  don't care who you are, where you come from, what your credentials are — we
  look at your code'; this anti-credentialist culture allows 16-year-olds to
  contribute world-class assembly while filtering out senior engineers from
  major corporations who can't match the bar, and the result is software that
  outperforms billion-dollar corporate teams.
