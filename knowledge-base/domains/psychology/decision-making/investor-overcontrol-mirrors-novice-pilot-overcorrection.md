Graham uses the novice pilot analogy to argue that investors systematically overcontrol portfolio companies. The aircraft overcorrection pattern—where vigorous corrections cause oscillation instead of smooth convergence—maps directly to how investor interventions can create more stress and instability than the problems they aim to solve.

This is a cross-domain application of control theory: the optimal controller applies minimal correction proportional to error magnitude. When the controller (investor) has imperfect information about the system (startup), aggressive corrections amplify noise rather than reducing it. The implication is that reducing investor power isn't just founder-friendly—it's system-optimal. People with less knowledge should have less control, and founders invariably understand their companies better than investors do.
