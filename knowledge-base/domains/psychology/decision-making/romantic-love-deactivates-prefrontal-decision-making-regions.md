Helen Fisher's fMRI research shows that intense romantic love activates the oldest brain regions linked with drive, craving, and motivation, while simultaneously shutting down more recently evolved cognitive regions in the prefrontal cortex responsible for decision-making and planning ahead. This explains why people in love make objectively irrational choices — falling for someone married, across the world, or from incompatible backgrounds — and genuinely believe 'we'll work it out.'

This has direct implications beyond romance: any high-stakes decision made during intense infatuation should be treated with the same skepticism as a decision made while intoxicated. Fisher's practical advice is to spend extended time with a partner before major commitments, allowing the neurochemical intensity to subside so prefrontal decision-making can re-engage. The same principle applies to any dopamine-driven obsession — startup ideas, investments, new technologies — where initial excitement may override rational evaluation.
