Kotkin is emphatic that Stalin wasn't initially power-hungry — he was a talented seminary student who gave up a promising life to fight what were genuinely severe tsarist injustices. He spent 20 years penniless, jobless, in and out of Siberian exile. The tsarist claims the revolutionaries fought against were legitimate. And yet, what Stalin built was 'much more unjust than the one he was fighting against.'

The same pattern runs through Kotkin's analysis of the Chinese Cultural Revolution, the French Revolution, and the failed constitutional revolutions in Mexico, Iran, Portugal, and Russia between 1905-1926. Revolutionary methods and the regimes that grow from them tend to be worse than the problem being addressed. The successful exceptions (West Germany, Japan, Taiwan, South Korea) all involved either foreign occupation or authoritarian industrialization that later transitioned peacefully — not revolution.

The implication is that the bar for supporting revolution should be extraordinarily high, and that 'evolutionary' solutions — even slow, unsatisfying ones under unjust regimes — are usually the better bet when available. This generalizes beyond politics: in organizations, product strategy, and even personal life, destroying an imperfect working system in the name of pure principles often produces something worse than what you started with.
