Dutton's framing challenges the assumption that psychopathic traits are purely destructive. In the footbridge scenario, the psychopath makes the objectively utilitarian choice (save five, lose one) without hesitation, while normal people's emotional circuits create paralysis or refusal — resulting in five deaths instead of one.

This has practical implications beyond thought experiments. Surgery, emergency triage, military command, and crisis negotiation all present personal moral dilemmas where emotional interference degrades decision quality. The insight isn't that psychopathy is desirable, but that the specific trait of calibrated emotional detachment — what Dutton calls cold empathy — has genuine functional value. This maps to the broader principle that emotional responses evolved for ancestral contexts and can misfire in modern scenarios where utilitarian calculation would produce better outcomes.
