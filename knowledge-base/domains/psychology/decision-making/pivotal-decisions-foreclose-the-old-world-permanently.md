Takahashi Korekiyo pulled Japan out of the Great Depression through deficit spending before Keynes was fashionable, and warned the Army that imperial conquest would be a capital sink requiring years of investment before yielding resources. In February 1936, young officers came to his house and literally hacked him apart. That was the moment the moderate faction lost.

By 1941, Prime Minister Prince Konoe knew Japan would lose a war against America and didn't want it, but War Minister Tojo didn't answer to him, and anyone who pushed back risked assassination. The 'save face and back down' option Americans imagined was already physically foreclosed.

The lesson generalizes: when analyzing any situation where moderates have already been violently purged, assume the trajectory is locked. Counterfactuals about 'if only we'd offered a face-saving exit' assume a functioning moderate faction that no longer exists. This applies to coups, purges, hostile takeovers, and any system where internal opposition has been eliminated.
