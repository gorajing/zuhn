People have multiple conflicting preference sets operating at different time horizons — a 'best self' that endorses generosity in the abstract and an 'immediate self' that resists parting with money right now. Voting for foreign aid taxes operates in far mode where the cost is distant and diffuse, while personal donation demands immediate sacrifice. This mirrors how people sincerely want to quit bad habits but only succeed when external structures (pledges, laws, social commitments) lock in the long-term preference. The implication is that dismissing tax-funded charity as 'spending other people's money' misreads what's actually a well-documented intrapersonal conflict between competing selves, not an interpersonal conflict between generous voters and unwilling taxpayers.
