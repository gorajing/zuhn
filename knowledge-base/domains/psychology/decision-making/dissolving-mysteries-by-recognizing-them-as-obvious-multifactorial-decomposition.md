Alexander describes his own intellectual journey: he initially presented the tradeoff-vs-failure duality in psychiatric conditions as a 'mysterious or at least complicated fact,' then later realized it was so obvious he felt silly for making a big deal of it. The dissolution came from generalizing: any complex outcome in a high-dimensional space will have many contributing factors, and those factors will naturally sort into 'by choice' and 'not by choice' categories. This pattern of dissolution — where a domain-specific puzzle becomes trivial once you see it as an instance of a universal pattern — is itself a powerful cognitive tool. It suggests that when something seems paradoxical in a specialized field, the first move should be asking whether you're looking at an obvious multifactorial decomposition dressed up in domain-specific jargon.
