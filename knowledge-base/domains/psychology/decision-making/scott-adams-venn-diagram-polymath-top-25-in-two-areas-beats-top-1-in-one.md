Scott Adams (Dilbert creator) articulated this rule: rather than being top-1% in a single area, find a Venn diagram where you're top-25% in one domain and top-25% in another, and you'll naturally be in the top-5-10% of the overlap because there are far fewer multi-domain practitioners. Joe applies this directly to GTM engineering: 'There are lots and lots of developers/engineers and lots of salespeople and people who understand sales tactics, but the overlap is very rare.' He himself isn't a credentialed engineer (he learned coding 2-3 years ago and can't pass a SWE interview), but combining that decent engineering with decent sales puts him in the top 5% of GTM engineers because almost nobody has both. Yash Tekriwal echoed this with the 'polymath effect' — AI dramatically shrinks the time to become decent at any single skill, which makes multi-domain skill-stacking more accessible than ever. The career implication: instead of pursuing 'world-class' in one thing (an arms race with vanishing marginal returns), pursue 'pretty good' in two complementary things — and pick complementary domains where the overlap creates genuine new value (engineering + sales, design + ML, finance + storytelling).
