RuBisCo's design was perfectly suited to early Earth's CO2-rich, oxygen-poor atmosphere. The decision to build around it wasn't flawed — the environment changed. As photosynthesis itself increased atmospheric oxygen, RuBisCo's inability to distinguish O2 from CO2 became a liability, but by then the entire plant kingdom was locked in.

This pattern appears in human decision-making: the most dangerous sunk-cost situations aren't ones where the original choice was obviously wrong, but where the choice was right for its context and the context shifted. These are harder to escape because the original reasoning still feels sound, making it psychologically difficult to acknowledge that what was rational then is irrational now. Recognizing that 'the environment changed, not just my judgment' is key to overcoming this specific form of lock-in.
