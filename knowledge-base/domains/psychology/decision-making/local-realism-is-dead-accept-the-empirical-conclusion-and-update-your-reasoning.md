Bell's 1964 insight: if hidden variables exist that determine particle outcomes in advance, the correlations between entangled particles are bounded by a specific statistical threshold (Bell's inequality). Quantum mechanics predicts stronger correlations than this threshold allows. Experimental tests starting with Clauser (1972) and refined through Aspect (1980s) and Zelinger (2017) — each closing additional loopholes — have consistently shown the Bell inequality is violated. The conclusion is no longer philosophical, it's empirical: local hidden variables do not exist. This means at least one of two assumptions you use daily must be abandoned: (1) Realism — that objects have definite properties whether or not they're measured. (2) Locality — that nothing influences anything else faster than light. Probably both. This is uncomfortable because both feel like obvious truths. The discipline this requires: when rigorous empirical evidence accumulates against a deeply intuitive assumption, you have to abandon the assumption even when the alternative feels impossible to live with. The same discipline applies to any field where intuition resists evidence — economics (efficient markets violations), medicine (everyone-knows treatments that turn out useless), and AI capabilities (models can do things we said were 'impossible' 5 years ago). The meta-rule: maintain a strict separation between 'this feels impossible' (psychological state) and 'this is impossible' (empirical claim), and update the latter on evidence even when the former resists.
