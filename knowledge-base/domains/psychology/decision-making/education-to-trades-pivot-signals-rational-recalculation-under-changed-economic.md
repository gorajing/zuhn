Since 2018, Gen Z has been pivoting away from traditional higher education: 39% of recent graduates would choose jail over their student debt. The shift toward trades and community colleges reflects a changing expected-value calculation — when tuition rises faster than medical costs, child care, and housing, and when 'the most impactful teachers are usually at community colleges,' the premium university path becomes a worse bet. Meanwhile, trade skills face less AI displacement risk and often offer higher starting wages than many degree-requiring jobs.

This represents a broader pattern in decision-making: when the environment changes dramatically enough, rational actors eventually update their priors despite strong cultural inertia. The interesting meta-insight is that Gen Z is doing this faster than Millennials did — possibly because information access lets them see the debt-to-income ratios and career outcome data that previous generations discovered only after committing. For anyone advising young people, the key is evaluating specific ROI of specific degree paths rather than defaulting to 'college is always worth it.'
