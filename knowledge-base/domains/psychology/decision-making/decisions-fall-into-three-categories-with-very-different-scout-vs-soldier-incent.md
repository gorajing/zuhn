Not all decisions are equally susceptible to motivated reasoning. For concrete everyday choices like what to order for lunch, scout thinking comes automatically because there is no ego investment — you naturally weigh the evidence and pick. For ideological issues like religion or partisan politics, the incentives push hard toward self-deception because the social and identity costs of changing your mind are enormous. For important personal decisions like whether your marriage is working or whether your startup idea is viable, there is genuine tension: you have strong incentives to believe reassuring things (avoid short-term pain) AND strong incentives to get it right (avoid long-term disaster). The practical consequence: calibrated rationality requires asking 'what category am I in?' before choosing how much vigilance to apply. Category 1: relax, your instincts are fine. Category 2: assume you're wrong about everything and proceed carefully. Category 3: notice the competing incentives and explicitly steelman the scout side to counterweight the soldier pull.
