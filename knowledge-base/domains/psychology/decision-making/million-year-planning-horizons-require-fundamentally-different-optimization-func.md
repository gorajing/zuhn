A civilization that builds stellar engines operates on timescales of millions to billions of years. At this scale, the rational calculus inverts: a supernova that's 'millions of years away' becomes an urgent threat worth massive resource allocation. This reveals how time horizons fundamentally reshape what appears rational. In human decision-making, we systematically underweight long-horizon risks because our optimization function is calibrated to decades. The stellar engine thought experiment forces recognition that 'too far away to worry about' is always relative to your planning horizon — and expanding that horizon can make previously irrational investments (climate infrastructure, asteroid defense, institutional resilience) suddenly appear obviously necessary.
