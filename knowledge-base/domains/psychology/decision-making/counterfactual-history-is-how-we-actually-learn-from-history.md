Dwarkesh notes that YouTube commenters dismiss his counterfactual questions as naive, but points out that without counterfactuals there is no way to extract lessons from history at all. If you cannot ask 'what if we had done X differently,' you cannot identify causes, assign weight to decisions, or generalize any lesson.

Paine, who teaches at the Naval War College, immediately agrees: 'we teach by counterfactuals' — replaying events and asking what other options existed. This is how professional military historians actually work. The anti-counterfactual stance that sounds sophisticated in comment sections is actually incompatible with serious historical reasoning.

For any decision-making practice — investing, strategy, product — this validates the value of rigorous post-mortem counterfactuals, but with discipline: you must honestly assess what options were actually feasible at the time, which Paine acknowledges is hard because she's 'just a professor' and lacks the operational experience to know what's feasible at the highest levels of government.
