Blankfein argues that 'most of what we do with respect to risk is not so much predicting... it's a lot of contingency planning.' In risk meetings he deliberately suppressed forecasting: 'forget about what you think the likelihood and probability of something happening is—what will you do if it does happen, and what can you do today to mitigate the consequences at a very low cost?' The discipline is to enumerate what could happen and pre-commit responses, not to estimate odds.

The payoff is speed of reaction, illustrated with a track-and-field metaphor: a sprinter who leaves within a tenth of a second of the gun is called for a false start because human reaction time is at least that long—so the only way to seem to anticipate is to have already heard the gun. 'When things get triggered and you have a plan, you get off the mark so quickly that people think you anticipated it, but what you really did is you heard the gun go off before anybody else.' This generalizes well beyond finance to startups, operations, and crisis leadership: pre-mortems and rehearsed playbooks beat probabilistic forecasting because the act of planning makes you alert and primed to act the instant a contingency materializes. It pairs naturally with his insurance heuristic—buy protection in the off-season when it's cheap, not when 'the hurricane is coming' and everybody needs it.
