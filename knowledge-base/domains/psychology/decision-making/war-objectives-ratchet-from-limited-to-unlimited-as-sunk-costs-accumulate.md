Paine's observation about WWI is a general principle of conflict escalation: nobody enters with the objective of overthrowing the other side's government, but after enough blood and treasure are spent, nothing less than regime change feels like adequate justification for the losses. The initial limited objective — 'push them out of our territory' — becomes 'make them pay for what they did to us,' which becomes 'replace the government that could do this.'

This is sunk-cost dynamics at civilizational scale. Each marginal casualty makes settling for the original objective feel like betraying the dead. The psychological pressure to 'make it worth it' drives objectives upward, which drives the conflict longer, which drives objectives higher still. The feedback loop is why limited wars are so hard to keep limited once they cross a certain casualty threshold.

The same dynamic shows up in business feuds, lawsuits, and organizational conflicts. A dispute that could have been settled cheaply early becomes intractable after enough resources are committed, because neither side can accept a resolution proportional to the original stakes. The practical implication: if you want to keep a conflict bounded, the window to settle on the original terms is narrow and closes fast.
