Graham argues that criticism should be evaluated by its specificity. If people say your work doesn't function, that's a real problem. But if the worst they can say is that you're unqualified or that what you're doing is inappropriate, they've implicitly conceded that the work itself is good — they're attacking your credentials rather than your output. 'Pointing out that someone is unqualified is as desperate as resorting to racial slurs.'

This creates a practical decision-making heuristic: treat 'inappropriate' as a homing beacon rather than a warning. The word is 'the adjective form of I don't like it' — it signals that you've entered territory the establishment considers its own but cannot defend on merit. The discomfort of incumbents, expressed through status-based rather than substance-based objections, is evidence you've found a genuine frontier.

The caveat is that this only works as a signal when the criticism is purely about status or qualification. Substantive criticism ('it doesn't work,' 'the logic is flawed') should be taken seriously regardless of who delivers it.
