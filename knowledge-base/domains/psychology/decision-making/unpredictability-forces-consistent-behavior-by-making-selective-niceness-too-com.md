Graham makes a subtle point about the cognitive cost of two-facedness: to behave well toward some people and badly toward others, you need a reliable model of who belongs in which category. In stable, legible environments this is feasible. In startup ecosystems — where a random college student can become a billion-dollar CEO in two years — that model breaks down.

The result is that selective kindness becomes a liability: the classification error rate is too high. The only robust strategy is uniform good behavior, and the only people who can sustain that long-term are people for whom it's natural. People 'performing' niceness will eventually slip when they think the cost-benefit ratio permits it — and they'll be wrong.

This generalizes beyond startups: any domain where status and influence are volatile (early careers, emerging industries, new platforms) penalizes strategic social behavior and rewards authentic character. The principle is not merely moral — it's an argument from information economics about when authenticity becomes a dominant strategy.
