A Tesla Model 3 charges from 0-50% in 15 minutes on a 250kW charger versus 17 minutes on a 150kW charger—nearly identical because the battery's acceptance rate, not the charger's output, becomes the bottleneck above 50% state of charge. Charging from 50-90% takes an additional 27 minutes regardless of charger speed. Two 0-50% charges (30-34 minutes total) deliver 200+ miles of range, matching the consumer's 31-minute tipping point.

This counterintuitive result—that the industry needs more chargers, not faster ones—is a general pattern in systems with diminishing returns. When a process has a concave returns curve, distributing effort across more instances outperforms concentrating effort into fewer, more powerful instances. This mental model applies to parallel computing, distributed teams, diversified portfolios, and any domain where the marginal return on intensity decreases while the marginal return on frequency remains constant.
