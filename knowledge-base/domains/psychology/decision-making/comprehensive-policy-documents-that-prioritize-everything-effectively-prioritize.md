South Africa's 1998 energy white paper correctly identified the looming capacity crisis but buried it at bullet point 7, behind fair access, environmental concerns, and wasteful usage — all legitimate issues but none as immediately existential as running out of power. The paper ran 110 pages and tried to reform everything simultaneously with vague mandates. The result was that the single most time-critical issue — capacity expansion — received no focused action, and the predicted crisis arrived exactly on schedule. This pattern repeats across organizations: comprehensive strategy documents that identify 15 priorities effectively have zero priorities. The ANC's broader governance failure follows the same shape — threading the needle between historical justice, economic growth, infrastructure expansion, and social reform meant that the hard, unglamorous work of maintaining existing systems was perpetually deprioritized. The actionable lesson is that any planning document should force-rank problems by time-to-irreversibility, not by political importance or comprehensiveness, and the top item should receive disproportionate resources.
