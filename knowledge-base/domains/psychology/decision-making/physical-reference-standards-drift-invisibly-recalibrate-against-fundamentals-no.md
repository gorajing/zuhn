The International Prototype Kilogram (Le Grand K) and its 40 replicas diverged by up to 50 micrograms over decades despite identical materials and storage conditions. No one could determine which cylinder had changed because there was no external reference — the artifact WAS the definition.

This is a precise analogy for how personal decision-making heuristics, team quality standards, and organizational metrics silently drift when anchored to tradition or precedent rather than first principles. The fix in metrology was to redefine the kilogram in terms of a fundamental constant (Avogadro's number or Planck's constant) rather than a physical object. The analogous fix in decision-making is to periodically recalibrate your standards against core principles rather than assuming your current reference point is still accurate.

The lesson is especially sharp because the drift was invisible — the replicas all looked and felt identical. Silent degradation of standards is the most dangerous kind because it removes the signal that recalibration is needed.
