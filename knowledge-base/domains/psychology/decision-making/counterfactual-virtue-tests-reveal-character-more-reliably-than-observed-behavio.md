The story's central mechanism — a drug that lets people live counterfactual lives — dramatizes a deep insight about character assessment. Observed behavior is contaminated by circumstance: a rich person never tested by poverty may appear virtuous simply because virtue was never costly. A poor person who steals may have been generous if born wealthy. The veil of ignorance framework strips away situational luck to reveal dispositional character.

This has practical applications beyond philosophy. In hiring, investing, and relationship decisions, we routinely confuse situational performance with dispositional quality. Someone who succeeded in a bull market isn't necessarily skilled; someone who failed in a recession isn't necessarily incompetent. The counterfactual question — 'what would this person do if circumstances were reversed?' — is harder to answer but more predictive of future behavior across varying conditions.
