When teams catalog customer jobs, they overwhelmingly list functional tasks — the visible, tangible things customers are trying to accomplish. But customers also have social jobs (impressing a boss, signaling status) and emotional jobs (feeling healthier, reducing anxiety) that are less visible but often more powerful drivers of behavior.

The pains associated with failing at social or emotional jobs are felt more deeply, and the gains from succeeding are pursued more aggressively. This means products that address emotional and social dimensions create stronger customer attachment and willingness to pay. The practical technique is to label each identified job as functional, social, or emotional, then deliberately push to fill gaps in the social and emotional categories.
