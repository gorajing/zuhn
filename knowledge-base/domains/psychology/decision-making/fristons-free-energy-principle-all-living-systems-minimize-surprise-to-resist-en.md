Karl Friston (most-cited living neuroscientist) formalized this principle: any system that persists as a coherent thing — cell, organism, society, organization — has an implicit model of itself and its environment, and survives by minimizing 'surprise' (the gap between what its model predicts and what it observes). It can do this two ways: (1) Update the model to better predict observations (perception, learning). (2) Act on the world to bring observations into line with the model (action, control). The combined process is called 'active inference.' What this unifies: homeostasis (the body's internal thermostats keeping temperature/glucose/blood-pressure in a narrow range), perception (the brain's predictive models of the world), learning (updating priors based on data), and intentional action (changing the world to match a goal). Why this matters far beyond neuroscience: organizations have the same shape — they have implicit models of their market/environment, and they survive by either updating the model (strategy pivots, customer learning) or acting on the world (sales, marketing, product changes). AI agents have the same shape. Markets have the same shape. The free energy principle is not just a neuroscience theory; it's a candidate for the most general principle of how persistent self-organizing systems work. Practical implication for builders: when something stops working, ask 'is my model wrong, or is my action wrong?' These are the only two failure modes. If your model is wrong, no amount of action fixes it; if your action is wrong, no amount of model-refinement fixes it. Most failure modes are confused about which one is broken. The corollary for AI design: agents that can't separately update their internal model from their action policy are structurally fragile, because real environments require both.
