Munger told Senra that he has 'an almost complete indifference to problems' because troubles are inescapable — but the distribution of troubles is wildly skewed by who and what you surround yourself with. Great businesses don't throw up big problem after big problem. Great partners don't manufacture chronic drama. Great friends don't demand constant crisis management. So rather than optimizing your crisis-response muscles, you should optimize the selection filter that decides which people and ventures get into your life in the first place.

This cuts against the common framing that resilience and problem-solving capacity are the key life skills. Munger's point is that problem-solving is a tax you can largely avoid-paying by exercising discrimination upstream. The rule has an obvious implication for investing (buy great businesses and hold them, don't buy mediocre businesses and try to manage them), but it extends directly to relationships and work. The practical question becomes: when you sense mediocrity in a business partner, employee, supplier, or even a commitment, the correct response is usually to exit the relationship rather than to build infrastructure around managing its problems. You can't escape all troubles — but you can skew the distribution enormously.
