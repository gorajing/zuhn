Cliff Weitzman's universal strategic principle, applied consistently across his career: 'I moved to the US when I was 13, didn't speak English. My college counselor recommended six schools. I realized that for kids with perfect SAT scores and perfect grades, you have about a 1-in-10 chance of getting in. I was like, OK, it's a lottery. How do you win a lottery? You buy more tickets than everybody else. I didn't get into many schools I wanted, but I got into a lot of schools I didn't expect to get into. My overall principle in life is volume of work. More reps, more shots on goal, more free throws — that's how you get on the bell curve unexpected outcomes.' The same principle compounds at Speechify: 'We test almost 1000 data-generated ads a day.' For sales meetings: 'If the CEO didn't respond, I would message the CMO. If the CMO didn't respond, the head of growth. If they didn't respond, I would politely continue until they told me to stop, which is less than 1% of the time.' For the main essay: 'Instead of making 3 variations or 5 variations, I wrote 48 drafts.' The structural argument: in fat-tailed distributions (where outcomes follow a power law), the expected value of an attempt is dominated by the upper tail. Each attempt has small probability of catastrophic success. The optimal strategy is to maximize the number of attempts, not the quality of any single attempt. Most people optimize the WRONG variable — they polish 1 essay to perfection rather than writing 10 mediocre ones. The 10 mediocre ones produce more discovery (you learn what works) and have more upper-tail exposure (any one might be the breakthrough). Domains where volume-of-work dominates: (1) Job applications (apply to many; the right offer is in the long tail). (2) Sales outreach (1000 emails > 100 perfectly-crafted ones). (3) Content (1000 posts > 10 great ones, because the algorithm rewards the upper tail). (4) Ad testing (1000 variants > picking the best 10). (5) Investor introductions (cast wide; the right intro is unpredictable). (6) Product experimentation (run 100 small tests > 1 big launch). The corollary: don't romanticize quality at low volume. The asymmetric path is high volume + ruthless selection of the winners. Apply the Alex Hormozi formula: Volume × Leverage = Output. Both variables matter, but volume is often the bottleneck. The corollary for AI in 2026: AI dramatically reduces the marginal cost of producing each attempt (each ad variation, each cold email, each blog post). The volume-of-work strategy becomes MORE valuable with AI because you can now run 10x or 100x the previous volume per unit of human effort. Most operators are still optimizing for the AI-zero-volume world.
