Graham identifies a practical hack for the core angel investing problem: you have very little information but must decide quickly, and indecisiveness is worse than a wrong decision. His solution is to reduce the investment size until the downside is psychologically manageable — for someone with $5M, that might be $15,000 per startup. At that level, the fear of loss doesn't paralyze judgment.

This principle generalizes beyond investing. In any domain with high uncertainty and costly indecision, you can calibrate your exposure until the emotional cost of being wrong drops below your threshold for action. The first few bets become tuition — $60,000 across four startups is 'less than a lot of graduate programs, plus you get equity.' This reframes early losses from failures into learning investments, which changes both the emotional and strategic calculus.
