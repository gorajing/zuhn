Paine cites a staggering statistic: 1.8 million of 2.1 million Japanese war deaths (85%) occurred in the last 14 months, after military leaders knew they were losing. A similar pattern held in Germany. This isn't explained by simple denialism — it's that the Western assumption 'governments exist for the well-being of individuals' doesn't describe how most regimes actually function. Leaders are playing a different game, in which continued resistance is rational for them even when it's catastrophic for the population.

Mechanically, the death toll compounds because late-war collapse destroys transportation networks that feed cities, pulls manpower out of agriculture, and creates mass starvation independent of combat. The final year isn't a linear extension of prior years — it's a phase transition where civil systems fail.

The broader lesson for anyone reasoning about stuck situations — failing companies, doomed projects, bad marriages, sunk-cost governments — is that the final phase is usually where most of the damage accumulates, not the middle phase. Exiting before the terminal collapse is worth enormous sacrifices of pride or optionality.
