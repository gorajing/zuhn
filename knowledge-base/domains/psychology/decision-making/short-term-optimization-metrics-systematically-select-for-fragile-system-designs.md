The global misimplementation of JIT reveals a deep incentive problem: resilience is invisible during normal operations. A manager who cuts inventory shows immediate cost savings on the quarterly report. A manager who maintains strategic buffers shows higher costs with no visible benefit — until a disruption hits. Over years of undisrupted operations, organizations systematically select for the first type of manager and against the second.

This is a variant of the 'turkey problem' — the turkey's model of the world improves every day the farmer feeds it, right up until Thanksgiving. Supply chain resilience costs are visible and continuous; the benefits are invisible and episodic. Any system where evaluation horizons are shorter than disruption cycles will drift toward fragility, because the people making decisions are rewarded for cutting buffers and never penalized until it's too late.
