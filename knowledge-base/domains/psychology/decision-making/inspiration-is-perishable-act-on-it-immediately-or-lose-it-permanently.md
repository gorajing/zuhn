Naval describes a pattern familiar to many creators: conceiving an idea (tweet storm, blog post, business solution) but hesitating or pausing, only to find the inspiration has died by the time he returns to it. The insight has never come back with the same energy or clarity.

This frames inspiration not as a renewable resource you can bank for later but as a perishable good with a short shelf life. The practical rule becomes: when you feel the pull to create or solve, drop lower-priority tasks and act. The cost of interrupting your schedule is almost always lower than the cost of losing an insight that won't return. This connects to Naval's broader framework of impatience with actions — the bias toward immediate execution whenever possible.
