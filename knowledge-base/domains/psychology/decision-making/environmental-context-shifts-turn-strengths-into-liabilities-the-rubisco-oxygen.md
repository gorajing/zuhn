RuBisCo was perfectly adequate when Earth's atmosphere was CO2-rich and oxygen-poor — its inability to distinguish between CO2 and O2 simply didn't matter. But as photosynthetic organisms succeeded and flooded the atmosphere with oxygen, RuBisCo's original design flaw became catastrophic: it now grabs oxygen instead of CO2 roughly half the time, producing toxic phosphoglycolate that plants must spend additional energy to neutralize.

The deeper insight is that success itself changed the environment in ways that undermined the very mechanism that created the success. This is directly applicable to decision-making: skills, strategies, and mental models optimized for one context become dangerous when context shifts. The more deeply embedded the approach (RuBisCo is foundational to all plant life), the harder it is to swap out when conditions change. This argues for regularly stress-testing core assumptions against current conditions rather than trusting that what worked in the past will continue to work.
