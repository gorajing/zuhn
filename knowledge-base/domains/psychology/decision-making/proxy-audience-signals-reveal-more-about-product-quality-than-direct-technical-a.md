Graham's 'wrong people like it' heuristic examines not just whether people use a technology, but which people and for what reasons. Java attracted suits who followed press coverage, enterprise programmers amazed by anything better than C++, and students chasing job prospects — all extrinsically motivated groups. Meanwhile, the programmers he most admired were indifferent to Java.

This inverts typical evaluation: instead of asking 'is this technically good?' ask 'do the people with the best taste voluntarily choose this?' Forced adoption (by management, funding pressure, or market expectations) is a strong negative signal because it means the technology can't win on intrinsic merit. The distinction between voluntary enthusiasts and reluctant users is one of the most underused evaluation heuristics in technology and product assessment.
