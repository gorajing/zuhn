Jessica Wu's mental model for surviving as a founder: 'Building a startup is really really unimaginably difficult. You're on a roller coaster, constantly experiencing very high highs and very low lows, and it's also compressed — morning might be your best day ever and then 2 hours later you're suffering from probably the worst news you've ever heard. This happens pretty much every day. As a founder, you learn that things sort of return to the norm. You have this comfort in your head that things will be okay even if they don't feel okay, because they always end up being okay provided that you are building in the right market and also that you have the right people around you.' Why this matters: the daily emotional swing is real and unavoidable — at any point in any week, both 'best deal ever' and 'worst churn ever' are possible. The founder who responds to each swing with full emotional intensity burns out fast; the founder who internalizes 'this will return to baseline' over months of evidence develops the equanimity to stay focused on the work. The two conditions Wu identifies — right market + right team — are necessary; without them the swings don't return to baseline (they drift down to failure). With them, the swings reliably regress. The implication for founders: actively study your past emotional swings to build the meta-pattern recognition. When the next bad news lands, recall the last three pieces of bad news you got and notice that they all resolved (or were absorbed). When the next good news lands, do the same to avoid getting overconfident from a single data point. The early team learns this too — and the team's collective ability to look at the big picture during local volatility is what makes endurance possible. The deeper psychological principle: regression to the mean is real in life as in statistics; emotional regulation comes from internalizing it, not from suppressing reactions.
