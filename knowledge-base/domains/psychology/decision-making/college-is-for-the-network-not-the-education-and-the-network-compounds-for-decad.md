Lucy Guo's closing advice to young entrepreneurs: 'If you're in college, go make friends with every single person that you know. There is literally no other time in your life that you're going to be surrounded by such intellectual people that all need friends. You're a freshman in college — no one has friends, everyone wants to make new friends. These people are going to be your future hires, your future investors, your future co-founders. If you miss out on that, it gets a lot harder as people start building new friend groups after they graduate.' Why college is structurally optimal for network-building: (1) Talent density: top universities concentrate the highest-IQ, most-ambitious 18-22 year-olds in human history into a few dozen acres. (2) Friend-seeking moment: everyone is simultaneously trying to form friend groups; the social opportunity cost of approaching strangers is near-zero. (3) 4-year duration: long enough for deep relationships, not short enough to be transactional. (4) Pre-status: peers haven't yet sorted into status hierarchies, so you can befriend future billionaires before they're billionaires. None of these conditions persist in post-college life: first jobs already have established cliques; co-workers come from narrow IQ slices; status hierarchies are calcified; the social cost of approaching strangers is high. The corollary on education itself: 'I don't think a single person from college is like, wow, I used everything I learned in college in my job.' The educational content is largely theoretical and forgotten. The implication for current college students: stop optimizing for grades; start optimizing for relationship breadth and depth with high-trajectory peers. For post-college: aggressively maintain college relationships through deliberate effort (frequent calls, shared trips, annual reunions) because the network was nearly free to acquire and nearly impossible to replicate later.
