The Vienna Convention's inviolability clause means host countries cannot enter embassy buildings, arrest diplomats, or directly enforce laws against protected individuals. When the Libyan embassy shooting killed a British policewoman in 1984, the UK's only recourse was to sever diplomatic relations entirely and expel all staff — a drastic action with decades-long consequences for bilateral relations.

This pattern generalizes beyond diplomacy: whenever one party has structural immunity from direct consequences, enforcement shifts to expensive, blunt mechanisms (severing the entire relationship, public shaming, economic sanctions). The enforcer pays a disproportionate cost relative to the violation, which means minor infractions go unpunished while only egregious violations trigger response. This dynamic favors the protected party in marginal cases and explains why diplomatic parking tickets in NYC go unpaid by the millions.
