Graham's nuclear reactor metaphor for startup optimism is unusually precise: optimism is the fuel that makes founding possible, but unshielded optimism creates dangerous dependencies. The practical failure mode isn't emotional — it's structural. When a founder believes a deal will close, they stop pursuing alternatives. The other party senses this dependence and exploits it in negotiation details.

The hypnotic suggestion Graham plants is: when you hear 'we want to invest in you,' automatically think 'don't get your hopes up' and continue operating as if the deal doesn't exist. Paradoxically, this indifference is the most likely thing to make the deal close, because it signals strength. The broader principle: the way to succeed is to focus on getting users while investors and acquirers scurry alongside trying to wave money in your face.
