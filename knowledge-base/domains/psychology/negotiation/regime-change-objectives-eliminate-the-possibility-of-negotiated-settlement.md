Paine draws a crisp distinction: with limited objectives, the loser can accept a bad outcome and still exist — they give up territory, pay reparations, accept constraints, but their regime survives. With unlimited objectives, the loser is personally eliminated, which means they have no incentive to stop fighting until they literally cannot continue. Limited wars can end in negotiation; unlimited wars can only end in destruction.

This has direct implications for any adversarial context where you want a path to resolution. If your stated objective is the counterparty's elimination — firing them, destroying their business, ending their career — you have removed their incentive to cooperate on any terms. They now rationally fight to the last resource because surrender and defeat are indistinguishable. Conversely, offering the opponent a survivable exit is not softness; it's what makes resolution possible at all.

The operational form: in negotiations, lawsuits, firings, and competitive disputes, always leave the other side a face-saving path that preserves their core identity or position. 'Unconditional surrender' framing is satisfying but strategically expensive — it converts every opponent into someone with nothing to lose. Bismarck understood this, which is why his limited-objective wars ended cleanly; Putin doesn't, which is why the Ukraine war has no obvious off-ramp.
