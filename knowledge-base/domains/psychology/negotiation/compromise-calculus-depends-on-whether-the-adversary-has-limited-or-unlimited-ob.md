Paine offers a clean decision rule against the usual 'always negotiate' vs 'never appease' debate. Classify the adversary's objective first. A limited objective is a bounded demand — a piece of territory, a preferential trade arrangement, a change in some specific policy. Against limited objectives, compromise is cheap and usually right: negotiate the sliver and move on.

An unlimited objective is regime change or worse: the extinction of your way of life, government, or people. Against unlimited objectives, every concession is a weapons transfer to the executioner — 'if you compromise with them, you are simply setting them up and putting them in a stronger position when they come at you for the final kill.' The appeasement-of-Hitler critique is really this, not a generic claim that compromise is weakness.

The hard work is classification, not the decision. Adversaries routinely disguise unlimited objectives as limited ones until it's too late. The diagnostic question is: does the other side's stated end-state leave you as a going concern with agency, or is it a waystation to your elimination? This is the same question a startup should ask about an 'acquisition interest' from a much larger competitor, an employee should ask about a 'reorganization,' and a nation should ask about a border dispute.
