McDermott's explicit rule is 'don't jump into the deep end of the pool — you can walk into the kitty pool first.' The first few minutes of a high-stakes meeting should be invested in human interest topics that are knowable in advance: philanthropic projects, social causes, hobbies, family milestones. These signal that you took the time to know the person, and they give you a chance to read the emotional state of the room before you need to be persuasive.

The functional reason is informational: whatever you planned to say is almost certainly going to need adjustment based on what meeting they just had, what their mental state is, and what they care about in this specific moment. If you lead with the pitch deck, you lose the one window you have to gather that signal. If you lead with a warm conversation, the buyer tells you — in tone, pace, and body language — whether the original plan still applies or needs to be torched.

The research prerequisite matters: the human-interest opener only works if it's specific (their philanthropic board, their son's college, the article they wrote) rather than generic small talk about weather or sports. This is why 'read the room' depends on upstream preparation, not improvisation. Founders who show up cold get one shot at reading the room, and they read it poorly.
