Paul Graham observes that speaking and idea quality are not just independent — they actively conflict. A great speaker engages audiences through ad-libbing, jokes, and emotional momentum, but ad-libbing limits thinking time to however long it takes to say a sentence. Writing, by contrast, lets you spend unlimited time refining each thought.

This creates a practical tradeoff: every hour spent rehearsing a talk is an hour not spent improving the ideas in it. Graham himself finds that when he sits down to rehearse, he ends up rewriting instead — which makes the delivery worse but the content better. The implication is that for anyone whose primary goal is idea quality, writing should be the default medium and speaking should be treated as a lossy compression format.
