Graham describes a Gresham's Law of credit: visible contributors drive out recognition of invisible ones. Livingston co-founded YC, defined its culture, had final say on all decisions, and built the alumni network — yet the standard narrative became 'PG started YC and his wife kind of helped.' This wasn't malicious; it was structural. Writers get disproportionate attention. People who engage with press get covered. Those who fight publicly get remembered. This creates a systematic bias in how organizational histories are told, which in turn shapes who gets recruited, funded, and celebrated. For any organization, if you want accurate attribution, you need deliberate mechanisms to surface quiet contributors — because the default information economy will erase them.
