Graham identifies a fundamental structural problem: the startup pitching environment systematically pairs technical founders who lack presentation experience with investors who lack technical depth. This isn't a failure of either side — it's an emergent property of the system. Even Google was turned down by investors who 'didn't get it.'

This framing has practical implications beyond just 'practice your pitch.' It means that communication quality is a bottleneck on capital allocation — genuinely great companies fail to get funded because of information transfer failures, not because of product quality. It also means that any founder who can bridge this gap (explain technical work clearly to non-technical audiences) has an asymmetric advantage that compounds across every investor interaction, customer conversation, and hiring pitch they'll ever give.
