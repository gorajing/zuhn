Graham identifies a fundamental dynamic in high-stakes presentations: when evaluators can't properly assess the substance (due to time constraints or knowledge gaps), they fall back on meta-signals — primarily, how confident the presenter seems. This isn't about telling people you're passionate or your product is great (audiences tune that out or write you off as a bullshitter). Confidence must be implicit, shown not told.

This creates a critical asymmetry: nervousness and apologetic behavior are actively destructive signals because they suggest even the creator doubts the work. Graham reframes the psychology — if you've truly made something good, you're doing investors a favor by telling them about it. The counter-intuitive advice is that attempting to seem more polished or corporate than you are can actually conceal the raw talent investors are looking for, because it replaces authentic confidence with performative professionalism.
