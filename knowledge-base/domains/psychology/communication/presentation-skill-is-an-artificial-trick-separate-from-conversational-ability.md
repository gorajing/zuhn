Graham observes that everyday life provides zero practice for addressing a roomful of people. The founders who'd been out in the world and presented to groups were visibly different from those who hadn't. His solution is counterintuitive: treat public speaking as a consciously artificial technique rather than an extension of natural conversation.

The parallel to writing is instructive — 'good writing is an elaborate effort to seem spontaneous.' The actor's rule applies: if you feel you're speaking too slowly, you're at about the right speed. You can memorize your entire presentation, but write it to sound like informal spontaneous speech. The goal is artificiality that reads as natural, not authenticity that reads as mumbling at your laptop screen.
