Seo argues that arguments are 'easy to start and hard to end' because any number of differences between two people can flood in once conflict begins. The solution is that every disagreement should start with a moment of agreement: explicitly naming what the disagreement is about, thereby excluding everything else.

This creates what Seo calls a 'contract' between disputants. When someone tries to expand the scope — shifting from the specific complaint to character attacks, bringing in past grievances, or changing the subject — you can reference the contract and pull the conversation back to its agreed parameters.

This principle maps directly to professional contexts: scope creep in project disputes, feature debates that morph into strategy arguments, code review discussions that balloon into architecture rewrites. The discipline of saying 'we are disagreeing about X and only X right now' is a meta-skill that makes every subsequent argument more productive.
