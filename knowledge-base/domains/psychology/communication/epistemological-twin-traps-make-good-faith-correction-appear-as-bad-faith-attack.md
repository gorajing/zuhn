Alexander identifies a deep structural problem in public discourse: the malicious streetlight effect and directional correctness are mirror images, and attempting to correct either one looks identical to committing the other. If you point out that crime statistics don't capture disorder concerns, you might be legitimately noting a streetlight effect — or you might be using 'directional correctness' to defend the false claim that murder is up.

This creates an epistemological trap with no clean exit. Good-faith corrections become indistinguishable from bad-faith manipulations. The practical implication is that in any polarized debate, the mere existence of both fallacy patterns gives bad actors cover: they can always accuse a corrector of committing whichever fallacy is the mirror of their own. The only partial solution Alexander finds is explicit scoping — stating upfront what you are and aren't addressing — but even that proved insufficient against motivated critics.
