Graham's advice to 'not try to seem more than you are' rests on an information asymmetry insight: experienced investors have seen thousands of pitches and have finely calibrated bullshit detectors, while first-time founders have essentially zero experience producing convincing deception. The expected outcome of attempted deception is therefore net negative — you won't fool them, and the attempt itself signals poor judgment.

More importantly, the effort to appear polished or corporate has an opportunity cost: it conceals the raw talent and genuine insight that investors are actually screening for. Smart investors look past superficial flaws (no office, no business experience) because they know Google was once in that exact position. Candor about what you haven't figured out signals intelligence and self-awareness — both positive investor signals. This principle generalizes to any domain where your evaluator has more pattern-matching experience than you have performance experience: job interviews, board meetings, expert consultations.
