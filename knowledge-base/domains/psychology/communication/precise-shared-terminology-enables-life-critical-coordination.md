Anatomy developed standardized directional terms (anterior/posterior, superior/inferior, medial/lateral, proximal/distal) because imprecise language like 'achey belly' is inadequate for surgical coordination. A doctor can pinpoint a swallowed toothpick as 'along the medial line, posterior to the heart, anterior to the vertebrae, inferior to the collarbone, superior to the stomach' — giving a surgeon a precise verbal map to the esophagus.

This illustrates a broader principle: domains that require precise coordination under high stakes inevitably develop specialized terminology. The jargon isn't gatekeeping — it's a compression technology that encodes spatial, relational, and functional information into compact phrases. Aviation, law, software engineering, and medicine all independently converged on this pattern. Learning a domain's terminology isn't memorization — it's acquiring the coordination tools that make expert collaboration possible.
