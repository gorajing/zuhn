Historically, the most taboo words in English referenced the supernatural—'Zounds' (God's wounds) and 'Gadzooks' (God's hooks) were genuinely shocking in Victorian England because people believed casual divine references caused physical harm to God. As secularization progressed, religious oaths lost their charge, and bodily/sexual terms filled the taboo vacuum.

Now that sex and disease have become increasingly medicalized and discussed openly, a new migration is underway: identity-based and class-based terms are becoming the new unspeakable words. John McWhorter predicts that words related to class disparity—'trash,' 'chav,' 'urban' as pejorative—will become the next generation of true profanity. This pattern suggests taboo is not random but tracks what a society currently fears most: the supernatural when faith dominated, the body when hygiene was precarious, and social inequality as awareness of systemic disadvantage grows.
