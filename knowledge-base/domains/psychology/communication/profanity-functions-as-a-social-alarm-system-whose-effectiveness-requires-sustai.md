Animals use distress vocalizations to startle attackers and alert others to danger. Human profanity serves the same function but with a cultural mechanism: words become effective alarms not through volume or pitch but through social taboo. Because using a bad word carries a social cost (disapproval, punishment, professional consequences), choosing to use one credibly signals that the speaker's emotional state is intense enough to accept that cost.

This creates a game-theoretic equilibrium: the words must remain somewhat forbidden to retain their signaling power. If profanity were fully normalized, it would lose its ability to convey urgency, and speakers would need to invent new taboo terms to replace the lost signal. Censorship and social policing of language, often criticized as arbitrary, may actually serve a functional role in maintaining this alarm system—ensuring that the emotional signal channel doesn't get degraded by overuse.
