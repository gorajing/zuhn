Professional investors experience pitch fatigue where presentations blur together into undifferentiated noise. The first survival filter isn't being impressive — it's being remembered at all. A single descriptive phrase like 'the Microsoft Word of ecommerce' (Viaweb's soundbite) creates a cognitive anchor that persists after the details fade.

This technique works bidirectionally: it helps the investor remember you, but the exercise of distilling your startup into one compelling phrase also forces clarity on the founders. If you can't describe your startup in a single sticky phrase, your plans may not be sufficiently focused. The phrase should be launched 'clearly but apparently casually' near the beginning of the talk — the casualness prevents it from feeling like a marketing slogan while the clarity ensures it registers. The Hollywood pattern ('X meets Y') and startup pattern ('the X of Y') work because they leverage existing mental models to make novel concepts instantly graspable.
