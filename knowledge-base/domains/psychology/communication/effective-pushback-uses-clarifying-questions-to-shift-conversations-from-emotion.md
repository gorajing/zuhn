Dhiman provides a specific toolkit for pushing back on ideas without being difficult to work with: ask clarifying questions that bring conversations to a logical space rather than an emotional one. His go-to questions include 'Why has this become suddenly important?', 'Can you help clarify xyz?', 'What would success look like?', and 'What time frame do you need this in?'

This technique works because it shifts the burden of justification to the proposer without creating adversarial dynamics. The questions are genuinely useful regardless of whether the idea is good or bad—they surface assumptions, timelines, and success criteria that improve decision quality either way. It exemplifies the broader PM trait of raising difficult issues without being difficult to work with.
