Politeness and social habit compel people to pay attention to someone standing near them and looking at them. This is a fragile contract: the moment you turn to fuss with your computer, the spell breaks and minds drift to errands and email. The practical fix is role separation — one person presents, another drives the demo.

This reveals a deeper principle about attention as a social negotiation rather than a content-quality issue. The audience isn't making a rational decision to stop listening when you look away; they're responding to the removal of a social obligation. Great content on screen cannot compensate for the loss of interpersonal attention pressure. This is why the best speakers treat presentation as a fundamentally embodied, audience-facing act with technology as background support, not the main event.
