RuBisCo, the enzyme at the heart of carbon fixation, was optimized for a world with negligible atmospheric oxygen. As photosynthesis itself pumped oxygen into the atmosphere over millions of years, RuBisCo began confusing oxygen for CO2 about 50% of the time—producing phosphoglycolate, a toxic byproduct that interferes with the Calvin cycle and requires its own energy-expensive cleanup pathway.

The plant can't abandon RuBisCo because it's already the dominant strategy, so it compensates by producing massive quantities of it and running a parallel detox system. The mechanism that created the oxygen-rich world is now partially broken by that world.

This pattern appears in other domains: a startup that disrupts a market must then compete in the new competitive landscape it created. A social media algorithm that maximizes engagement creates the polarized information environment that now makes users trust the platform less. Early design decisions that succeeded are often locked in precisely because of that success—making the cost of the subsequent degradation invisible until it's structural.
