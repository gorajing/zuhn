RuBisCo, the enzyme responsible for carbon fixation in photosynthesis, was evolved in a low-oxygen atmosphere and now fails up to 50% of the time by grabbing oxygen instead of CO2. Rather than evolving a superior replacement, plants compensated by producing staggering quantities — an estimated 40 billion tons on Earth at any time — and developing secondary pathways to handle its toxic byproduct (phosphoglycolate).

This is a powerful mental model for understanding lock-in across domains. Software systems accumulate workarounds for legacy code rather than rewriting (QWERTY keyboard, x86 architecture). Organizations build entire departments to compensate for a flawed core process rather than redesign it. The lesson isn't that lock-in is always bad — RuBisCo enabled plants to dominate Earth — but that the cost of switching away from an entrenched solution is almost always underestimated, and scaling a mediocre solution often wins over designing an optimal one.
