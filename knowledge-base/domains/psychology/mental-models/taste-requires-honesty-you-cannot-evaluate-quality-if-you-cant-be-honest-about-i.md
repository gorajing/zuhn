Chamath's structural claim: 'taste' — the faculty of knowing what is good, useful, exceptional — requires honesty as a precondition. If your evaluation is colored by emotional bias, social desire, or self-protective deception, you cannot reliably distinguish quality. People who achieve enduring excellence have, in the specific domain they excel in, the ability to be 'extremely honest' about quality. Maybe they're not honest about everything in their lives — but in their craft, they are zealots for honest evaluation.

Chamath's example from his own career: he was 'characterized as this bully that would make people cry' in Steven Levy's book. He doesn't remember it that way, but he was 'a zealot about honesty' — when emotional bias entered conversations, he would 'eviscerate' people because emotions are irrelevant and core truth is what matters. He's softened the style but not the principle.

For builders and operators: cultivate honest evaluation as a foundational skill. This includes self-evaluation (am I actually good at this? where am I deceiving myself?) and other-evaluation (is this work actually good? am I sugarcoating to preserve relationships?). The discipline isn't about being harsh; it's about removing emotional bias from quality assessment. For Zuhn editorially: this is the operational form of why most companies' 'quality bars' degrade over time — emotional considerations (sunk cost, team morale, vendor relationships) creep into evaluation and the taste faculty atrophies. Maintaining taste requires institutional discipline around honesty about quality.
