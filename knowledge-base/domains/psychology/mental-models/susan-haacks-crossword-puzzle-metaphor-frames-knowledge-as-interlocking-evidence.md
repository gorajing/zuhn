Philosopher Susan Haack proposed that knowledge works like a crossword puzzle rather than a logical chain. In a crossword, each answer is constrained by the clue (direct evidence) AND by intersecting letters from other answers (coherence with other beliefs). A word that fits its clue but conflicts with three crossing words is probably wrong. Similarly, a belief supported by one piece of evidence but contradicting multiple other well-supported beliefs should be suspect.

This foundherentist model is practically superior to both pure foundationalism (beliefs resting on self-evident axioms) and pure coherentism (beliefs justified only by fitting together). It gives you a concrete evaluation framework: when assessing any claim, ask how many independent constraints it satisfies. A flat Earth satisfies the visual intuition constraint but violates physics, astronomy, navigation, satellite imagery, and time zones — five crossing words that don't fit. This crossword test works for evaluating startup ideas, technical architectures, and investment theses just as well as scientific theories.
