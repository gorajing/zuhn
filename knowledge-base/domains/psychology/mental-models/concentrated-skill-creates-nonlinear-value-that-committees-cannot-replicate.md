Graham asks: would a basketball team trade one player for 100 random people? Would Apple's next product be better designed by a committee of 100 random people instead of Steve Jobs? The answer reveals that skill concentration produces nonlinear returns. A CEO or athlete may have only 10x the raw ability of an average person, but concentrating that ability in one individual 'makes all the difference.'

This has direct implications for hiring, team composition, and organizational design. It explains why small teams of exceptional people consistently outperform large teams of average ones, and why the market rationally pays 100x for concentrated excellence. The slave markets of ancient Rome priced this correctly — skilled individuals cost up to 50x more than unskilled ones — suggesting this isn't a modern distortion but a fundamental feature of human productivity.
