When Harper examines the mid-sixth century, he doesn't see an empire tipping over from accumulated rot. Justinian had retaken Africa, mostly retaken Italy, and was on a path of success when a plague killing a significant fraction of entire populations combined with a multi-decade volcanic cooling event to wreck agriculture. A reasonable counterfactual is that Rome survives as a recognizable pan-Mediterranean empire for centuries longer — much as China's dynastic cycles repeatedly reconstitute a core civilization after apparent collapse.

The generalizable lesson is that declinist narratives are epistemically cheap. Any complex system has enough internal problems to fit a story of inevitable fall after the fact. The harder question is whether the system would have survived the counterfactual world without the specific exogenous shock that happened to land. This applies to empires, companies, relationships, and markets.

When someone explains a collapse by pointing to structural weaknesses, ask: did the shock that triggered it exceed what that system had absorbed before? If so, the shock is doing most of the causal work, and the structural story is mostly pattern-matching. This is a key discipline for tail-risk thinking and for avoiding survivorship bias in lessons drawn from failure.
