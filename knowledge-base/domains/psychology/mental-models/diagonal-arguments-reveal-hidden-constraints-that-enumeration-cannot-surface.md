Cantor's diagonal argument works by taking the nth character of the nth entry and flipping it, producing a name that differs from every listed entry by at least one position. This technique generalizes far beyond mathematics: it is the template for proving that no checklist, process, or enumeration can capture all possibilities in a sufficiently complex domain. In decision-making, this means recognizing when a problem space is 'uncountably' rich — where no finite set of rules, heuristics, or categories can cover all cases. It is the formal basis for why exhaustive testing is impossible, why regulatory frameworks always have gaps, and why adversaries can always construct inputs that bypass enumerated defenses.
