The electron transport chain in photosynthesis is a textbook example of layered conversion: photon energy excites an electron, which passes through multiple protein complexes, each extracting a small amount of energy to pump protons, which then flow through ATP synthase to produce ATP, which finally powers the Calvin Cycle to fix carbon. No single step is efficient, but the chain is extraordinarily reliable.

This pattern appears everywhere in effective systems: compilers convert source code through multiple intermediate representations; supply chains convert raw materials through staged processing; learning converts experience through encoding, consolidation, and retrieval. The insight is that attempting direct conversion (sunlight → sugar in one step) would require impossibly precise molecular machinery. Chaining lossy but reliable steps is nature's — and good engineering's — preferred architecture for complex transformations.
