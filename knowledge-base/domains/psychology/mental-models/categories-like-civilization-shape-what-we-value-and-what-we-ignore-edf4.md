The standard markers historians use to identify 'civilizations' — monumental architecture, written records, military conquests, named rulers — systematically disadvantage the Indus Valley, which excelled in areas we don't typically celebrate: urban sanitation, standardized infrastructure, and apparently egalitarian social organization. Their writing remains undeciphered partly because we've invested far less effort in understanding it compared to Egyptian hieroglyphics or Mesopotamian cuneiform.

This reveals a deep cognitive bias: our categories determine our attention. In business, we celebrate 'unicorn' startups (valuation-based) rather than 'cockroach' startups (survival-based), which biases founders toward growth over resilience. In technology, we celebrate visible product features over invisible infrastructure reliability. The Indus Valley had better sewage systems than most European cities until the 19th century — but nobody builds museums about sewage. Recognition frameworks shape what gets built.
