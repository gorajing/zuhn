Across Tyson's three examples, a pattern emerges: we assume the obvious source powers the observed effect, and this assumption blinds us. Life needs sunlight (wrong — tidal heating works). Gravity needs matter (maybe wrong — could be a property of spacetime itself). A football flies straight (wrong — Earth's rotation deflects it).

This is a transferable decision-making heuristic: when analyzing any system, explicitly list what you assume is the energy source or causal driver, then ask 'what if the real driver is something I'm not even measuring?' In business, the obvious driver of growth might be marketing spend, but the real driver could be word-of-mouth from a feature nobody tracks. In health, the obvious cause of fatigue might be sleep, but the real driver could be micronutrient absorption. Questioning the assumed source is how you find leverage points that competitors miss.
