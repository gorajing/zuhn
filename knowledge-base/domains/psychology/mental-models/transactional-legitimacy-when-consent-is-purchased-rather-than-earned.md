Singapore reveals a mental model where political consent operates more like a subscription than a commitment. When 64% of citizens explicitly prioritize economic development over democracy, they are revealing that their support is conditional and transactional. The PAP's 2015 decision to prioritize housing maintenance for its voters made this exchange explicit.

This transactional model has a fascinating consequence: voters become strategic rather than sincere. Knowing the PAP will win regardless, Singaporeans use their vote as a 'polite protest' — sending calibrated signals without risking actual change. The PAP calls this 'irrational voter behavior' but it is perfectly rational within a system where votes are messages, not choices. This mental model applies to any relationship — employer/employee, platform/user — where one party has overwhelming structural power and the other optimizes within those constraints.
