Aerogel is naturally hydrophilic — it absorbs water readily. But when roughly 30% of its surface hydroxyl groups are replaced with hydrophobic methyl groups, the entire material becomes superhydrophobic, repelling water completely. This is not a gradual transition; it's a phase flip triggered by a minority change.

This maps to a powerful mental model: you don't need to change everything to reverse a system's behavior. In organizations, products, or habits, there may be a critical threshold — often surprisingly small — where modifying a fraction of the inputs completely transforms the output. The lesson is to look for these leverage points rather than attempting exhaustive overhauls. The 30% threshold challenges the intuition that big changes require proportionally big efforts.
