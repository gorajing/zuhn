When you evaporate liquid from a gel, surface tension at the liquid-gas interface pulls the solid structure inward, destroying it. Samuel Kistler's 1931 breakthrough wasn't to find a gentler way to evaporate — it was to eliminate the liquid-gas boundary entirely by pushing the fluid past its critical point, where the distinction between liquid and gas ceases to exist. No interface means no surface tension means no structural collapse.

This is a transferable problem-solving pattern: instead of optimizing within a constraint, ask whether the constraint itself can be dissolved. Many engineering and business problems persist because solvers accept the frame ('how do we carefully remove the liquid?') rather than questioning whether the frame itself is the obstacle ('what if liquid and gas aren't different things?'). The supercritical extraction mental model says: find the phase boundary causing your problem and see if you can make it disappear.
