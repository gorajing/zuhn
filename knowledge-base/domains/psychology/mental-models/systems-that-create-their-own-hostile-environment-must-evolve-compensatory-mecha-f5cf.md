RuBisCo was designed for a low-oxygen world. Photosynthesis itself flooded the atmosphere with oxygen, creating the very conditions that make RuBisCo malfunction via photorespiration — grabbing O2 instead of CO2 roughly half the time, producing toxic phosphoglycolate. Rather than evolving a better enzyme, plants evolved detoxification pathways (converting phosphoglycolate to glycine and usable Calvin cycle compounds), C4 photosynthesis (physically separating RuBisCo from oxygen), and CAM photosynthesis (temporal separation).

This is a universal systems pattern: success creates conditions that undermine the mechanism of success. Startups that grow fast create coordination overhead that slows them down. Social platforms that attract users create noise that degrades content quality. The response is almost always compensatory — adding layers of management, building recommendation algorithms — rather than redesigning the core. Recognizing this pattern early lets you ask: are we fixing the root cause or just building increasingly elaborate workarounds around a fundamentally flawed core process?
