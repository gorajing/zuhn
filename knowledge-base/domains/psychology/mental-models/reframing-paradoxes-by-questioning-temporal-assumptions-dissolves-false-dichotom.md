For decades, physicists debated two endpoints: information is destroyed (requiring new physics) or information is hidden (technically preserved but inaccessible). The breakthrough came from a third path — examining the intermediate process. Black holes store information on their event horizon surface during their lifetime, and Hawking radiation can carry it away. The paradox was never about the endpoints; it was about the process.

This pattern recurs in strategy and decision-making. When two options seem irreconcilable, the resolution often lies in examining the transition between states rather than the states themselves. 'Should we pivot or persist?' may be a false dichotomy — the answer might be in what you do with the current product while exploring the next one. Reframing from 'what is the final state' to 'what happens during the process' is a reliable paradox-dissolving technique.
