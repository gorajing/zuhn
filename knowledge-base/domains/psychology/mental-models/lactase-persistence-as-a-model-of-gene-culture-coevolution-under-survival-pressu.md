Around 11,000 years ago, the domestication of dairy animals created selection pressure: individuals who retained the lactase enzyme into adulthood could access a calorie-dense food source from animals eating otherwise useless vegetation. This advantage was strong enough to drive genetic change — lactase persistence arose independently in multiple populations and spread through migration of dairy-farming communities. Today, 65% of the global population loses lactase production after infancy, with rates varying dramatically by region (up to 90% in East Asia, much lower in Northern Europe).

This is a powerful mental model: cultural innovations don't just change behavior — they change biology. The feedback loop between technology adoption and genetic selection operates on surprisingly short evolutionary timescales when survival pressure is strong enough. It challenges the assumption that human genetics are 'fixed' relative to cultural change.
