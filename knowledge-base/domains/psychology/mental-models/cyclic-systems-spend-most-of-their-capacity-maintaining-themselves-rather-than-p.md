In the Calvin cycle, the apparent output (6 G3P molecules) dramatically overstates the actual yield. Five out of six G3Ps must be fed back into the cycle to regenerate the three RuBP starter molecules—consuming 9 ATP and 6 NADPH in the process. Only one G3P escapes as usable product. The cycle's primary activity, measured by molecular throughput, is sustaining itself.

This is a general property of cyclic systems that's easy to underestimate. A business team running sprint retrospectives, story pointing, and backlog grooming may be spending 60–70% of its capacity on cycle maintenance. A writing system that requires extensive outline-revision-review loops before publishing may have a similar overhead ratio. The output looks like the whole cycle, but the sustaining work is invisible.

When evaluating or designing cyclic systems, the relevant metric is not 'how much does one iteration produce' but 'what fraction of the cycle's total work escapes as net output versus returns as input to the next iteration.' High-leverage interventions usually reduce recycling overhead rather than speeding up individual steps.
