When 7 billion people jump, Earth moves away by 1/100th of a hydrogen atom — then immediately returns when everyone lands. Newton's Third Law guarantees symmetric displacement. The jump looks dramatic but is structurally incapable of producing lasting change because the return is built into the action itself.

This principle extends beyond physics into decision-making and strategy. Many organizational initiatives are structural equivalents of everyone jumping: dramatic coordinated effort that, because the return-to-baseline is built in, produces zero net displacement. Temporary promotions, one-off training events, and flash campaigns all share this reversibility structure. Lasting change requires asymmetric actions — ones where the system cannot simply snap back. The Japan earthquake permanently redistributed mass (1.8 microseconds shorter days forever), while the jump redistributed mass temporarily. When evaluating whether an action will produce durable results, ask whether the reversal is structurally coupled to the action itself.
