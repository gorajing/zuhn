Chamath's three concrete boundary conditions for living by process rather than objectives:

(1) **No debt** — debt forces short-term optimization (paychecks, money objectives) that displaces long-term learning and risk-taking. Especially insidious for younger generations because social media presents debt-financed 'fake life' as aspirational.

(2) **Humility** — be 'extremely truthful about the way things are today' so you can see reality, share truth with others, find solidarity. Self-deception is the standard failure mode.

(3) **Younger people** — surround yourself with people younger than you because their biases and frameworks are different. The default reflex is 'I've learned enough not to be told I'm wrong,' but everything you know is stuck in a moment in time and will decay. Younger people serve as an early warning system for the future.

For builders: this maps onto founder-mentor relationships, advisor selection, even product user research. The diversity of perspective from younger people prevents the 'I've figured this out' trap. For Zuhn editorially: this is a useful frame for personal and organizational longevity — the discipline isn't avoiding mistakes, it's maintaining the conditions under which mistakes get caught early.
