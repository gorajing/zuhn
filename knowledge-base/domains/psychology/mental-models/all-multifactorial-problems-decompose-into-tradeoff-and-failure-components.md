When analyzing any multifactorial negative outcome — poverty, illness, relationship failure, product defects — the contributing causes naturally sort into two megacategories: tradeoffs (where the negative is a side effect of pursuing some other good) and failures (where something simply went wrong with no compensating advantage). This decomposition is useful because the two types demand different interventions: failures call for fixing or prevention, while tradeoffs call for re-evaluating priorities or accepting costs. Scott Alexander argues this is so fundamental it should feel obvious once stated, yet people routinely confuse the two — assuming a condition must be all-tradeoff or all-failure when it's almost always both.
