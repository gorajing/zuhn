For 150 years, Darwinian gradualism held that evolution proceeds through slow accumulation of small mutations. Eldredge and Gould's 1970 punctuated equilibrium theory argued that the fossil record instead shows long stasis interrupted by sudden bursts at branch points. This was controversial because there was no clean mathematical framework to detect the pattern rigorously.

Jordan Douglas's 2025 model uses Bayesian phylogenetic analysis to measure branch lengths in family trees and detect 'saltative branching' — rapid evolution concentrated at nodes where lineages split, followed by independent rapid adjustment, then stability. Tested across 13 biological systems plus Indo-European languages, saltative branching appears everywhere.

The generalization matters: the same mathematical pattern describes molecular enzymes, multicellular body plans, and cultural artifacts like language. This suggests a universal dynamic — systems tend toward stability interrupted by rapid reorganization at decision points — rather than gradualism. For thinking about change in any complex adaptive system (including technology, organizations, markets), expect long equilibria punctuated by rapid branching transitions, not smooth curves.
