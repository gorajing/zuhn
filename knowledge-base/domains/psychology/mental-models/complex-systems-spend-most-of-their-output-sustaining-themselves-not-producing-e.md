In the Calvin Cycle, 3 molecules of RuBP are processed through carbon fixation, reduction, and regeneration to produce 6 molecules of G3P (the useful sugar precursor). But 5 of those 6 G3Ps must be fed back into the cycle to regenerate the 3 RuBP starting molecules. Only 1 G3P — roughly 17% of total output — actually leaves the system as net product.

This ratio appears across complex systems: most organizational energy goes to coordination, not production. Most code in a mature codebase serves infrastructure, not features. Most of a city's budget goes to maintaining existing services, not building new ones. Most of a human's caloric intake sustains existing cells rather than building new tissue.

The insight is that high overhead ratios in complex systems are not waste to be eliminated — they're structural requirements. When someone promises to 'cut the fat' from a complex system, ask whether they understand which 5/6 of the output is load-bearing. Naive efficiency drives that cut regeneration capacity will collapse the entire cycle.
