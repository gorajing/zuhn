Paine's central methodological claim is that Americans analyze wars and international affairs like watching only one half of a tennis court: they study what their team did or didn't do and treat that as sufficient causation. Football analysts, by contrast, study both sides down to individual players. Pearl Harbor, Japanese fighting-to-the-last-man, the Iraq post-conventional insurgency — all were 'surprises' only because the analysts never seriously modeled the other side's internal logic.

The fix is Sun Tzu's and Clausewitz's net assessment: political, military, geographic, economic factors of all sides — plus, Paine argues, culture. The trap to avoid is mirror imaging: deciding what the other side will do based on what you would do. 'If I'm not supposed to generalize from my own experience, what am I supposed to do instead?' The answer is to reconstruct the adversary's belief system on its own terms, even when it looks irrational from yours. Calling the other side 'stupid' feels clever but explains nothing.

This generalizes far beyond warfare. Any decision that turns on predicting an adversary, a competitor, a counterparty, or a foreign customer is vulnerable to half-court tennis. The corrective discipline is: before acting, write down the other side's view of the situation in their vocabulary, with their constraints, and ask whether your plan still makes sense against that model.
