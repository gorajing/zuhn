The distinction matters because it changes both the expected violence and the point of no return. A political revolution swaps out the government and can, in principle, be reversed by another political revolution — the losing faction still exists. A social revolution aims to permanently remove categories of people (landlords, kulaks, intellectuals, ethnic groups), and once executed it cannot be undone because the target population is gone.

Analysts who collapse the two into 'revolution' routinely underpredict the body count. They model regime change and get mass graves. The lecture's numbers make this concrete: civilian deaths under Mao exceed all deaths in World War II — not because of an unusually violent political transition, but because the project was categorically different in scope.

Application: when any actor — political, corporate, ideological — describes their program as wanting to 'eliminate,' 'wipe out,' or 'cleanse' a category of people or institutions, update toward social-revolution scale of violence rather than political-revolution scale. The rhetorical signal is usually accurate about intended scope, and the historical record shows the scale it implies.
