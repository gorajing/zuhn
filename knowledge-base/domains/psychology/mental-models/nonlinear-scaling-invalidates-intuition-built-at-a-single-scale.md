The square-cube law means that when an object scales linearly, its surface area grows as the square and volume as the cube. This creates a fundamental mismatch between how we intuit scaling and how it actually works — a 10x longer animal has 1000x the mass but only 100x the surface area to manage it.

This principle applies far beyond biology. Startups that work at 10 users break at 1000 because operational load scales cubically while capacity scales quadratically. Infrastructure costs, communication overhead, and failure modes don't grow linearly with team or user count. The core insight is that any system designed at one scale carries hidden assumptions about which forces dominate, and those assumptions silently become wrong as scale changes.
