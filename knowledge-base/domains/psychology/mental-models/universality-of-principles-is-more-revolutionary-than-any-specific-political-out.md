Despite cycling through constitutional monarchy, republic, terror, directory, and empire, the French Revolution's most enduring contribution was the Declaration of Rights of Man's assertion that laws derive from the general will and must apply equally to all. Zhou Enlai's famous remark that it's 'too soon to say' what the revolution's effects were captures how paradigm-level ideas operate on timescales far beyond their originating events.

This is a powerful mental model for evaluating impact: specific implementations (products, companies, governments) come and go, but the underlying paradigm shift persists. The American Revolution preserved existing power structures while the French Revolution, despite its chaotic execution, introduced universal principles that reshaped global political thought. When evaluating whether work is truly transformative, ask whether it changes the paradigm or just the implementation. Paradigm changes compound over centuries; implementation changes are replaced within decades.
