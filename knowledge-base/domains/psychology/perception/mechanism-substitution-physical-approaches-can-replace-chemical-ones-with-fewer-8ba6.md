Traditional waterproofing relies on chemical coatings — waxes, silicones, fluoropolymers — that degrade over time and can have environmental or health side effects. Aerogel achieves waterproofing through physical structure: its nanoscale pores trap air, creating a physical barrier that prevents water from contacting the surface. The Cassie-Baxter state means water sits on trapped air rather than the material itself.

This illustrates a broader problem-solving heuristic: when you're stuck optimizing a chemical or additive solution, ask whether the same outcome can be achieved through structural or physical means. Physical mechanisms tend to be more durable (no chemical degradation), more elegant (fewer side effects), and often more surprising in their effectiveness. This reframing — from 'what substance do I add?' to 'what structure do I create?' — opens solution spaces that chemical thinking alone would miss.
