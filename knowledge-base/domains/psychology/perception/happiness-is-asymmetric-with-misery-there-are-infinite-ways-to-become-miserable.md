Keating frames happiness through the lens of thermodynamic entropy: there are vastly more disordered states (ways to be miserable) than ordered states (ways to be happy). Doubling your followers, money, or status produces diminishing returns via the hedonic treadmill. But losing a child, a relationship, or your health can devastate you in ways that no amount of gain can counterbalance.

This maps directly to Nassim Taleb's via negativa principle and the 'loser's game' concept in investing. The practical implication: identify the things whose loss would devastate you, and prioritize protecting them over chasing marginal improvements. Keating uses Jim Simons as an example — one of the richest people on earth who lost two adult sons. No one would trade places with him if they had to take everything. The asymmetry between upside and downside means that defensive strategy (reducing fragility) typically matters more than offensive strategy (maximizing gains).
