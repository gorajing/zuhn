The team suspected the baseball was supersonic based on sound and destruction, but couldn't confirm it or measure the Mach number until they set up Schlieren photography — a specialized optical technique that makes density changes in air visible. The Schlieren image revealed the detached bow shock and Mach cone angle, which provided both confirmation and precise velocity data. The 'indisputable two stick method' (measuring time between two known positions at 28,500 fps) gave a second independent verification.

This principle applies broadly: many important phenomena in business, health, and engineering are 'invisible' not because they don't exist but because we lack the right measurement apparatus. Teams often operate on theoretical models ('we think users are churning because...') when purpose-built instrumentation (session recordings, funnel analytics, cohort analysis) would make the actual dynamics visible and actionable. The Schlieren setup cost relatively little compared to the cannon itself, but it transformed the project from 'impressive stunt' to 'actual science.'
