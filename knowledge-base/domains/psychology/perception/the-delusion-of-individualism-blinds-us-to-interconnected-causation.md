Klaas identifies what he calls 'the delusion of individualism' — the Western obsession with believing we're in control of our lives. This is at odds with both Eastern philosophy (which emphasizes relational interconnection) and scientific fact (chaos theory, complex systems).

The comfort of believing you're in control comes at a cost: you mislearn from experience. When something goes wrong and you ascribe intentionality or 'reason' to it, you've extracted the wrong lesson. When politicians claim they can control the economy or election outcomes, they're operating under the same delusion. Accepting the reality of interconnected causation doesn't mean passivity — it means acting with awareness that your influence radiates unpredictably through complex systems.
