Historical and modern evidence consistently shows censorship is self-defeating. The Romans' execution of Jesus was instrumental in creating Christianity, which grew larger than Judaism. Athens' execution of Socrates cemented his status as the most eminent philosopher of all time. Barbra Streisand's attempt to remove a photo of her mansion turned it from hundreds of viewers into millions.

The mechanism is psychological: people are both more interested in prohibited ideas and more likely to believe ideas that are being suppressed. This creates a perverse incentive structure where the act of censoring signals importance and credibility. The practical implication is that the messy, sometimes ugly public sphere of free speech is a cost worth bearing because the alternative -- censorship -- reliably achieves the opposite of its intended effect.
