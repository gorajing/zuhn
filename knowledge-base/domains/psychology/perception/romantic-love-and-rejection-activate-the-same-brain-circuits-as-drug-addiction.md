Fisher's brain scanning of people rejected in love revealed activity in three critical regions: the VTA (they still love the person), a physical pain center (the same region activated by toothaches, but the pain persists for months), and most importantly the nucleus accumbens — the brain region central to addiction to cocaine, heroin, alcohol, cigarettes, and gambling.

Subsequent analysis of happily-in-love subjects confirmed the same addiction circuitry. This means romantic love — both fulfilled and rejected — is literally an addiction at the neurological level. Fisher argues this has been profoundly underappreciated in clinical practice: therapists who casually advise patients to 'just leave' abusive partners fail to recognize they are asking someone to overcome an addiction as powerful as heroin. She advocates treating romantic rejection with the clinical seriousness and specific therapeutic approaches used for substance addiction, potentially drawing from AA principles.
