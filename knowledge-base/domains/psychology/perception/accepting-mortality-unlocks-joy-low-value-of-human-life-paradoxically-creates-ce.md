Kimbal describes a paradox from apartheid South Africa: in a society where human life had low value and violence was mundane, there was simultaneously a heightened capacity for joy and celebration. When people accept that life can end at any moment, they stop deferring happiness and fully inhabit the present.

This maps to the Stoic practice of memento mori — contemplating death not as morbid exercise but as liberation. The practical application is that explicitly accepting mortality (rather than living in anxious denial) frees you to enjoy every moment without the attachment and anxiety that comes from treating life as infinitely extendable. Kimbal argues we should 'live longer with the goal of joy, not some form of misery you choose to attach yourself to.'
