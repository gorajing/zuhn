The giant impact hypothesis elegantly explains the Moon's angular momentum, iron depletion, and evidence of being once-molten. But oxygen isotope analysis reveals Earth and Moon rocks are identical at 2-3 parts per million — and this identity extends across multiple elements. Since physics simulations predict a Moon made 70%+ from the impactor Theia, this isotopic match is essentially impossible unless Theia was an Earth twin.

The tension illustrates a fundamental principle: when a beautiful theory conflicts with precise measurement, measurement wins. Researchers keep taking the Apollo rocks out of storage, running them through more precise instruments, and the identity only gets stronger. This pattern applies to any domain where elegant models meet empirical reality — AI benchmarks that look clean but fail in production, economic theories that work in simulation but not in markets, or startup strategies that are logical but fail customer validation.
