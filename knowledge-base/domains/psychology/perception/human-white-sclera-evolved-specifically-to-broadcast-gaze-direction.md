Most predators have camouflaged eyes that hide where they're looking — visible gaze direction would betray their hunting strategy. Humans evolved the opposite: conspicuously white scleras that make the direction of our gaze instantly readable to others. This is paired with dedicated neural networks containing individual neurons that fire specifically when someone is looking directly at us and stop firing with just one or two degrees of deviation.

This represents a fascinating evolutionary trade-off: humans sacrificed stealth for social coordination. The ability to silently communicate attention direction — 'look where I'm looking' — is foundational to cooperative hunting, teaching, and joint attention. It's a biological reminder that for social species, transparency can be more advantageous than concealment. This principle extends beyond biology: in organizations and teams, making intentions visible (transparent roadmaps, open communication) often outperforms strategic opacity.
