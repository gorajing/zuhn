Harris — himself one of the 'New Atheists' — acknowledges that secular culture needs things it doesn't have. The trend toward secularism has been strong, but the institutions that replaced churches, synagogues, and mosques are inadequate. Guy groups, sports fandom, entertainment, book clubs — none of these replicate what a central community building that everyone goes to on Sunday for a century provides. The 'spiritual but not religious' piecemeal approach often doesn't make sense either. Harris identifies the specific components that are missing: a sense of the sacred that isn't embarrassing or irrational, community infrastructure, strong ethical instruction, and contemplative practice. This is notable because it comes from someone who spent years arguing against religion — it's an admission that winning the intellectual argument doesn't solve the institutional problem. The gap is not in ideas but in physical and social infrastructure. This has implications for anyone building communities, products, or institutions: the demand for meaning, belonging, and ethical frameworks is real and largely unmet by secular alternatives.
