Graham identifies a damaging asymmetry: adults conceal their own moral and intellectual failings while simultaneously demanding high standards from children. Kids then walk around feeling 'horribly evil for having used a swearword, while in fact most of the adults around them are doing much worse things.' This creates a systematic distortion where children compare their full, unfiltered inner experience — including every shameful thought and minor transgression — against adults' carefully curated public behavior. The result is chronic feelings of inadequacy that can persist well into adulthood. Graham extends this to intellectual confidence: less confident people feel compelled to always have answers, while truly confident people readily say 'I don't know.' The school system amplifies this by presenting teachers as omniscient authorities, making the eventual discovery of their limitations feel like a betrayal rather than a normal part of growing up.
