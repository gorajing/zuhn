The shadow blister effect — where two shadows appear to bulge toward each other before touching — demonstrates how readily we perceive absence as a positive entity with behavior. Shadows don't travel; they are cross-sections of three-dimensional regions where light is blocked. The 'motion' we see is actually new light filling or vacating gaps at the speed of light.

This reification bias extends well beyond optics. In economics, we treat debt as a 'thing' rather than an absence of assets. In organizations, we discuss 'culture problems' as entities rather than gaps in specific behaviors. Recognizing when we've reified an absence into an object can prevent us from trying to 'move' or 'fix' something that has no independent existence — the real intervention is always on the positive phenomenon (the light, the assets, the behaviors) rather than the shadow.
