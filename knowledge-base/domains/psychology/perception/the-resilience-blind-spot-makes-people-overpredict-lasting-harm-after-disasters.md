Bonanno coined 'the resilience blind spot' to describe a phenomenon he observed after 9/11, Superstorm Sandy, COVID-19, and the Japan tsunami. During large-scale disasters, many people are affected simultaneously, creating shared anxiety amplified by social contagion, media coverage, and interpersonal conversations.

The key mechanism is affective forecasting failure (from Dan Gilbert's research): when we feel strong emotion, we cannot imagine it is temporary. We put on blinders against the possibility that we will be okay. This leads to systematic overprediction of lasting trauma at both individual and institutional levels — driving over-intervention, over-diagnosis, and resource misallocation. Understanding this blind spot allows for more measured, evidence-based responses to collective adversity.
