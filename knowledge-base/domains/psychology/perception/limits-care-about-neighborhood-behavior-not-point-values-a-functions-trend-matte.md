The course opens its treatment of limits with the sushi bar example: food costs $10/pound, except exactly 1 pound is free. The price function has a limit of $10 as weight approaches 1 pound, even though the actual price at exactly 1 pound is $0. This immediately establishes a counterintuitive principle: the limit is about the trend, not the destination. A function can have a hole, a jump, or even be completely undefined at a point, and still have a well-defined limit there.

This mathematical insight maps directly onto how we should evaluate systems, people, and trajectories. A company's current revenue (the point value) matters less than its revenue trajectory (the limit). A person's current skill level is less informative than the direction and rate of their growth. In investing, the current stock price is the 'point value' while the trend is the 'limit.' The mathematical formalism of limits gives precise language to the intuition that trends dominate snapshots. It also warns about three failure modes: left-right disagreement (the trend depends on which direction you approach from), unbounded behavior (vertical asymptotes), and wild oscillation — all of which have real-world analogues in discontinuous systems.
