When Malice mentions writing a book about North Korea, 90% of people immediately start telling him everything they know about the subject — despite knowing almost nothing. He describes a cultural norm where admitting 'I don't know anything about that, tell me more' feels like a social failure.

This is an increasingly important insight in the age of social media and AI-generated content: the pressure to opine on everything dilutes the value of genuine expertise. The ability to say 'I don't know' is itself a form of intelligence. For anyone in knowledge work, content creation, or leadership, the discipline of staying silent on topics outside your competence isn't just humility — it preserves the credibility of your actual expertise and creates space for learning from those who genuinely know more.
