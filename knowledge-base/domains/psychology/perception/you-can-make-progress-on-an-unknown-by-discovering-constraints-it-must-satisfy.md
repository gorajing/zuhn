Sanderson demonstrates a powerful problem-solving pattern with the integral function A(x): 'We don't know what A(x) is, that remains a mystery. But we do know a property that this mystery function must have' — namely, that dA/dx ≈ x². By discovering this constraint (the derivative relationship), the unknown function becomes determinable through reverse-engineering.

This is the indirect approach to hard problems: instead of computing the answer, compute what the answer must look like. Constraints narrow the search space. Enough constraints uniquely determine the solution. This pattern appears everywhere — in engineering (specify the behavior, derive the mechanism), in hiring (define the outcomes, find who produces them), in strategy (identify the constraints, the strategy follows).
