The video builds a crescendo of awe-inspiring future events — Betelgeuse supernovas, the Milky Way–Andromeda collision, skies glowing with newborn stars — seemingly to maximize FOMO. Then it inverts the frame: those future beings will never see total solar eclipses, Niagara Falls, or Saturn's rings. They'll wish they'd been born in our time to witness the birth of the Internet.

This symmetry reveals that temporal FOMO is a cognitive illusion. No matter where you sit on the timeline, you are simultaneously missing spectacular things and witnessing others that will never recur. The practical takeaway is that FOMO about the future (or nostalgia for the past) is structurally identical — both distort present-moment appreciation by anchoring to an inaccessible reference point.

This reframe is useful for decision-making: when you catch yourself envying a future state (a better product, a more mature market, a later career stage), remember that your current position has irreplaceable advantages that will erode with time — just like Niagara Falls eroding one foot per year.
