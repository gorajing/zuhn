Harris introduces a powerful metaphor: autodidact commentators like Dave Smith play tennis without a net. They can hit the ball as hard as they want, claim expert-level authority, debate on the largest platforms — but when they're wrong, they simply pull the ripcord and say 'I'm just a comedian, not an expert.' There is no reputational penalty in their ecosystem for being wrong. Meanwhile, genuine experts like Douglas Murray play with the net up — every factual error, every misattributed source carries real professional consequences. The audience doesn't notice this asymmetry. They see both players hitting the ball equally hard and assume the game is equivalent. This creates a structural incentive problem in public discourse: the people with the least to lose from being wrong are the most aggressive in their claims, while those with the most expertise are constrained by the very standards that make them credible. This pattern applies far beyond politics — it appears in any domain where credentialed experts compete for attention with confident non-experts.
