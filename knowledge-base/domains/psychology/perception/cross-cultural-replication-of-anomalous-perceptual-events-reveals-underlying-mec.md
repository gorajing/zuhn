The 1917 Fatima sun miracle — spinning, color-shifting sun seen by ~100,000 — has long been cited as empirical proof of Catholic faith. But a 1998 event at Thailand's Dhammakaya Temple reproduced nearly identical phenomena (spinning sun, color shifts, vivid hallucinations) in a crowd of 20,000 Buddhists with no connection to the Catholic tradition.

This cross-cultural replication is epistemically powerful: it rules out tradition-specific divine intervention as the cause, since both traditions can't be 'right' in their specific theological claims. Instead, it strongly suggests a shared human perceptual mechanism — likely related to kasina-style meditation on bright light — that produces predictable visual effects (spinning, color change, pareidolic visions) regardless of the observer's religious framework. The specific content of hallucinations (Virgin Mary vs. monk Luang Pu Sodh) maps to each group's priming, but the structural phenomenon is identical.

This is a clean example of how independent replication disambiguates between competing explanations: coincidence, culture-specific miracle, or universal mechanism. The replication kills the first two.
