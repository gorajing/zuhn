Graham makes a sharp distinction between two modes of engaging with art that split apart in the twentieth century. In Paris, looking through windows at dusk, you see people who genuinely care about what paintings look like — art as stuff. In New York, the center of the art business, buyers primarily evaluate art as brand — requiring only a 'sufficiently identifiable style' to anchor value, with actual visual quality being secondary.

This explains the paradox of why the world's art market capital isn't the world's art appreciation capital. The American pattern of art engagement, even among sophisticated buyers, 'rarely gets past judging it by the brand name of the artist.' This is a specific instance of a broader pattern: when money becomes the dominant evaluation metric in a domain, it tends to replace the domain's native quality signals with financial proxy signals. Paris's lower position in the art market but higher position in genuine aesthetic engagement suggests these two things are not just different but potentially in tension.
