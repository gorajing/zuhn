Carlson describes a specific mechanism by which surveillance destroys democratic function: the NSA accessed his Signal messages, knew about his Putin interview plans before he'd told anyone publicly, and intelligence agencies leaked his private meeting with Snowden to Semafor. The issue isn't the surveillance itself but the selective weaponization — agencies choose what to leak and to whom, effectively controlling narratives about specific individuals.

This is structurally different from both overt censorship (which is visible and can be opposed) and mass data collection (which is passive). The active step — intelligence services leaking to friendly journalists to shape political outcomes — creates a system where media appears independent but functions as 'an auxiliary of the national security state.' Two independent sources with decades of intelligence experience confirmed to Carlson that 'nothing is secure, everything is monitored all the time' by state actors. The practical implication is that privacy as a prerequisite for freedom is not a theoretical concern but an operational reality: without private communication, opposition to government policy becomes structurally disadvantaged.
