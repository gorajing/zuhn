Johnson argues that the public fixation on psychedelic 'hallucinations' (which technically are not true hallucinations — reality testing remains intact) obscures the actual mechanism of change. Visual effects are easy to describe, so they dominate lay understanding. But the therapeutic power comes from deeper effects on self-identification — how people define themselves and reality.

When someone's sense of identity completely dissolves, they can emerge with a fundamentally different relationship to their problems. An addict who has convinced themselves 'I'm just a failure' can experience their addiction as 'small potatoes' after a mystical experience. Depression's self-fulfilling prophecy breaks when the self that was prophecying dissolves. This is why therapeutic protocols use eye shades — to redirect attention from external perceptual effects toward internal identity-level experiences.
