Peterson makes the case that beauty is not subjective decoration but evidence of something fundamental about reality's structure. He uses the burning bush metaphor: the fire that doesn't consume the tree represents the optimal balance between transformation and structure, and what attracts Moses to it is precisely its beauty. Great art, like great cathedrals built atop plague bones, compresses layers of meaning that 'reach into the back tendrils of your mind at levels you can't even comprehend.'

The practical implication challenges the common dismissal of aesthetic experience as mere preference. Peterson points out that even self-proclaimed secular people make pilgrimages to European cathedrals and pay enormous sums for classic paintings — behaviors that function identically to religious worship. Religious images have been 'unpacked for 4,000 years and still have' more to reveal. This suggests that treating beauty as data rather than decoration could inform better decision-making in design, architecture, and even scientific inquiry — the most enduring and valuable human creations are those that achieve the balance between order and chaos that registers as beauty.
