Tetlock tracked experts for 20 years and found that 'hedgehogs' — experts who relate everything to a single central vision and speak with tremendous confidence — performed worst at prediction, sometimes below random chance. 'Foxes' — skeptical of big theories, comfortable with complexity and uncertainty — were more accurate but terrible media guests because they say things like 'it depends.'

This creates a systemic filter: the experts who appear on podcasts and media are disproportionately hedgehogs because confidence is entertaining and frameworks are sellable. The practical implication is a strong prior against anyone offering 'the formula for success' or '33 laws of business.' Real expertise breeds humility; ignorance breeds confidence (Dunning-Kruger). When evaluating advice, weight the tentative, nuanced thinkers over the charismatic formula-givers.
