The McGill study tested accessory-assisted placebo on children with real clinical conditions. A 12-year-old with dermatillomania showed dramatic skin improvement and stopped picking. A boy with daily post-concussion migraines became completely migraine-free for the first time in nearly two years. A child with ADHD and impulse control disorder showed immediate behavioral improvements — sleeping during the day (unusual for him), thinking before acting, and staying off medication all summer.

What makes this remarkable is that conventional treatments had already failed these children. The placebo worked where real medicine hadn't — suggesting that for certain conditions, the bottleneck isn't pharmacological but psychological. The children's brains always had the capacity to regulate these symptoms; they just needed a convincing framework to 'give permission' for healing. This has implications for pediatric treatment design: structured suggestion protocols could serve as adjuncts or even alternatives to medication for conditions with strong psychosomatic components.
