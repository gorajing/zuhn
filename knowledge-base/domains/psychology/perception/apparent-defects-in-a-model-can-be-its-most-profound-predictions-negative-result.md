When Dirac presented his equation in 1928, the negative energy solutions provoked a crisis. Heisenberg called it 'the saddest chapter in modern physics' and tried to abandon quantum physics entirely. Pauli left physics to write a utopian novel. The entire establishment viewed these solutions as a fatal flaw proving the equation was wrong.

But Dirac spent three years defending his equation. In 1931, he proposed that the negative solutions predicted a new kind of particle — an anti-electron with the same mass but opposite charge. In 1932, Carl Anderson accidentally discovered exactly this particle (the positron) in cosmic ray tracks. The 'bug' was actually the most important feature.

This pattern recurs across disciplines: anomalies that seem to break a model are often signals pointing to deeper reality. The key distinction is between anomalies that arise from errors (wrong math, flawed assumptions) and anomalies that arise from the model being more right than you expected. Dirac's negative energies fell into the second category — the math was telling him something true about nature that human intuition couldn't accept.
