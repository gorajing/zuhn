The BYU acoustics researcher explains that in a normal room, reverberations tell your auditory system about the size of the space around you. In an anechoic chamber, the complete absorption of echoes makes the room 'feel' like a coffin regardless of its actual size. This is a pure expectation-violation anxiety response — your brain expects reverb as confirmation of spatial openness, and when it's absent, it flags a mismatch.

This mechanism generalizes beyond acoustics: much of human anxiety stems not from genuine danger but from the absence of expected background signals. Removing familiar environmental cues (ambient noise, visual landmarks, social feedback) can produce discomfort disproportionate to actual risk. Understanding this distinction — threat vs. missing-signal anxiety — is practically useful for designing environments, managing anxiety, and distinguishing real warnings from false alarms.
