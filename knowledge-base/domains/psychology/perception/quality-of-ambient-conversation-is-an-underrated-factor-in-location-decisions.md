Graham surfaces an underappreciated heuristic for evaluating environments: the quality of ambient, involuntary information exposure. Walking through Cambridge at dusk, you see bookshelves through windows. Walking through Palo Alto, you see TV screens. A friend who moved to Silicon Valley complained that the 'eavesdropping quality' was terrible — which sounds eccentric until you realize that overheard conversations are an unfiltered signal of what the people around you actually care about.

This matters because a city's message is transmitted primarily through accident — things you can't opt out of rather than things you seek out. The implication is that when evaluating where to live or work, you should pay less attention to official narratives (rankings, branding, stated values) and more attention to the involuntary ambient signals: what do people talk about at dinner, what's visible in their homes, what do they wear, what do they get excited about? These unchoreographed signals are the actual mechanism through which environment shapes ambition.
