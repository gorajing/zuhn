Silverglate's provocative argument: 'I'm much more interested in knowing whom I should not turn my back on than I am interested in figuring out who loves me.' When hate speech is banned, hostility doesn't disappear — it goes underground where it becomes invisible and therefore more dangerous.

This inverts the standard framing. The usual argument for banning hate speech is protection from pain. Silverglate's counter: the emotional discomfort of hearing hate speech is the price of living in a free society, and it's a small price compared to the alternative — a world where you cannot identify who poses a genuine threat. For organizations and platforms: surfacing hostility is safer than hiding it.
