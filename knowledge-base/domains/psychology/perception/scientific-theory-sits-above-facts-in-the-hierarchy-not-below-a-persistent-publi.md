Carroll highlights a critical communication failure: the word 'theory' means the opposite in scientific versus everyday language. On the street, theory means 'my best guess.' In science, a theory is the highest category — assembled from hundreds of tested hypotheses and independent lines of evidence that cohere. Hypotheses are the guesses; theories are what survive rigorous testing.

This linguistic mismatch has real consequences. When people hear 'theory of evolution,' they parse it as 'guess about evolution,' undermining 160 years of evidence. The lesson extends beyond biology: whenever a technical term has a different everyday meaning, the communicator bears the burden of bridging that gap. Precision in language is not pedantic — it shapes what people believe.
