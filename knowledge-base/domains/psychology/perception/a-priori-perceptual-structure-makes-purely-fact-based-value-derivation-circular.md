Peterson identifies what may be the most fundamental challenge to Harris's 'moral landscape' project: you cannot perceive the world without a priori structure, and that structure has an inherently dogmatic element. The antique analogy makes this concrete — you can disassemble an antique searching for where its 'value' resides, but value exists in the interpersonal hierarchy of meaning, not in the physical substrate. Harris concedes this partly by locating value in 'beliefs and desires and conscious states of all the people involved.'

This has practical implications for anyone trying to make purely 'data-driven' decisions: the choice of what to measure, which metrics matter, and how to weight competing objectives always smuggles in prior values. Peterson's critique doesn't invalidate empiricism but reveals its limits — you can use facts to navigate between values, but you can't bootstrap the initial value framework from facts alone. This is directly relevant to AI alignment: the same circularity problem appears when trying to derive AI values from human behavioral data.
