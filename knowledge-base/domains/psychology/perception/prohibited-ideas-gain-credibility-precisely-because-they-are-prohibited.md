Beyond the attention-amplification of the Streisand Effect, there is a deeper psychological mechanism at work. When people learn that an idea is being suppressed, they often infer that the idea must contain some threatening truth — otherwise, why would anyone bother to censor it?

This creates a perverse dynamic where censorship functions as an implicit endorsement of the censored content's importance and truthfulness. The stronger the suppression, the stronger the inference of hidden truth. This psychological reactance means that censorship is not merely ineffective at reducing belief in an idea — it actively increases belief in it.
