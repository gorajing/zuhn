Rubin articulates a profound observation about human cognition: when we encounter something that doesn't make sense (a man running from a house), the brain instantly generates a plausible story ('he's chasing his dog') to resolve the ambiguity. This happens unconsciously and automatically. The critical insight is that we then treat this fabricated explanation as if it were observed fact — telling others later 'he was chasing his dog' with complete confidence, never realizing we invented the story.

Rubin extends this to a sweeping claim: 'Our whole lives are reacting to things, making up a story of what we think may have happened, without realizing that's what we're doing.' This connects to his wrestling metaphor — professional wrestling is 'closer to reality' because at least everyone acknowledges it's a performance, whereas everyday life involves the same storytelling without that acknowledgment. For creative practitioners, recognizing this confabulation tendency is essential: much of what we 'know' about what works, what audiences want, and why past projects succeeded is likely post-hoc narrative rather than genuine understanding. This awareness creates space for the beginner's mind that Rubin considers essential to creativity.
