The common assumption is that G-force tolerance is purely cardiovascular — blood pools in the legs, brain loses oxygen, you black out. But Destin's actual failure point was his vestibular system. When the split-S maneuver caught him off guard and his head bobbled, the fluid in his semicircular canals was already spinning in one axis. The head movement introduced rotation in a second axis, creating a Coriolis cross-coupling effect where the brain receives conflicting rotational signals from multiple canals simultaneously.

NASA physiologist Sharon demonstrated this with a simple spinning chair: after sustained rotation, the canal fluid catches up with the walls, so stopping feels like spinning backwards. Adding head tilt during rotation floods the brain with contradictory signals from all three canal pairs. This is why Destin couldn't recover — it wasn't that his body was failing, it was that his brain's orientation system was giving impossible data. He recovered fully in just 3 minutes of straight-and-level flight, confirming it was vestibular, not cardiovascular.
