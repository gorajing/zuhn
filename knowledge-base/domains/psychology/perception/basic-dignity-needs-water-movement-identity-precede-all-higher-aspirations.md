Multiple Palestinians describe conditions that strip away the possibility of higher aspiration: waiting for water delivery, living in camps where 'in Europe the dog lives in better conditions,' inability to travel freely. One man simply wants 'to destroy this wall — you come to me, I come to you, that's it.'

This maps directly to Maslow's hierarchy but with a political dimension: when basic dignity needs are unmet, people cannot meaningfully engage with abstract frameworks about peace, democracy, or economic development. For anyone building in developing contexts — whether in business, technology, or policy — the lesson is that infrastructure enabling basic dignity (water, connectivity, mobility) must precede any attempt at higher-order transformation.
