At relativistic speeds, you begin catching up to light that was previously traveling away from you, dramatically expanding your field of view to nearly 360 degrees. This floods your visual field with new information from the sides and behind, which compresses the forward-facing scene into a smaller apparent area — making your target look like it's shrinking away.

This is a vivid example of how progress toward a goal can create perceptual signals that mimic regression. In complex systems, early-stage advances often look like setbacks because they expand the scope of what you can see. A startup entering a new market may feel further from product-market fit as they discover more customer segments. A learner may feel less competent as they uncover how much they don't know. The physics here provides a concrete mechanism: expanding your field of awareness compresses the apparent size of your original target.
