Before time zones, every meridian had its own solar noon — technically the most accurate local time. But this meant people a few kilometers apart would disagree on the time, making train schedules and telegraph coordination impossible. The solution was time zones: deliberately rounding local solar time to the nearest hour, sacrificing accuracy for coordination.

This trade-off — destroying local precision to enable global coordination — appears everywhere in system design. Standardized APIs lose expressiveness but enable interoperability. Common currencies sacrifice local economic control for trade fluidity. Even language standardization (dictionaries, grammar rules) trades regional precision of meaning for mutual intelligibility. The insight is that standardization is always a lossy compression of local reality, and the question is never 'should we standardize?' but 'how much local accuracy are we willing to sacrifice for how much coordination gain?'
