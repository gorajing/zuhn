Stan Lee's distillation of creative fulfillment has three components: doing what you want to do, doing it the way you think it should be done, and being able to look at it afterward and say 'I did that and I think it is pretty damn good.' This is not about ignoring feedback or being stubborn — Lee was responsive to what worked. It is about the starting point being internal conviction rather than external validation.

The Spider-Man story illustrates this perfectly. Lee gave Spider-Man personal problems and made him a teenager not because market research suggested it, but because he thought it would make the character more relatable and interesting. The creative choices that made Spider-Man revolutionary came from Lee following his own creative instinct against explicit expert opposition. The broader principle: when creative instinct and expert opinion diverge, the instinct often sees something the expert framework cannot account for — because the expert framework is built on what has already worked, not what could work.
