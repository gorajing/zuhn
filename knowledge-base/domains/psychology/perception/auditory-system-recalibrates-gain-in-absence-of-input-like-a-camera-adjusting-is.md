In an anechoic chamber, people report hearing their own heartbeat, blood flowing, and even a faint ringing. This isn't the brain malfunctioning — it's the auditory system doing exactly what it's designed to do: adjusting its sensitivity to match the environment. The mechanism is analogous to a camera raising its ISO in low light, or your eyes adapting to darkness.

This gain-control model explains why the experience feels strange but isn't dangerous. The sounds were always there; the brain was simply filtering them as irrelevant. When external reference points disappear, the perceptual floor drops and these signals cross the awareness threshold. This same principle operates in everyday life — you notice the refrigerator hum only after it stops, because removal of masking noise triggers recalibration. Understanding this mechanism demystifies sensory deprivation and reframes it as the brain's adaptive intelligence rather than its vulnerability.
