O'Connor argues the problem of suffering is the strongest counterargument to traditional theism, but carefully scopes its reach. The argument: if a loving, omnipotent God supervised the universe's creation and chose its parameters, why did that God select evolution by natural selection — survival of the fittest, which is simultaneously the destruction and suffering of the weakest — as the mechanism to produce human life?

For billions of years, 99.9% of all species have been wiped from existence, usually through predation and disease. If humans are the purpose of creation, all this animal suffering was built into the plan. This does not logically disprove God's existence — it is not contradictory to say God had reasons we cannot understand. But it is unexpected, and it specifically challenges the attribute of goodness. An agnostic position that acknowledges some foundational principle while remaining uncertain about its nature may be the most intellectually honest stance.
