Lukianoff explains that when you tell people they can't express an opinion, you don't change their mind — you just encourage them to stop sharing it with people who might challenge it. They retreat to echo chambers where group polarization takes off. This is what Tim Kuran calls 'preference falsification': society ends up with an inaccurate picture of what people actually think.

The practical consequence is that censorship creates a worse epistemic environment, not a better one. Activists who push for speech restrictions often end up thinking people are more prejudiced than they actually are, because they've created a system that hides true beliefs. For anyone building communities or platforms, the lesson is that surfacing disagreement — even uncomfortable disagreement — produces better outcomes than suppression.
