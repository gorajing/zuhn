Chinese historians were trained in Confucian classics that emphasized moral behavior as the key to maintaining power. They then wrote histories that proved this thesis, which became the curriculum for the next generation of historians. The circularity is elegant: scholars define what good governance looks like, train rulers in their framework, then evaluate rulers by that same framework.

This pattern appears everywhere institutions control their own evaluation criteria. Academic peer review, consulting firms that define the problems they solve, and platform companies that set the metrics by which they're judged all exhibit this same self-reinforcing circularity. The insight isn't that this is always malicious — it's that the people best positioned to describe a system are also the ones with the strongest incentive to describe it in ways that preserve their role within it.
