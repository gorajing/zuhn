One of Bonanno's three key myths to debunk is that people carry around hidden traumas that unconsciously cause difficulties. His response as a scientist is direct: where are they? We do not have biological mechanisms for trauma being stored and hidden in our bodies while continuing to cause harm.

What we do know is that the stress response creates fragmented, decontextualized memories — bits and pieces focused on survival-relevant details. These fragments are later reconstructed into narratives through normal memory consolidation and reconsolidation processes. Each time we remember, the memory changes. Resilient people's trauma memories become more benign over time, while those with lasting PTSD maintain disturbing memories. The narrative we construct after the event matters more than a mythical stored trauma.
