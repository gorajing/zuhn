O'Connor traces the chain of reasons behind any action: why are you working? To earn money. Why? To provide for your family. Why? To raise healthy children. Why? At some point, the person answers: 'What do you mean why? That is just what it is about.' This is the self-justifying principle — the point where the question 'why' feels not just unanswered but inappropriate.

For the religious, this bottoms out in God. For parents, in their children's wellbeing. For intellectuals, in the pursuit of understanding. The nihilist observes that any self-justifying principle could have been different — it is ultimately contingent on the person. But O'Connor notes this does not invalidate the experience: finding that principle, whatever it is, provides the functional experience of meaning. The practical implication is that meaning is not discovered as an objective feature of reality but constructed through finding something you care about so deeply that further justification feels unnecessary.
