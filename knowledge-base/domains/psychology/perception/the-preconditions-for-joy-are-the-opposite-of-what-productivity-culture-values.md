Bowler identifies the key preconditions for joy: emotional availability, connection, and openness to being surprised. A person who wants to be surprised is much more likely to find joy. But these preconditions are the direct opposite of what our machine selves optimize for: control, efficiency, predictability, screen engagement.

This creates a structural conflict. The more we optimize for productivity and control, the more we close ourselves off from joy. Joy requires being experientially surprisable, which means accepting vulnerability, putting down devices, and allowing unplanned moments. It is not a bonus level of happiness you earn through optimization; it is a fundamentally different state that requires letting go of optimization.
