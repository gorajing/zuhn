At normal playback speed, an AK-47 firing underwater appears as a single chaotic explosion. At 27,450 frames per second, three cleanly separated physical processes become visible, each with distinct timing, geometry, and governing equations. The phenomena were always there — only the observer's temporal resolution changed.

This has direct implications for debugging, diagnosis, and analysis in any domain. When you can't find the cause of a problem, the issue may not be complexity but resolution — you're not measuring at the right granularity. Adding logging at millisecond intervals, breaking quarterly metrics into weekly cohorts, or decomposing user sessions into micro-interactions all follow the same principle: increase temporal resolution to reveal causal layers that are invisible at coarser scales.
