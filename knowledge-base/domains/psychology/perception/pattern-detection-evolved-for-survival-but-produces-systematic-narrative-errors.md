From an evolutionary perspective, over-detecting patterns is safer than under-detecting them. If you hear rustling grass and assume a saber-tooth tiger, you survive whether or not one is actually there. But if you assume nothing and it IS a tiger, you die. This asymmetry means our brains are calibrated to find patterns even where none exist.

The cost in modern life is that we're 'allergic to the explanation that it was just arbitrary.' We stitch together neat A-to-B narratives from messy, contingent events. We understand this when thinking about the past (don't squish a bug during time travel!) but refuse to accept it in the present. The noise we're told to ignore is actually where many of the most consequential events in life take place.
