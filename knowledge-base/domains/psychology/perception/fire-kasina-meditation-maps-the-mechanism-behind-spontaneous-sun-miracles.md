Fire kasina is a Buddhist meditation practice involving sustained concentration on a flame or bright light source. Practitioners reliably report a specific sequence of visual phenomena: spinning lights, shifting color swatches, afterimage persistence, and eventually vivid hallucinations. These map almost exactly onto the reports from both Fatima and Dhammakaya.

Critically, the Dhammakaya tradition practices āloka kasina (bright light kasina) — classified by scholars as a variant of kasina practice, though practitioners wouldn't describe it that way. The crowd had been meditating immediately before the event. This creates a direct mechanistic link: they were already in a kasina-adjacent perceptual state when they turned to gaze at the sun, and the sun provided exactly the kind of bright-light stimulus that kasina practice uses to generate these effects.

The confidence is low because the specific neuroscience remains unclear — we don't fully understand why sustained attention on bright light produces these particular perceptual artifacts. But the phenomenological mapping between kasina practice, sungazer reports, and sun miracle testimonies is striking enough to constitute a strong hypothesis. If correct, 'sun miracles' are not mysterious at all — they're the predictable output of a known contemplative technique accidentally triggered at scale.
