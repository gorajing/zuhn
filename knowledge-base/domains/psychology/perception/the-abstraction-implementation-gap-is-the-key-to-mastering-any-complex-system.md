The course opens with a critical distinction: an abstract data type (ADT) defines what operations are available and what behavior to expect, while a data structure is the concrete implementation. A queue ADT can be implemented via arrays, singly linked lists, or doubly linked lists — each with different performance tradeoffs but identical external behavior.

This abstraction-implementation separation is a universal reasoning tool. In business, a 'customer support system' is an ADT — it could be implemented as a call center, chatbot, or community forum. In investing, 'exposure to emerging markets' is an ADT implementable through ETFs, individual stocks, or direct investment. Recognizing this gap lets you evaluate alternatives systematically: first define the interface you need, then compare implementations on their tradeoffs. The course demonstrates that excellent practitioners in any field are those who instinctively think in terms of 'what do I need?' before 'how do I build it?'
