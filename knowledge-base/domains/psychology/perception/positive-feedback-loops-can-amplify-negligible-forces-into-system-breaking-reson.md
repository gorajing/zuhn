Engineers designed the Millennium Bridge to avoid vertical resonance at human walking frequency (2 Hz), but missed that lateral forces at 1 Hz (half the walking frequency, matching left-foot-only cadence) could trigger wobble. Once the bridge moved slightly sideways, pedestrians instinctively adjusted their gait to stabilize themselves — inadvertently synchronizing their lateral forces and pumping more energy into the bridge. The system underwent a phase transition at exactly 166 people.

This illustrates a general principle: when a system inadvertently couples independent actors, forces that are individually negligible (lateral walking force is 1/10th of downward force) can compound catastrophically. The cause-effect relationship was inverted from expectations — it wasn't synchronized walking causing wobble, but wobble causing synchronized walking. This inversion pattern appears in markets (price drops causing panic selling causing more drops), social media (outrage causing engagement causing more outrage), and organizational dynamics.
