The difference quotient — (f(x+h) - f(x))/h — computes average rate of change over an interval h. The derivative is what happens as h approaches zero: the average rate becomes the instantaneous rate. This conceptual move from discrete samples to continuous truth is the foundational idea of calculus, and it has deep epistemological implications.

In practice, we almost never observe instantaneous rates directly. We measure quarterly revenue, weekly active users, monthly churn. The calculus insight is that by progressively shrinking the measurement window and observing the trend, we can infer the true instantaneous rate without ever measuring it directly. This is the mathematical formalization of iterative refinement — each shorter interval gives a better approximation, and the limit is the truth. The same principle applies to A/B testing (shorter test windows with more data approach the true conversion rate), sensor fusion (higher sample rates approach continuous signals), and skill development (tighter feedback loops approach real-time correction).
