Before the 1984 Hawaii conference, no scientist ranked any Moon origin theory as 'probable.' The giant impact hypothesis was not even on the survey. Yet it emerged as the consensus winner — not because evidence proved it, but because it failed less badly than alternatives at matching observable constraints like angular momentum and iron depletion.

This 'least bad theory' pattern is pervasive in science, business strategy, and technology decisions. Textbooks then present the consensus as settled truth, and the caveats disappear. The video captures this perfectly: 'There was a book, headlines, we all put that in the textbooks and the science continued but the rest of the world was like we did it.' The lesson is to distinguish between 'best available explanation' and 'proven explanation' — especially when making high-stakes decisions. The best available theory should be treated as a working hypothesis to test, not a conclusion to defend.
