Destin had dreamed about and studied supersonic flight for years, expecting 'a shake or maybe a little rumble.' Instead: nothing. The only evidence was the altimeter jumping — first dropping 500 feet (as pressure increased at the static port approaching Mach 1) then spiking up 1,300 feet (as the shock wave moved past the Pitot tube's static ports into the rarefaction zone behind the shock). The pilot confirmed they'd broken Mach only by reading instruments.

This reveals a broader pattern about phase transitions and reference frames. The sonic boom is dramatic for observers on the ground, but the aircraft smoothly accelerates through the transonic regime. Similarly, many significant thresholds — becoming profitable, achieving product-market fit, crossing a skill threshold — may feel anticlimactic from inside while being dramatic from outside. The measurable evidence of crossing (instrument readings, metrics) matters more than felt experience for identifying threshold events.
