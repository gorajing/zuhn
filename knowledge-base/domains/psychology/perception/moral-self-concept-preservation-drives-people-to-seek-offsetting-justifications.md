Tim Miller recounts a telling anecdote: an anonymous Trump supporter admitted he knows Trump 'does some bad stuff' but copes by grabbing onto 'one or two things he's right about' and focusing intensely on those. Sarah Longwell confirms this pattern from focus groups — supporters will cite border security, Israel policy, or anti-woke stances as moral anchors that let them 'feel like they're on the side of good.' This is a specific form of moral licensing: rather than changing the compromised position, people seek out offsetting moral credits from adjacent positions. The mechanism explains why political debates often derail into whataboutism — it's not just rhetorical deflection but a genuine psychological need to maintain moral self-concept. This pattern appears in corporate contexts too: employees at ethically questionable companies often over-emphasize the firm's charitable work or one positive product to maintain self-respect. The insight suggests that when trying to persuade someone who's deeply committed, attacking their moral offset directly (e.g., 'your border stance doesn't justify X') is likely to trigger defensive entrenchment rather than reconsideration.
