De Botton draws a powerful analogy: none of us remember learning to speak, yet we mastered incredibly complex grammar — pluperfect, subjunctive — without knowing such things exist. The same invisible process occurs with emotional language. Between ages 0 and 10, we absorb a complete emotional vocabulary from caregivers: whether humans can be trusted, whether we are worth something, what happens when someone is angry, how to communicate important things.

Just as parents with regional accents pass those accents to children, caregivers pass 'regional accents of emotional intelligence.' Your dialect might include 'every man is frightening' or 'everyone who is kind is also slightly sadistic.' These emotional accents stick for life unless deliberately examined. The 70% statistic is striking: life satisfaction is determined up to 70% by the quality of personal relationships. Yet we spend almost no educational time learning how relationships work — we rely on instinct in a domain that accounts for most of our happiness.
