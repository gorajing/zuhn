Klaas makes a fundamental distinction between control and influence. Control implies predictable outcomes from our actions. Influence means our actions matter enormously but in ways we cannot predict or direct. Kids kicking a soccer ball off a cliff 80 miles away saved a drowning man 10 days later. One person infected with COVID changed the trajectory of 8 billion lives.

This is not nihilism but a recalibration. We are constantly reshaping the future through every interaction, but the outcomes are chaotic and unpredictable. The time-travel intuition is correct: every small act matters. But the illusion of control, the belief that we can engineer specific outcomes, is wrong. Accepting this distinction reduces anxiety about outcomes while increasing responsibility for actions.
