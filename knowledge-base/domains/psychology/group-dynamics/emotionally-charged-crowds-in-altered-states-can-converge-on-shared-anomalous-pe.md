The standard skeptical dismissal of mass anomalous experiences invokes either fraud or mass hysteria/suggestion — someone plants the idea, and social pressure spreads it. But both the Fatima and Dhammakaya events are poorly explained by this model. At Dhammakaya, someone spontaneously cried out about seeing a vision, and then thousands independently confirmed similar structural experiences. At Fatima, the crowd was primed to expect 'a miracle' but not specifically a sun miracle.

The more parsimonious explanation is convergent perceptual experience: when a large group simultaneously engages in bright-light gazing while in an emotionally heightened or meditative state, the perceptual mechanism fires similarly across individuals because the mechanism is biological, not cultural. This is analogous to how many people independently report similar geometric patterns during migraine auras — the neural substrate constrains the output.

This has implications for how we evaluate eyewitness testimony of extraordinary events. Mass corroboration is usually treated as strong evidence ('hundreds of people can't all be wrong'). But if the phenomenon is generated by a shared perceptual mechanism triggered by shared conditions, mass corroboration tells you about the mechanism, not about external reality.
