During the critical manual course correction burn, Lovell realized he couldn't keep Earth centered in the window alone because the attitude controller had three independent axes (pitch, roll, yaw). His solution was to partition the problem: he took up-and-down, Swigert took side-to-side, and they used manual start/stop buttons for the engine burn timing. Neither astronaut tried to manage the whole system—they explicitly divided cognitive load by axis.

This is a powerful pattern for startup teams in crisis. When facing a complex, time-sensitive problem—a production outage, a critical demo, a launch-day failure—the instinct is for the most senior person to take over everything. The Apollo 13 approach is the opposite: decompose the problem into orthogonal dimensions and assign each to one person. This works because human working memory is severely limited under stress, and narrowing each person's scope to one dimension lets them perform that dimension well rather than performing all dimensions poorly.
