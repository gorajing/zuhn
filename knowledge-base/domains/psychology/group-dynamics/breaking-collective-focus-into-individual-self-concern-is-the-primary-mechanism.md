Tear gas is effective at dispersing crowds not primarily because of the pain it inflicts, but because it forces each person to stop their collective action and worry about themselves individually. The discomfort shifts attention from the shared goal to personal well-being, breaking the deindividuation that makes collective action possible. Each person becomes an individual again rather than a node in a group.

This principle generalizes beyond riot control to any situation where you need to dissolve coordinated opposition or groupthink. The mechanism is attention reallocation — anything that forces participants to shift from 'we' thinking to 'I' thinking disrupts collective momentum. In organizational contexts, this explains why personal accountability mechanisms can break dysfunctional team dynamics, and why crises that affect people differently can fracture previously unified coalitions.
