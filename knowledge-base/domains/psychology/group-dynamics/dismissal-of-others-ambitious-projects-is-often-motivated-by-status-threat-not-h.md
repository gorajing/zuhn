Beyond inexperience and wanting to seem clever, there's a darker reason people are harsh on early ambitious work: they hope you'll fail because your success would place you above them. This isn't always conscious — it can be an unconscious impulse, and in some cultures it's institutionalized as a national trait (tall poppy syndrome, crab mentality). The key insight is that the criticism is about the critic's status anxiety, not the work's actual merit.

Silicon Valley partially solves this through aligned incentives rather than moral superiority. Investors want you to succeed because they profit from it. The broader community benefits from being associated with success. Over time, this self-interested encouragement has calcified into genuine cultural benevolence — it simply seems like what one does with startups. This suggests that the antidote to status-motivated dismissal isn't moral exhortation but incentive alignment: create structures where others benefit from your success.
