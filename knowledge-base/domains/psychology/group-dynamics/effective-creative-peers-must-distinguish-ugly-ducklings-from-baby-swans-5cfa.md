Graham makes a subtle but important distinction: the goal isn't to surround yourself with cheerleaders. Indiscriminate encouragement gets discounted quickly and becomes noise. What you need are colleagues who possess the rare skill of distinguishing genuinely promising early work from genuinely bad early work — people who can tell an ugly duckling from a baby swan.

The best candidates for this role are people working on similar ambitious projects themselves. They have calibrated intuitions about what early-stage messiness looks like in your domain. This is why university departments and research labs are effective — not because of institutional resources, but because they concentrate people with domain-specific judgment about early work. The practical implication is to actively seek out others attempting new things in your field, not just supporters or mentors, but fellow practitioners whose judgment you'd actually trust.
