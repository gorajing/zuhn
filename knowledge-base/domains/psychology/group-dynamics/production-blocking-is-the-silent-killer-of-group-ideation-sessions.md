Alison Coward identifies 'production blocking' as the core dysfunction of traditional group discussions: when one person speaks, everyone else must listen, which interrupts their own thought processes and prevents contribution. This is not merely a turn-taking problem — the act of listening actively interferes with idea generation.

The solution is environmental: tools that allow simultaneous parallel input (sticky notes on a wall canvas, for example) bypass production blocking entirely. Everyone contributes at the same time rather than waiting in a serial queue. This explains why silent brainstorming consistently outperforms verbal brainstorming in research — it eliminates the blocking mechanism rather than trying to manage it with facilitation rules.
