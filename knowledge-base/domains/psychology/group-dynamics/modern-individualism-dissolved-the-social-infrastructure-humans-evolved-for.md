Humans evolved for stable groups of 50-150 people maintained across a lifetime. The structural dissolution of this arrangement began with Renaissance individualism and Protestant theology emphasizing individual responsibility, accelerated through the Industrial Revolution as people left villages for factories, and continues today as people relocate for jobs, education, and relationships. The mean number of close friends in the US dropped from 3 in 1985 to 2 in 2011.

This historical lens matters because it shows loneliness is an environmental mismatch problem, not a personal deficiency. Our biology is calibrated for a social world that no longer exists. The implication is that individual solutions (therapy, social skills training) are necessary but insufficient — the deeper fix requires rebuilding social infrastructure: community spaces, stable neighborhoods, work cultures that don't consume all available time. The most common path into chronic loneliness isn't dramatic rejection but gradual drift: getting busy with work and life, sacrificing friend time as the 'most convenient thing to cut,' until isolation becomes the default.
