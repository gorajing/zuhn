The Rajapaksa brothers ended Sri Lanka's 26-year civil war in 2009, earning genuine hero status. This legitimacy buffer allowed them to survive corruption allegations, questionable Chinese infrastructure deals, and a debt-to-GDP ratio of 119% without serious political consequences. Citizens weighted security and national pride above economic governance.

But this tolerance had a precise breaking point: when the crisis shifted from abstract (debt ratios, currency depreciation) to visceral (no fuel, no food, no medicine, cancelled school exams), the legitimacy evaporated almost overnight. Protesters stormed the presidential residence within months of the crisis becoming tangible. This pattern suggests that legitimacy from past achievements operates as a depreciating asset — it covers governance failures up to the point where citizens' immediate survival is threatened, then collapses discontinuously rather than gradually. The implication for political analysis is that approval ratings in crisis-prone states are lagging indicators; the real question is how close the population is to the survival threshold.
