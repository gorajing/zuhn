Graham makes a subtle observation about how Silicon Valley's supportive culture formed: it didn't start from moral superiority but from aligned incentives. Investors explicitly profit from founders' success. Employees hope to rise with successful companies. Even casual acquaintances benefit from association with someone who later becomes famous. Over decades, this self-interested encouragement practiced so consistently that it calcified into genuine custom — people now encourage startups because that's simply what one does.

This matters because it suggests a replicable mechanism: you don't need to find morally exceptional people to build encouraging environments. You need to design incentive structures where helping others succeed also helps you. The contrast with cultures where others' success threatens your status (what Graham calls a 'sinister reason' for dismissing new ideas) shows that the difference is structural, not moral. The practical implication is that when building teams or communities, aligning incentives so that members benefit from each other's breakthroughs will eventually produce genuine supportiveness — even if it starts as calculated self-interest.
