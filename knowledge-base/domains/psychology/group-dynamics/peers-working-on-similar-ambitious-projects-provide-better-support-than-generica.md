Graham draws a sharp distinction between people who encourage you generically and people who can actually evaluate early-stage ambitious work. Generic encouragement gets discounted quickly — you learn that your cheerleaders can't tell good from bad, so their praise stops meaning anything. What you need instead is people with domain proximity and enough skin in the game to have calibrated judgment.

The reason university departments and research labs work is not bureaucratic but epistemic: they concentrate people working on similar ambitious projects who can actually distinguish genuinely promising early work from work that's bad in a way that matters. This is rare. Most environments don't have this.

The practical implication is to be selective about who you show early work to. The right audience is not people who will be kind, but people who will be accurate — and accuracy requires that they themselves are doing similar things. Seeking out such people proactively (rather than waiting for institutions to assemble them) is one of the highest-leverage moves available when starting something ambitious.
