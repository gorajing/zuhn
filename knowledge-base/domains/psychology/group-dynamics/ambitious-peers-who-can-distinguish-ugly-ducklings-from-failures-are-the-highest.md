Graham distinguishes between two types of supportive environments: people who always encourage you (which you quickly learn to discount) and people who can actually tell whether your ugly early work has real promise. The latter group is almost exclusively people working on ambitious projects themselves, because only they have calibrated intuition for what promising early-stage work looks like.

This explains why university departments, research labs, and startup hubs produce disproportionate creative output — not because of resources or prestige, but because they concentrate people with the rare ability to evaluate early work accurately. The practical implication is that when building your professional circle, optimize for peers who are themselves doing ambitious, uncertain work. Their feedback will be both more honest and more useful than that of observers, mentors, or cheerleaders who lack direct experience with the ugly-duckling phase.
