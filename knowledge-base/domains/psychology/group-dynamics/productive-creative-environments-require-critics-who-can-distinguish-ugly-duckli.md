Creating a supportive environment for ambitious work isn't about collecting people who are always encouraging—you'd quickly learn to discount unconditional praise. The critical ingredient is colleagues who can actually distinguish between genuinely bad work and work that merely looks bad because it's early. The people best equipped for this judgment are those working on similar ambitious projects of their own, because they've internalized what promising early-stage work looks like in that domain. This is why university departments and research labs function so well as creative incubators—they naturally concentrate people with calibrated judgment about early work. The same dynamic can be created without institutions; motivated people working on new things naturally coalesce when given the chance. But actively seeking out such peers is worth the effort because the alternative—facing the social headwind of uncalibrated judgment—is the single biggest environmental factor that kills ambitious projects early.
