Graham warns that simply collecting encouraging people isn't enough — you'll quickly learn to discount blanket positivity. What you need are colleagues who can actually evaluate whether your ugly early work has genuine promise or is genuinely bad. The best source of such colleagues is people working on their own ambitious projects, because they have calibrated intuitions about what early-stage promise looks like.

This is why university departments and research labs work well as environments for ambitious work — not because of institutional resources, but because they concentrate people with the right evaluative skills. Graham suggests actively seeking out others attempting new things rather than waiting for these clusters to form naturally. The implication for anyone doing ambitious work is that curating your peer group for discernment, not just supportiveness, is a high-leverage activity.
