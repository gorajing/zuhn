Graham makes a crucial distinction about the social environment needed for ambitious work: collecting people who are always encouraging teaches you to discount their feedback, making it useless. What you actually need are colleagues who can distinguish between work that looks bad because it's early-stage ambitious and work that looks bad because it's actually bad. The people best equipped for this judgment are those working on similar projects of their own, which explains why university departments and research labs are effective incubators. This means the right peer group is both encouraging and discriminating—a combination that naturally emerges when peers share your domain and ambition level but is almost impossible to manufacture artificially.
