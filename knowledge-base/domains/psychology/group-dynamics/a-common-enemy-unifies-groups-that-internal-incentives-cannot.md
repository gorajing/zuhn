The Spanish deliberately exploited tribal differences among Pueblo peoples to prevent unified resistance. For decades this worked. But when Franciscan friars escalated religious suppression — destroying the cultural identity that defined each tribe — Popé united 2,000 warriors across tribal lines to drive out the Spanish in 1680. The population had collapsed from 60,000 to 17,000, creating the desperation needed for collective action.

The pattern is universal: startups that fiercely compete will band together against a platform's extractive policy change; political factions unite against an existential candidate. But the Pueblo case also shows the fragility — after the Spanish returned with more tolerant policies, the coalition dissolved. This means common-enemy coalitions are powerful for achieving a single objective but unreliable as long-term organizational structures. If you're building on coalition energy, convert it into institutional bonds before the external threat fades.
