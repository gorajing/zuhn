Over 60% of Barrow's residents are Iñupiat — people whose ancestors have lived in this exact location for more than 1,500 years, making it one of North America's oldest continuously inhabited settlements. For these residents, the question of 'why live here' is fundamentally different than for the 40% drawn by oil money. Cultural identity, ancestral connection, and community belonging create a form of resilience that transcends economic rationality.

This has broad implications for understanding community persistence and organizational culture. Groups with deep shared identity will absorb extraordinary costs — extreme weather, $500/week grocery bills, months of darkness — that would cause purely economically-motivated groups to disperse. This mirrors how mission-driven organizations retain talent through conditions (low pay, high stress, uncertain outcomes) that mercenary employees would never tolerate. The lesson: when building anything meant to endure, cultural roots and shared identity are more durable foundations than economic incentives alone.
