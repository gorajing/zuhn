The video documents a sequence of individually defensible actions — mainland immigration controls in a Hong Kong train station, election rule changes, anthem-insult laws, extradition proposals — none of which alone constitutes a dramatic breach of the 'one country, two systems' agreement, but which cumulatively shift the balance of power. Each change can be rationalized ('it's just for convenience,' 'protestors are overreacting') which makes organized resistance difficult.

This pattern — sometimes called 'salami tactics' — appears in organizational politics, negotiations, and personal boundaries. The defense against it requires evaluating the trajectory of changes rather than each change in isolation, and establishing bright-line rules that trigger resistance regardless of how reasonable any individual step appears. The Hong Kong case shows that by the time the pattern is widely recognized, the cumulative shift may already be irreversible.
