Graham makes a subtle but important distinction about the kind of social support that actually helps creative work. Simply collecting encouraging people fails because you quickly learn to discount unconditional praise—it carries no information. What you need are colleagues with enough context and taste to provide credible signal about whether your ugly early work is on a promising trajectory.

The people best positioned to give this signal are those working on similar ambitious projects themselves, which explains why university departments and research labs are effective creative environments. They provide peer evaluation that is simultaneously supportive and credible. This suggests that when building a creative support network, the key filter isn't warmth or enthusiasm but whether the person has enough domain experience and active practice to make their encouragement meaningful.
