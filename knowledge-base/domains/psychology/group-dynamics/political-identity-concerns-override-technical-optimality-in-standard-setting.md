The original base unit of mass was the 'grave' — a clean, prefix-free name derived from Latin 'gravitas.' But during the French Revolution, it sounded too much like the aristocratic title 'graf,' so it was politically unacceptable. Scientists switched to the gram (a thousandth of a grave), then realized it was too small, and awkwardly bolted 'kilo-' onto it. Two centuries later, the kilogram remains the only SI base unit with a prefix — a permanent scar from a political constraint.

This pattern repeats everywhere standards are set by committees or communities. Technical standards bodies (W3C, IETF, ISO) regularly adopt suboptimal solutions because they're politically palatable to all stakeholders. Programming language design carries historical naming mistakes forward indefinitely (JavaScript isn't Java; HTTP referer is misspelled forever). The insight for anyone designing shared systems: social acceptability is as hard a constraint as technical correctness, and the compromises you make under political pressure will outlive the politics by orders of magnitude.
