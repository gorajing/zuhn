Paine surfaces a bug in moral accounting that shows up everywhere in politics: populations do not weight deaths and dignity on the same scale. A leader who kills 40 million of his own people but restores a group's sense of collective standing can end up with a higher net legitimacy score than a peaceful leader who presided over perceived national decline. The Korean War — a brutal stalemate — gets read as a victory because for the first time in a century Chinese forces faced the full capitalist coalition and did not lose.

This maps onto brand, product, and political narratives broadly. Customers forgive outages if a platform is coded as 'ours' against a foreign rival. Electorates re-elect leaders who presided over scandals if those leaders are associated with restoring national pride. Employees stay at companies that treat them badly if the company's mission confers identity.

Application: when predicting whether a leader, brand, or institution will survive a disaster, look first at whether it is embedded in an identity-restoration narrative. If yes, the accounting is dignity-weighted, not outcome-weighted, and normal performance metrics will badly underpredict its durability.
