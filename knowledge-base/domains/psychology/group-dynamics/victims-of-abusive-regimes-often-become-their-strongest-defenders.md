Paine highlights a counterintuitive pattern: Xi Jinping's father was a purged high-level communist, Xi himself was sent down to the countryside and at one point was turned away from his mother's home when he came back starving after his father was denounced. By naive expectation he should loathe Maoism. Instead he reveres Mao and is rebuilding Mao's centralized system.

The same pattern holds for rank-and-file Chinese: the numbers on Mao's death toll are public, yet Mao remains revered. Paine attributes this to the universal human need for national pride — it's psychologically very hard to look at the dark side of your own country, and Mao is undeniably consequential for modern China's founding. She draws an explicit parallel to America's inability to fully reckon with slavery.

For anyone modeling political behavior: don't assume victims will oppose the system that victimized them. The pull of belonging and the need for a proud origin story often override personal grievance. This is why regime-change strategies premised on 'the people will rise up when they see the truth' routinely fail.
