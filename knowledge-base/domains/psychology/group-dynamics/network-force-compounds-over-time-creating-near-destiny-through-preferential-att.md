The article connects several mathematical phenomena — Zipf's Law, preferential attachment (Matthew Effect), and power-law distributions — to show that network advantages compound over time. A node that is 'ahead' gets picked more often because being ahead reduces friction and increases benefit for connecting nodes. This repeats across many interactions, systematically directing more resources to already-advantaged nodes.

The practical implication is that network gravity increases with each crossroads decision as you accumulate 'network mass.' Like asteroids colliding to form larger asteroids, each network you join integrates with existing ones, exponentially heightening the energy cost of changing course. This means early network decisions (family, high school, college) have outsized effects because they set the initial trajectory that preferential attachment then amplifies. Understanding this math reveals why mid-life career changes feel so difficult — it's not just psychology, it's fighting accumulated network gravity.
