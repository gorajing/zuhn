George Soros's reflexivity theory—that once a belief enters the market, every additional belief reinforces it—maps precisely onto venture capital dynamics. Kahneman's work on how strong signals compress independent judgment completes the picture: when traction, credentials, and sharp storytelling all point the same direction, few investors want to diverge.

This creates a feedback loop where consensus capital becomes a survival mechanism rather than a shortcut. When optimizing for downstream capital or round momentum, being early isn't always rewarded—being legible is. Looking 'too strange, too soon' can stall deals. So founders polish pitches to match market priors, investors lean into what the market already approves, and the myth of the contrarian fades.

The cross-domain insight is that reflexivity operates identically in any system where participants both observe and influence the market: social media algorithms, hiring pipelines, and academic publishing all exhibit the same signal-compression dynamics that turn independent judgment into coordinated consensus.
