Graham makes a subtle distinction about social support during the early-stage-ugliness phase: simply collecting cheerleaders doesn't work because you'll learn to discount unconditional encouragement. What you need are peers with enough expertise to recognize signal in noise—people who can tell an ugly duckling from a baby swan. This is why university departments and research labs are so effective: colleagues working on similar problems have the calibration to give meaningful rather than merely supportive feedback.

The implication for founders and creators is that peer selection is a high-leverage decision. The ideal creative environment isn't the most positive one—it's the one with the highest signal-to-noise ratio in feedback. People working on similar ambitious projects naturally develop the pattern-recognition needed to spot genuine promise, making them far more valuable than generic supporters.
