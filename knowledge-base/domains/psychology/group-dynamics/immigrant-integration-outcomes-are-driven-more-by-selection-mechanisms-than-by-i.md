Alexander's data shows that US Afghan immigrants (largely translators and guides for US forces) are incarcerated at 1/10th the native rate, while Chinese and Venezuelan immigrants (largely well-off people fleeing communism) are at 1/20th and 1/4th respectively. Meanwhile, the same nationalities might show very different rates in European countries with different selection mechanisms.

This implies that debates framed around immigrant identity ('are X people good or bad immigrants?') are asking the wrong question. The selection mechanism — who gets through, via what pathway, with what screening — dominates the outcome. US immigration, despite its dysfunction, selects differently than European systems due to geography (ocean vs. land borders), legal frameworks, and historical relationships. This reframes immigration policy from 'which groups to admit' to 'which selection mechanisms produce the best outcomes.'
