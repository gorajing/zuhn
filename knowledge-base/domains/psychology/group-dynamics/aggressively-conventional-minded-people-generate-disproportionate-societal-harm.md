Graham identifies a dangerous dynamic specific to the aggressively conventional-minded: they don't just enforce rules, they compete with each other over who enforces them most zealously. An enforcer who tolerates a borderline idea creates an opening for another enforcer to accuse them of insufficient purity. This competition eliminates the margin for error that any idea-filtering process needs, because the people doing the filtering are incentivized to ban more rather than less. The result is a ratchet effect where the boundary of acceptable thought only moves in one direction. Many post-Enlightenment institutions — freedom of speech, academic freedom, separation of church and state — exist specifically to contain this dynamic. Understanding this mechanism matters for anyone building organizations or communities: you need structural protections against enforcement cascades, not just good intentions.
