The satirical dialogue reveals how 'China will beat us' functions not as a rational concern to be addressed but as an emotional trigger that short-circuits engagement with details. The supporter offers concrete proposals — monitoring frameworks, conditional triggers, graduated strategies, precedent from nuclear treaties — but none penetrate because the opponent's threat-response system has been activated by the geopolitical competition frame.

This pattern extends beyond AI governance to any domain where national security framing is invoked: climate agreements, technology regulation, trade policy. Once the 'adversary will get ahead' frame is activated, the brain processes proposals through a zero-sum competitive lens where any restraint equals surrender. Effective communication in these contexts requires either defusing the threat frame first (e.g., showing the adversary also wants to pause) or reframing restraint as competitive advantage (e.g., 'controlled development means we build it right while they build it fast and wrong').
