Peachtree's 90 miles of golf-cart-specific roads created a community where 9,000 families use golf carts as second vehicles and children drive to school at age 12. Juneau, Alaska's lack of road connections means all access is by air or sea, fundamentally shaping its economy and culture. The Northwest Angle's residents casually cross international borders as a daily routine.

In each case, infrastructure didn't just enable behavior — it made the behavior so natural that it became identity. Nobody in Peachtree thinks of golf-cart commuting as unusual; it's just how things work there. This pattern matters for anyone trying to change behavior at scale: building infrastructure that makes the desired behavior the path of least resistance is dramatically more effective than regulation or incentives. The same principle explains why bike-friendly cities actually increase cycling (not just accommodate existing cyclists), and why open-plan offices change communication patterns regardless of company policy. Infrastructure is policy made physical, and physical defaults beat written rules every time.
