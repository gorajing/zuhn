The proposed mechanism for the sharp 2023-2025 crime decline illustrates a counterintuitive dynamic in social movements: a backlash can trigger a counter-backlash that doesn't merely restore the status quo ante but overshoots it. The anti-police sentiment after BLM may have initially reduced police effectiveness and increased crime, but the resulting spike in crime generated public demand for even more policing than existed before.

This pattern — disruption → backlash → counter-backlash → overcorrection — appears in many domains: financial regulation after crises, content moderation policies, immigration politics, and corporate DEI initiatives. The practical implication is that advocates for change should model not just the first-order backlash to their efforts, but the second-order reaction, which can be larger than either the original change or the backlash. This makes the long-run equilibrium of social interventions inherently difficult to predict.
