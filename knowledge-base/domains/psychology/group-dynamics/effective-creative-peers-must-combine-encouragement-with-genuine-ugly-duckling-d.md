Graham makes a subtle distinction that most advice about 'supportive environments' misses: collecting people who are always encouraging is useless because you'll learn to discount their feedback. What you actually need is people who can tell an ugly duckling from a baby swan — and the only people reliably able to do this are those working on similar ambitious projects themselves.

This explains why university departments and research labs work so well as creative incubators, and why the generic advice to 'surround yourself with positive people' falls flat. The mechanism is specific: peers doing similar work have calibrated intuitions about what promising-but-ugly looks like in your domain. Their encouragement carries signal precisely because they could credibly discourage you instead. This suggests that seeking out peer groups should optimize for shared ambition level and domain proximity, not general positivity.
