Most network effects operate through passive value contribution — an Uber driver doesn't intend to make the platform more valuable; they just want to earn money. But tribal network effects are fundamentally different. Members of tribes (alumni networks, military units, fraternities, YC alumni) consciously invest in other members' success, defend the tribe's reputation against outsiders, and actively recruit new members.

The manual identifies six characteristics that strengthen tribal network effects: identity integration ('it's who I am'), intentional value creation between members, a defined out-group rival, perceived higher-status attributes, shared hardship/adversity, and barriers to entry that create exclusivity. The more of these six a community has, the stronger the tribal effect. This explains why YC's network is more defensible than a typical accelerator — the selective admission process, shared intensity of the batch, alumni identity, and the implicit out-group (non-YC startups) combine to create a self-reinforcing tribal dynamic where members go out of their way to help each other, creating a density of connections far exceeding what passive network participation would produce.
