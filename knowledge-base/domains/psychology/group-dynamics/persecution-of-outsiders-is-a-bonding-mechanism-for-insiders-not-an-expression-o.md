Graham argues that bullying is fundamentally a coalition-building tool: nothing brings people closer than a common enemy, and if no real enemy exists, one must be manufactured. The worst bullying comes from groups rather than individuals because the social bonding function requires witnesses and participants. The victims are essentially interchangeable — it's nothing personal, they just need something to chase.

This reframes anti-bullying interventions: targeting individual bullies misses the structural incentive. The real lever is the alliance-building function. Organizations that provide legitimate bonding mechanisms (shared missions, external competitions, collaborative projects) reduce the incentive to manufacture scapegoats. This pattern appears in corporate politics, international relations, and online communities — anywhere closed groups lack external purpose and resort to internal enemy-creation for cohesion.
