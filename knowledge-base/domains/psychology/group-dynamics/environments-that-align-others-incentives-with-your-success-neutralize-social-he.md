Graham identifies a sinister dynamic: when someone attempts something ambitious, many around them unconsciously hope for failure because success would elevate the ambitious person above them. In some cultures this is so embedded it becomes national character. Silicon Valley overcomes this not through moral superiority but through incentive structures — investors, colleagues, and community members all stand to benefit from your success.

Over time, this self-interested encouragement has calcified into genuine cultural benevolence. The practice of encouraging startups has been repeated so long it's become a custom — people do it because that's what one does, not because they're calculating expected returns. This suggests that the right environment isn't one where people suppress jealousy, but one where the incentive structure makes jealousy irrational. The implication for anyone doing ambitious work: deliberately seek environments where others' success is correlated with yours.
