Graham distinguishes between two types of supportive environments: indiscriminate encouragement (which you'll learn to discount) and informed peer judgment from people working on similarly ambitious projects. The latter is why university departments and research labs are effective—your peers understand the domain well enough to recognize genuine promise in ugly early work versus genuinely bad ideas. This is a higher bar than 'find supportive people.' The ideal creative environment requires peers with enough expertise to provide calibrated signal about early-stage work, which means they must be doing similar work themselves. This peer group naturally coalesces given proximity and opportunity, but actively seeking it out dramatically accelerates creative output. The implication for founders and creators is to optimize for peer quality over peer quantity—a few colleagues doing ambitious work in adjacent areas are worth more than a large cheerleading section.
