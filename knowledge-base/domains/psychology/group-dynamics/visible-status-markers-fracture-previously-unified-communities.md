Graham's experiment with orange usernames for high-scoring commenters immediately fractured what had been a unified community into haves and have-nots. He notes he didn't realize how united the culture was until he saw it divided. The damage was fast and visceral enough that he reversed the change.

This reveals something important about community psychology: perceived equality is itself a binding force, and it's fragile. The moment you make implicit status hierarchies explicit and visible, you activate competitive social dynamics that override collaborative ones. This applies broadly — to open-source projects adding contributor tiers, companies making performance ratings visible, or any community considering gamification through leaderboards. The lesson isn't that status differences don't exist, but that making them visible changes behavior in ways that are hard to predict and easy to regret.
