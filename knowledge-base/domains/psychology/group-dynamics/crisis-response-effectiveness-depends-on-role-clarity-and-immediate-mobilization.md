When army ants attack a leafcutter colony, the response is immediate and role-based: soldiers swarm to the attack site forming a frontline, smaller workers grab and immobilize enemies, dedicated teams conduct flanking operations behind the line, and other workers rush to barricade entrances as contingency. This happens without deliberation or central command.

The lesson for teams is that crisis response quality is determined long before the crisis. Teams that have pre-defined roles, practiced escalation paths, and parallel contingency planning (fight at the front while preparing fallback) handle emergencies dramatically better than those that try to figure out who does what in the moment. The leafcutters simultaneously fight and prepare for failure — they don't wait to see if defense works before activating Plan B.
