Paine's aside — 'there's a reason why you don't want to have people inherit offices, they don't tend to be very good at it' — is a compressed argument about selection mechanisms. Royal succession selects on ancestry, which has near-zero correlation with strategic judgment. When the decision-makers of five great powers were all selected this way, none of them noticed what Bismarck was doing until it was too late. A peer group selected for competence would have caught the pattern after the second war at latest.

The principle generalizes beyond monarchy: any selection mechanism that correlates weakly with the actual skill the role demands will produce systematic underperformance. Family businesses that pass to children rather than best-qualified candidates, board seats allocated by friendship, executive roles filled by tenure rather than capability — all exhibit the same failure mode. The organization can coast on inherited advantages for a while, but someone operating on merit in an adjacent space will eventually outmaneuver them.

The practical lesson for anyone building an organization: resist the gravitational pull of non-merit selection for any role where judgment matters. It's not just about fairness; it's about whether your decision-makers will see what a Bismarck across the table is actually doing.
