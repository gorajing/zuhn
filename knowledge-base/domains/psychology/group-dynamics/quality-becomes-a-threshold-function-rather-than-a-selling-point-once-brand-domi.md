Graham identifies a subtle but important transformation in how quality functions once brand takes over. In the golden age, quality (accuracy, thinness) was the primary selling point — more quality meant more sales in a roughly linear relationship. In the brand age, quality transforms into a threshold: it must be 'good enough' to maintain the brand's credibility, but exceeding that threshold provides no additional value.

Mechanical watches at 5 seconds/day were far worse than quartz (0.5 seconds/day), but accurate enough that wearers didn't feel foolish. Had they been off by a minute per day, the leap from timekeeper to status symbol would have been impossible — it would have seemed 'too manifestly unluxurious.' This threshold model applies broadly: once brand dominates, the brand must not break character. Quality below the threshold destroys the brand; quality above it is wasted investment. This explains why luxury brands maintain adequate but not exceptional product quality while pouring resources into marketing and scarcity management.
