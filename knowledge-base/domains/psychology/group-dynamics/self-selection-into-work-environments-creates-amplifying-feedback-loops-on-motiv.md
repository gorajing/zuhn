Graham identifies a compounding social dynamic in career choice: the reason you choose a type of work determines the character of your peers, which in turn amplifies that original motivation. Finance attracts people optimizing for compensation, creating an environment where compensation obsession intensifies. Research attracts people optimizing for intellectual interest, creating an environment where curiosity deepens.

This has a practical screening implication: before committing to a career path, look at who your colleagues will be, because you'll converge toward their values and temperament. Graham's thought experiment — would these people do this work for free? — distinguishes environments where intrinsic motivation dominates from those where extrinsic motivation dominates. The former tend to produce great work; the latter tend to produce burnout.
