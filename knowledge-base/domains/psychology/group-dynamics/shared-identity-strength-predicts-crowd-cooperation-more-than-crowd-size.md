Research on crowd psychology has overturned the classical 'mob mentality' theory. Rather than crowds degenerating into mindless masses, studies show that shared identity — being fans of the same team, victims of the same disaster, protesters for the same cause — is the primary driver of cooperative behavior. During the 2005 London Underground bombings, victims displayed remarkable selflessness precisely because the shared identity of 'disaster survivor' was so strong. This explains the counterintuitive finding that emergency evacuations rarely produce crushes despite higher stakes — the sudden shared identity as victims triggers cooperation rather than panic.

This principle extends beyond crowds into any group dynamic: teams, communities, organizations. The implication is that cooperation isn't primarily about rules or enforcement but about the strength of felt shared identity. Designing for identity salience (making group membership feel real and specific) may be more effective than designing for compliance.
