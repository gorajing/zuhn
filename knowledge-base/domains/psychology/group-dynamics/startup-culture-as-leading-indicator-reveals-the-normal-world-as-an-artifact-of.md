Graham's most provocative move is inverting Kate's list to describe the 'normal' world: people who talk a lot with one another as they work slowly but harmoniously on conservative, expensive projects whose destinations are decided in advance, carefully adjusting their manner to reflect their position in the hierarchy. He then notes this is also an accurate description of the past, suggesting startup culture is a leading indicator of broader societal change.

This reframing is powerful because it challenges the assumption that startup culture is a weird subculture. Instead, it positions the conventional professional world as the artifact — a leftover from an era when coordination required physical proximity, information was expensive, and hierarchy was the only scalable management structure. As those constraints dissolve, the traits that seem odd about startups (radical iteration, low capital requirements, flat hierarchies, deep solo work) may simply become how work is done everywhere.
