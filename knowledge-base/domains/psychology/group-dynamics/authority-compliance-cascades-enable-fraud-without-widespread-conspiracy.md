The WorldCom fraud involved $11 billion in misclassified entries, yet only a handful of people—primarily CFO Scott Sullivan and head accountant David Myers—understood what was happening. The rank-and-file accountants who physically made the entries didn't know why they were making them; they were 'just doing as they were told by their seniors.' This mirrors the Milgram obedience findings but in a corporate context: hierarchical authority substitutes for individual judgment.

The SEC investigation concluded the fraud persisted because of 'the lack of courage to blow the whistle on senior officials'—everyone assumed the person above them was doing the right thing. This creates a dangerous organizational dynamic where fraud doesn't need a large conspiracy, just a small number of bad actors at the top and a culture of deference below. The structural defense is empowering and protecting internal audit functions that report outside the normal chain of command, exactly as Cynthia Cooper's team eventually did.
