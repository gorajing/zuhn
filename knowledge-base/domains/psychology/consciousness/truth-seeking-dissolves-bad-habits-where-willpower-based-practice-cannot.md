Naval distinguishes two paths to behavior change: practice (willpower, repetition, techniques) and understanding (seeing truth deeply enough that the behavior dissolves). Practice always maintains a gap — there's a 'you' and a 'thing you're trying to become,' creating ongoing internal conflict. Understanding collapses that gap.

He gives the smoking example: techniques are painful and often fail, but a lung cancer diagnosis or seeing a friend destroyed by the same habit can make the desire vanish instantly. The mechanism is that genuine understanding changes your identity, not just your behavior. This extends to his broader thesis that 'the path toward peace is truth' — and that self-improvement is itself a form of self-conflict. The practical application is to invest more in deeply understanding why you do what you do (through curiosity, not judgment) rather than adding more willpower-based systems on top of behaviors you haven't truly examined.
