Harris's meditation guides practitioners to notice that while the contents of consciousness — sounds, sensations, emotions, thoughts — are in constant flux, the awareness that registers all of these is itself unchanging. You can be aware of pain or pleasure, boredom or excitement, and the quality of awareness itself does not change color based on its contents.

This observation has a practical application in emotional regulation and decision-making under stress. If you can identify with the unchanging awareness rather than with the changing contents of experience, stressful situations lose their ability to hijack your cognition. It's the difference between being angry and noticing anger appearing in awareness — the latter preserves executive function.
