While all drugs are shaped by context, Johnson emphasizes that psychedelics are 'particularly more so.' The Johns Hopkins protocol builds an elaborate contextual scaffold: hours of preparatory rapport-building, a warm welcoming room, eye shades, curated music, and trained guides. The instructions to participants — 'trust, let go, and be open' — are themselves a form of context engineering.

This reveals a deep principle: for experiences that dissolve the self-model, the surrounding container becomes the primary variable. The same 30mg psilocybin dose in a clinical vs. chaotic setting produces radically different outcomes. This has implications beyond psychedelics — any intervention targeting deep psychological change (meditation retreats, intensive therapy, transformative education) should invest as heavily in environment design as in the intervention itself.
