Huberman introduces a practical neuroscience framework: before meditating, do a quick self-assessment. Close your eyes and notice whether your attention naturally pulls inward (interoception — awareness of heartbeat, breathing, gut sensations) or keeps getting pulled to external stimuli (exteroception — sounds, the urge to check your phone). Your position on this continuum varies by time of day, stress level, sleep quality, and context. People with high anxiety often have excessive interoceptive awareness — they're hyperaware of every heart rate shift and gut sensation. For them, an interoceptive meditation could worsen anxiety, while an exteroceptive (eyes-open, external-focus) meditation would be therapeutic. This framework replaces 'meditation is always sitting quietly with eyes closed' with a dynamic, neuroscience-informed selection process. The three brain structures driving this — left dorsolateral prefrontal cortex, anterior cingulate cortex (ACC), and insula — form a triad that interprets internal state relative to external context.
