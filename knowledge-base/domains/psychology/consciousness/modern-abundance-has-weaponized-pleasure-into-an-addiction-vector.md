Naval upgrades Miyamoto Musashi's ancient advice ('do not pursue pleasure for its own sake') for the modern context. The key difference is availability: Musashi didn't face unlimited processed food, internet pornography, or on-demand alcohol. Today's superstimuli are engineered to hijack reward circuits, creating desensitization that makes normal life feel inadequate.

The deeper structural problem is that breaking addictions requires changing your entire social infrastructure — not just the substance. If your friendships are held together by drinking, quitting alcohol means losing those friends and finding new ones. Naval observes that looking forward to weekends and holidays is itself a diagnostic signal: it means you've accepted a life where most of your time is spent in a state you're trying to escape. The practical takeaway is to audit which pleasures are genuine and which are substitutes for things evolution designed you to get from real relationships, real achievement, and real physicality.
