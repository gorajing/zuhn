Rather than viewing depression, addiction, OCD, and PTSD as separate diseases with distinct mechanisms, Johnson proposes they share a common structural feature: the mind becomes trapped in a narrowed repertoire of thoughts, emotions, and behaviors. The depressed person ruminates in the same loops. The addict's world collapses around the substance. The OCD patient repeats compulsions. The PTSD sufferer relives trauma.

Psychedelics appear to work transdiagnostically — across multiple disorder categories — precisely because they don't target a specific pathway like SSRIs or benzodiazepines. Instead, they massively increase neural entropy, forcing the brain out of its entrenched patterns and into novel configurations. This is consistent with the entropic brain hypothesis and suggests that the mechanism of action is pattern disruption itself, not correction of a specific chemical imbalance. The implication is that 'narrowed repertoire' may be a more useful clinical concept than traditional diagnostic categories.
