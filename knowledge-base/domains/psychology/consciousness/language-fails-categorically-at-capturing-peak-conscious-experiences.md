Harris describes language as 'the wrong tool for the job' when it comes to psychedelic experience — not merely insufficient but categorically inappropriate, like trying to describe music by snapping your fingers. He uses the metaphor of wading into a roiling ocean of meaning with a thimble: what you bring back can't begin to indicate the immensity even to yourself as an aid to memory.

This has important implications for how we approach consciousness research and first-person reports generally. If peak states are structurally resistant to linguistic encoding, then the entire framework of self-report measures in psychology hits a ceiling. The 70-80% of participants who rate psilocybin among their top-5 life experiences are pointing at something that their rating can't actually communicate. This suggests that any science of consciousness must develop new methodological tools beyond verbal report — perhaps combining neuroimaging with behavioral measures — to bridge the gap between experience and description.
