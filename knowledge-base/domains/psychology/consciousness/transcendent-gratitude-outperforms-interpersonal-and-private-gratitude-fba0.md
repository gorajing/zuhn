Research randomly assigned participants to three gratitude conditions: prayer to a deity, self-help journaling, or expressing gratitude to another person. Prayer-based gratitude produced the strongest health and well-being outcomes.

The mechanism appears tied to sacred meaning: when people imbue goals or relationships with transcendent significance, they invest more effort and derive more benefit. For non-religious people, directing gratitude toward nature, the fact of existence, or something beyond the self can activate a similar effect. This suggests the target of gratitude matters as much as the practice itself.
