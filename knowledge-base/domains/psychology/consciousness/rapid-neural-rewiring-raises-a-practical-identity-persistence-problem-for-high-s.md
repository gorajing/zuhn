The conversation surfaces a legitimate problem: investors have written psychedelic-abstinence clauses into deal docs because founders who take strong doses sometimes leave the company, the marriage, and the strategy. Johnson's framing — 'am I the same person?' — is not just a philosophical curiosity. If neuroplasticity is real and strong, then a person who changes their values after a 20-minute drug experience is, in a functionally meaningful sense, a different decision-maker than the one who signed the commitment.

This is the correct lens: not 'were they irresponsible' but 'did they pre-commit to a protocol that accounts for this risk.' Practical implications: (1) founders considering high-dose psychedelics should discuss it with stakeholders before, not after, (2) long-horizon commitments should include something like a post-experience cooling-off period before acting on value shifts, (3) the counter-frame that psychedelics are a 'liability' to investment theses is epistemically honest even if it sounds paternalistic.
