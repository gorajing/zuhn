One of the five common structural features the essay identifies is 'a loss of fear of death.' This is phenomenologically interesting because it suggests an architectural relationship: the fear of death may be a property of a self that has something to lose, rather than an independent primitive dread. If the self is the thing that fears its own cessation, then dissolving the self removes the fearer rather than the threat. Whether or not this is literally true, it's a testable prediction — people who report deep ego-dissolution experiences (including from psilocybin trials for end-of-life anxiety) tend to also report reduced fear of death. The clinical literature on psilocybin-assisted therapy for terminal cancer patients is the closest modern empirical probe of this ancient claim, and the early results are broadly consistent with what the contemplatives reported.
