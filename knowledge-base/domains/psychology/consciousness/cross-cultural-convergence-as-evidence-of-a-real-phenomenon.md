The Buddha, Lao Tzu, Jesus, Rumi, and Ramana Maharshi worked in radically different cultures, spoke different languages, and built no shared theology — yet each reported a strikingly similar sequence: the sense of a separate self dissolves, the subject-object split collapses, and something unconditioned is recognized as the ground of experience. The argument is straightforward: if five scientists with no contact described the same physical phenomenon, we'd assume it exists. The same logic should apply to first-person reports. Convergence across insulated observers is exactly the kind of signal that's hard to fake, because culture alone cannot explain why the reports share structure despite differing metaphors (Tao, Kingdom, Love, Self, Unconditioned). This is not proof, but it is the shape evidence takes when multiple independent measuring instruments agree.
