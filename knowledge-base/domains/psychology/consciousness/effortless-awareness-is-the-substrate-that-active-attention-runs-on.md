Harris draws an explicit distinction: 'You don't have to make any effort to hear [sounds]. They're continuously arising and changing and passing away.' This contrasts with the effort required to return attention to the breath. The implication is that awareness itself — the capacity to have experience — is effortless and always-on. What costs energy is the directed, selective form: choosing what to attend to and sustaining that choice.

This is consistent with attention research distinguishing between bottom-up (stimulus-driven, automatic, effortless) and top-down (goal-directed, effortful) attention. The meditator's task is learning to recognize when bottom-up capture has occurred (thought-wandering) and reinstate top-down direction. Crucially, the substrate — the field of awareness — is never impaired; only the steering mechanism fatigues.

The design implication for knowledge work: cognitive fatigue is more precisely 'directed-attention fatigue' rather than a depletion of awareness itself. Recovery strategies that restore top-down capacity (nature walks, which engage bottom-up attention; naps; defocused states) work precisely because they let the effortful steering mechanism rest while effortless awareness continues. This reframes 'mental rest' as not an absence of experience but a shift from active steering to passive receiving.
