Jonathan Basile's Library of Babel website deterministically generates every possible 3200-character combination of English letters, commas, spaces, and periods — 10^5000 pages compared to 10^80 atoms in the observable universe. Every truth, lie, poem, and description already 'exists' in it. Yet the video highlights a deep intuition: there's a categorical difference between a program permuting symbols and a person meaning something.

This touches on the hard problem of meaning in an age of generative AI. Large language models produce fluent text by predicting likely continuations — a sophisticated form of permutation over a learned distribution. The Library of Babel is the reductio ad absurdum: if containing all possible statements doesn't constitute having said anything, then generation capability alone doesn't create meaning. What's missing is agency, intention, and the selection pressure of actually choosing one statement over all alternatives. This has implications for how we evaluate AI-generated content: fluency and correctness are necessary but not sufficient for meaningful communication.
