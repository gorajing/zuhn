Harris makes a crucial distinction: consciousness in the fundamental sense is bare felt experience — the sensation of warmth, the experience of seeing green, the feeling of pressure against skin. This can exist without complex thought, as in a young infant who feels sensation before being able to process or understand it.

This distinction matters because if consciousness requires complex thought, then only sophisticated neural systems could be conscious. But if consciousness is just felt experience, it could exist in much simpler organisms — worms, plants, or even simpler systems. Separating consciousness from thought opens the scientific question of how far down in nature experience goes.
