Deutsch derives morality from epistemology: humans are fallible and mistakes are our normal condition. Given this, the most important thing is not making fewer mistakes (impossible) but maintaining the ability to correct them. 'Don't destroy the means of error correction' becomes the heart of morality. This applies at every scale — from personal relationships to political systems.

The North Korea example makes this concrete: a regime that has destroyed the means of political error correction (no elections, armed monopoly on power) has trapped humanity in a local minimum that's extremely difficult to escape. If too much of the world falls into similar patterns, the species could stagnate permanently — not because we lack the knowledge to progress, but because we've 'lost our biggest discovery, which was the ability to make new discoveries.' This frames existential risk not as resource depletion or AI takeover, but as the destruction of error-correcting institutions. Anti-rational memes that restrict knowledge growth are the real civilizational threat.
