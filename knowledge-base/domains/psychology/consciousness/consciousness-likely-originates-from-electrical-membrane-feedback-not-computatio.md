Lane rejects both panpsychism (everything is conscious) and pure emergence (consciousness appears at sufficient computational complexity). Instead, he proposes that consciousness originates from the electrical fields on cell membranes that provide real-time feedback about an organism's state relative to its environment. Even bacteria have electrical discharges associated with 'blind panic' responses, and these electrical states have been selected over billions of generations.

This has implications for AI consciousness debates: if consciousness is rooted in the biophysics of living membranes — the same electrical architecture that powered the first cells in hydrothermal vents — then computational systems, no matter how sophisticated, may process information without experiencing feelings. Lane draws a sharp distinction between information processing (which AI can match or exceed) and the 'currency of feelings' that evolved from membrane electrochemistry. The key test he proposes: can a system override rational decisions with emotional ones when faced with irresolvable complexity? That's what consciousness does in his view.
