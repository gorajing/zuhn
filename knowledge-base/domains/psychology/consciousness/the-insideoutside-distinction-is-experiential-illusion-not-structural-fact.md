Harris systematically dismantles the inside/outside boundary through a series of exercises: closing and opening eyes, visualizing objects superimposed on the visual field, and noticing that thoughts, sensations, and visual percepts all arise in the same 'space.' His key move is showing that the feeling of being located behind the face, looking out at a world, is itself just another appearance in consciousness — not the container of consciousness. When you open your eyes, it feels like experience 'got bigger,' but Harris argues consciousness didn't actually expand; a different type of content simply appeared in the same space. This challenges the naive realist assumption that serves as default operating system for most cognition. The practical application extends beyond meditation: recognizing that the subject/object boundary is constructed rather than given opens up questions about where other supposedly fundamental boundaries in experience might also be constructed.
