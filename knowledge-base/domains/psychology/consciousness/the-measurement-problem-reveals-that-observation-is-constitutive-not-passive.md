Classical physics assumes measurement is passive: a thermometer doesn't create temperature. Quantum mechanics breaks this assumption entirely. Before measurement, a particle's properties are not merely unknown — they are undefined. The wave function describes what could be happening, not what is happening. Observation doesn't reveal reality; it appears to create it.

This has profound implications beyond physics. In any system of observation — markets, user behavior, organizational metrics — the act of measuring can change what's being measured. The quantum measurement problem is the most rigorous formulation of an insight that applies broadly: what you choose to observe shapes what exists. The observer is never truly external to the system being observed.
