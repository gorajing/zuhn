Peterson frames the tension between tradition and innovation as an inescapable existential dilemma: 'If we stay locked in our current mode of apprehension, hell is gonna break loose. If we generate a whole bunch of new solutions, most of them are going to be wrong.' This isn't a solvable problem but a permanent tension to be managed. The parallel to biological evolution is direct — most mutations are harmful, but without mutation, species can't adapt to changing environments.

This framework has immediate application to organizational strategy and personal decision-making. It explains why both rigid conservatism and unconstrained experimentation fail. The optimal strategy is maintaining a 'portfolio' approach: a stable core of proven practices (tradition/dogma) with a margin allocated to experimentation (heresy/innovation). The key insight is that you can't know in advance which new ideas will be vital, so the filtering must happen post-hoc through empirical testing, not through a priori rejection. This maps directly to startup strategy (most pivots fail but the occasional one saves the company) and scientific research (most hypotheses are wrong but the process requires testing them).
