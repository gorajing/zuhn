Johnson describes the default mode network as 'the engine that constructs self and ego' — the substrate of constant inner monologue, self-evaluation, and behavioral rumination. The falsifiable claim is that DMN connectivity patterns (1) stiffen with chronological age along predictable axes, (2) are measurably quieter in children, and (3) can be acutely disrupted by serotonergic psychedelics like psilocybin and 5-MeO-DMT, with the disruption visible on Kernel-style optical imaging.

This maps cleanly onto published neuroimaging literature (Carhart-Harris, Robin L.; entropic brain hypothesis) and is one of the few places where first-person psychedelic reports align with third-person measurement. The useful frame is: the brain has a self-model that can be taken offline, and that self-model is what gets narrower as people age. This is useful regardless of whether psychedelics are the right intervention — it suggests DMN-targeted measurements are valid aging biomarkers.
