Peterson traces a fascinating developmental arc: behavioral patterns (wolves submitting) → dramatic rituals (human sacrifice) → psychologized concepts (delayed gratification, parental sacrifice for children's education). The key insight is that this isn't just moral progress — it's a map of how humans developed abstract thought. We first acted out patterns with survival significance, then became self-conscious enough to represent those patterns as stories, then abstracted the stories into psychological principles.

The practical relevance is that many 'primitive' cultural practices may encode cognitive discoveries that haven't been fully psychologized yet. When Peterson asks 'how often did people with no birth control and insufficient food have to sacrifice a child for the family's survival?', he's pointing at the brutal empirical foundation underlying what became the abstract concept of 'sacrifice what you value now for future benefit.' Understanding this developmental arc helps distinguish which traditional practices encode genuine wisdom (delayed gratification) from which are merely artifacts of ignorance (sympathetic magic).
