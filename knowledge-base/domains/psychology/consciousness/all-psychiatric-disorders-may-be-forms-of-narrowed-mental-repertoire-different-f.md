Johnson proposes a unifying framework: whether someone is addicted to tobacco, trapped in depressive rumination, or locked in PTSD flashback loops, the underlying pattern is the same — a narrowed mental and behavioral repertoire. 'Reality used to be like this, and now your reality is like this.' Psychedelics, in the right setting, 'blow people out of that narrowed story.'

This is a bold theoretical claim with significant implications. If correct, it explains why psilocybin shows therapeutic promise across such diverse conditions (addiction, depression, end-of-life anxiety, OCD). The common mechanism isn't treating a specific neurotransmitter imbalance but disrupting the pattern of constriction itself. This aligns with predictive processing models of the brain, where psychedelics may work by relaxing overly rigid prior beliefs that constrain perception and behavior.
