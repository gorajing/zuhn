The framing has practical consequences. If awakening is an attainment, then you evaluate practice by whether it's getting you closer to a destination, and you can fail. If awakening is recognition, then the 'thing' is already in every moment of experience and practice is about removing obscurations — seeing something that was always there. The two framings lead to very different relationships with effort, progress, and failure. The recognition frame is harder to sell as a product (nothing new is promised) but also harder to falsify through non-achievement, since the claim is about noticing rather than acquiring. The essay argues this recognition-not-construction framing is what the five teachers converge on — consciousness doesn't create something new when it turns inward, it reveals what was already there.
