Harris describes his approach to staying sane amid political chaos not as having better arguments or thicker skin, but as a simple practice: paying attention to what he's doing with his mind. When he feels unhappy or irritable, a 'mindfulness alarm' goes off. The intervention isn't to fix the external situation or win the argument — it's to notice the quality of attention itself. This is a subtle but crucial distinction from typical advice about stress management. Most coping strategies operate on the content level (reframe the thought, solve the problem, avoid the trigger). Harris's approach operates at the meta-level: the relationship between awareness and its objects. By making attention itself the object of attention, the emotional charge of any particular content naturally diminishes. He calls this 'the universal solvent for unhappiness' — suggesting it works regardless of the specific source of distress.
