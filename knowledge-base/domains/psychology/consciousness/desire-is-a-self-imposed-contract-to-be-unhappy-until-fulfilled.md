Naval frames desire as a literal contract: you agree to be unhappy until you get what you want. The critical insight is what happens after fulfillment — you don't reach a blissful peak that persists. You revert to whatever state you were in before the desire arose. This is the hedonic treadmill described in clinical terms.

The implication is not to eliminate desire (which Naval argues is itself a trap — 'whatever you deny yourself becomes your new prison') but to become conscious of the contract you're signing each time. This awareness creates a choice point: is the temporary disturbance worth the temporary satisfaction? For essential goals the answer may be yes, but most desires are reflexive rather than deliberate. Recognizing the contractual nature of desire makes you more selective about which contracts to sign.
