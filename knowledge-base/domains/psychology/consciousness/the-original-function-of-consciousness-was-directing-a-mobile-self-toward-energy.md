The simplest animals like Trichopax adhaerens find food through random motion — slowing near food, speeding in its absence — with no need for awareness. But directed motion toward food, as seen in flatworms using chemoreceptors, requires integrating internal state (hunger) with external sensing, which represents the first credible step toward consciousness.

This 'consciousness as energy-seeking navigation' framing suggests that awareness at every level remains fundamentally tied to resource acquisition. Even human consciousness — our ability to plan, communicate, and build civilizations — can be traced back to increasingly sophisticated strategies for securing the next meal. This lens is useful for understanding why attention and motivation systems are so deeply linked to reward and survival circuits.
