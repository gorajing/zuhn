Johnson describes what he calls 'duh moments' — experiences where people feel they've learned something at a fundamental level that they already knew cognitively. Cancer patients who knew intellectually that fear was limiting them suddenly feel it in their bones and hearts. 'They knew it up here, they could tell you the words, but now they feel it in their heart.'

This reveals a critical distinction between two types of knowledge: propositional (I can state the fact) and embodied (I feel and act from the truth). Traditional talk therapy operates primarily at the propositional level — helping people understand their patterns. Psychedelic therapy appears to bridge the gap between understanding and feeling, converting head-knowledge into body-knowledge. This has implications for any domain where knowing isn't enough — behavior change, skill acquisition, and leadership development all struggle with this same propositional-to-embodied gap.
