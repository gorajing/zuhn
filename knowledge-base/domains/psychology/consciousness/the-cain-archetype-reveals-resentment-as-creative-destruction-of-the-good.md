Peterson unpacks the Cain and Abel story as the foundational pattern of human destructiveness. Cain makes sacrifices but holds something back — he's not fully committed. When his half-hearted efforts don't produce results while Abel thrives, Cain becomes resentful. God tells Cain his problems are self-made and that he's invited sin in 'like a sexually aroused predatory animal.' Rather than self-correct, Cain kills Abel — destroying the very thing he secretly admired.

The critical insight is that resentment isn't passive. Peterson frames it as a creative process: the resentful person actively broods and generates something 'creative new and awful.' This explains why resentment escalates — from individual murder (Cain killing Abel) to weapons of war (Cain's descendants) to civilizational collapse (the flood narrative). The pattern maps to modern contexts: the employee who resents a successful colleague doesn't just suffer quietly but actively undermines them; the ideology that resents successful systems doesn't just critique but actively destroys them while claiming moral justification.
