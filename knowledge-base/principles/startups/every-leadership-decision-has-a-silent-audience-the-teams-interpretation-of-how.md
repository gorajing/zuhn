Management decisions radiate beyond the direct participants. When a leader fires someone, promotes someone, or tolerates underperformance, every employee watches and draws conclusions about what the organization actually values. Critical decisions must be evaluated through the eyes of every employee, not just the people in the conversation. Leaders who stall on feedback to gather 'objective evidence' let emotional resentment compound. The three most powerful words a leader can say are 'I don't know' because it invites collective problem-solving. But this only works if the silent audience has seen consistency — saying 'I don't know' from a leader who always pretends to know everything reads as weakness, while from a consistently honest leader it reads as strength.
