    Three insights reveal that pricing and information management share

    Premium pricing signals reliability (INS-260320-B7DA): Coconote
    charged $99/year for a student app when everyone said students
    won't pay. Raising to $129 increased BOTH users and revenue. Why?
    Students are trusting you with their grades. Premium positioning
    says "we're reliable enough that you won't lose your lecture notes."
    The price IS the trust signal.

    Trial extensions beat discounts (INS-260320-00C2): When users tried
    to cancel, "Do you just need more time?" outperformed 30% discounts
    and 3-month pauses. Discounts train customers to expect deals and
    devalue the product. Extensions preserve the price point while
    addressing the real objection (not enough time to evaluate).

    Compartmentalize acquisition talks (INS-260320-39A3): Zack's wife
    didn't know about the Quizlet acquisition until the wire hit. The
    team didn't know until close day. Why? Deals fall apart. Premature
    sharing creates noise that damages trust and focus. "A great leader
    shelters others from signal-to-noise."

    The meta-pattern: trust is built through consistent quality of
    signal, whether that signal is a price, a product experience, or
    information shared with your team. Diluting any of these — through
    discounts, broken features, or premature announcements — erodes
    the trust that compounds over time.

Three insights reveal that pricing and information management share a
mechanism: they signal trustworthiness. Premium pricing says "we're
reliable." Trial extensions preserve price while addressing the real
objection. Compartmentalizing acquisition news preserves team focus.

The meta-pattern: trust is built through consistent quality of signal.
Diluting it — through discounts, broken features, or premature
announcements — erodes the trust that compounds over time.
