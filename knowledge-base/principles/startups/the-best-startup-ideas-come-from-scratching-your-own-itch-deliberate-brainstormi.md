Founders building things for themselves or for fun historically produce the most valuable startups. Deliberately brainstorming startup ideas produces plausible-sounding but fundamentally flawed ideas. Your unconscious mind prevents you from even seeing ideas that involve difficult, unpleasant work. If your startup idea came from a podcast or VC content, it is by definition not unique. Attractive-sounding ideas are poor bets because many people pursue them. Ideas dismissed as toys often indicate genuine opportunities everyone overlooked. Reframing ideas as questions ('could one make X?') instead of assertions lowers defensiveness and opens exploration space. The meta-pattern: the best ideas don't come from looking for ideas — they come from being deeply embedded in a problem and noticing what's broken.
