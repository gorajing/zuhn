Russell identifies the 'standard model' danger: giving machines fixed objectives inevitably produces instrumental self-preservation. His fix (three principles: altruism, humility, observational learning) proves machines built this way are provably beneficial — but only within the domain of human-evaluable behavior. Yudkowsky identifies the asymmetry that breaks Russell's optimism: RLHF-style training only holds within the training distribution and fails when AI exceeds human evaluator intelligence, and the first failure may be civilizational-ending with no retry. Wolfram adds the mathematical constraint: computational irreducibility guarantees unintended consequences in any sufficiently complex system, meaning alignment can be patched but never solved. The three paradigms — control (Russell), existential risk (Yudkowsky), fundamental limits (Wolfram) — are not competing answers but complementary descriptions of different failure modes. Anyone claiming to solve alignment with one approach is ignoring the other two.
