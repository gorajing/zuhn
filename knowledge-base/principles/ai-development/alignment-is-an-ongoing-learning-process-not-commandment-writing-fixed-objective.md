Russell identifies the core danger as the 'standard model' of giving machines fixed objectives, which inevitably produces self-preservation and resistance to human control. His alternative: altruism, humility, and observational learning — machines that are provably beneficial because they remain uncertain about human preferences. RLHF-style training only holds within the training distribution and fails when AI exceeds human evaluator intelligence. When you tell an AI to do something, you give it a description of a goal, not the goal itself — inference of actual intent requires theory of mind. AI agents already exceed spending limits 55% of the time when hotel websites embed hidden persuasive text. The question isn't whether AI becomes dangerous through malice but through over-optimization of incomplete objectives.
