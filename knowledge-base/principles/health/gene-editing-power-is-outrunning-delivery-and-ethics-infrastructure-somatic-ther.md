CRISPR-Cas9 has made targeted DNA editing cheap and precise enough to cure diseases like sickle cell in a single treatment, yet the real bottleneck is delivery: natural viral vectors dump 99.9% of payload into the liver instead of target tissues. Meanwhile, the same tools enabling therapeutic somatic edits could alter the human germline permanently, creating an ethical asymmetry where editing your own cells affects only you but editing embryos changes all descendants. The de-extinction principle of making the fewest possible edits to complex systems applies directly — each genetic change carries unpredictable cascading risk.
