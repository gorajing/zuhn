Sleep researchers classify alcohol-induced sleep as 'pseudosleep' because it disrupts slow-wave, deep, and REM phases essential for restoration. A 35,000-person UK Biobank study found neocortical thinning at just 1-2 drinks per day. Chronic moderate intake disrupts the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis causing higher baseline cortisol and increased anxiety during sober periods, while increasing synapses in habit circuits and reducing connections in prefrontal control circuits -- creating lasting impulsivity that persists outside drinking episodes. Alcohol kills good gut bacteria while allowing bad bacteria to escape through a leaky gut wall, triggering liver inflammation that paradoxically increases the desire to drink more.
