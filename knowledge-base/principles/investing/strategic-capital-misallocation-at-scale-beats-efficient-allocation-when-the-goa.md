China's banking system lets strategic-sector companies borrow on the promise of producing units, not profits. This looks financially insane — it produces world-leading manufacturing capacity alongside massive capital destruction — but at scale, effectiveness of outcomes beats efficiency of resource use. The same pattern applied in post-WWII West Germany (95% marginal tax rates forced Porsche and peers to plow profits into R&D and engineering), Japan's export discipline, and post-2008 China's infrastructure buildout. Countries that shelter domestic firms from world competition create brittle monopolies; countries that force firms to compete with world-class rivals build enduring advantages. The critical insight: forecasting consensus repeatedly failed on China, Japan, and the USSR because it mistook financial inefficiency for strategic failure, and it will keep failing for the same reason.
