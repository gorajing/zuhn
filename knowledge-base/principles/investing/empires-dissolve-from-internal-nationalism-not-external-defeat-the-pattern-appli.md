The Soviet Union collapsed not from ideological or economic failure but because it was the last European empire to disintegrate under constituent nationalisms, including Russian nationalism itself. Ukraine's departure made the union structurally non-viable, and Putin's framing of 1991 as worse than WWII reveals that great-power leaders value territorial unity over human life. This pattern -- internal fragmentation driven by identity, not external pressure -- is the historically dominant mode of large-state dissolution and a leading indicator for future geopolitical realignments.
