Islam spread along the same Silk Road and Indian Ocean trade routes that Arab merchants already dominated — the commercial network became the evangelism network. Christianity spread across the Roman Empire's road network and administrative structure, then outlasted the empire itself by inheriting its institutional infrastructure. The First Crusade mobilized tens of thousands to march at their own expense because the spiritual incentive was more powerful than any paycheck — Pope Urban reframed a small request for mercenaries into a continental holy war. The Thirty Years War began as Catholic-Protestant religious conflict but became pure power struggle when Catholic France allied with Protestant Sweden. The Abbasid revolution united Persian converts, non-Arab Muslims, Shia partisans, and discontented Arab tribes — each dissatisfied for different reasons — into an unstoppable coalition. When the Abbasid Caliphate politically fragmented, Islamic civilization flourished more because independent courts competed to attract scholars.
