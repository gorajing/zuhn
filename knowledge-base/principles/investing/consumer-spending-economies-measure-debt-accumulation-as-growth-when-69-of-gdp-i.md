When consumer spending drives the majority of GDP and that spending is increasingly debt-funded, GDP growth becomes a measure of debt accumulation rather than genuine economic health. The pattern manifests across multiple signals: fast food sales plummeting as the canary in the economic coal mine, 3.8% GDP growth coexisting with record defaults and declining living standards, Canada's GDP growing while per-capita income stagnated for a decade, and America representing 4% of global population but 33% of consumer spending on increasingly borrowed money. Traditional metrics like GDP and unemployment obscure this dynamic because they were designed for production economies, not consumption economies running on credit.
