Alexander led cavalry charges and sustained wounds alongside his men, creating fanatical loyalty that held his army together across 20,000 miles. Napoleon seized a flag and personally led the charge at Arcole when his troops wavered. Nelson broke naval formation without orders at Cape St. Vincent. Desaix marched to the guns at Marengo without instructions. The pattern repeats: when leaders visibly share danger rather than commanding from safety, it generates a loyalty and morale effect that no amount of strategic brilliance from the rear can match. This is distinct from unified command — it is specifically about the force-multiplying effect of visible personal exposure to the same risks as those being led.
