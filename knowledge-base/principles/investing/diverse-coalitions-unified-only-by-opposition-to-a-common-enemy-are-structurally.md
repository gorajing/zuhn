The White armies in Russia's Civil War were monarchists, democrats, socialists, and nationalists who couldn't agree on anything beyond opposing Bolshevism and fought each other as much as the Reds. Coalitions held together by gold rather than shared survival instinct are brittle under battlefield pressure. This pattern -- victory-through-negative-unity dissolving into incoherence -- repeats from the 1848 revolutions to Napoleon's coalition opponents, because the organizational advantage of a shared enemy disappears precisely at the moment of success.
