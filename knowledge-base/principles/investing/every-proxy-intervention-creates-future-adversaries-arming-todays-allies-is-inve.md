US-armed Afghan fighters became Al-Qaeda, the installed Shah was overthrown into hostile revolution, and supported Saddam later invaded Kuwait. The pattern repeats because proxy relationships are transactional -- the moment the shared enemy disappears, alignment evaporates and the transferred capabilities become threats. Bin Laden's radicalization had a specific trigger: his offer to defend Saudi Arabia was rejected in favor of US military presence on Islamic holy land, demonstrating how intervention decisions cascade into decades of blowback.
