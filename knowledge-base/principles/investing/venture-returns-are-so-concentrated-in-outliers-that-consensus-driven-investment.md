Venture returns are so concentrated in a few outliers that non-outlier investments are economically irrelevant. Consensus-driven processes guarantee that the most outlier ideas get rejected because they require every partner to see potential that by definition most people miss. VCs review ~200 deals yearly, advance 8 to partners, diligence 4, and close 1. Larger fund sizes create a structural math problem where required ownership of winners becomes impossible, favoring smaller disciplined funds. VC illiquidity prevents investors from making behavioral mistakes by removing the option to sell during drawdowns. Mega funds follow outliers and bet big — they're precision machines, not diversified portfolios. The paradox: the discipline that produces good average returns (consensus, diversification) directly opposes the discipline that produces great venture returns (conviction on outliers).
