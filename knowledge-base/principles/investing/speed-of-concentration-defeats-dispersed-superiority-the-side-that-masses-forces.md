Napoleon's greatest weapon was not army size but the speed at which he moved before coalition forces could unite. Belisarius conquered the Vandal Kingdom in weeks by attacking before they assembled. At Leipzig, Napoleon finally faced four converging armies and could no longer use his signature move of defeating each in isolation — the pattern's failure mode is as instructive as its success. Prussia's army was considered Europe's finest based on 50-year-old victories but collapsed in a single day because it had not modernized. Spanish guerrillas couldn't win in open battle but made occupation so costly that France bled out. The principle applies beyond warfare: in markets, the first mover who concentrates resources on one beachhead before competitors coordinate outperforms the larger player who spreads thin.
