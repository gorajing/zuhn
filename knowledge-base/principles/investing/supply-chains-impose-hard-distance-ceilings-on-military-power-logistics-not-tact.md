From Napoleon's Russian disaster to Germany's Schlieffen Plan to Austria's Alpine supply problems, military campaigns consistently fail when they exceed their logistical reach. The constraint is not combat capability but the hard physical limit of how far supplies can travel reliably. Naval blockades exploit this same principle in reverse — Britain starved Germany's economy in WW1 without firing a shot on land, and Napoleon's Egyptian army was stranded when Nelson destroyed his fleet. The pattern is universal: force projection scales linearly, but supply chain difficulty scales exponentially with distance.
