Most people treat argument as either natural instinct or something to avoid. Both are wrong. Productive disagreement follows a learnable protocol: first verify the disagreement is real (not miscommunication), important (worth the relational cost), specific (not a proxy war), and aligned (both parties want resolution). Only then engage on substance. The hierarchy of argument quality ranges from name-calling (DH0) to refuting the central point (DH6), and lower forms can never be convincing regardless of execution. Treating disagreement as craft rather than combat creates a virtuous cycle: better arguments build confidence in disagreeing, which further improves argument quality.
