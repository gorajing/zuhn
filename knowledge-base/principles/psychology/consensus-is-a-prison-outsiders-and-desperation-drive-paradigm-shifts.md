The history of science reveals a brutal pattern: the stronger the consensus, the longer it takes truth to break through, and breakthroughs almost always come from outsiders, marginal figures, or acts of desperation rather than from the establishment. Aristotle's 'nature abhors a vacuum' persisted for over a thousand years on sheer intuitive authority. Michelson and Morley believed their own revolutionary null result was a failure. Senior scientists attacked younger colleagues for following the math on black holes. Meanwhile, Bohm and Aharonov -- exiled and marginalized -- overturned 200 years of physics precisely because orthodoxy couldn't constrain them, and Planck and Bohr's desperate ad hoc quantizations worked spectacularly before anyone understood why. The most confident, framework-driven experts are empirically the worst predictors (Tetlock). This principle applies far beyond science: wherever strong consensus exists, the corrective will likely come from the margins.
