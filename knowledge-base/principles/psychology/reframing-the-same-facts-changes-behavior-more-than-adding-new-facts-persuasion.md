Changing how a fact is presented often matters more than changing which facts are presented. Reframing a purchase as hours-of-life-worked creates visceral aversion that dollar amounts cannot; showing compounded future costs changes spending behavior; the debt snowball method wins through psychological momentum, not mathematical optimality. The same principle applies to persuasion: 'but' redirects attention, listing failure modes is easier than listing success paths, and inverting the question frame reliably produces different answers.
