Point particles are admittedly fictional yet predict electron properties to 14 decimal places. Black hole singularities with infinite density likely don't exist — they mark where general relativity's equations stop working, not where physics actually goes to infinity. Equations can have valid solutions that describe nothing real. The rule: models are tools, not territory. Use the ones that make correct predictions even when you know they're wrong, and treat mathematical pathologies (infinities, inconsistencies, unphysical solutions) as signals that you've reached the model's boundary, not as facts about reality. We default to simpler models not because they're better but because they're solvable — this creates systematic blind spots exactly where reality diverges most from the tractable case.
