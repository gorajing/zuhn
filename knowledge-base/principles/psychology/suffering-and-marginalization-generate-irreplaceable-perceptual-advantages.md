Those who have endured pain, exclusion, or proximity to death consistently develop capabilities inaccessible to the comfortable. African-Americans, Jewish Americans, gay Americans, and Soviet emigres dominate comedy because marginalization forges the outsider perspective that sees what insiders cannot. People who live alongside death develop deeper capacity for joy. Academic exiles like Bohm and Aharonov overturned 200 years of physics because their marginalization freed them from orthodoxy. Even synesthetes and the neurodivergent perceive reality differently in ways that enable extraordinary creative output. The pattern is universal: the system's rejects become its innovators because exclusion is a lens-grinding process.
