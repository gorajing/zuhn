We have perhaps 1-2% of ancient Mesopotamian texts, making our entire understanding of those civilizations based on a non-representative sample. Greek and Roman statues were vibrantly painted but our image of austere white marble — known for over a century to be wrong — still shapes perception. Finding children's shoes at Roman military bases overturned the assumption that posts were male-only. Archaeologists assumed quickite lumps in Roman concrete were errors until MIT discovered they were the self-healing mechanism that made structures last 2,000 years. A hairdresser, survivalists, and indigenous communities have all corrected academics because practical craft knowledge reveals what artifact analysis misses. Cuneiform was used for over 3,000 years — the most durable information technology ever — but when the handful of living scholars who can read it die, direct access to thousands of ancient texts dies with them. Indigenous peoples have accumulated millennia of ecological knowledge through direct observation that Western science is only now catching up to.
