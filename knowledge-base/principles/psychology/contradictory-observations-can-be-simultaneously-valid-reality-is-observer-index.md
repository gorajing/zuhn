Multiple domains independently demonstrate that mutually exclusive descriptions of the same phenomenon can both be correct, depending on the observer's reference frame or decision framework. An outside observer sees objects freeze forever at a black hole's horizon while the falling observer crosses without noticing -- both descriptions are simultaneously valid in general relativity. Newcomb's Paradox splits smart people 50/50 because evidence-based and causal reasoning are both internally consistent but yield opposite answers. Compatibilism shows that free will is functionally real from inside a deterministic system because the inability to predict from within makes the distinction operationally meaningless. The anthropic principle reveals that the Moon's improbable formation should be expected -- because we can only ask the question in universes where it happened. The implication: when two competent observers reach opposite conclusions, check whether they occupy different reference frames before assuming one is wrong.
