When agents -- whether nations, partners, or network nodes -- cannot reliably read each other's intentions, rational behavior defaults to worst-case preparation, which itself triggers escalation. The fundamental driver of international conflict is the inability to know future intentions; networks are poised on instability edges where one defection tips the system; and reducing humans to statistics (averaging 10,000 casualties a month) strips moral weight, enabling further escalation. Conversely, principled pre-commitment and individual storytelling counteract this: committing in advance to behavior yields better outcomes than case-by-case optimization, and telling individual stories resists statistical dehumanization. The pattern holds from geopolitics to relationships: opacity breeds hostility, transparency breeds cooperation.
