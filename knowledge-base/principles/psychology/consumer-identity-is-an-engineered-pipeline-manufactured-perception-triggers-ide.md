What consumers experience as taste, value, and quality is largely constructed through deliberate manipulation of sensory cues, payment structures, and branding signals. Products like Stanley tumblers succeed not as functional tools but as tribal membership signals — the fragrance industry captures 95%+ margin above production cost purely through identity value. Anti-consumption content generates as much commercial activity as consumption because it creates a new identity to perform. Once identity fusion occurs, switching costs become psychological rather than economic: Apple users stay not because of product superiority but because leaving means abandoning self-identity. The full pipeline — manufactured perception → identity performance → psychological fusion → evaluation collapse — means that rational consumer choice is the exception, not the norm, and the gap between constructed perception and reality widens as marketing sophistication increases.
