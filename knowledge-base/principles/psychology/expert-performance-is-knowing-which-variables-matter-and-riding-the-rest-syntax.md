Byron the archer controls arrow spine precisely but rides the wobble — knowing which variables matter and accepting noise on the rest is the essence of expert performance. Knowing programming syntax creates a dangerous illusion of competence because the high-value skill is algorithmic thinking, not language fluency. Programming languages shape how practitioners think, making adoption a cognitive transformation — which is why features take decades to go mainstream. The gap between good and great hackers is not intelligence but empathy. Making a tool part of your identity makes you worse at evaluating alternatives. Sustained proof-of-work builds a reputation signal that eventually outcompetes brand theatrics. Nasty little problems don't just waste time — they actively make you stupider because they lack the learnable patterns that build expertise.
