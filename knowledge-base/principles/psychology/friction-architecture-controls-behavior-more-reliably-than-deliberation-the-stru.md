Systems that impose friction (prior authorization denials with 81.7% overturn rates, upfront membership fees) control outcomes not through genuine evaluation but through attrition. Conversely, tools that flag unrealistic inputs at the moment of entry prevent more bad decisions than any post-hoc review. The implication is that redesigning choice architecture -- when and how decisions are presented -- dominates improving the quality of the decision-maker.
