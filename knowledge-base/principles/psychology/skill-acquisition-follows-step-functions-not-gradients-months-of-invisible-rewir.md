Destin Sandlin spent 8 months of daily practice with zero visible progress on the backwards bicycle, then could suddenly ride it one day — a discrete phase transition, not gradual improvement. His 6-year-old son learned the same skill in 2 weeks (16x faster), demonstrating that childhood neural plasticity creates fundamentally different acquisition dynamics, not just faster versions of adult learning. Critically, knowing the rule ('steering is reversed') provided zero benefit — declarative knowledge and procedural skill are neurologically separate. Once acquired, new procedural skills are extremely fragile and instantly collapse back to legacy algorithms under stress or distraction. This has direct implications: compress practice into shortest possible timeframes to force neurological adaptation, and protect newly acquired skills from interference.
