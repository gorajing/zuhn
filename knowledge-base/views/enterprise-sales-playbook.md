## Phase 0: Mindset & Identity

Before tactics, get the operating system right. Sales is not manipulation — it's emotional leadership.

### Core Frame: You Are the Doctor
- **Diagnose before prescribing** — start every conversation with: "Is it okay if I ask a few questions to figure out what's going on? Prescribing without diagnosis is malpractice." This sets the frame for the entire relationship.
- **Sales is conviction transfer** — 80% of the close is the conviction in your voice and energy. People buy your eyes, your passion, and your belief before they buy features. If you don't genuinely believe your product solves their problem, nothing else matters.
- **Treat your territory like your business** — top enterprise AEs ask: "If this was my company and I had to succeed, would I shut my laptop at 5pm?" The ones earning $300k+ treat their quota like their P&L.

### Mental Resilience
- **Roll your own objections** — the same technique you use to reframe a prospect's objection ("I hear you, can I challenge that belief?") works on internal objections like "I'm not good enough" or "this is too risky."
- **Front-load sacrifice for compounding** — in your 20s/early career, work 7am-7pm then prospect until 10pm. The repetitions compound into skills and relationships that let your 40-year-old self earn top-tier income with less grinding.
- **When you're hot, systematize** — good closers try to get hot; great closers never get cold because they've analyzed exactly what they do differently during hot streaks and systematized every detail.

## Phase 1: Targeting & Prospecting

The pipeline is everything. Targeting quality > email copy quality.

### Targeting
- **Quality over volume** — 100 perfectly targeted emails with high open rates beats 1,000 untargeted emails. No copywriting can make someone buy what they don't need.
- **Avoid the easy-conversation trap** — the most dangerous founder sales mistake is chasing friendly contacts, startups, and bottom-up users when your product is top-down. It creates an illusion of progress with zero revenue.
- **Cold email funnel math** — plan for ~800 emails per customer (50% open → 10% reply → 25% to demo → 10% to customer). That means 50+ personalized emails per day.

### Cold Outreach
- **Write emails like texts to a friend** — put their actual name in the "to" field, use emotional language ("I'd love to," "it would mean a lot"), read it aloud. Anything you wouldn't say face-to-face gets rewritten.
- **Find uncommon commonalities** — don't say "love what you're doing at [Company]." Find specific things you uniquely share: same college building, used their specific product feature, niche shared interest.
- **Be the pattern interrupt** — 99% of salespeople sound the same. Do something genuinely different, specific, and human. That's what makes prospects pay attention.

### Speed to Lead
- **Sub-60-second response = 4x close rate** — contacting a lead within 60 seconds of opt-in produces a 391% increase in close likelihood. 50% of prospects buy from whoever responds first.
- **Route best leads to best closers** — round-robin is fair but wasteful. Giving best leads to best closers can 5x a location's revenue output.

## Phase 2: Discovery & Qualification

Discovery is where deals are won or lost. Most reps skip it and jump to demo.

### The Discovery Framework
- **Lead with perspective, not product** — "I've thought through this problem at length and have a very specific perspective on solving it" beats "Let me walk you through our product." Nine out of ten founders miss this.
- **The breadcrumb technique** — when a prospect gives a vague answer ("our current tools aren't working"), don't move on. Pick up the breadcrumb: "Can you dig into what you're using right now and where the gaps are?" This surfaces real buying context.
- **Surface deprivation** — ask about everything they've already tried and failed at. This creates the psychological urgency (deprivation) that drives buying decisions. The pain cycle is the real pitch.

### Qualification (BANT+)
- **Pre-handle objections early** — ask "have you talked to your partner about this?" and "have you set aside budget to solve this?" in the first call. Every objection at the close is one you failed to surface earlier.
- **Vet your champion** — a 30-year-old trying to make a name for themselves who has never bought software can tank your deal. They demo without security approval, go to leadership without political cover, and get shut down by their CTO.
- **Single-threading kills deals** — never have just one contact in an account. One Oracle veteran lost five months of work when his sole champion went silent overnight. No other contacts = months wasted.

### Enterprise-Specific
- **Enterprise inverts the SMB cycle** — SMB: easy to motivate, hard to enable. Enterprise: hard to motivate (risk-averse, process-heavy), but easier to enable once committed.
- **Career risk is the real decision variable** — entire departments have staked careers on being experts in legacy systems. Even with clear ROI, the VP of IT who only knows on-premise sees your cloud solution as a career threat.
- **Be prescriptive about the buying process** — many champions want to buy but don't know how. It's your job to prescribe: architecture review → implementation planning → security assessment → executive sponsorship.

## Phase 3: Demo & Presentation

The demo is a movie, not a manual.

### Demo Structure
- **The demo is a movie script, not a feature tour** — start by recapping their problem (proves you listened), then show a personalized story of their user solving it — using their logo, their data, their team names — with magic moments of delight.
- **Sell architectural decisions, not feature matrices** — feature comparison matrices are one-sided and savvy buyers ignore them. Explain the specific decisions you made and why they matter (like Dropbox's delta sync winning NBC Universal).
- **Identify leverage points** — the same product sells 10 different ways to 10 different people. Your job is to find each prospect's specific pain and plug it in.

### Momentum Management
- **Sell call #2, not everything on call #1** — resist showing "one more thing." Enterprise sales is about selling the engagement. Getting the next meeting is more important than cramming everything into this one.
- **BAMFAM** — Book A Meeting From A Meeting. Schedule the next call before hanging up, send the invite while they're on the line, and commitment-lock: "If something comes up, could you text me beforehand?"

## Phase 4: Negotiation & Close

### Pricing
- **Pitch price with Starbucks-cashier energy** — present your price with casual certainty, like a cashier holding out the card reader. No flinching, no weird energy. "Here's the investment to get you there."
- **Price uncomfortably high** — quote a price that makes you uncomfortable, then observe the reaction. Even 5x overpricing often still converts. Higher prices signal seriousness and surface your most desperate (best) customers.

### Closing Frameworks
- **The CLOSER framework** — six repeatable steps: **C**larify why they're here, **L**abel the problem, **O**verview past pain, **S**ell the vacation (outcome, not process), **E**xplain away concerns, **R**einforce the decision.
- **Pre-call edification + BANT** — edify the closer and the business before the call so the prospect is pre-sold before the main conversation begins.

### After the Close
- **Solidify with positive future-pacing** — "People love texting me when they hit their first milestone — would you keep me in the loop?" This cements commitment, shifts you from rep to partner, and prevents buyer's remorse.
- **Document everything** — spend 5 minutes after every call logging everything: names, pain points, key issues, team structure. Repetition kills trust; recall builds it.

## Phase 5: Post-Sale & Expansion

The sale ends when they use your product habitually, not when the contract is signed.

### Implementation Ownership
- **Implementation is your responsibility** — Optimizely closed six-figure deals where customers never ran a single A/B test because they treated implementation as the customer's job. The fix: project-manage implementation like a high-priority internal project.
- **Joint sales-CS game tape reviews** — when sales hears onboarding calls and CS hears sales calls weekly, expectations align and the 5-10% of deals that would churn from misalignment are saved.

### Expansion
- **Ask for referrals at the first aha moment** — the moment a customer has their first "wow" result, immediately ask: "Is there one or two people just like you that we could help?" Gratitude and excitement peak at that moment, not months later. This alone can increase sales volume 30%.

### Pipeline Optimization
- **Show-up rate > close rate** — a 20% improvement in show-up rates produces the same revenue gain as a 20% close rate improvement, but is far easier to achieve through systematic reminder sequences.
- **Real-time feedback in role-play** — stopping mid-role-play to correct and repeat trains faster than post-hoc notes. Speed of feedback is inversely correlated with reps needed to learn.

## Phase 6: Founder Sales (Pre-PMF)

If you're pre-product-market-fit, everything above still applies — plus these founder-specific rules.

- **Founders must sell pre-PMF** — sales at this stage is fundamentally entrepreneurial. It requires your vision, domain expertise, and conviction, which no hired sales rep can replicate.
- **Optimize for learning, not unit economics** — early sales conversations are research. Price uncomfortably high, watch reactions, and extract maximum information about what the market actually values.
- **Enterprise for founders** — the Startup School sequence: find 5 target companies → cold outreach → discovery → demo → negotiate → close. The key insight: selling call #2 matters more than perfecting call #1.
